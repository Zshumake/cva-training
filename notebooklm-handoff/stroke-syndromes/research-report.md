## SECTION 1: MIDDLE CEREBRAL ARTERY (MCA) SYNDROME

### Anatomy and Vascular Territory
The MCA is the largest branch of the internal carotid artery and the most commonly affected artery in ischemic stroke. It supplies the lateral surface of the cerebral hemisphere including frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes, and extends deep to supply the putamen and posterior limb of the internal capsule via lenticulostriate branches. The MCA divides into superior and inferior divisions at the sylvian fissure. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo, Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation Board Review, Ch. 1; DeLisa, PM&R Principles and Practice, 6th Ed, Ch. 18; StatPearls NBK556132

### Motor and Sensory Deficits
- Contralateral hemiparesis: face and arm GREATER THAN leg (leg representation is medial/ACA territory) [BOARD-CORE]
- Contralateral hemisensory loss: face and arm greater than leg [BOARD-CORE]
- Contralateral homonymous hemianopia (optic radiations) [BOARD-CORE]
- Head and eyes deviated TOWARD the lesion (frontal eye field damage) [BOARD-CORE]
- Upper motor neuron pattern: initially flaccid, then spastic with flexor synergy in arm and extensor synergy in leg [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; app content stroke_syndromes_content.dart

### Dominant Hemisphere MCA (Usually Left)
- Broca's aphasia (inferior frontal gyrus): nonfluent, effortful speech; comprehension relatively preserved; frustrated patient [BOARD-CORE]
- Wernicke's aphasia (superior temporal gyrus): fluent but meaningless speech; poor comprehension; unaware patient [BOARD-CORE]
- Global aphasia (large MCA infarct): both expressive and receptive language devastated [BOARD-CORE]
- Conduction aphasia (arcuate fasciculus): fluent speech, good comprehension, poor repetition [BOARD-CORE]
- Gerstmann syndrome (angular gyrus): agraphia, acalculia, finger agnosia, left-right confusion [BOARD-CORE]
- Apraxia (ideomotor): cannot pantomime learned motor acts on command [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18; app content

### Non-Dominant Hemisphere MCA (Usually Right)
- Left hemispatial neglect: more severe and more persistent than right neglect [BOARD-CORE]
- Anosognosia: denial or unawareness of deficit [BOARD-CORE]
- Aprosodia: loss of emotional tone/melody in speech [BOARD-CORE]
- Constructional apraxia: difficulty with spatial tasks [BOARD-CORE]
- Dressing apraxia: difficulty dressing contralateral side [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18; app content

### MCA Branch Syndromes

| Branch | Territory | Deficits |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Superior division | Frontal and anterior parietal | Broca's aphasia, contralateral face/arm weakness, face/arm sensory loss |
| Inferior division | Temporal and posterior parietal | Wernicke's aphasia, homonymous hemianopia, Gerstmann syndrome |
| Lenticulostriate branches | Basal ganglia, internal capsule | Pure motor hemiparesis (face=arm=leg), no cortical signs |
| Complete MCA | Entire MCA territory | Global aphasia, hemiplegia, hemisensory loss, hemianopia, gaze deviation |

[BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1, Table 1-2B; app content

### Board Pearl: Gerstmann Tetrad
The four components (agraphia, acalculia, finger agnosia, left-right confusion) localize to the dominant (left) angular gyrus (Brodmann area 39). If a question lists any 3-4 of these deficits, the answer is dominant angular gyrus or dominant parietal lobe. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** App content; Cuccurullo Ch. 1

### Rehabilitation Prognosis (MCA)
- Arm recovery is generally worse than leg recovery due to the MCA territory predominantly affecting upper extremity motor cortex [BOARD-CORE]
- Patients who survive regain control of head and eye movements with return of normal consciousness [BOARD-CORE]
- Severe deficits in motor, visuospatial, and language function usually persist [BOARD-CORE]
- Left neglect patients have worse rehabilitation outcomes due to anosognosia [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** DeLisa Ch. 18; app content

## SECTION 2: ANTERIOR CEREBRAL ARTERY (ACA) SYNDROME

### Anatomy and Vascular Territory
The ACA supplies the medial surface of the frontal and parietal lobes. It accounts for approximately 3-5% of all strokes, making it far less common than MCA strokes. If occlusion is proximal to the anterior communicating artery, it is usually well tolerated due to collateral supply from the contralateral ACA. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18; StatPearls

### Clinical Features
- Contralateral hemiparesis: LEG greater than arm or face (opposite of MCA pattern) [BOARD-CORE]
- Contralateral hemisensory loss: leg greater than arm [BOARD-CORE]
- Urinary incontinence: damage to medial frontal micturition center (paracentral lobule) [BOARD-CORE]
- Gait apraxia: "frontal gait disorder" with magnetic/shuffling gait [BOARD-CORE]
- Personality changes: apathy, abulia, flat affect (medial frontal/cingulate damage) [BOARD-CORE]
- Transcortical motor aphasia (dominant hemisphere): reduced spontaneous speech but preserved repetition [BOARD-CORE]
- Grasp reflex and paratonic rigidity (gegenhalten) [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18; app content

### Board Pearl: ACA vs MCA Pattern
ACA = leg > arm/face. MCA = face/arm > leg. The triad of leg weakness + incontinence + gait apraxia is classic ACA. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** App content; Cuccurullo Ch. 1

### Bilateral ACA Syndrome
- Akinetic mutism: awake but no voluntary movement or speech [BOARD-CORE]
- Abulia: lack of will or initiative [BOARD-CORE]
- Bilateral leg weakness/paraparesis [BOARD-CORE]
- Alien hand syndrome: one hand acts independently (corpus callosum involvement) [BOARD-CORE]
- Occurs when both ACAs arise from a single trunk (anatomical variant) [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** App content; DeLisa Ch. 18

### ACA vs MCA Comparison Table

| Feature | ACA Stroke | MCA Stroke |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Motor deficit | Leg > arm/face | Face/arm > leg |
| Sensory deficit | Leg > arm | Face/arm > leg |
| Language | Transcortical motor aphasia (if dominant) | Broca's, Wernicke's, global, or conduction |
| Behavioral | Abulia, apathy, personality change | Neglect (non-dominant), anosognosia |
| Other key features | Urinary incontinence, gait apraxia | Gaze deviation toward lesion, hemianopia |
| Frequency | ~3-5% of strokes | Most common stroke syndrome |

[BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 3: POSTERIOR CEREBRAL ARTERY (PCA) SYNDROME

### Anatomy and Vascular Territory
The PCA supplies the occipital lobe (primary visual cortex), inferior temporal lobe, thalamus, and portions of the midbrain. Anatomically, 70% of the time both PCAs arise from the basilar artery; 20-25% have one PCA from basilar and one from ICA; 5-10% have both from carotids. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; app content; StatPearls NBK532296

### Clinical Features
- Contralateral homonymous hemianopia WITH macular sparing (dual blood supply from MCA and PCA) [BOARD-CORE]
- Visual agnosia: inability to recognize objects by sight [BOARD-CORE]
- Prosopagnosia: inability to recognize familiar faces (bilateral or right PCA) [BOARD-CORE]
- Color anomia and color agnosia [BOARD-CORE]
- Memory impairment (hippocampus/medial temporal involvement) [BOARD-CORE]
- Minimal or no motor deficit (motor cortex is in MCA/ACA territory) [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18; app content

### Board Pearl: Macular Sparing
Macular sparing in homonymous hemianopia is the hallmark of PCA (occipital cortex) stroke. The macula has dual blood supply from both PCA and MCA, distinguishing this from optic radiation lesions. [BOARD-CORE]

### Special PCA Syndromes

**Anton Syndrome** (Bilateral PCA) [BOARD-CORE]
- Cortical blindness with DENIAL of blindness
- Patient confabulates visual experiences
- Pupillary light reflexes are PRESERVED (brainstem reflex arc intact)
- Optokinetic nystagmus is ABSENT (distinguishes from hysterical blindness)

**Alexia Without Agraphia** (Dominant PCA + splenium) [BOARD-CORE]
- Disconnection syndrome: can write but cannot read what was written
- Left visual cortex destroyed AND visual input from intact right cortex cannot cross damaged splenium to reach left angular gyrus (reading center)

**Balint Syndrome** (Bilateral parieto-occipital) [BOARD-CORE]
- Simultanagnosia: can only perceive one object at a time
- Optic ataxia: cannot accurately reach for objects under visual guidance
- Ocular apraxia: cannot voluntarily direct gaze to target
- Can occur from bilateral watershed infarcts (MCA-PCA border zones) after hypotensive episodes

**Dejerine-Roussy Syndrome** (Thalamic PCA) [BOARD-CORE]
- Central post-stroke pain from thalamic (VPL nucleus) infarction
- Initially presents as contralateral sensory loss (acute phase)
- Weeks to months later: severe contralateral burning, lancinating pain
- Allodynia: pain from light touch
- Affects up to 8% of stroke survivors
- First-line treatment: amitriptyline or lamotrigine; second-line: gabapentin/pregabalin
- Motor cortex stimulation (MCS) and rTMS targeting M1 have emerging evidence
- Opioids are NOT supported by evidence for CPSP
- Hyperbaric oxygen therapy shows promise in case reports (2024)

**Source:** App content; StatPearls NBK554490; Frontiers in Neurology 2024; Journal of Pain 2024 systematic review

[BOARD-CORE for classic features; BOARD-LIKELY for rTMS/MCS treatment; BOARD-EMERGING for HBOT]

**Parinaud Syndrome** (Dorsal midbrain) [BOARD-CORE]
- Paralysis of upgaze, light-near dissociation, convergence-retraction nystagmus

**Top of Basilar Syndrome** [BOARD-CORE]
- Occlusion of rostral basilar artery: bilateral PCA territory infarction
- Cortical blindness (often with Anton syndrome)
- CN III palsies, vertical gaze palsy
- Thalamic infarction: altered consciousness, memory loss
- Peduncular hallucinosis (vivid visual hallucinations)
- May present with sudden coma (reticular activating system involvement)
- Often embolic (cardiac or artery-to-artery)
- 2025 data: Patients receiving both IVT and mechanical thrombectomy had better outcomes (median mRS 4 vs 5 for MT only)

**Source:** App content; Neurocritical Care 2025 (Springer); PMC 10550261

[BOARD-CORE for classic features; BOARD-LIKELY for thrombectomy outcomes data]

### PCA Territory Deficits by Structure

| Structure | Deficit |
|-----------|---------|
| Primary visual cortex (calcarine) | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing |
| Visual association cortex | Visual agnosia, prosopagnosia |
| Hippocampus / medial temporal | Anterograde memory impairment |
| Thalamus (VPL nucleus) | Dejerine-Roussy syndrome (thalamic pain) |
| Splenium of corpus callosum | Alexia without agraphia (disconnection) |
| Bilateral occipital cortex | Anton syndrome (cortical blindness with denial) |
| Midbrain (perforators) | Weber syndrome, Parinaud syndrome |

[BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 4: BRAINSTEM STROKE SYNDROMES

### General Principles
Brainstem strokes produce characteristic "crossed" or "alternating" findings: ipsilateral cranial nerve deficits with contralateral long tract signs (motor and sensory). This pattern occurs because cranial nerve nuclei are at the level of the lesion while descending/ascending tracts cross at the medullary pyramids. [BOARD-CORE]

**Board Pearl:** Ipsilateral cranial nerve deficit + contralateral hemiparesis = brainstem lesion. This crossed pattern is PATHOGNOMONIC. If all deficits are on the same side, the lesion is above the brainstem (cortical or subcortical). [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; app content

### Rule of 4 -- Brainstem Localization

**4 Medial structures (all start with M):**
1. Motor pathway (corticospinal tract)
2. Medial lemniscus (proprioception/vibration)
3. MLF (medial longitudinal fasciculus -- conjugate gaze)
4. Motor nucleus of CN (CN III, VI, XII are medial)

**4 Lateral structures (all start with S):**
1. Spinothalamic tract (pain/temperature)
2. Spinocerebellar pathways (cerebellar peduncles)
3. Sympathetic tract (Horner syndrome)
4. Sensory nucleus of CN V (facial pain/temperature)

**CN number tells the level:** III/IV = midbrain, V-VIII = pons, IX-XII = medulla [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** App content; Cuccurullo Ch. 1

### Medial vs Lateral Brainstem Syndromes

| Feature | Medial Syndromes | Lateral Syndromes |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Artery involved | Paramedian perforators (anterior spinal, basilar perforators) | Circumferential arteries (PICA, AICA, SCA) |
| Motor deficit | YES -- contralateral hemiparesis | NO motor weakness (corticospinal tract spared) |
| Sensory deficit | Contralateral proprioception/vibration loss (medial lemniscus) | Contralateral pain/temperature loss (spinothalamic) |
| Cranial nerve | Medial CNs: III (midbrain), VI (pons), XII (medulla) | Lateral CNs: V, VII, VIII, IX, X + sympathetics |
| Cerebellar signs | Usually absent | Usually present |
| Classic example | Medial medullary syndrome | Wallenberg syndrome |

[BOARD-CORE]

### Wallenberg Syndrome (Lateral Medullary)

The most commonly tested brainstem stroke syndrome on PM&R boards. Caused by PICA (most common) or vertebral artery occlusion. [BOARD-CORE]

**Ipsilateral findings:**
- Facial pain and temperature loss (descending spinal trigeminal nucleus)
- Horner syndrome: miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis (descending sympathetics)
- Cerebellar ataxia (inferior cerebellar peduncle)
- Dysphagia and hoarseness (nucleus ambiguus, CN IX/X)
- Vertigo, nystagmus, nausea (vestibular nuclei)

**Contralateral findings:**
- Body pain and temperature loss (spinothalamic tract)

**Critical Board Point:** NO motor weakness -- the corticospinal tract runs in the medial medulla and is spared in lateral lesions. [BOARD-CORE]

**Arteries involved (by frequency):** Vertebral artery (8 in 10 cases), PICA, superior lateral medullary artery, middle lateral medullary artery, inferior lateral medullary artery. [BOARD-CORE]

**Prognosis:** Generally good functional outcome compared to most other stroke syndromes. Most patients can return to satisfactory ADL performance. Recovery occurs via compensation from unaffected medullary structures. Dysphagia and vertigo may persist longest. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1, Table 1-2A; DeLisa Ch. 18; app content; StatPearls NBK551670

### Medial Medullary Syndrome (Dejerine Syndrome)

Caused by occlusion of paramedian branches of anterior spinal artery or vertebral artery. Rare (ratio of medial to lateral medullary infarct approximately 1:10). [BOARD-CORE]

**Classic triad:**
1. Ipsilateral tongue deviation (CN XII -- hypoglossal palsy, tongue deviates toward the lesion)
2. Contralateral hemiparesis (corticospinal tract)
3. Contralateral loss of proprioception and vibration (medial lemniscus)

**Imaging:** MRI with DWI is highly sensitive (95-100%) and can detect lesions within 30 minutes of onset. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1, Table 1-2B; app content; StatPearls NBK560590

### Weber Syndrome (Ventral Midbrain)

Occlusion of interpeduncular branches of PCA or posterior choroidal artery. [BOARD-CORE]
- Ipsilateral CN III palsy (eye "down and out," dilated pupil)
- Contralateral hemiplegia (cerebral peduncle)

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18; StatPearls NBK559158

### Benedikt Syndrome (Tegmental Midbrain)

Occlusion of interpeduncular branches affecting midbrain tegmentum. [BOARD-CORE]
- Ipsilateral CN III palsy
- Contralateral tremor/chorea/athetosis (red nucleus involvement)
- Contralateral hemisensory loss (medial lemniscus)

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1; app content

### Claude Syndrome (Midbrain)

Similar to Weber but involves red nucleus and brachium conjunctivum instead of cerebral peduncle. [BOARD-CORE]
- Ipsilateral CN III palsy
- Contralateral ataxia (superior cerebellar peduncle/red nucleus)
- NO contralateral hemiplegia (cerebral peduncle spared)
- 2024 data: Claude syndrome was the most common midbrain infarct pattern (43.8% of isolated mesencephalic infarcts)

**Source:** Neurology India 2024; Cuccurullo Ch. 1

[BOARD-CORE for classic features; BOARD-LIKELY for epidemiologic data]

### Millard-Gubler Syndrome (Ventral Pons)

Occlusion of circumferential branches of basilar artery. [BOARD-CORE]
- Ipsilateral CN VI (lateral rectus palsy) and CN VII (facial paralysis)
- Contralateral hemiplegia
- Extension to medial lemniscus = Raymond-Foville Syndrome (with gaze palsy toward side of lesion)

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1, Tables 1-2B and 1-3

### Locked-In Syndrome

Caused by bilateral ventral pontine infarction (basilar artery occlusion). [BOARD-CORE]
- Complete quadriplegia (bilateral corticospinal tract destruction)
- Anarthria (bilateral corticobulbar tract destruction)
- Preserved consciousness and cognition (reticular activating system is dorsal, spared)
- Only voluntary movements: vertical eye movements and blinking (CN III nucleus is in midbrain, above the lesion)
- EEG shows normal waking patterns (distinguishes from coma or vegetative state)
- Must NOT be confused with coma -- patient is fully aware

**Rehabilitation outcomes (recent data):**
- 21% show evidence of motor recovery after 3-6 months rehabilitation
- 42% regain ability to swallow
- 28% regain verbal communication
- 50% weaned off ventilator
- 2024 Mayo data: 33.5% discharged to acute inpatient rehabilitation; 29.8% died or discharged to hospice
- If medically stabilized and survives first year, 5-year survival rate can reach 86%

**Source:** App content; DeLisa Ch. 18; StatPearls NBK559026; Journal of Stroke and Cerebrovascular Diseases 2024

[BOARD-CORE for classic features; BOARD-LIKELY for rehabilitation outcome data]

### Brainstem Syndromes Summary Table

| Syndrome | Artery/Location | Key Features |
|----------|----------------|--------------|
| Wallenberg | PICA or vertebral | Ipsilateral: facial pain/temp loss, Horner's, ataxia, dysphagia, vertigo. Contralateral: body pain/temp loss. NO motor weakness |
| Medial medullary | Anterior spinal artery | Ipsilateral CN XII. Contralateral: hemiparesis, proprioception/vibration loss |
| Weber | PCA perforators (ventral midbrain) | Ipsilateral CN III. Contralateral hemiparesis |
| Benedikt | PCA perforators (tegmental midbrain) | Ipsilateral CN III. Contralateral tremor/chorea, hemisensory loss |
| Claude | PCA perforators (midbrain) | Ipsilateral CN III. Contralateral ataxia |
| Millard-Gubler | Basilar perforators (ventral pons) | Ipsilateral CN VI + VII. Contralateral hemiparesis |
| Locked-in | Basilar artery (ventral pons) | Complete quadriplegia, anarthria, preserved consciousness. Only vertical eye movements/blinking |

[BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 5: LACUNAR STROKE SYNDROMES

### General Principles
Lacunar strokes result from occlusion of small, deep penetrating arteries causing infarcts less than 15 mm. They constitute approximately 13-20% of all strokes. The usual etiology is lipohyalinosis of small perforating arteries or microatheroma. Hypertension is the #1 risk factor (correlation up to 81%). [BOARD-CORE]

**DEFINING feature:** ABSENCE of cortical signs -- no aphasia, no neglect, no hemianopia, no seizures, no altered consciousness. If any cortical sign is present, it is NOT a lacunar syndrome. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1, Table 1-4; DeLisa Ch. 18; app content; StatPearls NBK563216

### Five Classic Lacunar Syndromes

**1. Pure Motor Hemiparesis** [BOARD-CORE]
- Most common lacunar syndrome (33-50%)
- Location: Posterior limb of internal capsule (most common) or basis pontis
- Presentation: Equal weakness of face, arm, and leg contralaterally
- NO sensory loss
- Proportional (face=arm=leg) weakness distinguishes from MCA

**2. Pure Sensory Stroke** [BOARD-CORE]
- Location: VPL nucleus of thalamus (thalamic perforators from PCA)
- Presentation: Numbness/paresthesias of contralateral face, arm, and leg
- All sensory modalities affected
- NO motor deficit

**3. Ataxic Hemiparesis** [BOARD-CORE]
- Second most frequent lacunar syndrome
- Location: Posterior limb of internal capsule, basis pontis, or corona radiata
- Presentation: Weakness + cerebellar ataxia on SAME side (contralateral to lesion)

**4. Dysarthria-Clumsy Hand Syndrome** [BOARD-CORE]
- Location: Basis pontis or genu of internal capsule
- Presentation: Dysarthria + contralateral hand weakness and clumsiness
- Hand dysfunction out of proportion to other deficits

**5. Sensorimotor Stroke** [BOARD-CORE]
- Largest lacunar infarct type
- Location: Thalamocapsular junction (junction of internal capsule and thalamus)
- Presentation: Combined motor AND sensory loss, contralateral face/arm/leg

### Lacunar vs Cortical Stroke Comparison

| Feature | Lacunar Stroke | Cortical Stroke |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|
| Size | < 15 mm | Variable, often large |
| Motor pattern | Face = arm = leg equally | Face/arm > leg (MCA) or leg > arm (ACA) |
| Aphasia | Absent | Present if dominant hemisphere |
| Neglect | Absent | Present if non-dominant |
| Visual field | Absent | Homonymous hemianopia common |
| Seizures | Absent (subcortical) | May occur |
| Consciousness | Preserved | May be impaired |
| Prognosis | Generally good | Variable, often worse |
| Imaging | CT may be normal; MRI superior | CT usually positive |
| Prevention | Antiplatelet + aggressive BP control | Varies by etiology |

[BOARD-CORE]

### Early Neurological Deterioration in Lacunar Stroke
Recent 2024 research highlights that early neurological deterioration (END) occurs in a subset of lacunar stroke patients and may be related to branch atheromatous disease rather than classical lipohyalinosis. This is an area of active investigation. [BOARD-EMERGING]

**Source:** PMC 11669265 (2024)

### Clinical Pearl: Lacunar State (Etat Lacunaire)
Multiple lacunar infarcts over time cause "lacunar state" with vascular parkinsonism, pseudobulbar palsy (emotional lability, dysphagia, dysarthria), and stepwise cognitive decline. [BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 6: CEREBELLAR ARTERY SYNDROMES (PICA, AICA, SCA)

### Overview
The cerebellum is supplied by three paired arteries: PICA (from vertebral), AICA (from basilar), SCA (from basilar). PICA strokes are most common, AICA strokes are least common. [BOARD-CORE]

### PICA Territory
- Supplies: lateral medulla, inferior cerebellum, inferior cerebellar peduncle
- Presentation: vertigo, headache (more common than AICA/SCA), ataxia, inability to walk/stand
- May produce Wallenberg syndrome (lateral medullary involvement)
- Headache is 20-30% more common than in AICA or SCA infarcts

[BOARD-CORE]

### AICA Territory
- Supplies: anterior inferior cerebellum, middle cerebellar peduncle, CN VII and VIII
- Presentation: dysmetria, unilateral hearing loss/tinnitus (CN VIII), ipsilateral facial paralysis (CN VII), contralateral pain/temperature loss
- Brainstem symptoms are MORE PROMINENT than in SCA/PICA territory infarcts
- Key distinguishing feature: hearing loss (labyrinthine artery is branch of AICA)

[BOARD-CORE]

### SCA Territory
- Supplies: superior cerebellum, cerebellar nuclei, superior cerebellar peduncles
- Presentation: more ataxia, dysarthria, nystagmus; less vertigo/headache/vomiting than PICA
- Obtunded consciousness and severe ataxia more frequent than in PICA
- May produce ipsilateral Horner syndrome + contralateral pain/temperature loss

[BOARD-CORE]

### Board Pearl: PICA vs AICA vs SCA
- PICA = vertigo + headache + Wallenberg features
- AICA = hearing loss + facial palsy + brainstem signs
- SCA = severe ataxia + dysarthria + obtundation

[BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** StatPearls NBK470416; Brains 2024 14(8):763; AHA Stroke 2014

## SECTION 7: WATERSHED (BORDER ZONE) INFARCTS

### Classification
Watershed infarcts occur at the junction between two main arterial territories, constituting approximately 10% of all brain infarcts. [BOARD-CORE]

**External (cortical) border zone:** Between ACA-MCA or MCA-PCA territories on the cortical surface [BOARD-CORE]
- Primarily caused by microembolic phenomena
- May produce "man in a barrel" syndrome (bilateral ACA-MCA watershed): bilateral arm weakness with preserved leg and face function

**Internal (subcortical) border zone:** Deep white matter between lenticulostriate perforators and cortical branches [BOARD-CORE]
- Primarily caused by hemodynamic compromise
- Rosary-like pattern of white matter infarctions parallel to lateral ventricle on imaging
- Associated with severe hemodynamic impairment

### Risk Factors and Mechanism
- Hemodynamic disturbances: postural hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias, excessive antihypertensive use, cardiac surgery (especially in elderly) [BOARD-CORE]
- Severe carotid stenosis with poor collaterals [BOARD-CORE]
- Syncope or near-syncope at stroke onset supports hemodynamic mechanism [BOARD-CORE]

### Board Pearl: Watershed Infarct Patterns
- ACA-MCA watershed: proximal arm/shoulder weakness ("man in a barrel")
- MCA-PCA watershed: visual field deficits, Balint syndrome (bilateral)
- Internal watershed: cognitive decline, "rosary bead" white matter lesions

[BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** RadioGraphics 2011; PMC 10478671 (2023); AHA Stroke

## SECTION 8: ANTERIOR CHOROIDAL ARTERY SYNDROME

### Anatomy
Branch of the ICA supplying posterior limb of internal capsule (lateral portion), lateral thalamus, and lateral geniculate body/optic tract. [BOARD-CORE]

### Classic Triad
1. Contralateral hemiplegia (posterior limb of internal capsule)
2. Contralateral hemisensory loss (lateral thalamus)
3. Contralateral homonymous hemianopia (lateral geniculate body)

### Board Pearl
May mimic MCA stroke, but anterior choroidal tends to produce equal face/arm/leg deficits AND lacks cortical signs (no aphasia, no neglect). Full syndrome is relatively uncommon due to collateral supply. [BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** App content; Cuccurullo Ch. 1

## SECTION 9: VISUAL FIELD DEFICITS BY LESION LOCATION

| Lesion | Visual Field Deficit | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Optic nerve | Monocular vision loss (ipsilateral) | Only one eye affected; APD present |
| Optic chiasm (central) | Bitemporal hemianopia | Pituitary tumor or AComm aneurysm |
| Optic tract | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia (incongruent) | Incongruent = fields don't match between eyes |
| Temporal lobe (Meyer's loop) | Contralateral superior quadrantanopia ("pie in the sky") | MCA inferior division territory |
| Parietal lobe | Contralateral inferior quadrantanopia ("pie on the floor") | MCA superior division territory |
| Occipital cortex (PCA) | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia WITH macular sparing | Macular sparing = PCA lesion |
| Bilateral occipital | Complete cortical blindness (Anton if with denial) | Pupillary reflexes intact |

[BOARD-CORE]

**Source:** Cuccurullo Ch. 1, Figure 1-11; app content

## SECTION 10: HIGH-YIELD BOARD REVIEW SUMMARY

### Most Commonly Tested Concepts (PM&R Boards)
1. MCA = face/arm > leg; ACA = leg > arm/face [BOARD-CORE]
2. Dominant MCA = aphasia; Non-dominant MCA = neglect [BOARD-CORE]
3. Gerstmann syndrome = dominant angular gyrus [BOARD-CORE]
4. Wallenberg = NO motor weakness + crossed pain/temp + Horner + ataxia + dysphagia [BOARD-CORE]
5. Crossed findings = brainstem lesion [BOARD-CORE]
6. Lacunar = NO cortical signs; pure motor is most common [BOARD-CORE]
7. Macular sparing = PCA (occipital) stroke [BOARD-CORE]
8. Locked-in = bilateral ventral pons; preserved consciousness and vertical eye movements [BOARD-CORE]
9. Anton syndrome = cortical blindness + denial + preserved pupillary reflexes [BOARD-CORE]
10. Alexia without agraphia = dominant PCA + splenium disconnection [BOARD-CORE]

### Medial vs Lateral Quick Reference
- **Medial = 4 Ms:** Motor, Medial lemniscus, MLF, Medial CN (III, VI, XII)
- **Lateral = 4 Ss:** Spinothalamic, Spinocerebellar, Sympathetic, Sensory CN V

### Brainstem Level by CN
- **Midbrain:** CN III, IV
- **Pons:** CN V, VI, VII, VIII
