# SECTION 1: BRAIN IMAGING IN STROKE

## 1.1 Non-Contrast CT (NCCT)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Non-contrast CT is the first-line imaging study for acute stroke evaluation. It is fast (5-10 minutes), widely available 24/7, and the gold standard for detecting acute hemorrhage.

**Key imaging findings:**
- Acute hemorrhage appears BRIGHT (hyperdense/white) on CT — detectable immediately. Sensitivity for acute hemorrhage: >95%.
- Acute ischemia appears DARK (hypodense) — may NOT be visible for 6-12 hours. Sensitivity for ischemic stroke in first 6 hours: only approximately 60%.
- CT may be completely normal in the first 6 hours of ischemic stroke.
- Early ischemic signs: loss of gray-white matter differentiation, sulcal effacement, hyperdense MCA sign (indicating clot in the MCA).

**Board Pearl — CT Density Rules:** On CT: DARK = ischemic (hypodense). BRIGHT = hemorrhagic (hyperdense). Mnemonic: "Blood is Bright." A normal CT does NOT rule out ischemic stroke in the acute setting. The hyperdense MCA sign (bright MCA on CT) indicates acute thrombus and is an early sign of large vessel occlusion.

**From Cuccurullo (Chapter 1, Diagnostic Studies):** If an intracerebral hemorrhage is suspected, a head CT without contrast is the study of choice. This avoids confusing blood with contrast, as both appear white on CT scan. For ischemic infarction, the infarcted area appears as a hypodense (dark) lesion usually after 24 to 48 hours. Subtle CT changes may be seen early with large infarcts at 3-6 hours, such as obscuration of gray-white matter junction, sulcal effacement, or early hypodensity.

**From DeLisa (Chapter 18, Evaluation of Stroke Etiology):** A basic evaluation of stroke cause includes cerebral imaging (CT or preferably MRI), an electrocardiogram, noninvasive carotid studies, and an echocardiogram.

## 1.2 MRI with Diffusion-Weighted Imaging (DWI)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

MRI with DWI is the most sensitive imaging modality for detecting acute ischemic stroke. DWI detects restricted diffusion (cytotoxic edema) within minutes of stroke onset.

**Key findings:**
- Restricted diffusion appears BRIGHT on DWI and DARK on ADC map (confirms true restriction vs. T2 shine-through).
- Sensitivity for acute ischemic stroke: >95% (vs approximately 60% for CT).
- Can differentiate acute from chronic infarcts using DWI/ADC correlation.
- FLAIR sequence helps determine stroke timing (DWI+/FLAIR- suggests <4.5 hours — "DWI-FLAIR mismatch").
- Gradient echo (GRE) or susceptibility-weighted imaging (SWI) detects hemorrhage (blood products appear DARK with blooming artifact). SWI is more sensitive than GRE.

**Limitations:** Longer acquisition time (20-45 minutes), less available especially overnight, contraindicated with certain implants, motion artifact.

**From Cuccurullo:** MRI is more sensitive than CT scan in detecting acute ischemic infarcts (including small lacunes and posterior fossa infarcts). DWI MRI has emerged as the most sensitive and specific imaging technique for acute infarct, far better than CT or any other MRI sequence. DWI has a high sensitivity and specificity for detecting infarcted regions, even within minutes of symptom onset.

### MRI Sequences in Stroke Evaluation

| Sequence | What It Shows | Stroke Application |
|---|---|---|
| DWI | Restricted water diffusion (cytotoxic edema) | Acute ischemia — BRIGHT within minutes; most sensitive for acute infarct |
| ADC Map | Apparent diffusion coefficient | True restriction = DARK on ADC (vs T2 shine-through = bright on ADC) |
| FLAIR | T2 with CSF signal suppressed | Subacute/chronic infarcts; used in DWI-FLAIR mismatch |
| T1 | Anatomic detail | Fat-suppressed T1 for arterial dissection (intramural hematoma = bright) |
| T2 | Fluid bright; edema detection | Vasogenic edema, chronic infarcts |
| GRE/SWI | Blood products DARK (blooming) | Hemorrhage detection (acute and chronic microbleeds) |
| MRA | Vascular anatomy | LVO, stenosis, aneurysm, dissection |

### CT vs MRI Comparison

| Feature | CT | MRI |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | 5-10 minutes | 20-45 minutes |
| Availability | Widely available 24/7 | Less available overnight |
| Acute hemorrhage | Excellent (>95%) | Good (GRE/SWI) |
| Acute ischemia | Poor first 6 hours (approximately 60%) | Excellent with DWI (>95%) |
| Posterior fossa | Limited (bone artifact) | Superior |
| TIA evaluation | Often normal | DWI may show small infarcts |

## 1.3 CT Perfusion (CTP)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

CT perfusion maps cerebral blood flow (CBF), cerebral blood volume (CBV), mean transit time (MTT), and time to peak (TTP). It identifies the ischemic penumbra (salvageable tissue) vs. infarct core.

**Core vs. Penumbra:**
- Penumbra: decreased CBF but maintained CBV (autoregulatory vasodilation) — salvageable tissue.
- Core: decreased CBF AND decreased CBV — irreversible infarction.
- Mismatch between core and penumbra supports treatment up to 24 hours in select patients.

**Board Pearl — Core vs Penumbra Mismatch:** The mismatch concept is the foundation of extended-window thrombectomy. "Core" is irreversibly infarcted tissue (low CBF + low CBV on CTP, or restricted diffusion on DWI). "Penumbra" is ischemic but salvageable (low CBF but maintained CBV). A large mismatch ratio (penumbra >> core) means significant brain tissue to rescue.

**Key Trials:**
- DAWN trial: required core <21 mL (NIHSS >= 10) or <31 mL (NIHSS >= 20) at 6-24 hours.
- DEFUSE-3 trial: required mismatch ratio >= 1.8 with core <70 mL at 6-16 hours. Also required NIHSS >= 6, core infarct volume <= 30 mL, reversible ischemia >= 15 mL.

### RAPID Automated CT Perfusion Software
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

RAPID is the most widely used FDA-cleared automated CT perfusion software. It differentiates penumbra from core infarct using Tmax and CBF thresholds.

**How RAPID works:**
- Core infarct: relative CBF <30% of normal brain (displayed in pink).
- Penumbra (salvageable tissue): Tmax >6 seconds (displayed in green).
- The software generates automated mismatch maps sent directly to the stroke team's mobile devices.

**Thrombectomy decision criteria from RAPID:**
1. Ratio of hypoperfused tissue to ischemic core >1.8
2. Ischemic core volume (Tmax >6 seconds) <70 mL
3. Severely delayed volume (Tmax >10 seconds) <100 mL

**Quality assurance considerations:** Truncation (incomplete capture of contrast bolus) causes RAPID to overestimate core infarct and underestimate penumbra. Successful implementation depends on understanding software mechanics, controlling technical factors, and identifying technically adequate studies.

**Competing platforms (2024-2025):** New software such as UGuard has shown strong agreement with RAPID for ischemic core volume (ICC 0.92) and penumbra volume (ICC 0.8), providing alternative automated perfusion analysis options.

*Source: Practical Neurology, "RAPID Automated CT Perfusion in Clinical Practice"; Nature Scientific Reports 2025, UGuard validation study*

## 1.4 ASPECTS Score (Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

ASPECTS is a 10-point quantitative topographic CT score for early ischemic changes in the MCA territory.

| Score | Interpretation | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 10 | Normal CT | Best candidate for reperfusion |
| 7-9 | Small early ischemic change | Good candidate for thrombectomy (>= 6 required) |
| 4-6 | Moderate ischemic changes | ASPECTS <6 predicts poor outcome; higher hemorrhagic risk |
| 0-3 | Extensive ischemic changes | Large infarct; thrombectomy generally NOT beneficial |

**10 regions scored:** Caudate (C), lentiform nucleus (L), internal capsule (IC), insular cortex (I), and MCA cortical regions M1-M6. Subtract 1 point for each region with early ischemic change.

**Board Pearl:** ASPECTS >= 6 is the standard threshold for thrombectomy eligibility. ASPECTS <6 predicts >85% chance of poor functional outcome regardless of treatment.

### AI-Automated ASPECTS Scoring
**[BOARD-EMERGING]**

FDA-cleared AI tools now calculate ASPECTS automatically from NCCT scans, achieving 88.6% accuracy for region-based analysis and 80.4% for dichotomized classification (above/below threshold). Key advantages include reduced inter-reader variability and faster scoring in acute settings.

*Source: PMC, "AI in Stroke Care: A Narrative Review," 2025*

## 1.5 DWI-FLAIR Mismatch for Wake-Up Stroke
**[BOARD-CORE]**

When stroke onset time is unknown (e.g., "wake-up stroke"), DWI-FLAIR mismatch estimates whether the stroke is within the treatable window.

- DWI positive + FLAIR negative (mismatch) suggests stroke onset <4.5 hours.
- DWI positive + FLAIR positive suggests stroke onset >4.5 hours.
- FLAIR changes lag behind DWI by several hours — this temporal gap creates the mismatch window.

**WAKE-UP trial:** tPA guided by DWI-FLAIR mismatch improved outcomes in wake-up strokes. Enables thrombolysis in patients who would otherwise be excluded due to unknown onset time.

**Approximately 25% of ischemic strokes present as wake-up strokes.**

### Extended-Window Thrombolysis Updates (2024-2026)
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

Current European Stroke Organisation guidelines now recommend extended time window reperfusion therapies: 4.5-9 hours for thrombolysis and 6-24 hours for thrombectomy, all based on advanced imaging selection. The paradigm has shifted from time-based to tissue-based treatment eligibility. In patients presenting outside the traditional 4.5-hour window with imaging showing salvageable tissue, thrombolysis is associated with improved 90-day functional outcomes.

*Source: AHA Journals, "Guidelines in Action: Extending the Treatment Window," 2025; ESO Guidelines*

# SECTION 2: VASCULAR IMAGING

## 2.1 CT Angiography (CTA)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

CTA is the primary imaging modality for rapid detection of large vessel occlusion (LVO) in acute stroke.

**Key capabilities:**
- Can be performed immediately after NCCT with same contrast bolus.
- Detects ICA, MCA (M1/M2), basilar artery occlusions with >95% sensitivity.
- Identifies tandem lesions (extracranial ICA + intracranial occlusion).
- CTA source images can estimate infarct core.
- CTA collateral grading predicts treatment response — good collaterals = better outcomes.
- Required for thrombectomy triage in most stroke center protocols.

**Board Pearl — CTA Spot Sign:** Contrast extravasation within an intracerebral hemorrhage on CTA. Indicates active bleeding and predicts hematoma expansion. Associated with higher mortality and worse outcomes.

### AI-Automated LVO Detection on CTA
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

FDA-cleared AI tools now detect LVO on CTA in under 5 minutes with high accuracy:
- Sensitivity: >= 87% with NPV >= 93% in external validation across 4 hospitals.
- New AI systems outperform some existing FDA-cleared tools by 25-45% for sensitivity.
- Clinical deployment has reduced CTA-to-door-in transfer intervals by an average of 22.5 minutes at primary stroke centers.
- AI tools send automated alerts to stroke teams when LVO is detected, enabling faster triage and transfer to thrombectomy-capable centers.

**Deep learning architectures:** Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are the primary technology for LVO detection, while random forest algorithms are used for automated ASPECTS scoring.

*Source: Stroke (AHA), "Machine Learning-Enabled Automated LVO Detection," 2024; Nature Communications, 2023*

## 2.2 Carotid Doppler Ultrasound
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Non-invasive screening test for carotid stenosis using peak systolic velocity (PSV).

**PSV thresholds:**
- PSV >125 cm/s suggests >50% stenosis
- PSV >230 cm/s suggests >70% stenosis

**Board Pearl:** Carotid endarterectomy is indicated for symptomatic stenosis >70% (NASCET trial). Patients with 50-69% symptomatic stenosis may also benefit from CEA.

### Carotid Ultrasound vs CTA: Updated Comparison
**[BOARD-CORE]**

**Duplex ultrasound remains first-line** for initial carotid evaluation per current guidelines, with sensitivity 92% and specificity 89% for detecting significant stenosis. However, overall concordance with CTA is only 79.1%, with discrepancies in 44% of patients.

**Ultrasound limitations:**
- Operator-dependent
- Limited to extracranial carotid (cannot visualize intracranial vessels)
- Less accurate for distal cervical ICA lesions
- May be nondiagnostic with heavy calcification
- Cannot differentiate high-grade stenosis from complete occlusion

**CTA advantages over ultrasound:**
- Better accuracy for vertebral artery assessment
- Visualizes intracranial vasculature simultaneously
- More reliable for distinguishing near-occlusion from occlusion
- Less operator-dependent

**Clinical approach:** Start with duplex ultrasound for screening. If nondiagnostic, inconclusive, or pre-surgical planning needed, proceed to CTA or MRA as confirmatory imaging.

*Source: PMC, "Comparison of Doppler Ultrasound and CTA," 2023; Carelon Clinical Guidelines 2024-2025*

---

## 2.3 MR Angiography (MRA)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

- No radiation, no iodinated contrast, can be done without contrast (time-of-flight, TOF).
- Slower than CTA, tends to overestimate stenosis, flow artifacts.
- Used for carotid/intracranial stenosis, aneurysm screening, follow-up imaging.

---

## 2.4 Conventional Angiography (DSA)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Digital subtraction angiography is the GOLD STANDARD for cerebral vascular imaging.

**Indications:** Definitive aneurysm characterization before surgery/coiling, AVM nidus mapping, vasculitis diagnosis, moyamoya disease.

**Risks:** Stroke (approximately 1%), groin hematoma, contrast reaction, vessel dissection.

**Allows simultaneous endovascular intervention** (coiling, stenting, thrombectomy).

---

## 2.5 Transcranial Doppler (TCD)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

**Key applications:**
- Vasospasm monitoring after SAH — most important clinical application.
  - MCA mean flow velocity >120 cm/s = mild vasospasm; >200 cm/s = severe vasospasm.
  - Lindegaard ratio (MCA velocity / extracranial ICA velocity) >3 confirms vasospasm vs. hyperemia.
- Detection of microembolic signals (MES).
- PFO detection with agitated saline (bubble study).
- Sickle cell disease screening — elevated velocities predict stroke risk (STOP trial).
- Limited acoustic window in 10-15% of patients.

---

## 2.6 Vascular Imaging Comparison Table

| Modality | Advantages | Limitations | Primary Uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| CTA | Fast, widely available, excellent for acute LVO | Radiation, iodinated contrast, may overestimate stenosis | Acute stroke (LVO detection), carotid stenosis |
| MRA | No radiation, no iodinated contrast | Slower, overestimates stenosis, flow artifacts | Carotid/intracranial stenosis, follow-up |

| TCD | Non-invasive, bedside, real-time | Limited acoustic window in 10-15% | Vasospasm monitoring, PFO detection |

# SECTION 3: CARDIAC EVALUATION IN STROKE

## 3.1 Echocardiography: TTE vs TEE
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Cardioembolism accounts for 20-30% of ischemic strokes. Cardiac workup identifies structural heart disease, arrhythmias, and intracardiac thrombi.

| Feature | TTE | TEE |
|---|---|---|
| Approach | External chest wall probe | Probe in esophagus |
| Invasiveness | Non-invasive | Semi-invasive (requires sedation) |
| LV thrombus | Good sensitivity | Good sensitivity |
| LA appendage thrombus | Poor | Excellent — gold standard |
| PFO/ASD | Moderate with bubble study | Superior — gold standard |
| Valvular vegetations | Moderate | Superior for small (<5 mm) |
| Aortic arch atheroma | Cannot visualize | Excellent |

**Board Pearl:** TEE is SUPERIOR to TTE for: LAA thrombus, PFO, small valvular vegetations, and aortic arch atheromas. TTE is the initial screening study, but TEE should be pursued when cardioembolism is strongly suspected and TTE is unrevealing — especially in young patients with cryptogenic stroke.

### Updated Evidence on TTE vs TEE (2021-2025)
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

Recent prospective multicenter studies have further clarified TEE's diagnostic value:
- TEE yields treatment-relevant findings in approximately 1 out of 7 patients aged 60 years or younger with cryptogenic stroke.
- In one study, TEE findings impacted management in 23.9% of all patients who underwent the test.
- TEE is especially useful in patients with ESUS or cryptogenic stroke where standard TTE is unrevealing.

**Current AHA/ASA guideline position:** Echocardiography in patients with cryptogenic stroke is reasonable, and TEE is reasonable in patients with embolic stroke of undetermined source (ESUS). However, there is no absolute recommendation mandating TEE over TTE in all cases.

*Source: ESO, "To TTE or TEE?"; AHA Stroke Journal, "Treatment-Relevant Findings in TEE After Stroke," 2022; PMC, "Evaluation and Prevention of Cardioembolic Stroke," 2023*

---

## 3.2 Bubble Study (Agitated Saline Contrast Echocardiography)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Detects right-to-left shunting through a PFO or ASD.

- Microbubbles normally stay on the right side (filtered by lungs).
- Bubbles appearing in the left atrium within 3-5 cardiac cycles = intracardiac shunt (PFO/ASD).
- Bubbles appearing after >5 cardiac cycles = pulmonary AV shunt.
- Valsalva maneuver increases sensitivity (transiently raises RA pressure).
- TTE with bubble = initial screening; TEE with bubble = gold standard for PFO.
- TCD bubble study detects right-to-left shunt regardless of location.

---

## 3.3 Electrocardiography and Rhythm Monitoring
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Atrial fibrillation is the most commonly identified cardiac source, found in up to 25% of all ischemic stroke patients.

**Monitoring progression:**
1. 12-lead ECG — obtained on ALL stroke patients (may show AFib, acute MI, LVH).
2. Continuous telemetry — minimum 24 hours during hospitalization.
3. Holter monitor (24-48 hours) — outpatient rhythm monitoring.
4. Extended cardiac monitoring (14-30 day event recorder) — detects paroxysmal AFib in up to 15%.
5. Implantable loop recorder (ILR) — continuous monitoring for up to 3 years.

### Holter Monitor vs ILR Comparison

| Feature | Holter/Event Monitor | ILR |
|---|---|---|
| Duration | 24-48 hours (Holter); 14-30 days (event) | Up to 3 years |
| Invasiveness | Non-invasive | Minor subcutaneous implant |
| AF detection yield | approximately 5% at 24h; approximately 15% at 30 days | approximately 12% at 12 months; approximately 30% at 36 months |
| Best for | Initial outpatient monitoring | Cryptogenic stroke with negative initial monitoring |
| Key trial | EMBRACE (30-day: 16% AF detection) | CRYSTAL-AF (ILR: 30% AF at 3 years) |

### Updated ILR Evidence (2021-2025)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

**CRYSTAL-AF trial (landmark):** ILR detected AF in 30% of cryptogenic stroke patients at 3 years. This changes management from antiplatelet to anticoagulation therapy.

**Reveal LINQ Registry (2024):** Large real-world registry confirmed AF detected in 28.2% at 36 months, with median time to detection of 7.9 months. AF detection rates: 4.73% at 1 month, 13.45% at 6 months, 17.5% at 12 months, 20.69% at 24 months, 25.98% at 36 months.

**LOOP Trial:** In individuals with stroke risk factors (but not necessarily prior stroke), ILR screening resulted in 3x increase in AF detection and anticoagulation initiation, but NO significant reduction in stroke risk. This suggests not all detected AF may warrant anticoagulation, and that screen-detected AF in the general at-risk population may differ from AF found after cryptogenic stroke.

**Meta-analyses:** AF detection is 2.78- to 5.31-fold higher using ILRs vs. conventional monitoring.

**Board Pearl — Cryptogenic Stroke Workup:** In cryptogenic stroke, extended cardiac monitoring with ILR identifies paroxysmal AF in approximately 25-30% of cases at 3 years. This changes management from antiplatelet to anticoagulation therapy. The LOOP trial's negative result applies to primary prevention screening, NOT to post-stroke monitoring.

*Source: JAHA, "Reveal LINQ Registry," 2024; Lancet, "LOOP Study," 2021; CRYSTAL-AF trial*

## 3.4 ESUS (Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source)
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

ESUS refers to nonlacunar ischemic stroke with no identifiable source after standard workup (imaging, cardiac monitoring, vascular studies).

**Key trial findings:**
- NAVIGATE-ESUS and RE-SPECT ESUS: Rivaroxaban and dabigatran were NOT superior to aspirin for recurrent stroke prevention in unselected ESUS patients.
- However, in ESUS patients WITH PFO, anticoagulation was associated with significantly lower ischemic stroke risk in meta-analysis.

**PFO Closure in ESUS:**
- In ESUS patients <60 years old with no other identified etiology, percutaneous PFO closure reduces the risk of subsequent ischemic stroke (CLOSE, RESPECT, REDUCE trials).

**Current approach:** Rather than treating all ESUS patients with anticoagulation, the trend is toward identifying specific mechanisms (subclinical AF, PFO, atrial cardiopathy) and targeting therapy accordingly.

*Source: ACC, "Embolic Strokes of Undetermined Source: Key Points," 2024; Neurology, 2024*

## 4.2 Hypercoagulable Workup
**[BOARD-CORE]**

**When to consider:** Stroke at age <50, cryptogenic stroke, venous sinus thrombosis, recurrent events despite treatment, family history of thrombosis.

**Key thrombophilias:**
- Factor V Leiden — most common inherited thrombophilia (5% of Caucasians)
- Prothrombin gene mutation (G20210A) — second most common
- Antiphospholipid antibodies — lupus anticoagulant, anticardiolipin, anti-beta2 glycoprotein I (must be positive on 2 occasions 12 weeks apart)
- Protein C deficiency — risk of warfarin-induced skin necrosis
- Protein S deficiency — decreased in acute illness, pregnancy, liver disease
- Antithrombin III deficiency — heparin resistance (heparin works through antithrombin)
- Homocysteine — elevated is a stroke risk factor; treat with folate + B6 + B12

**Timing:** Test for protein C, S, and antithrombin when NOT on anticoagulation and NOT in acute phase (results unreliable acutely).

**From DeLisa:** Coagulation disorders may account for up to 20% of cases with thrombotic infarction in young adults. Deficiencies of antithrombin III, protein C, and protein S are among the most important coagulopathies. Each requires long-term treatment with warfarin.

### ASH 2023 Thrombophilia Testing Guidelines Update
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

The American Society of Hematology released comprehensive 2023 guidelines with 23 recommendations on thrombophilia testing:

**Key conditional recommendations for testing:**
- Patients with VTE associated with nonsurgical major transient or hormonal risk factors
- Cerebral or splanchnic venous thrombosis where anticoagulation would otherwise be discontinued
- Individuals with family history of antithrombin, protein C, or protein S deficiency
- Pregnant women with family history of high-risk thrombophilia

**Stroke-specific approach:** Antiphospholipid syndrome (acquired, immune-mediated) is the strongest thrombophilia risk factor for arterial events including ischemic stroke, especially in young patients. A stepwise approach should exclude acquired conditions before pursuing primary (genetic) thrombophilia testing.

**Strong recommendation AGAINST:** Testing the general population before starting combined oral contraceptives.

*Source: ASH, "2023 Guidelines for Thrombophilia Testing," Blood Advances; AHA Stroke, "Thrombophilia Testing After Ischemic Stroke"*

# SECTION 5: SPECIAL ETIOLOGIC WORKUP

## 5.1 Cerebral Vasculitis (PACNS) Screening
**[BOARD-CORE]**

| Finding | Details |
|---|---|
| Laboratory | ESR, CRP, ANA, ANCA |
| Imaging | Segmental narrowing on angiography ("string of beads") |
| Gold standard diagnosis | Brain/meningeal biopsy |
| Key feature | Elevated inflammatory markers |

### 2023 ESO Guidelines on PACNS
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

The European Stroke Organisation released the first evidence-based guidelines for PACNS diagnosis in 2023:

**Key diagnostic points:**
- PACNS has a long prodromal period (mean 170 days from onset to diagnosis).
- Presents with highly variable symptoms: headache, psychosis, stroke.
- CSF analysis (hyperproteinorrachia, pleocytosis) lacks evidence as standalone diagnostic tools.
- Non-invasive vascular imaging (CTA/MRA) has lower sensitivity than conventional DSA — caution in interpretation.
- Neuropathological examination remains the gold standard, though not always feasible.
- Three diagnostic levels: neuropathological (gold standard), neuroimaging (most widely used), and clinical.

*Source: European Stroke Journal, "ESO Guidelines on PACNS," 2023*

## 5.2 Arterial Dissection
**[BOARD-CORE]**

- **Diagnosis:** MRI/MRA with fat-suppressed T1 (neck), CTA.
- **Key findings:** Intramural hematoma (crescent sign on axial MRI), string sign or flame-shaped occlusion on angiography.
- **From Cuccurullo:** Carotid dissection may occur with minimal or no antecedent trauma and result in stroke in the MCA territory. Vertebral artery dissection may occur after high-velocity chiropractic manipulation.

---

## 5.3 Moyamoya Disease
**[BOARD-CORE]**

- **Diagnosis:** MRA or conventional angiography.
- **Key finding:** Bilateral ICA stenosis/occlusion with "puff of smoke" collateral vessels.

---

## 5.4 Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis
**[BOARD-CORE]**

- **Diagnosis:** CT venography (CTV) or MR venography (MRV).
- **Key finding:** Empty delta sign on contrast CT (absent flow in superior sagittal sinus).
- **Consider in:** Young women on OCPs with headache + seizure.

## 5.5 Genetic Testing for Young Stroke
**[BOARD-LIKELY]**

Monogenic disorders account for approximately 1-5% of all strokes. Genetic testing should be considered in young patients with stroke and suggestive clinical features.

### Key Monogenic Stroke Conditions

**CADASIL (Cerebral Autosomal Dominant Arteriopathy with Subcortical Infarcts and Leukoencephalopathy):**
- Most common hereditary cause of stroke in adults
- Autosomal dominant, NOTCH3 gene mutation (chromosome 19p13)
- Presents with recurrent subcortical lacunar infarcts, migraine with aura, cognitive decline, mood disturbances
- MRI: characteristic anterior temporal pole and external capsule white matter lesions
- Diagnosis: genetic testing for NOTCH3 mutations or skin biopsy (granular osmiophilic material, GOM)

**Fabry Disease:**
- X-linked lysosomal storage disorder (alpha-galactosidase A deficiency, GLA gene)
- May explain up to 1% of all strokes in the young (3-5% of cryptogenic strokes)
- Only monogenic stroke condition with SPECIFIC TREATMENT: enzyme replacement therapy
- Also presents with acroparesthesias, angiokeratomas, corneal verticillata, renal/cardiac involvement

**MELAS (Mitochondrial Encephalopathy, Lactic Acidosis, Stroke-like Episodes):**
- Mitochondrial DNA mutation (>80% are A3243G point mutation)
- Stroke-like episodes do NOT follow vascular territories (unlike typical stroke)
- Elevated serum/CSF lactate; muscle biopsy shows ragged red fibers

**Other monogenic causes:** COL3A1 (vascular Ehlers-Danlos), HTRA1 (CARASIL), sickle cell disease

**Clinical impact:** Genetic testing can impact medical management (e.g., enzyme replacement in Fabry), avoid unnecessary invasive testing (e.g., catheter angiography in CADASIL), inform reproductive counseling, and prompt cascade testing of relatives.

*Source: Stroke (AHA), "Genetic Testing for Monogenic Stroke," 2024; European Academy of Neurology Consensus, 2020*

# SECTION 6: ADDITIONAL DIAGNOSTIC STUDIES

## 6.1 Lumbar Puncture for SAH
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Indicated when SAH is clinically suspected but CT is negative.

**CT sensitivity for SAH decreases over time:**
- >95% at 6 hours
- approximately 93% at 12 hours
- approximately 80% at 24 hours
- approximately 50% at 1 week

**Xanthochromia** (yellow CSF supernatant) is the key finding — caused by bilirubin from RBC breakdown. Takes 6-12 hours to develop.

**SAH vs Traumatic Tap:** SAH shows consistent RBCs across all tubes + xanthochromia. Traumatic tap shows decreasing RBCs and no xanthochromia.

---

## 6.2 EEG in Stroke
**[BOARD-CORE]**

- Post-stroke seizures occur in 5-10% of patients.
- Early seizures (<7 days) more common with hemorrhagic stroke and cortical involvement.
- Late seizures (>7 days) = post-stroke epilepsy.
- Continuous EEG for unexplained altered consciousness (nonconvulsive status epilepticus).
- Locked-in syndrome: EEG shows normal waking alpha rhythm (distinguishes from coma).

# SECTION 7: CLINICAL ASSESSMENT SCALES

## 7.1 NIHSS (NIH Stroke Scale)
**[BOARD-CORE]**

Standardized 15-item neurological examination scored 0-42.

**Severity categories:**
- 0 = no deficit
- 1-4 = minor
- 5-15 = moderate
- 16-20 = moderate-severe
- 21-42 = severe

**LVO prediction:** NIHSS >6 suggests LVO; NIHSS >10 strongly suggests LVO.

**Board Pearl — NIHSS Limitations:** The NIHSS is weighted toward anterior circulation and LEFT hemisphere deficits. Aphasia (item 9) scores up to 3, while neglect (item 11) scores max 2. Left MCA strokes (causing aphasia) often score higher than right MCA strokes (causing neglect) of similar size. Posterior circulation strokes are under-represented and may have low NIHSS despite severe deficits.

### NIHSS Scoring Components

| Item | Domain | Score Range |
|---|---|---|
| 1a | Level of consciousness | 0-3 |
| 1b | LOC questions (month, age) | 0-2 |
| 1c | LOC commands (open/close eyes, grip/release) | 0-2 |

## 8.1 Current AI Applications in Stroke Diagnosis
**[BOARD-EMERGING]**

AI is increasingly integrated into clinical stroke workflows. Key applications:

**1. Automated NCCT Analysis:**
- ICH detection: 94.6% accuracy

**2. Automated CTA Analysis:**
- LVO detection: >= 87% sensitivity, >= 93% NPV

**3. Automated CT Perfusion:**
- RAPID and competing platforms provide real-time core/penumbra maps

- Transfer time reduction: 22.5 minutes average improvement
- Reduced inter-reader variability for ASPECTS
- Enables faster decision-making at primary stroke centers
