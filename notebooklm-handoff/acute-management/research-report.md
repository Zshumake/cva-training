## SECTION 1: IMMEDIATE STROKE MANAGEMENT

Acute stroke is a medical emergency. The immediate priorities are stabilization of the patient, rapid neurological assessment, emergent brain imaging to distinguish ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke, and initiation of time-sensitive reperfusion therapy if indicated. Every minute of delay means more irreversible brain damage.

### Emergency Department Stroke Protocol [BOARD-CORE]

1. **ABCs**: Secure airway, breathing, circulation; intubate if GCS <=8 or unable to protect airway. Airway obstruction can occur with paralysis of the throat, tongue, or mouth muscles and pooling of saliva. Aspiration is also a concern in hemorrhagic strokes (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
2. **Vital signs and monitoring**: Continuous cardiac monitoring, pulse oximetry, temperature.
3. **Establish IV access**: Two large-bore IVs; draw labs (glucose, CBC, BMP, coags, troponin). Use normal saline or Ringer's lactate -- avoid hypotonic solutions or excessive fluid loading as it may worsen brain edema (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
4. **Finger-stick glucose**: Immediately -- hypoglycemia can mimic stroke and must be corrected first. If hypoglycemia detected, bolus 50% IV dextrose. Insulin if blood sugar >300 mg/dL (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
5. **NIHSS assessment**: Standardized neurological examination to quantify deficit severity.
6. **Emergent non-contrast CT**: Must be obtained within 25 minutes of arrival (door-to-CT); rule out hemorrhage. Head CT without contrast is the study of choice to differentiate ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke, as both blood and contrast appear white on CT (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
7. **Determine thrombolytic eligibility**: Within 4.5 hours of symptom onset; review inclusion/exclusion criteria. Per the 2026 AHA/ASA guidelines, either alteplase or tenecteplase may be used (Source: AHA/ASA 2026 Guideline, Stroke).
8. **NPO status**: Nothing by mouth until formal swallowing evaluation (dysphagia risk). Dysphagia occurs in 37-78% of acute stroke patients.

### Time Benchmarks (AHA/ASA Guidelines) [BOARD-CORE]

- Door-to-physician: <10 minutes
- Door-to-CT completion: <25 minutes
- Door-to-CT interpretation: <45 minutes
- Door-to-needle (thrombolytic administration): <60 minutes
- Door-to-groin puncture (thrombectomy): <90 minutes
- Each 15-minute reduction in door-to-needle time is associated with improved outcomes

### Initial Orders and Supportive Care [BOARD-CORE]

- Head of bed at 0-15 degrees (flat) for ischemic stroke to maximize cerebral perfusion (unless ICP concerns)
- Head of bed at 30 degrees for hemorrhagic stroke (balance ICP and perfusion)
- IV normal saline -- avoid hypotonic fluids (can worsen cerebral edema)
- Treat hyperthermia aggressively -- fever worsens stroke outcomes; target normothermia
- Maintain glucose 140-180 mg/dL -- avoid both hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia
- DVT prophylaxis: intermittent pneumatic compression devices immediately; pharmacologic when safe
- Avoid Foley catheter if possible (UTI risk) -- use bladder scanning and intermittent catheterization
- Check baseline ECG and troponin; should not delay thrombolytic administration (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)

## SECTION 2: BLOOD PRESSURE MANAGEMENT

Blood pressure management in acute stroke is nuanced and differs based on stroke type and treatment decisions. In ischemic stroke, permissive hypertension is generally maintained to support perfusion through collateral pathways, while hemorrhagic stroke requires more aggressive BP reduction to limit hematoma expansion.

### BP Targets by Stroke Type and Scenario [BOARD-CORE]

| Clinical Scenario | BP Target | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Ischemic stroke -- NOT receiving thrombolysis | Permissive HTN; treat only if >220/120 mmHg | Higher BP maintains perfusion to ischemic penumbra through collateral vessels |
| Ischemic stroke -- RECEIVING thrombolysis | Must be <185/110 BEFORE; maintain <180/105 for 24 hours AFTER | Higher BP increases risk of hemorrhagic transformation with thrombolysis |
| Ischemic stroke -- post-thrombectomy | <180/105 mmHg for 24 hours | Same rationale as post-thrombolysis |
| ICH (intracerebral hemorrhage) | Target SBP <140 mmHg (INTERACT2 trial) | Reduces hematoma expansion; shown to be safe |
| SAH -- pre-securing | SBP <160 mmHg until aneurysm secured | Prevent rebleeding while maintaining cerebral perfusion |
| SAH -- post-securing with vasospasm | Allow induced hypertension (SBP 160-200, triple H therapy) | Higher BP needed to push blood through spastic vessels |

### ENCHANTED2/MT Trial -- Critical Update [BOARD-LIKELY]

The ENCHANTED2/MT trial found that intensive blood pressure lowering to SBP <120 mmHg after successful endovascular thrombectomy was HARMFUL. More intensive BP lowering was associated with worse functional outcomes, with more early neurological deterioration in the intensive group. The trial was terminated early for safety concerns.

**Clinical implication**: After successful reperfusion (thrombolysis or thrombectomy), maintain BP <180/105 mmHg but do NOT aggressively lower below 120 mmHg systolic.

Source: Anderson et al., Lancet 2022; ENCHANTED2/MT trial

### 2026 AHA/ASA Guideline Update on BP [BOARD-LIKELY]

The 2026 guidelines state that more intensive blood pressure reduction does not improve functional outcome after IV thrombolysis and may result in harm after EVT. Intensive systolic BP lowering to less than 140 mmHg is NOT recommended after reperfusion therapy.

Source: AHA/ASA 2026 Guideline for Early Management of AIS, Stroke

### Antihypertensive Agents for Acute Stroke [BOARD-CORE]

- **IV labetalol** -- first-line; 10-20 mg IV push over 1-2 minutes; can repeat. Does not cause cerebral vasodilation or worsen ICP (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
- **IV nicardipine** -- continuous infusion 5-15 mg/hr; more predictable and titratable
- **IV clevidipine** -- alternative calcium channel blocker infusion
- **IV hydralazine** -- second-line; less predictable
- **Avoid**: sublingual nifedipine (precipitous drops), nitroprusside (raises ICP), oral agents acutely (unpredictable absorption)

### Board Pearl: Permissive Hypertension [BOARD-CORE]

In acute ischemic stroke WITHOUT thrombolysis, do NOT aggressively lower BP unless >220/120 mmHg. The elevated BP is a compensatory response to maintain perfusion through collaterals to the ischemic penumbra. Rapidly lowering BP can extend the infarct.

## SECTION 3: INTRACRANIAL PRESSURE MANAGEMENT

Elevated intracranial pressure (ICP) is a life-threatening complication of large ischemic strokes and hemorrhagic strokes. The Monro-Kellie doctrine states that the intracranial compartment is a fixed volume: an increase in any component (brain, blood, or CSF) must be offset by a decrease in another, or ICP rises.

### ICP Fundamentals [BOARD-CORE]

- Normal ICP: 5-15 mmHg (or 70-200 mm H2O)
- ICP >20 mmHg requires treatment
- Cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP) = MAP - ICP
- Target CPP >60 mmHg (some guidelines suggest >70 mmHg)
- Fever, hyperglycemia, hyponatremia, and seizures can worsen cerebral edema by increasing ICP (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)

### Stepwise ICP Management [BOARD-CORE]

1. **Head of bed elevation**: Elevate HOB to 30 degrees; keep head midline to facilitate venous drainage
2. **Correct underlying factors**: Treat hypercarbia, hypoxia, hyperthermia, acidosis, hypotension, hypovolemia (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)
3. **Osmotic therapy**: Mannitol 20% (0.25-1 g/kg IV) or hypertonic saline (23.4% via central line, 3% via peripheral)
4. **Controlled hyperventilation**: Temporary measure only; target PaCO2 25-35 mmHg; reduces ICP via cerebral vasoconstriction. Use with caution -- may lead to brain ischemia (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)
5. **Sedation**: Propofol or midazolam to reduce cerebral metabolic demand
6. **CSF drainage**: External ventricular drain (EVD) if hydrocephalus present
7. **Decompressive surgery**: Decompressive craniectomy for malignant MCA infarction (age <60, within 48 hours)

### Osmotic Agents Comparison [BOARD-CORE]

| Agent | Mechanism | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| Mannitol 20% | Osmotic diuresis; draws water from brain parenchyma across intact BBB | 0.25-1 g/kg IV bolus; hold if serum osmolality >320; can cause hypovolemia and renal injury |
| Hypertonic saline (3%, 7.5%, 23.4%) | Osmotic gradient draws water from brain; expands intravascular volume | 23.4% requires central line; 3% can be given peripherally; does not cause diuresis; preferred in hypovolemic patients |

### Decompressive Craniectomy for Malignant MCA Infarction [BOARD-CORE]

Decompressive craniectomy (hemicraniectomy) for malignant MCA infarction reduces mortality from approximately 80% to approximately 20% when performed in patients <60 years old within 48 hours of stroke onset.

**Key Trials:**
- **DECIMAL trial**: Confirmed mortality reduction with early surgery
- **DESTINY trial**: Hemicraniectomy within 48 hours reduced mortality (NNT = 2)
- **HAMLET trial**: Benefit seen ONLY when performed within 48 hours (NOT at 96 hours)

### DESTINY II Trial: Patients >60 Years [BOARD-CORE]

DESTINY II enrolled 112 patients aged 61-82 years with malignant MCA infarction. Hemicraniectomy significantly improved survival (38% without severe disability vs 18% control), BUT most survivors had severe disability (mRS 4-5). Mortality was 51.3% in the >60 group vs 20.8% in <60 group.

**Clinical implication**: In patients >60, hemicraniectomy is a shared decision-making conversation with families -- survival benefit exists but functional outcome is often poor.

Source: Juttler et al., NEJM 2014; DESTINY II trial

### Posterior Fossa Decompression [BOARD-CORE]

- Cerebellar hemorrhage >3 cm diameter: neurosurgical evacuation recommended
- Cerebellar hemorrhage with brainstem compression or hydrocephalus: emergent surgical indication
- EVD for obstructive hydrocephalus
- Surgery can be life-saving with excellent functional recovery potential (unlike supratentorial)
- Posterior fossa is a small, rigid compartment -- less tolerance for mass effect
- Patients who survive surgical evacuation of a cerebellar hemorrhage usually make a good functional recovery (DeLisa, Ch. 18)

### Hemorrhagic Transformation Classification (ECASS) [BOARD-CORE]

| Type | Definition | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| HI-1 | Small petechiae along margins of infarct | Usually clinically silent; common after reperfusion |
| HI-2 | Confluent petechiae within infarct without mass effect | Usually clinically silent |
| PH-1 | Hematoma <30% of infarct area, mild mass effect | May be symptomatic; hold antithrombotics |
| PH-2 | Hematoma >30% of infarct area with significant mass effect | CLINICALLY SIGNIFICANT -- neurological deterioration, increased mortality; most feared complication of thrombolysis (~2-6% of treated patients) |

## SECTION 4: IV THROMBOLYSIS (tPA / ALTEPLASE / TENECTEPLASE)

### Alteplase (tPA) [BOARD-CORE]

- **Class**: Thrombolytic / fibrinolytic agent
- **Mechanism**: Recombinant tissue plasminogen activator; converts plasminogen to plasmin, which lyses fibrin clots
- **Indication**: Acute ischemic stroke within 4.5 hours of symptom onset
- **Dosing**: 0.9 mg/kg IV (max 90 mg total); 10% given as IV bolus over 1 minute, remaining 90% infused over 60 minutes
- **Side effects**: Symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage (~6%), systemic bleeding, angioedema
- **Board Pearl**: The dose for stroke (0.9 mg/kg, max 90 mg) is LOWER than the dose for MI (100 mg)

NINDS trial (1995): Patients given tPA within 3 hours of onset were 30% more likely to have minimal or no disability at 3 months (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).

ECASS III (2008): Extended window from 3 to 4.5 hours with additional exclusion criteria. NNT = 14 for 3-4.5 hour window vs NNT = 7-8 for 0-3 hour window.

### Tenecteplase -- Now Guideline-Endorsed [BOARD-LIKELY]

- **Class**: Modified tPA with longer half-life, greater fibrin specificity, PAI-1 resistance
- **Dosing**: 0.25 mg/kg IV single bolus (vs alteplase's 60-minute infusion)
- **Key advantage**: Single IV bolus administration; ideal for drip-and-ship transfers; no infusion pump needed

**2026 AHA/ASA Guideline**: Class 1 recommendation for either tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg OR alteplase 0.9 mg/kg within 4.5 hours. This is a major change from prior guidelines.

Source: AHA/ASA 2026 Guideline, Stroke

**Recent Trial Evidence:**
- **AcT Trial** (2022): Tenecteplase non-inferior to alteplase; may have higher early recanalization for LVO
- **TASTE Trial** (2024): Non-inferiority confirmed in perfusion-imaging-selected patients
- **ATTEST-2 Trial** (2024): Tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg non-inferior to alteplase within 4.5 hours
- **ORIGINAL Trial** (2024): Primary outcome 72.7% tenecteplase vs 70.3% alteplase (non-inferiority met)
- **Meta-analysis** (2025): Aggregate data suggest likely superiority of tenecteplase for excellent outcomes (mRS 0-1)

Sources: JAMA 2024 (ORIGINAL); Lancet Neurol 2024 (ATTEST-2, TASTE)

### Tenecteplase vs Alteplase Comparison [BOARD-LIKELY]

| Feature | Alteplase | Tenecteplase |
|---|---|---|
| Administration | 10% bolus + 60-minute infusion | Single IV bolus over 5-10 seconds |
| Dose | 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg) | 0.25 mg/kg (max 25 mg) |
| Half-life | 4-5 minutes | 20-24 minutes |
| Fibrin specificity | Moderate | Higher (14x more fibrin-specific) |
| PAI-1 resistance | Low | High (80x more resistant) |
| Key advantage | Decades of evidence | Simpler dosing; ideal for transfers |
| Hemorrhagic risk | ~6% sICH | Similar (~6% sICH) |

### Thrombolytic Inclusion Criteria (0-3 Hour Window) [BOARD-CORE]

- Clinical diagnosis of ischemic stroke with measurable neurological deficit
- Symptom onset (or last known well time) within 3 hours
- Age >= 18 years
- CT brain showing NO hemorrhage and no large completed infarct
- BP <185/110 mmHg (can be achieved with treatment)

### Extended Window (3-4.5 Hours) Additional Criteria [BOARD-CORE]

- Age <80 years
- NIHSS <25
- NOT on oral anticoagulants (regardless of INR)
- No history of both diabetes AND prior stroke
- No imaging evidence of ischemic injury involving more than one-third of MCA territory (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)

### Absolute Exclusion Criteria [BOARD-CORE]

- Evidence of ICH on CT
- Symptoms suggestive of SAH (even with normal CT)
- BP >185/110 mmHg despite treatment
- Active internal bleeding or known bleeding diathesis
- Platelet count <100,000/mm3
- INR >1.7 or PT >15 seconds
- Heparin within 48 hours with elevated PTT
- Current use of direct thrombin inhibitors or factor Xa inhibitors with elevated lab tests
- Head trauma or prior stroke within 3 months
- Major surgery within 14 days
- Intracranial neoplasm, AVM, or aneurysm
- GI or urinary tract hemorrhage within 21 days
- Arterial puncture at non-compressible site within 7 days
- Blood glucose <50 or >400 (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)

### 2026 Guideline Change: Non-Disabling Deficits [BOARD-LIKELY]

For patients with non-disabling deficits in the 4.5-hour window, trials have FAILED to demonstrate benefit of thrombolysis. Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) is now preferred and recommended instead.

Source: AHA/ASA 2026 Guideline, Stroke

### Post-Thrombolysis Management [BOARD-CORE]

- ICU or stroke unit admission for minimum 24 hours
- Neuro checks every 15 minutes for 2 hours, then every 30 minutes for 6 hours, then hourly for 16 hours
- BP monitoring: maintain <180/105 mmHg for 24 hours
- NO anticoagulants or antiplatelets for 24 hours
- NO arterial punctures, central lines, or invasive procedures for 24 hours
- Repeat CT at 24 hours (before starting antiplatelets)
- If neurological deterioration: STOP infusion, emergent CT, check coagulation studies, consider cryoprecipitate and tranexamic acid

## SECTION 5: MECHANICAL THROMBECTOMY

Mechanical thrombectomy (endovascular thrombectomy, EVT) is the standard of care for acute ischemic stroke caused by large vessel occlusion (LVO) in the anterior circulation.

### Thrombectomy Eligibility Criteria [BOARD-CORE]

- Large vessel occlusion (LVO): ICA, MCA M1 (and possibly M2) segments
- Standard window: within 6 hours of symptom onset
- Extended window: up to 24 hours with favorable perfusion imaging (DAWN, DEFUSE-3)
- NIHSS >= 6
- Pre-stroke functional independence (mRS 0-1)
- ASPECTS score >= 6 on CT
- Thrombolysis should NOT be withheld if eligible -- thrombectomy is complementary, not a replacement

### Landmark Thrombectomy Trials [BOARD-CORE]

| Trial | Year | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| MR CLEAN | 2015 | First RCT showing benefit of EVT for LVO; NNT = 7 |
| ESCAPE | 2015 | EVT within 12 hours; emphasis on good collaterals; NNT = 4 |
| EXTEND-IA | 2015 | Perfusion imaging selection; high recanalization rates |
| SWIFT-PRIME | 2015 | Stent retriever + tPA superior to tPA alone |
| REVASCAT | 2015 | EVT benefit up to 8 hours |
| DAWN | 2018 | Extended window to 24 hours using clinical-imaging mismatch |
| DEFUSE-3 | 2018 | Extended window to 16 hours using perfusion imaging mismatch |

### DAWN Trial (6-24 Hour Window) [BOARD-CORE]

- Enrolled patients 6-24 hours from last known well with anterior LVO
- Used clinical-imaging mismatch: large NIHSS deficit + small infarct core
- Group A: age >= 80, NIHSS >= 10, core <21 mL
- Group B: age <80, NIHSS >= 10, core <31 mL
- Group C: age <80, NIHSS >= 20, core 31-51 mL
- Results: 49% vs 13% functional independence (mRS 0-2) -- NNT = 2.8
- Paradigm shift: "time is brain" evolved to "tissue is brain"

### DEFUSE-3 Trial (6-16 Hour Window) [BOARD-CORE]

- Perfusion imaging mismatch: ratio >= 1.8, core <70 mL, penumbra >= 15 mL
- Results: 45% vs 17% functional independence -- NNT = 3.6
- Automated software (RAPID) processes CTP to calculate core and penumbra volumes

### Large Core Thrombectomy Trials -- NEW [BOARD-LIKELY]

Six RCTs have now demonstrated benefit of thrombectomy even in patients with LARGE ischemic cores (ASPECTS 3-5), previously considered ineligible:

- **SELECT2** (2023): EVT + medical care superior to medical care alone in large core strokes up to 24 hours. NNT approximately 5. Sustained benefit at 1-year follow-up.
- **ANGEL-ASPECT** (2023): Thrombectomy improved 90-day functional outcomes in large cores up to 24 hours.
- **RESCUE-Japan LIMIT** (2022): Benefit of EVT for large anterior circulation infarctions.
- **TENSION** (2023): EVT improved functional outcome, survival, and quality of life at 12 months in large infarcts within 12 hours. 12-month follow-up (2024) confirmed sustained benefit.
- **LASTE** (2023): Additional confirmatory evidence.

**AHA Science Advisory (2024)**: Endorsed thrombectomy for large core strokes as evidence-based.

Sources: NEJM (SELECT2, ANGEL-ASPECT); Lancet Neurol 2024 (TENSION 12-month); AHA Stroke 2024

### Posterior Circulation Thrombectomy -- NEW [BOARD-LIKELY]

Two landmark trials established benefit of thrombectomy for basilar artery occlusion:

- **ATTENTION Trial** (2022): 46% good functional status (mRS 0-3) vs 23% control. Published in NEJM.
- **BAOCHE Trial** (2022): Thrombectomy 6-24 hours after basilar artery occlusion. 46% vs 24% good functional status. 90-day mortality 37% thrombectomy vs 55% control.
- **3-year follow-up (2025)**: 38.4% thrombectomy vs 18.3% medical management achieved ambulatory/self-care outcomes -- benefit sustained long-term.
- **Meta-analysis (BEST, BASICS, ATTENTION, BAOCHE)**: Twofold increase in odds of favorable outcome with EVT.

**2026 AHA/ASA Guideline**: Now formally includes patients with basilar artery occlusions as eligible for thrombectomy.

Sources: NEJM 2022 (ATTENTION, BAOCHE); Neurology Today 2025 (3-year follow-up)

### Medium-Vessel Occlusion (MeVO) Thrombectomy -- NOT Proven [BOARD-EMERGING]

Several 2025 trials (ESCAPE-MeVO, DISTAL, DISCOUNT) examined thrombectomy for medium-vessel or distal occlusions beyond M1. Results did NOT show added benefit over best medical therapy, and some reported higher rates of intracranial hemorrhage.

**Clinical implication**: Thrombectomy for distal/medium vessel occlusions is NOT currently recommended.

### Thrombectomy Procedure [BOARD-CORE]

- Performed under general anesthesia or conscious sedation
- Femoral or radial artery access
- Stent retriever technique: deployed across clot, waits 3-5 minutes, withdrawn with clot
- Direct aspiration (ADAPT technique): suction catheter applied directly to clot face
- Goal: successful recanalization (TICI 2b-3 flow) -- achieved in approximately 80-90% with modern devices
- NNT for good outcome: approximately 2.5-4

## SECTION 6: GLUCOSE MANAGEMENT IN ACUTE STROKE

Both hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia worsen stroke outcomes. Glucose control is critical in the acute phase.

### Key Principles [BOARD-CORE]

- Target glucose: 140-180 mg/dL in acute stroke
- Hyperglycemia (>180 mg/dL) increases infarct size, hemorrhagic transformation risk, and mortality
- Hyperglycemia promotes anaerobic glycolysis, lactic acidosis, and free radical production in ischemic tissue
- Insulin infusion for persistent hyperglycemia >180 mg/dL
- Hypoglycemia (<60 mg/dL) can mimic stroke AND worsen ischemic injury
- Check finger-stick glucose immediately on arrival

### SHINE Trial [BOARD-CORE]

The Stroke Hyperglycemia Insulin Network Effort (SHINE) trial compared intensive glucose control (target 80-130 mg/dL with IV insulin) vs standard control (target 80-179 mg/dL with subcutaneous sliding scale) in 1,151 patients.

**Results**: Intensive glucose control did NOT improve 90-day functional outcomes and caused more hypoglycemia.

**Post-thrombolysis analysis (2024)**: Intensive insulin was not associated with reduced risk of post-thrombolysis sICH. However, early post-thrombolysis hyperglycemia was independently associated with higher sICH risk, particularly in older patients with more severe strokes.

Sources: JAMA 2019 (SHINE trial); Neurology 2024 (SHINE post-thrombolysis analysis)

## SECTION 7: TEMPERATURE MANAGEMENT IN ACUTE STROKE

### Key Principles [BOARD-CORE]

- Fever occurs in 25-50% of stroke patients in first 48 hours
- Every 1 degree C rise in temperature worsens stroke outcome
- Hyperthermia increases metabolic demand, excitotoxicity, BBB breakdown, and free radical production
- Target normothermia: 36.0-37.5 degrees C
- Treat with acetaminophen 650-1000 mg every 4-6 hours; consider ibuprofen
- Cooling blankets and surface cooling devices for refractory fever
- Search for and treat underlying infection (UTI, pneumonia, line infection)
- Antipyretics should be given early while the source of fever is being ascertained (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)

### Therapeutic Hypothermia Status [BOARD-EMERGING]

Therapeutic hypothermia (33-35 degrees C) for stroke remains INVESTIGATIONAL, unlike its established use post-cardiac arrest. Mild hypothermia to 35 degrees C appears safe and feasible in spontaneously breathing patients but controlled normothermia may lead to similar outcomes without requiring sedative agents.

The COTTIS pilot study (2024) examined combination of targeted temperature management and thrombectomy after acute ischemic stroke. Definitive clinical benefit has not yet been established.

## SECTION 8: DVT PROPHYLAXIS IN ACUTE STROKE

### Key Principles [BOARD-CORE]

- DVT occurs in 20-75% of untreated stroke patients; 60-75% in the affected extremity (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)
- 25% are proximal DVT; 1-2% PE
- Start intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) devices immediately in immobile patients

### CLOTS 3 Trial [BOARD-CORE]

IPC reduced DVT risk by 34% and improved survival in immobile stroke patients. IPC should be considered in all immobile stroke patients.

### Pharmacologic Prophylaxis [BOARD-CORE]

- **Ischemic stroke**: Prophylactic-dose subcutaneous LMWH (enoxaparin preferred over unfractionated heparin) for the duration of acute and rehabilitation hospitalization or until the patient regains mobility (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
- **PREVAIL study**: Enoxaparin 40 mg daily reduced DVT/PE risk compared to UFH 5000 U BID (absolute risk reduction 7.9%).
- **ICH**: May start LMWH prophylaxis between days 2-4 over no DVT prophylaxis.
- **Graduated compression stockings**: NOT useful in ischemic stroke or ICH (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).

## SECTION 9: DYSPHAGIA SCREENING

### Key Principles [BOARD-CORE]

- Dysphagia prevalence: 25-65% in stroke patients. Occurs in 67% of brainstem strokes, 28% of left hemispheric strokes, 21% of right hemispheric strokes (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
- All stroke patients must be screened BEFORE any oral intake
- Current guidelines recommend screening within 4 hours of hospital arrival using a validated tool
- Aspiration pneumonia is a leading cause of death in stroke patients
- Aspiration is missed on bedside swallowing evaluations in 40-60% of patients (silent aspiration) (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)

### Screening and Assessment [BOARD-CORE]

- **Bedside swallow screen**: Should be performed first. Includes medical history review, evaluating gag reflex, observing for cough/difficulty during swallowing trials.
- **Videofluoroscopic swallowing evaluation (VFSS)**: "Gold standard" for evaluation and treatment of dysphagia. Modified barium swallow (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).
- **FEES (Fiberoptic endoscopic evaluation of swallowing)**: Comprehensive functional evaluation of the pharyngeal stage; visualizes anatomy, bolus flow, and containment (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1).

### 2026 AHA/ASA Guideline Update [BOARD-LIKELY]

The 2026 guidelines include updated recommendations for dysphagia management, potentially accelerating adoption of more active dysphagia therapies. Early dysphagia screening decreases not only pneumonia but also mortality, dependency, and length of stay.

Source: AHA/ASA 2026 Guideline, Stroke

## SECTION 10: EARLY MOBILIZATION AND REHABILITATION

### AVERT Trial [BOARD-CORE]

A Very Early Rehabilitation Trial (AVERT) -- 2,104 patients across 56 stroke units in 5 countries.

**Key finding**: Very early aggressive mobilization (within 24 hours of stroke onset, with high-dose activity) was associated with WORSE outcomes. Fewer patients in the very early mobilization group (46%) had a favorable outcome than usual care (50%).

**Dose-response analysis**: Improved outcomes were associated with increased FREQUENCY of out-of-bed sessions but REDUCED outcomes with increased DURATION (minutes per day) of mobilization.

**Clinical implication**: Mobilize stroke patients early but with short, frequent sessions rather than prolonged early activity. Avoid aggressive mobilization within the first 24 hours.

Source: Lancet 2015 (AVERT); Neurology 2016 (dose-response analysis)

### Current Rehabilitation Timing Evidence [BOARD-CORE]

- Most primary stroke centers initiate passive bed exercise on day 1, head elevation on day 1, out-of-bed mobilization on day 2
- Admission to inpatient rehabilitation within the first 20 days is associated with better functional outcomes
- Patients who begin rehabilitation within 4 weeks have greater functional gains and shorter hospital stays
- 2024 VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guideline update: Rehabilitation should be initiated early but without the very early aggressive mobilization approach shown to be harmful in AVERT

Sources: Annals of Internal Medicine 2025 (VA/DoD CPG synopsis); AVERT dose-response analysis

### Stroke Unit Care Evidence [BOARD-CORE]

Organized stroke unit care produces better outcomes than general medical ward care. Per Cochrane review:
- Two extra survivors per 100 patients
- Six more living at home per 100 patients
- Six more living independently per 100 patients
- No indication of longer hospital stay

Stroke ward care and mixed rehabilitation ward care show the most benefit. Stroke team consultations (without a dedicated unit) may show little difference.

Source: Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2020 (Organized inpatient stroke unit care, network meta-analysis)

### Benefits of Early Mobilization (When Appropriate) [BOARD-CORE]

Early mobilization reduces risks of: DVT, deconditioning, aspiration pneumonia, contracture formation, skin breakdown, and orthostatic intolerance. Also provides positive psychological benefits (DeLisa, Ch. 18).

### Criteria for Inpatient Rehabilitation Admission [BOARD-CORE]

Per AHA guidelines, stroke survivors should be admitted to an IRF when they:
- Have a stable neurological status
- Have significant persisting neurological deficit
- Have identified disability in at least two areas (mobility, self-care, communication, bowel/bladder, swallowing)
- Have sufficient cognitive function to participate
- Have physical ability to tolerate at least 3 hours/day of therapy
- Have achievable therapeutic goals
(Cuccurullo, Ch. 1; DeLisa, Ch. 18)

## SECTION 11: SEIZURE MANAGEMENT

### Key Principles [BOARD-CORE]

- Post-stroke seizures occur in 5-10% of patients (10% in ICH -- Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)
- More common with hemorrhagic stroke and cortical involvement
- Early seizures (<7 days): often provoked, may not require long-term treatment
- Late seizures (>7 days): considered post-stroke epilepsy, require long-term AED therapy
- First-line: levetiracetam (Keppra) -- preferred due to fewer drug interactions and no hepatic metabolism
- Avoid phenytoin if possible -- interacts with warfarin, can impair recovery
- Seizures can substantially worsen elevated ICP (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)
- Prophylactic anticonvulsants are NOT recommended for ischemic stroke
- Status epilepticus: IV lorazepam (first-line), then levetiracetam or fosphenytoin

## SECTION 12: DIAGNOSTIC STUDIES QUICK REFERENCE

### CT vs MRI in Acute Stroke [BOARD-CORE]

| Study | Acute Infarct | Hemorrhage |
|---|---|---|
| CT | Dark (hypodense); often normal in first hours. Best at 3-4 days. | Bright (hyperdense/white); seen immediately |
| MRI/DWI | Bright on T2/DWI; DWI detects infarct within minutes | Dark/black on T1 and T2 acutely |

- DWI-MRI is the most sensitive and specific technique for detecting acute ischemic infarct (Cuccurullo, Ch. 1)
- Non-contrast CT is preferred initial study to rapidly rule out hemorrhage before thrombolysis
- CT angiography (CTA) identifies LVO for thrombectomy decision-making
- CT perfusion (CTP) identifies core vs penumbra for extended-window treatment decisions

---

## SECTION 13: MOBILE STROKE UNITS -- NEW [BOARD-EMERGING]

The 2026 AHA/ASA guidelines formally endorse mobile stroke units (MSUs) for rapid thrombolysis delivery. MSUs with onboard CT scanners can be brought directly to the patient, enabling treatment before hospital arrival.

Source: AHA/ASA 2026 Guideline, Stroke

## SECTION 14: PEDIATRIC STROKE -- NEW [BOARD-EMERGING]

The 2026 AHA/ASA guideline includes the first detailed recommendations for diagnosing and treating stroke in children. This is a new addition not present in prior guidelines.

Source: AHA/ASA 2026 Guideline, Stroke
