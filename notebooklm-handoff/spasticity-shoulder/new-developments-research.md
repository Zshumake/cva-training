## CVA Training App -- PM&R Board Prep
## Research Date: 2026-03-21
## Coverage: 2020-2026 literature

## Executive Summary

This report identifies 12 significant developments in post-stroke spasticity and shoulder pain management from 2020-2026 that are relevant to PM&R board preparation. The most impactful changes include: (1) the Tardieu Scale being elevated as the sole recommended spasticity assessment tool over the MAS, (2) ultrasound guidance emerging as the preferred injection technique, (3) kinesiotaping achieving top ranking for shoulder subluxation/pain treatment in network meta-analysis, and (4) growing evidence for early botulinum toxin treatment timing.

---

## TOPIC 1: Spasticity Assessment -- MAS vs Tardieu Paradigm Shift

### What Changed
A 2025 clinical outcomes review (Gal et al, Movement Disorders) explicitly excluded Ashworth-derived scales from recommendations for assessing spasticity, recommending the Tardieu Scale as the ONLY clinician-reported outcome for measuring true spasticity.

### Key Evidence
- Beard et al (2025): MAS and Modified Tardieu Scale differ significantly in classification of lower limb spasticity; MAS classified spasticity presence more frequently, potentially overestimating the neural component
- Gal et al (2025): Comprehensive review emphasizing that MAS does not align with contemporary velocity-dependent definitions of spasticity (Lance definition)
- MAS has demonstrated low sensitivity to detecting changes over time due to coarse 0-4 grading

### Board Relevance: BOARD-EMERGING
This represents a paradigm shift not yet reflected in most board review materials. Residents should know both scales thoroughly but understand why Tardieu is considered more specific. The R1-R2 concept (dynamic catch angle vs full passive ROM) remains the key testable concept.

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Multiple independent 2025 publications support this shift. However, MAS remains in widespread clinical use and will continue to appear on board exams as established content.

### Sources
- Gal et al, Movement Disorders 2025 (doi: 10.1002/mds.30062)
- Beard et al, Clinical Rehabilitation 2025 (doi: 10.1177/02692155251337306)
- StatPearls Modified Ashworth Scale (NBK554572, updated 2024)
- RehabMeasures Database (sralab.org)

## TOPIC 2: Injection Guidance Hierarchy -- Ultrasound as Preferred Technique

### What Changed
A 2023 Bayesian network meta-analysis established a definitive hierarchy for botulinum toxin injection guidance techniques: ultrasound > electrical stimulation > EMG > anatomic localization.

### Key Evidence
- Level 1 evidence supports guided injections over anatomic localization
- US ranked best for accuracy and clinical outcomes in limb spasticity
- US more effective than e-stim for spastic equinus specifically
- US provides real-time visualization of muscles, nerves, and vessels; particularly valuable for deep or atrophied muscles
- Combined US + e-stim offers advantages in complex cases

### Board Relevance: BOARD-LIKELY
Board questions increasingly test injection guidance knowledge. US guidance is becoming the expected answer for optimal technique.

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Bayesian network meta-analysis with multiple supporting studies.

### Sources
- PMC 10145352 (2023 Bayesian Network Meta-analysis)
- Sagepub 2023 (US vs e-stim RCT for triceps surae)
- PMC 9631842 (US vs e-stim crossover study)
- MDPI Toxins visual guide 2025

## TOPIC 3: Botulinum Toxin Timing -- Early Treatment Evidence

### What Changed
Growing evidence supports earlier botulinum toxin injection (within 12 weeks post-stroke), though the picture is nuanced -- benefits for contracture prevention and goal attainment are clearer than benefits for pain reduction.

### Key Evidence
- Early BoNT-A (within 12 weeks) reduces contracture development (2020 RCT, mean 18-day intervention)
- Early treatment within 1 year positively impacts goal attainment (2026 retrospective cohort, MDPI Toxins)
- 2025 secondary analysis: early injection may improve motor recovery
- HOWEVER: 2025 meta-analysis found NO significant difference between early BoNT-A and placebo for pain-related spasticity specifically
- International consensus (19 rehabilitation specialists, 250+ combined years experience) recommends individualized treatment intervals based on patient needs

### Board Relevance: BOARD-LIKELY
Board questions may test optimal timing. Key teaching point: early treatment prevents secondary complications (contracture) even if pain outcomes are equivocal.

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED with nuance -- Benefits real but not universal across all outcomes.

### Sources
- PubMed 33040610 (2020 RCT on early BoNT and contractures)
- MDPI Toxins 2025;17(5):258 (meta-analysis, early vs late)
- MDPI Toxins 2025;17(11):558 (secondary analysis, motor recovery)
- MDPI Toxins 2026;18(2):68 (retrospective cohort, goal attainment)
- J Rehab Med consensus guide (doi: 10.2340/16501977-2753)

## TOPIC 4: Kinesiotaping -- Network Meta-analysis Elevates Status

### What Changed
A 2025 network meta-analysis ranked kinesiotaping as the most effective intervention for enhancing functional recovery and reducing pain during activity in post-stroke shoulder subluxation, surpassing FES and slings.

### Key Evidence
- 2025 network meta-analysis (JCM): kinesiotaping ranked #1 for functional recovery and activity-related pain
- 2022 meta-analysis (9 RCTs, 253 patients): significantly reduced pain (MD -1.59), improved ROM (+7 degrees), reduced subluxation distance (-0.42 mm), reduced spasticity scores
- No significant side effects reported
- Does NOT improve active upper limb function (FMA-UE) or ADL independence
- Mechanism: proprioceptive feedback, joint stability, pain modulation, lymphatic drainage

### Board Relevance: BOARD-LIKELY
Previously considered "emerging evidence" -- now elevated to recommended treatment with network meta-analysis support. May appear as correct answer for non-invasive shoulder pain management.

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Multiple meta-analyses with consistent findings.

### Sources
- JCM 2025;14(19):6913 (network meta-analysis)
- Deng et al, Clin Rehabil 2021 (doi: 10.1177/0269215520964950)
- AJTR 2022 (PMC 9185082)

## TOPIC 5: FES for Shoulder Subluxation -- Optimal Parameters Defined

### What Changed
2025 systematic reviews have established specific optimal NMES/FES parameters for shoulder subluxation, and a new bilateral training approach (BT-CCFES) has emerged.

### Key Evidence
- NMES ranked most effective for reducing subluxation in network meta-analysis
- Optimal parameters defined: frequency 10-36 Hz, pulse width 200-300 microseconds, 4-8 week duration
- Critical finding: effects may NOT persist beyond the intervention period
- BT-CCFES (bilateral training guided by contralaterally controlled FES): superior to NMES in all outcomes except pain relief
- Early initiation (within first few weeks post-stroke) yields best results

### Board Relevance: BOARD-CORE (FES concept), BOARD-LIKELY (specific parameters and BT-CCFES)

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Multiple 2025 publications with convergent findings.

### Sources
- Frontiers Neurology 2025 (low-frequency ES meta-analysis)
- Tandfonline 2025 (NMES parameters systematic review)
- Zhou et al, Sagepub 2025 (BT-CCFES RCT)
- JCM 2025 network meta-analysis

## TOPIC 6: Suprascapular Nerve Block -- Combined Approach Evidence

### What Changed
A 2025 case series introduces a combined treatment protocol: suprascapular nerve block plus intra-articular corticosteroid injection for hemiplegic shoulder pain, with evidence of additive benefit.

### Key Evidence
- 2021 systematic review/meta-analysis (8 studies, 281 patients): SSNB is safe and effective
- 2020 RCT: local anesthetic + corticosteroid > placebo for pain reduction
- Improvements in pain-related spasticity and passive ROM
- Quality of life improvements sustained at 1 month
- 2025: combined SSNB + intra-articular corticosteroid proposed as optimal multimodal strategy
- Safety review (2025, Sagepub): physical harms are minimal but warrant awareness

### Board Relevance: BOARD-LIKELY
SSNB is increasingly tested as a treatment option for hemiplegic shoulder pain. Combined approaches may appear in management algorithm questions.

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Meta-analysis plus RCT support. Combined approach is newer (case series level).

### Sources
- Frontiers Neurology 2021 (systematic review/meta-analysis)
- PubMed 32388647 (2020 RCT)
- Frontiers Neurology 2025 (combined approach case series)
- Sagepub 2025 (safety systematic review)

## TOPIC 7: Oral Antispasticity Agents -- Evidence Remains Weak

### What Changed
A 2023 meta-analysis (J Pharm Bioallied Sci) confirmed that oral antispasticity drugs have only marginal efficacy in non-progressive neurological diseases, with high rates of adverse effects.

### Key Evidence
- 12 studies, 469 patients (6 stroke, 3 SCI, 3 CP)
- Tizanidine, dantrolene, baclofen, diazepam, gabapentin all more effective than control
- BUT efficacy is "marginal at best"
- Trials are small, short, methodologically weak
- No quality-of-life outcome data
- High incidence of drowsiness, sedation, and weakness
- Tizanidine has comparable efficacy to baclofen with better global tolerability

### Board Relevance: BOARD-CORE
This reinforces existing teaching that oral agents are step 3 in the treatment ladder, used when non-pharmacologic measures are insufficient, and that focal treatments (BoNT, phenol) are preferred when spasticity is localized.

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Meta-analysis with clear findings.

### Sources
- J Pharm Bioallied Sci 2023;15(1) (PMC 10259735)
- Neurology 2005 (Yelnik et al, original practice parameter)

## TOPIC 8: Intrathecal Baclofen for Stroke -- Moderate Evidence

### What Changed
A 2024 meta-analysis (Brain and NeuroRehabilitation) provided the first stroke-specific meta-analysis of ITB, establishing moderate-level evidence for efficacy.

### Key Evidence
- Moderate-level evidence supports ITB for stroke spasticity
- Reduces both UE and LE Ashworth scores (LE: 3.3 to 1.4)
- SISTERS study: ITB improves pain and quality of life vs oral medications
- Product Surveillance Registry (long-term data, Neuromodulation 2020): efficacy maintained long-term
- Complications remain frequent: 25.7% device-related (50.3% catheter, 21.3% pump)
- Discontinuation due to adverse events is rare (0.3%)
- Uncommonly used in stroke vs SCI/MS due to comorbidities

### Board Relevance: BOARD-CORE (concept), BOARD-LIKELY (stroke-specific outcomes)

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Meta-analysis with longitudinal registry data.

### Sources
- Brain and NeuroRehab 2024;17(e3)
- Neuromodulation 2020 (Product Surveillance Registry)
- Stroke 2019 (SISTERS study, doi: 10.1161/STROKEAHA.118.022255)
- PMC 10544553 (2023 case report, painful spastic hemiplegia)

## TOPIC 9: Phenol Neurolysis -- Modern Guidance Reduces Complications

### What Changed
A 2021 study demonstrated that early phenol neurolysis may reduce total botulinum toxin requirements, and ultrasound-guided techniques have reduced dysesthesia rates to 0.7%.

### Key Evidence
- Early phenol use (within 12 weeks post-stroke, 52.9% of cases) reduces total BoNT-A needed
- Most commonly injected nerves: tibial motor branches, sciatic motor branches, pectoral nerves, obturator nerve, musculocutaneous nerve
- US-guided technique: dysesthesia incidence only 0.7% (vs historical rates of 2-10%)
- Obturator nerve remains most common target (44.8% of procedures)
- 92% immediate improvement reported across all documented encounters
- Phenol + BoNT combination is increasingly used for multifocal spasticity

### Board Relevance: BOARD-CORE (phenol basics), BOARD-LIKELY (modern guidance benefits)

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Multiple studies with consistent findings on safety and efficacy.

### Sources
- Frontiers Rehab Sci 2021 (early phenol reduces BoNT)
- StatPearls NBK525978 (Phenol Nerve Block, updated)
- PMC 9732339 (phenol neurolysis descriptive study, 2022)
- BJMP (combined BoNT + phenol)

## TOPIC 10: CRPS Post-Stroke -- Upper Limb Inactivity Association

### What Changed
A 2022 cross-sectional study established a significant association between upper limb inactivity and post-stroke CRPS severity, reinforcing the importance of early active mobilization.

### Key Evidence
- Upper limb inactivity significantly associated with CRPS severity
- Supports early mobilization and active rehabilitation as prevention
- CRPS prevalence in stroke: 12.5-50% depending on diagnostic criteria used
- Preventive measures (active PT, early mobilization) may reduce incidence from historical 25% to 12%
- Central sensitization and cortical reorganization increasingly recognized in pathogenesis

### Board Relevance: BOARD-CORE (CRPS basics), BOARD-LIKELY (inactivity association)

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Cross-sectional study with physiological rationale.

### Sources
- PMC 9587704 (2022 cross-sectional study)
- Mayo Clinic CRPS overview (updated 2024)
- NINDS CRPS information page (updated 2024)

## TOPIC 11: rTMS for Hemiplegic Shoulder Pain

### What Changed
Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) is being investigated as a non-invasive treatment for hemiplegic shoulder pain, with a 2024 systematic review summarizing preliminary evidence.

### Key Evidence
- 2024 systematic review (Neurological Sciences) evaluated rTMS for post-stroke HSP
- Evidence is still preliminary -- insufficient for routine clinical use
- Mechanism: modulation of cortical excitability and pain processing pathways
- May be used as adjunct to conventional rehabilitation

### Board Relevance: BOARD-EMERGING
Too new for current exam cycles. Residents should be aware of the concept but not expect tested questions.

### Verification Status
PRELIMINARY -- Systematic review of small studies. Not yet practice-changing.

### Sources
- Neurological Sciences 2024 (Springer, doi: 10.1007/s10072-024-07961-3)

## TOPIC 12: Botulinum Toxin for Hemiplegic Shoulder Pain Specifically

### What Changed
A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis confirmed BoNT-A efficacy specifically for hemiplegic shoulder pain (as opposed to just spasticity reduction), and a 2025 comprehensive meta-analysis of injection therapies provides updated comparative data.

### Key Evidence
- 2021 meta-analysis: BoNT-A effective for HSP reduction
- Common targets for HSP: subscapularis, pectoralis major (internal rotator spasticity)
- Reducing internal rotator spasticity improves shoulder biomechanics and reduces impingement
- 2025 injection therapy meta-analysis (Frontiers Neurology): comprehensive comparison of SSNB, BoNT-A, and anesthetic interventions through April 2025
- Repeated BoNT-A applications maintain spasticity reduction (2025 cohort study, JCM)

### Board Relevance: BOARD-LIKELY
BoNT-A for shoulder pain (via subscapularis/pectoralis targeting) is an increasingly tested concept distinct from BoNT for limb spasticity.

### Verification Status
CONFIRMED -- Meta-analysis level evidence.

### Sources
- PubMed 33454279 (2021 meta-analysis, BoNT-A for HSP)
- Frontiers Neurology 2025 (injection therapy meta-analysis)
- JCM 2025;14(8):2735 (repeated BoNT-A cohort study)
