# SECTION 1: APHASIA

## 1.1 Aphasia Classification [BOARD-CORE]

Aphasia is an acquired language disorder caused by brain damage, most commonly from left hemisphere stroke. It affects approximately 21-38% of acute stroke patients. Aphasia occurs in about one-third of acute stroke patients; by 6 months, only 12-18% retain identifiable aphasia (DeLisa Ch. 18).

Classification relies on three key parameters: **fluency, comprehension, and repetition**.

### Classical Aphasia Classification Table [BOARD-CORE]

| Type | Fluency | Comprehension | Repetition | Naming | Lesion Site |
|------|---------|---------------|------------|--------|-------------|
| Global | Nonfluent | Impaired | Impaired | Impaired | Large L MCA territory (frontal + temporal + parietal) |
| Broca (Expressive) | Nonfluent | Intact | Impaired | Impaired | L inferior frontal gyrus (Brodmann 44, 45) |
| Wernicke (Receptive) | Fluent | Impaired | Impaired | Impaired | L superior temporal gyrus (Brodmann 22) |
| Conduction | Fluent | Intact | Impaired | Impaired | Arcuate fasciculus |
| Transcortical Motor | Nonfluent | Intact | INTACT | Impaired | Anterior/superior to Broca area (watershed) |
| Transcortical Sensory | Fluent | Impaired | INTACT | Impaired | Posterior to Wernicke area (watershed) |
| Transcortical Mixed (Isolation) | Nonfluent | Impaired | INTACT | Impaired | Watershed territory (isolation of speech area) |
| Anomic | Fluent | Intact | Intact | Impaired | Angular gyrus or various L hemisphere locations |

**BOARD PEARL -- The Repetition Rule:** REPETITION is the key discriminating feature for classification. Transcortical aphasias have INTACT repetition because the perisylvian language zone (Broca + Wernicke + arcuate fasciculus) is intact -- the lesion is OUTSIDE this zone in the watershed areas. All other aphasias (Broca, Wernicke, Global, Conduction) have IMPAIRED repetition because the perisylvian zone is damaged. (Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18)

**BOARD PEARL -- Conduction Aphasia:** Caused by damage to the arcuate fasciculus connecting Wernicke to Broca area. Hallmark: disproportionately impaired REPETITION with preserved fluency and comprehension. Patients make phonemic paraphasic errors and demonstrate "conduit d'approche" -- repeated self-correction attempts that progressively approach the target word. (Cuccurullo Ch. 1)

### Dual-Stream Model Update [BOARD-LIKELY]

The classical Wernicke-Lichtheim-Geschwind model remains the foundation for board examinations. However, modern diffusion tensor imaging has confirmed two main language pathways:
- **Dorsal stream** (superior): overlaps with the classical Broca-Wernicke model; related to verbal production, phonological processing, and repetition
- **Ventral stream** (inferior): projects from Wernicke area to anterior temporal lobe; involved in semantic processing and comprehension

This dual-stream model explains why some patients do not fit neatly into classical aphasia categories. The ventral stream was not described in classical models. (Source: Hickok & Poeppel dual-stream model; Neurology Perspectives 2022)

### Speech Terminology [BOARD-CORE]

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Paraphasia (Literal/Phonemic) | Substitution of similar sounds (e.g., "sound" for "found") |
| Paraphasia (Verbal/Semantic) | Word substituted for another from same semantic class (e.g., "fork" for "spoon") |
| Agrammatism | Absence of grammatical structure in sentences |
| Anomia | Word-finding difficulty; unimpaired comprehension and repetition |
| Echolalia | Echoing words/phrases of another person |
| Circumlocution | Roundabout description of a word that cannot be recalled |
| Neologism | Fabricated word with meaning only to the speaker |
| Jargon | Incomprehensible fluent speech; associated with Wernicke aphasia |
| Stereotype | Repetition of nonsensical syllables (e.g., "no, no, no") during communication attempts |

(Source: Cuccurullo Ch. 1)

### Speech vs. Language -- Critical Distinction [BOARD-CORE]

- **SPEECH** = motor production of sounds (articulation, phonation, resonance, prosody)
- **LANGUAGE** = symbolic system for communication (semantics, syntax, comprehension, expression)
- **Dysarthria** = SPEECH disorder -- slurred articulation from neuromuscular impairment (UMN, LMN, cerebellar, extrapyramidal)
- **Dysphonia** = SPEECH disorder -- abnormal voice quality from vocal cord dysfunction
- **Aphasia** = LANGUAGE disorder -- impaired formulation or comprehension of language
- **Apraxia of speech** = motor programming disorder -- inconsistent errors, groping for articulatory positions; distinct from both dysarthria and aphasia

A patient with dysarthria has slurred speech but NORMAL language. A patient with aphasia may have clear articulation but WRONG words (paraphasias).

### Crossed Aphasia [BOARD-CORE]

Crossed aphasia = aphasia from a RIGHT hemisphere lesion in a RIGHT-HANDED person. Rare (<5% of right-handed aphasic patients) because 95-99% of right-handed individuals have left hemisphere language dominance. In left-handed patients, language is still left-lateralized in approximately 70% and bilateral in approximately 15%. (Cuccurullo Ch. 1)

### Formal Aphasia Assessment Tools [BOARD-CORE]

- **Boston Diagnostic Aphasia Examination (BDAE)**: Classifies aphasia type and severity; produces aphasia severity rating
- **Western Aphasia Battery (WAB)**: Measures fluency, comprehension, repetition, naming; produces Aphasia Quotient (AQ) score
- **Porch Index of Communicative Ability (PICA)**: Evaluates verbal, gestural, and graphic responses; highly structured format; provides outcome prediction
- **Functional Communication Profile**: Rates overall functional communication; not diagnostic but useful for recovery tracking

(Source: DeLisa Ch. 18, Box 18-5)

## 1.2 Aphasia Recovery Patterns [BOARD-CORE]

| Aphasia Type | Recovery Pattern | Prognosis |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| Anomic | Best recovery -- mildest form, naming deficits often improve significantly | BEST prognosis |
| Broca | Comprehension remains strong; verbal output improves but often remains nonfluent/telegraphic | Moderate -- expressive deficits frequently persist |
| Wernicke | Comprehension typically improves over first 3-6 months; paraphasias decrease | Variable -- self-monitoring deficits may persist |
| Conduction | Repetition and naming improve; phonemic paraphasias decrease | Good -- often evolves into anomic aphasia |
| Global | Limited recovery if large lesion (full MCA territory); some may evolve to Broca pattern | POOREST prognosis |
| Transcortical Motor | Often recovers well due to intact perisylvian zone | Good -- intact repetition is favorable |

Key recovery facts:
- Greatest improvement occurs in first 2-3 months after onset (Cuccurullo Ch. 1)
- Significant drop in recovery rate after 6 months
- Spontaneous recovery unlikely after 1 year, but therapy-driven improvements can continue
- Language recovery is slower and more prolonged than motor recovery, with improvements continuing >1 year (DeLisa Ch. 18)
- Global aphasia with large lesions: minimal recovery; smaller lesions may improve substantially

## 1.3 Aphasia Rehabilitation [BOARD-CORE and BOARD-LIKELY]

### Established Therapies [BOARD-CORE]

- **Constraint-Induced Language Therapy (CILT/CIAT)**: Forces verbal output by constraining compensatory strategies (gestures, writing); modeled after CIMT for motor recovery
- **Melodic Intonation Therapy (MIT)**: Uses singing/melodic patterns to access right hemisphere language networks; most effective for nonfluent aphasia with intact comprehension (Broca)
- **Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC)**: Ranges from low-tech (gestures, picture boards) to high-tech (speech-generating devices, tablet apps)
- **Speech-language pathology**: Early and intensive therapy yields better outcomes; dose-response relationship confirmed

### CILT/CIAT -- Updated Evidence [BOARD-LIKELY]

A 2020 systematic review of 12 RCTs showed statistically significant improvement on the Western Aphasia Battery, though no significant differences were found on the Boston Naming Test or Aachen Aphasia Test. Critically, when CILT was compared to equally intensive multimodality treatments, effects were similar -- suggesting that **training intensity may be the potent factor** rather than the constraint itself. A 2023 umbrella review of systematic reviews and meta-analyses confirmed that CILT effects often did not surpass comparison treatments for naming, comprehension, and repetition. (Sources: J Speech Lang Hearing 2023; Int J Rehab Res 2020)

**Board takeaway**: CILT is effective, but intensity of therapy appears to be the key ingredient rather than the constraint component specifically.

### Melodic Intonation Therapy -- Updated Evidence [BOARD-LIKELY]

A multi-level meta-analysis of RCTs (2022) found a small-to-moderate effect in noncommunicative language expression. DTI studies show increased fractional anisotropy, fiber number, and path length in right hemisphere white matter tracts after MIT. Growing neuroimaging evidence suggests MIT promotes neuroplastic changes not only in the right hemisphere but also bilaterally, challenging earlier models of purely right-hemisphere recruitment. A 2025 systematic review of 10 RCTs confirmed enhancement of repetition and naming abilities, though methodological inconsistencies limit evidence strength. (Sources: PMC 2022; NYAS 2025; iScience 2023)

### Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS) for Aphasia [BOARD-EMERGING]

A 2024 systematic review of 16 reviews (nine with meta-analyses) found:
- Consistent improvements in **naming** following tDCS, particularly anodal stimulation over the left inferior frontal gyrus
- Improvements in repetition and speech fluency
- **No significant effects on comprehension**
- Most benefit in chronic stroke patients
- tDCS is used as an adjunct to speech-language therapy to amplify neural excitability, not as standalone treatment
- Questions remain about clinical significance for functional aphasia recovery
- Optimal protocols not yet standardized

(Sources: AJSLP 2024; Stroke 2023; Frontiers Hum Neurosci 2021)

**Board takeaway**: tDCS is an emerging adjunct that may enhance naming outcomes when combined with speech therapy, but is not yet standard of care.

# SECTION 2: HEMISPATIAL NEGLECT & PERCEPTUAL DEFICITS

## 2.1 Hemispatial Neglect Overview [BOARD-CORE]

Hemispatial neglect is a failure to attend to, respond to, or orient toward stimuli contralateral to a brain lesion. Most commonly occurs with RIGHT parietal lobe damage (left neglect). It is one of the strongest negative predictors of functional recovery after stroke.

Left neglect (from right hemisphere damage) is far more common and severe than right neglect because the RIGHT hemisphere attends to BOTH sides of space, while the LEFT hemisphere primarily attends to the RIGHT side. When the right hemisphere is damaged, the left hemisphere cannot compensate for the left hemispace. (Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18)

### Types of Neglect [BOARD-CORE]

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Personal neglect | Failure to attend to left side of own body |
| Peripersonal neglect | Failure to attend to objects within reach on the left |
| Extrapersonal neglect | Failure to attend to far space on the left |
| Motor neglect | Failure to move left limbs even when strength is present |
| Sensory neglect | Extinction to simultaneous bilateral stimulation |
| Representational neglect | Failure to represent left side in mental imagery |

### Bedside Assessment [BOARD-CORE]

| Test | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Line bisection | Patient marks center of horizontal line (deviates ipsilaterally to lesion) |
| Star/letter cancellation | Patient crosses out targets on page (misses contralesional side) |
| Clock drawing | Numbers crowded on ipsilesional side |
| Copy drawing | Omits contralesional side of copied figure |
| Double simultaneous stimulation | Extinguishes contralesional stimulus when bilateral |
| Catherine Bergego Scale (CBS) | Standardized assessment of functional neglect in ADLs |
| Behavioral Inattention Test (BIT) | Comprehensive standardized battery (conventional + behavioral subtests) |

## 2.2 Neglect Treatment [BOARD-CORE and BOARD-LIKELY]

### Established Approaches [BOARD-CORE]

- Visual scanning training: Teach patient to systematically scan to the neglected side
- Verbal/visual cuing: Cue patient to look left
- Environmental modifications: Place important items on the non-neglected side initially, then gradually shift to the neglected side
- Limb activation: Activate the neglected limb in neglected hemispace

### Prism Adaptation [BOARD-LIKELY]

Fresnel prisms shift visual images rightward, inducing a leftward aftereffect. A 2021 systematic review of 8 RCTs (244 patients) showed significant improvement on BIT and Star Cancellation Test. A 2025 meta-analysis of 7 RCTs (227 participants) confirmed short-term efficacy on neglect assessments but NOT on the Catherine Bergego Scale (functional measure). Larger prism angles (>10 degrees) produced greater effects. Long-lasting beneficial effects confirmed at 1 month post-treatment. (Sources: AJPMR 2021; JRM 2025)

**Board takeaway**: Prism adaptation has evidence for short-term improvement on standardized neglect tests, though translation to functional improvement remains debated.

### Non-Invasive Brain Stimulation [BOARD-EMERGING]

- **Continuous theta-burst stimulation (cTBS)**: Applied over the contralesional (left) posterior parietal cortex to reduce interhemispheric inhibition. Two weeks of cTBS improved BIT scores by 16.3% post-treatment and 22.6% at 1-month follow-up. Also reduces ADL disability.
- **Optokinetic stimulation (OKS)**: Leftward OKS activates spared frontoparietal regions (FEF, IPS). However, a recent RCT combining OKS with reading therapy did not achieve clinically meaningful neglect remission.
- **rTMS**: Promising add-on therapy, particularly theta-burst protocols.

(Sources: Neurology 2012; ScienceDirect 2022; Frontiers Hum Neurosci 2013)

## 2.3 Anosognosia [BOARD-CORE]

Unawareness or denial of neurological deficit. Most commonly seen with RIGHT hemisphere stroke. Key features:
- Patient denies or is unaware of hemiplegia, visual field cut, or neglect
- Neurological deficit, NOT psychological denial
- Associated with right parietal and insular lesions
- Severe negative prognostic indicator for rehabilitation
- Patients may attempt unsafe activities
- May partially improve over weeks to months
- **Anosodiaphoria** = awareness of deficit but lack of concern about it

(Sources: Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18)

# SECTION 3: APRAXIA

## 3.1 Apraxia Classification [BOARD-CORE]

| Type | Definition | Lesion Site | Example |
|------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Ideomotor | Cannot perform learned motor act on command despite understanding and intact motor function | Left parietal, SMA, premotor | Cannot pantomime brushing teeth on command but does it spontaneously |
| Ideational | Cannot sequence multi-step tasks or conceptualize the plan | Left parietal, diffuse | Cannot sequence steps to make coffee |
| Constructional | Cannot draw or copy spatial figures | Right parietal (spatial), Left parietal (simplified) | Cannot copy a cube or draw a clock |
| Dressing | Cannot orient clothing to body | Right parietal | Puts arm through head opening |

**BOARD PEARL -- Ideomotor Apraxia:** Most tested type. Patient KNOWS what to do and has motor ability but cannot execute on COMMAND. Key feature: performance improves with imitation and is best with actual tool use (voluntary-automatic dissociation). Most commonly from LEFT hemisphere lesions. (Cuccurullo Ch. 1; DeLisa Ch. 18)

## 3.2 Apraxia Rehabilitation [BOARD-LIKELY]

A meta-analysis of RCTs found that **gesture training** and **strategy training** had statistically significant effects on ADL performance. Gesture training showed significant between-group differences for ideomotor apraxia immediately post-treatment, though benefits may not persist at follow-up. The evidence base remains limited overall, but strategy-based approaches that teach compensatory techniques for sequencing and tool use are the current standard. (Sources: ScienceDirect 2022; Cochrane Review 2018)

# SECTION 4: AGNOSIA

## 4.1 Agnosia Classification [BOARD-CORE]

Agnosia = inability to recognize stimuli despite intact primary sensory function. Classified by modality:

### Visual Agnosias [BOARD-CORE]

| Type | Description | Lesion |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Apperceptive | Cannot perceive basic visual features; cannot copy shapes | Bilateral parieto-occipital |
| Associative | Perceives features but cannot recognize object; can copy but not identify | Bilateral inferior temporal-occipital |
| Prosopagnosia | Cannot recognize familiar faces | Fusiform face area (inferior temporal cortex, fusiform gyrus) |
| Simultanagnosia | Cannot perceive multiple objects simultaneously | Bilateral parieto-occipital |
| Pure alexia (alexia without agraphia) | Cannot read but can write | Left occipital + splenium of corpus callosum |

### Other Agnosia Types [BOARD-CORE]

| Type | Description | Lesion |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Auditory agnosia | Cannot recognize sounds | Bilateral temporal |
| Pure word deafness | Cannot comprehend spoken words; can read, write, speak | Bilateral superior temporal |
| Astereognosis (tactile agnosia) | Cannot recognize objects by touch | Contralateral parietal |
| Anosognosia | Unawareness of deficit | Right parietal/insula |
| Finger agnosia | Cannot identify fingers (part of Gerstmann syndrome) | Left angular gyrus |

### Agnosia Rehabilitation [BOARD-LIKELY]

Rehabilitation focuses on compensatory strategies using intact sensory modalities. For visual agnosia, teach verbal description and tactile exploration. For auditory agnosia, compensate via visual modality (lip reading, written communication, gestures). Intensive task-specific practice can improve deficits through neuroplasticity. (Sources: StatPearls 2024; Strokengine 2020)

# SECTION 5: COGNITIVE DEFICITS AFTER STROKE

## 5.1 Post-Stroke Cognitive Impairment (PSCI) [BOARD-CORE]

Cognitive impairment affects **up to 70-77%** of stroke survivors. Updated prevalence data from 2022-2024 studies using MoCA confirm that approximately 72% meet criteria for cognitive impairment at baseline (MoCA <=25), and 65% retain a PSCI diagnosis at follow-up. (Sources: Aging Clin Exp Res 2022; Scientific Reports 2024)

### Cognitive Domains and Stroke Localization [BOARD-CORE]

| Domain | Deficit | Common Lesion Location | Functional Impact | Assessment |
|--------|---------|----------------------|-------------------|------------|
| Attention | Sustained, selective, divided attention | Right hemisphere, reticular formation, prefrontal | Cannot follow multistep commands, easily distracted | Digit span, Trail Making A |
| Memory | Encoding, retrieval, working memory | Hippocampus, thalamus, prefrontal cortex | Cannot learn new routines, medication nonadherence | MoCA delayed recall, Rey AVLT |
| Executive Function | Planning, initiation, sequencing, flexibility, judgment | Prefrontal cortex (bilateral), caudate | Cannot plan meals, manage finances, impulsive behavior | Trail Making B, Wisconsin Card Sorting, verbal fluency |
| Processing Speed | Slowed information processing | White matter tracts, diffuse | Needs more time for all activities | Symbol Digit Modalities Test, timed tasks |
| Visuospatial | Spatial perception, figure-ground, depth perception | Right parietal, right temporal-occipital | Navigation difficulty, dressing, driving impaired | Line bisection, block design, figure copy |

## 5.2 MoCA vs MMSE [BOARD-CORE]

**MoCA is the preferred screening tool for post-stroke cognitive impairment.**

A 2025 comparative meta-analysis confirms that MoCA is superior to MMSE for detecting PSCI:
- **MoCA**: 30-point scale; score <=25 suggests impairment (+1 point if <=12 years education); tests executive function, visuospatial skills, attention, memory (5-word delayed recall)
- **MMSE**: 30-point scale; score <=23 suggests dementia; overemphasizes orientation and language; underrepresents executive function
- Up to **50% of patients** with PSCI score NORMAL on MMSE but ABNORMAL on MoCA
- Both short-form and standard MoCA are effective for persistent PSCI identification
- Short-form MoCA may be more practical in acute stroke units given time constraints
- Important limitation: age- and education-specific MoCA cutoffs for PSCI are not yet established

(Sources: J Neurol 2025 meta-analysis; Aging Clin Exp Res 2022; Scientific Reports 2024)

## 5.3 Right Hemisphere Cognitive Syndrome [BOARD-CORE]

Key features of right hemisphere stroke:
- Left hemispatial neglect (most prominent)
- Anosognosia (denial of deficits)
- Impaired visuospatial processing
- Prosopagnosia
- Flat affect or aprosodia
- Impulsive behavior and poor safety awareness
- Constructional apraxia
- Left homonymous hemianopia
- Often MORE functionally disabling than left hemisphere stroke despite preserved language

## 5.4 Left Hemisphere Cognitive Syndrome [BOARD-CORE]

Key features of left hemisphere stroke:
- Aphasia (most prominent)
- Ideomotor and ideational apraxia
- Gerstmann syndrome (right-left confusion, agraphia, acalculia, finger agnosia) -- from LEFT angular gyrus lesion
- Depression (slightly more common)
- Cautious, slow behavior
- Right homonymous hemianopia
- Alexia

## 5.5 Gerstmann Syndrome [BOARD-CORE]

LEFT angular gyrus lesion producing four deficits:
1. Finger agnosia
2. Right-left confusion
3. Agraphia
4. Acalculia

The full tetrad is not always present. Classic board question localizing to the dominant parietal lobe.

## 5.6 Vascular Cognitive Impairment and Dementia [BOARD-CORE]

Vascular dementia is the second most common cause of dementia after Alzheimer disease.

### Subtypes [BOARD-CORE]

| Subtype | Mechanism | Key Features |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Multi-infarct dementia | Cumulative effect of multiple cortical/subcortical infarcts | Stepwise deterioration with each new stroke |
| Strategic single-infarct dementia | Single stroke in critical location | Thalamus, angular gyrus, hippocampus, caudate |
| Subcortical ischemic vascular dementia (Binswanger disease) | Diffuse white matter disease | Executive dysfunction, psychomotor slowing |

### Distinguishing Features [BOARD-CORE]

- Executive dysfunction and processing speed MORE impaired than memory (unlike Alzheimer where memory predominates)
- **Hachinski Ischemic Score**: clinical tool to differentiate vascular from Alzheimer dementia (score >=7 = vascular)
- Post-stroke dementia in approximately 30% of survivors within first year
- Prevention = stroke prevention (controlling vascular risk factors)

### Updated Diagnostic Criteria [BOARD-LIKELY]

The **VasCog-2-WSO criteria** (published 2025 in JAMA Neurology) update the VasCog criteria for vascular cognitive impairment and dementia (VCID), providing operationalization and guidance on neuroimaging and fluid biomarkers. DSM-5-TR classifies vascular dementia as an etiological subtype of major or minor neurocognitive disorder. Key diagnostic requirements:
- Cerebrovascular disease identified by stroke history OR neuroimaging evidence of silent cerebrovascular disease
- Significant impairment in only 1 cognitive domain is sufficient for diagnosis
- Two certainty levels: probable and possible
- AHA/ASA and DSM-5 criteria both identify cases with predominant vascular pathology

(Sources: JAMA Neurology 2025; PMC 2024; StatPearls 2024)

## 5.7 Executive Dysfunction Rehabilitation [BOARD-LIKELY]

Approximately 75% of stroke survivors experience executive dysfunction. Recent evidence (2020-2025) supports several approaches:

- **Metacognitive strategy training**: For mild-moderate executive dysfunction; teaches "top-down" approach to evaluating and adapting performance in everyday activities. Evidence supports this approach at the highest level.
- **Computerized cognitive training (CCT)**: Cost-effective and accessible; meta-analyses show negligible to moderate effects on attention, memory, visuospatial and executive functioning. A 2025 RCT of home-based adaptive CCT showed significant cognitive improvements.
- **Dual-task training**: A 2025 meta-analysis found benefits for cognitive function in stroke patients.
- **Aerobic exercise**: Enhances left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex activation, improving executive function. Growing evidence supports combined cognitive + physical training.
- **AI-driven telerehabilitation**: A 2025 RCT showed self-guided AI-driven cognitive telerehabilitation delivered improvements comparable to therapist-supervised rehabilitation in subacute stroke.

(Sources: PMC 2023; JMIR 2025; Frontiers Neurol 2025; Stroke 2022)

# SECTION 6: POST-STROKE EMOTIONAL DISORDERS

## 6.1 Post-Stroke Depression (PSD) [BOARD-CORE]

### Prevalence and Risk [BOARD-CORE]

- Affects 33-50% of stroke survivors within the first year (Cuccurullo reports 25-79%)
- Peak onset: 3-6 months post-stroke (can occur at any time)
- Most prevalent 6 months to 2 years post-stroke
- Previously attributed to left frontal lesions (Robinson hypothesis) -- now debated; current evidence suggests location is less important than severity and disability
- Underdiagnosed, especially in aphasic patients
- Associated with worse functional outcomes, increased mortality, and is an independent risk factor for recurrent stroke

### Etiology [BOARD-CORE]

- **Organic**: Catecholamine depletion through lesion-induced damage to frontal noradrenergic, dopaminergic, and serotonergic projections
- **Reactive**: Grief/psychological responses to loss of function, independence, and roles

### Screening [BOARD-CORE]

- **PHQ-9 or PHQ-2**: Most commonly used; AHA/ASA considers PHQ-9 most pragmatic
- **Stroke Aphasic Depression Questionnaire (SADQ)**: For aphasic patients
- **Visual Analog Mood Scale (VAMS)**: For patients with communication barriers
- **Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HAM-D)**: For severity assessment (less feasible in clinical practice)
- Screen at admission, during rehab, and at follow-up visits
- Caregiver depression also common (30-40%)

### Treatment [BOARD-CORE]

- **SSRIs are first-line**: Citalopram, sertraline, escitalopram
- Avoid tricyclics as first-line (anticholinergic effects, cardiac risk, lower seizure threshold)
- SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) are second-line
- **Methylphenidate/stimulants**: For apathy and psychomotor retardation; rapid-onset effect while waiting for SSRI (2-4 weeks onset); combination frequently used in rehabilitation settings
- CBT when feasible
- Exercise has demonstrated antidepressant effects
- Treat for at least 6-12 months

### SSRI Prevention Update [BOARD-LIKELY]

The AHA/ASA 2016 scientific statement on PSD has not been updated. Recent evidence is mixed on preventive SSRI use:
- The **AFFINITY trial (2021)**: Fluoxetine 20 mg for 26 weeks did NOT prevent or alleviate PSD
- A 2022 meta-analysis of 7 RCTs showed escitalopram may be effective (significant reduction on Hamilton Depression Scale vs placebo)
- Current consensus: **Using antidepressants preemptively to prevent PSD is not supported**

(Sources: AHA Scientific Statement 2016; JAHA 2022; NDT 2023)

## 6.2 Pseudobulbar Affect (PBA) [BOARD-CORE]

### Key Features [BOARD-CORE]

- Involuntary emotional display DISPROPORTIONATE or INCONGRUENT to internal emotional state
- Prevalence: 10-20% of stroke survivors; emotional lability occurs in approximately 20% post-stroke (DeLisa Ch. 18)
- Also known as: emotional lability, pathological crying/laughing, involuntary emotional expression disorder (IEED)
- Mechanism: bilateral disruption of corticobulbar tracts releasing brainstem emotional motor system from cortical inhibition
- Episodes are brief (seconds to minutes) but distressing and socially disabling
- Patient often reports not actually feeling the emotion displayed

### Treatment [BOARD-CORE]

- **Dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta)**: Only FDA-approved treatment for PBA (approved October 2010)
  - Dosing: DM 20mg/quinidine 10mg BID
  - Quinidine inhibits CYP2D6 to increase DM bioavailability approximately 20-fold
  - **PRISM II trial**: Evaluated DM/Q in stroke, dementia, and TBI patients; most common adverse events: nausea (24.8%), headache (22.8%), dizziness (19.5%)
- **SSRIs** (sertraline, citalopram): Also effective; lower-cost alternative

### PBA vs Depression [BOARD-CORE]

| Feature | Depression | Pseudobulbar Affect |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Nature | Sustained mood disorder | Involuntary emotional display |
| Duration | Weeks to months | Brief episodes (seconds to minutes) |
| Congruence | Emotion matches internal state | Display may NOT match internal state |
| Treatment | SSRIs, psychotherapy | Nuedexta (DM/Q), SSRIs |
| Lesion | Variable, frontal/subcortical | Bilateral corticobulbar tracts |

## 6.3 Aprosodia [BOARD-CORE]

Aprosodia = impaired emotional tone/inflection of speech, typically from RIGHT hemisphere damage.

### Updated Evidence [BOARD-LIKELY]

- Receptive emotional aprosodia occurs in approximately **70%** of acute right hemisphere stroke patients; 12-44% in subacute/chronic stages
- Right frontal/antero-superior lesions: more associated with expressive aprosodia
- Right temporal/postero-lateral lesions: more associated with receptive aprosodia
- White matter damage (not just gray matter) impairs affective prosody recognition
- Spousal burden: Partners report prosodic deficits as significant communication issues, associated with increased caregiver depression and decreased marital satisfaction
- Some patients improve from acute to chronic stage; others remain impaired -- intervention targeting emotional prosody recognition is warranted

(Sources: ScienceDirect 2021; PMC 2022; PMC 2020; Brain Sci 2025)

## 6.4 Post-Stroke Fatigue [BOARD-LIKELY]

- Prevalence: 25-85% depending on definition and timing
- Distinct from depression (can occur WITHOUT depressed mood) and from physical deconditioning (persists after physical recovery)
- Mechanism: disruption of reticular activating system, thalamic connections, neuroinflammation
- Major barrier to rehabilitation participation
- Treatment: energy conservation, graded exercise, sleep hygiene
- Pharmacologic: modafinil, methylphenidate (limited evidence, off-label)
- Screening: Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS)
- Often persists for YEARS after stroke

# SECTION 7: FUNCTIONAL OUTCOMES

## 7.1 Return to Work [BOARD-LIKELY]

- Only 25-50% of working-age stroke survivors return to work
- Strongest predictors: younger age (<55), mild stroke severity, white-collar occupation
- Cognitive function (especially executive function and processing speed) often MORE limiting than motor deficits
- Aphasia severity: moderate-severe aphasia = very low return-to-work rates
- Fatigue is a major underrecognized barrier
- Depression independently reduces return-to-work rates
- ADA mandates reasonable accommodations

## 7.2 Driving After Stroke [BOARD-LIKELY]

- Most states require 3-6 month waiting period
- Laws are STATE-SPECIFIC
- Comprehensive Driving Evaluation (CDE) by certified driver rehabilitation specialist (CDRS)
- Visual field requirements: most states require minimum 120-degree horizontal field
- Hemianopia: varies by state
- Cognitive requirements: attention, judgment, processing speed, decision-making
- Seizure: typically requires 3-12 month seizure-free interval (state-specific)
- Vehicle modifications: left-foot accelerator, hand controls, spinner knob, adaptive mirrors
