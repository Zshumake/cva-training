## TOPIC 1: IDDSI Framework (Replacing NDD)

### Status: BOARD-CORE (now established standard)

**Key Finding:** As of October 2021, IDDSI is the ONLY texture-modified diet framework recognized by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics' Nutrition Care Manual. The NDD system is officially retired.

**Timeline:**
- 2012: International collaboration began developing IDDSI
- 2017: Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics and ASHA announced support
- 2019: IDDSI began replacing NDD
- October 2021: NDD removed from Nutrition Care Manual entirely

**Clinical Impact:**
- Standardized testing methods (fork drip test, spoon tilt test) replace subjective descriptions
- Numbered levels 0-7 with color coding replace old NDD terminology (nectar-thick, honey-thick)
- Levels 3-4 bridge drinks and foods
- Research from East Asian countries (2021-2022) demonstrates universal applicability across food cultures

**Board Relevance:** The transition from NDD to IDDSI is now established enough for board examination. Residents must know the IDDSI levels, testing methods, and the fact that it replaces NDD.

**Sources:**
- ASHA: https://www.asha.org/slp/healthcare/international-dysphagia-diet-standardisation-initiative/
- IDDSI Official: https://www.iddsi.org/standards/framework
- PMC9592427 (East Asian feasibility study, 2022)
- PMC8994209 (Aged care IDDSI compliance, 2022)

## TOPIC 2: FEES vs VFSS Comparison

### Status: BOARD-CORE (established) with BOARD-LIKELY updates

**Key Finding:** A 2023 narrative review in Radiography confirms VFSS remains the gold standard, but FEES is increasingly recognized as a beneficial first-line examination with complementary strengths.

**Updated Evidence:**
- FEES aspiration detection: sensitivity 0.80, specificity 0.92
- FEES penetration detection: sensitivity 1.00, specificity 0.83
- FEES limitations: "white-out" period during pharyngeal swallow, invasive endoscope
- VFSS limitations: radiation exposure, patient transport required
- Recommendation: FEES as first-line, VFSS following to ensure aspiration events are not missed

**Clinical Practice Shift:** FEES is gaining preference as first-line due to portability, no radiation, and ability to assess secretion management. This does not change VFSS as gold standard for board purposes.

**Sources:**
- PubMed 36640583 (2023 narrative review)
- ScienceDirect S1078817422002152
- Frontiers Neurology 2024;1380287 (network meta-analysis of screening)

## TOPIC 3: Aspiration Pneumonia Prevention Evidence

### Status: BOARD-CORE (oral hygiene) with BOARD-LIKELY updates

**Key Findings:**

1. **Oral hygiene remains the most important modifiable risk factor.** A 2025 study (PubMed 40966864) confirmed that oral hygiene conditions are significantly associated with aspiration pneumonia incidence in elderly stroke patients.

2. **Phase-specific interventions (NEW 2025):** Oral hygiene is more closely associated with INITIAL aspiration pneumonia onset, while oral FUNCTION plays a greater role in RECURRENCE. This offers a nuanced framework: hygiene-focused care in acute settings, functional rehabilitation during recovery.

3. **Structured oral care programs:** A 2025 quasi-experimental study (PMC11931980) demonstrated that standardized nursing education programs for oral hygiene significantly decrease hospital-acquired pneumonia rates.

4. **The NPO Paradox (NEW 2025):** A bicenter study in Scientific Reports (2025) found that NPO patients were 2.5x MORE likely to develop stroke-associated pneumonia than those without dietary restrictions (p = 0.008). This challenges the traditional assumption that restricting oral intake prevents aspiration pneumonia. The explanation: aspiration of oral secretions (not food) is the primary driver.

**Board Relevance:** Oral hygiene as prevention is BOARD-CORE. The NPO paradox finding is too new for current exams but important for clinical practice awareness.

**Sources:**
- PubMed 40966864 (2025)
- Scientific Reports s41598-025-22020-3 (NPO paradox, 2025)
- PMC11931980 (Kozub 2025, nursing education)
- PMC7607750 (2020 pilot RCT, intensified oral hygiene)
- Frontiers Rehab Sciences 2024 (impaired oral health review)

## TOPIC 4: Pharyngeal Electrical Stimulation (PES)

### Status: BOARD-EMERGING (moving toward BOARD-LIKELY for tracheotomized patients)

**Key Findings:**

1. **For tracheotomized patients (strongest evidence):** PHAST-TRAC trial showed 75% decannulation rate in treatment vs 20% in sham group. ESO-ESSD 2021 guideline now suggests PES to accelerate decannulation.

2. **For general stroke dysphagia (mixed):** STEPS trial found PES safe but did not improve dysphagia in general subacute stroke. PHADER cohort study (2020, Lancet eClinicalMedicine) found PES safe and associated with reduced dysphagia severity.

3. **Mechanism update (2025):** Transcutaneous electrical stimulation effects act primarily through neurophysiological/sensory pathways rather than direct muscle stimulation (Scientific Reports 2025).

4. **PhEED trial (2025):** Active PES significantly reduced dysphagia severity at day 83 with improvements in diet subscales and functional oral intake scores.

**Board Relevance:** PES for tracheotomized patients is approaching board-testable status given the ESO-ESSD guideline endorsement. General PES remains too emerging.

**Sources:**
- Lancet eClinicalMedicine 2020 (PHADER cohort)
- J Rehab Med 2025 (PhEED trial)
- Scientific Reports 2025 (mechanism study)
- Scientific Reports 2026 (cost-effectiveness)
- ESO-ESSD 2021 guideline

## TOPIC 5: EMST (Expiratory Muscle Strength Training)

### Status: BOARD-CORE (concept) with mixed evidence

**Key Findings:**

1. **Positive outcomes:** EMST effective for developing suprahyoid muscle activity, improving PAS scores by 41%, and cough acceleration volume by 103% in a 4-week intervention.

2. **Mixed evidence caveat:** A 2020 systematic review (Plowman, PubMed 31999193) found that conclusions differed on whether swallowing improved following EMST. Heterogeneity in etiologies, baseline severity, and measurement methods is a barrier.

3. **Broader resistance training (2025):** A Frontiers in Physiology meta-analysis (2025) confirmed that resistance exercise significantly enhances swallowing safety, oral intake capacity, and psychological wellbeing, suggesting broader resistance training approaches may complement EMST.

**Board Relevance:** EMST as a concept and its mechanism (strengthening suprahyoid/expiratory muscles) is testable. The mixed evidence status is important context for board answers.

**Sources:**
- PubMed 31999193 (2020 systematic review)
- PMC7752042 (2020 retrospective pilot)
- Frontiers Physiology 2025 (resistance exercise meta-analysis)
- Park et al. 2016; Pitts et al. 2016

## TOPIC 6: McNeill Dysphagia Therapy Program (MDTP)

### Status: BOARD-LIKELY

**Key Finding:** A 2020 double-blind placebo-controlled trial (Carnaby, J Oral Rehab; PMC7067660) demonstrated MDTP's superiority over both NMES and usual care for post-stroke dysphagia.

**Outcomes:**
- MDTP patients 4.3x more likely to return to pre-stroke diet (95% CI: 1.08-17.2, p<0.03)
- Greater positive change in oral intake than NMES or usual care arms
- Improved functional outcome by 3 months post-stroke (RR = 1.72, 1.04-2.84)

**Program Details:**
- Exercise-based, systematic therapy using exercise physiology and motor learning principles
- High-intensity program for safely advancing oral intake
- Applicable to stroke, TBI, Parkinson's, and head & neck cancer patients

**Board Relevance:** MDTP is increasingly recognized and may appear on boards as a named therapy program. The key board point is that exercise-based swallowing therapy outperformed NMES in the head-to-head trial.

**Sources:**
- PMC7067660 (2020 RCT)
- Carnaby-Mann original studies

## TOPIC 7: Cortical vs Brainstem Dysphagia Patterns

### Status: BOARD-CORE (established) with BOARD-LIKELY updates

**Key Findings:**

1. **Lateralization:** Swallowing is asymmetrically represented in the cortex. The swallowing-dominant hemisphere is NOT related to hand dominance and varies between individuals.

2. **Cortical dysphagia:** Shows bilateral reduction of cortical swallowing activation. Recovery depends on cortical reorganization in the unaffected hemisphere.

3. **Brainstem dysphagia:** Leads to more severe dysphagia than cortical lesions. Lateral medulla highest incidence, followed by pons, medial medulla, then midbrain.

4. **Brainstem compensation (2011/replicated):** Right hemispheric lateralization observed in brainstem stroke, interpreted as acute cortical compensation.

5. **Lesion-location mapping (2024):** Specific lesion locations correlate with distinct dysphagia characteristics. Insular cortex lesions associated with pharyngeal phase dysfunction; internal capsule lesions with oral phase dysfunction.

**Board Relevance:** The lateralization concept and Wallenberg syndrome dysphagia are definite board topics. The lesion-specific mapping is emerging.

**Sources:**
- PMC7844337 (cortical and subcortical control review, 2021)
- PMC3061896 (cortical swallowing processing)
- MDPI 2039-4349/15/6/156 (brainstem stroke review, 2024)
- ScienceDirect S1052305724001277 (lesion-location mapping, 2024)

## TOPIC 8: Silent Aspiration Detection

### Status: BOARD-CORE (concept) with BOARD-EMERGING new methods

**Key Findings:**

1. **Established:** Silent aspiration is missed in 40-60% of bedside clinical examinations. VFSS and FEES remain the gold standards for detection. PAS score 8 = silent aspiration.

2. **Cough reflex testing (2023):** A feasibility study in hyperacute stroke (Trimble 2023) found CSE may have higher sensitivity than CRT for silent aspiration identification. Accuracy of CRT lacks consensus.

3. **Simplified cough test:** Sensitivity 87.1%, specificity 66.7% for detecting silent aspiration.

4. **Emerging technologies:**
   - Machine learning models for aspiration screening (Scientific Reports 2023)
   - Real-time monitoring with deep learning using multisource signals (PMC10331777, 2023)
   - Voxel-based lesion mapping of silent aspiration in infratentorial stroke (PMC10957678, 2024)

**Board Relevance:** The concept of silent aspiration and its 40-60% miss rate at bedside is high-yield board material. New detection methods are too emerging.

**Sources:**
- Trimble 2023 (Int J Lang Commun Disord)
- Frontiers Neurology 2025 (systematic review meta-analysis)
- PMC10331777 (deep learning monitoring, 2023)
- PMC10957678 (lesion mapping, 2024)

## TOPIC 9: Dysphagia Screening Tools (TOR-BSST, GUSS)

### Status: BOARD-CORE / BOARD-LIKELY

**Key Findings:**

1. **TOR-BSST:** Sensitivity 91.3-100%; Specificity 64%. Water-only protocol. Can be administered by trained nurses. Simple and accurate across settings.

2. **GUSS:** Sensitivity 100%; Specificity 50-69%. Uses water PLUS semisolid and solid consistencies. Stepwise approach. Negative predictive value 100%.

3. **Both are among the best-performing tools** per Cochrane systematic review, though based on single studies with small sample sizes.

4. **2024 Network Meta-Analysis (Frontiers Neurology):** Comprehensive comparison of multiple screening tools emphasizes need for personalized screening approaches.

5. **ESO-ESSD 2021 Recommendation:** All stroke patients should receive formal dysphagia screening using water swallow test or multiple consistency test as soon as possible after admission.

**Board Relevance:** Knowing that validated screening tools exist and their general performance characteristics is testable. The ESO-ESSD screening recommendation is important.

**Sources:**
- Martino et al. 2009 (TOR-BSST validation)
- Trapl et al. 2007 (GUSS validation)
- Cochrane systematic review
- Frontiers Neurology 2024;1380287
- Dziewas et al. 2021 (ESO-ESSD guideline)

## TOPIC 10: Timing of Oral Feeding Initiation

### Status: BOARD-CORE (general principles) with BOARD-LIKELY specifics

**Key Findings:**

1. **ESO-ESSD 2021:** NPO until formal screening is completed and swallowing judged safe. This is the clearest guideline recommendation.

2. **No consensus on exact timing:** Guidelines vary from within 24 hours to within 7 days.

3. **Early feeding benefits:** Early initiation of oral feeding after clinical stabilization may safely improve survival, reduce chest infection rates, shorten hospital stay, and improve swallowing function.

4. **Avoid day-1 feeding:** Not advisable to start feeding on the first day of stroke because the patient's condition may still be unclear.

5. **FOOD Trial evidence:** NG preferred over early PEG in first 2-4 weeks. No benefit of early PEG over NG in early stroke.

**Board Relevance:** The general principle of screening before feeding and NG before PEG timeline are board-testable. The FOOD trial is a landmark study.

**Sources:**
- Dziewas et al. 2021 (ESO-ESSD guideline)
- Frontiers in Stroke 2025 (nutrition CPG review)
- Frontiers in Nutrition 2025 (enteral nutrition review)
- PMC9730258 (factors for oral intake, 2022)
- FOOD Trial (Lancet 2005)

## TOPIC 11: NMES/VitalStim Evidence

### Status: BOARD-CORE (controversial/mixed)

**Key Findings:**

1. **2023 Frontiers meta-analysis:** NMES is more effective than treatment without NMES, but effect sizes are SMALL.

2. **2025 network meta-analysis:** Combined NMES + conventional therapy superior to either alone. Standard therapy remains foundation.

3. **Cochrane review position unchanged:** Insufficient evidence to support or refute NMES for dysphagia.

4. **Electrode placement critical:** Incorrect placement may pull the larynx DOWN, paradoxically worsening aspiration.

5. **Board answer remains:** Evidence is inconclusive; traditional swallowing therapy is the standard of care. NMES may be used as adjunct but not standalone.

**Sources:**
- Frontiers Neurology 2023;1163045
- Frontiers Neurology 2025;1700317
- Cochrane Library (Bath 2018)
- PMC10286766 (sensory NMES review, 2023)

## BONUS TOPIC: ESO-ESSD 2021 Guideline

### Status: BOARD-LIKELY (major guideline, increasingly testable)

**Significance:** This is the first comprehensive evidence-based guideline specifically for post-stroke dysphagia, published in European Stroke Journal 2021 by Dziewas et al. It used GRADE methodology with 20 clinical questions.

**Key Recommendations:**
1. Formal dysphagia screening for all stroke patients ASAP after admission
2. NPO until screening completed
3. VFSS or FEES for patients who fail screening
4. Texture modification and thickened liquids to reduce pneumonia risk
5. Behavioral swallowing therapies recommended
6. Oral care interventions to reduce pneumonia risk
7. Neurostimulation as adjunct, preferably in clinical trials
8. PES suggested for tracheotomized patients to accelerate decannulation

**Sources:**
- PMC8564153 (full text)
- Dziewas et al., European Stroke Journal 2021;6(3):LXXXIX

## BONUS TOPIC: Lancet Neurology 2024 Review

### Status: BOARD-LIKELY

**Paper:** Labeit et al., "Dysphagia after stroke: research advances in treatment interventions," Lancet Neurology 2024;23(4):418-428.

**Key themes:**
- Dietary adjustments and oral hygiene mitigate aspiration pneumonia risk
- Nutritional supplementation including tube feeding for malnutrition prevention
- Behavioral strategies showing promise in small studies
- Pharmaceutical agents (capsaicin, TRPV-1 agonists) altering pharyngeal sensation
- Neurostimulation techniques (tDCS, rTMS, PES) promoting neuroplasticity
