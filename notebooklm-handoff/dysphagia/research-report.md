## 1. EPIDEMIOLOGY AND OVERVIEW

### Prevalence by Stroke Location
- Dysphagia affects 25-65% of acute stroke patients overall [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Gordon et al., 1987; Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38; DeLisa Ch.18 p.25)
- Brainstem strokes: ~67% prevalence (highest risk) [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Gordon et al., 1987; Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38)
- Left hemisphere strokes: ~28% prevalence [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Gordon et al., 1987)
- Right hemisphere strokes: ~21% prevalence [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Gordon et al., 1987)
- Bilateral hemispheric strokes carry high risk for persistent dysphagia [BOARD-CORE] (Source: DeLisa Ch.18 p.25-26)
- More common in large-vessel than small-vessel strokes [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38)
- Unilateral hemispheric dysphagia affects approximately 40% of patients [BOARD-CORE] (Source: DeLisa Ch.18 p.25, ref 75)

### Natural History and Recovery
- Most hemispheric dysphagia resolves within 2-4 weeks [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42; Stein et al., 2015)
- Within 7 days, swallowing dysfunction recovers for about half of patients [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- At 6 months, only 11-13% have persistent swallowing dysfunction [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Stein et al., 2015; Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- Brainstem dysphagia may be prolonged or permanent [BOARD-CORE] (Source: DeLisa Ch.18 p.25-26)
- Recovery of swallowing function in most brainstem strokes occurs in the first 3 weeks [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Logemann, 1991; Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- Delayed pharyngeal swallow is the most common cause of dysphagia post-stroke [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Veis and Logemann, 1985; Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38)

### Consequences
- Aspiration pneumonia is the leading cause of death in subacute stroke [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.39)
- Malnutrition found in up to 49% of stroke rehabilitation admissions (as high as 22% in one report) [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.41; DeLisa Ch.18 p.26)
- Increased length of stay, healthcare costs, and reduced quality of life [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Multiple)

## 2. ANATOMY AND PHYSIOLOGY OF SWALLOWING

### Cranial Nerves Essential for Swallowing
| Cranial Nerve | Name | Role in Swallowing |
|---|---|---|
| CN V | Trigeminal | Motor: muscles of mastication (temporalis, masseter, pterygoids). Sensory: oral cavity sensation, palate [BOARD-CORE] |
| CN VII | Facial | Lip closure (orbicularis oris), buccal tone (buccinator) -- prevents food pocketing in cheeks [BOARD-CORE] |
| CN IX | Glossopharyngeal | Pharyngeal sensation (gag reflex afferent), taste posterior 1/3 tongue, stylopharyngeus muscle elevates pharynx [BOARD-CORE] |
| CN X | Vagus | Soft palate elevation (levator veli palatini), pharyngeal constrictors, laryngeal elevation, vocal cord closure, UES relaxation, esophageal peristalsis [BOARD-CORE] |
| CN XII | Hypoglossal | All intrinsic and most extrinsic tongue muscles -- tongue manipulation, bolus formation, posterior propulsion [BOARD-CORE] |

### Nucleus Ambiguus
The nucleus ambiguus in the lateral medulla contains motor neurons of CN IX and X that control pharyngeal and laryngeal muscles. It is the critical brainstem center for the pharyngeal swallow reflex. Lateral medullary (Wallenberg) syndrome damages this nucleus, causing severe dysphagia with ipsilateral pharyngeal weakness, vocal cord paralysis, and absent gag reflex. This is why lateral medullary strokes produce the most severe and persistent dysphagia of any stroke type. [BOARD-CORE] (Source: App content; neuroanatomy standard references)

### Four Phases of Swallowing

#### Phase 1: Oral Preparatory Phase
- Voluntary phase under cortical control [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 Table 1-9)
- Variable duration influenced by consistency of material and number of chews
- Lip closure prevents anterior spillage; mastication by CN V; tongue manipulation by CN XII; buccal tension by CN VII
- Requires tension in labial and buccal musculature, rotary jaw motion, lateral tongue movement
- Problems: drooling, pocketing [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 Table 1-9)

#### Phase 2: Oral (Propulsive/Transit) Phase
- Voluntary phase, lasts usually less than 1 second [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 Table 1-10)
- Tongue tip elevates, presses against hard palate, sequential posterior stripping motion
- Triggers pharyngeal swallow at anterior faucial arches
- Problems: prolonged oral transit time, premature spillage, drooling, pocketing, head tilt [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 Table 1-10)

#### Phase 3: Pharyngeal Phase (Most Critical for Airway Protection)
- Reflex/involuntary phase, lasts approximately 0.6-1 second [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 Table 1-11)
- Aspiration is MOST likely to occur during this phase [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 Table 1-11)
- Most commonly affected phase in brainstem strokes [BOARD-CORE]

**Sequential Events in the Pharyngeal Swallow:**
1. Soft palate elevation closes nasopharynx (velopharyngeal closure) [BOARD-CORE]
2. Hyoid bone and larynx elevate anteriorly and superiorly [BOARD-CORE]
3. Epiglottic inversion -- epiglottis deflects posteriorly and inferiorly to cover laryngeal inlet [BOARD-CORE]
4. Vocal fold adduction (true and false cords close) -- second layer of airway protection [BOARD-CORE]
5. Arytenoid cartilages tilt forward to contact epiglottic base -- third layer of closure [BOARD-CORE]
6. Pharyngeal peristalsis (sequential contraction of superior, middle, and inferior pharyngeal constrictors) [BOARD-CORE]
7. Cricopharyngeal relaxation -- UES opens via muscle relaxation AND mechanical pull from hyolaryngeal excursion [BOARD-CORE]
8. Bolus enters esophagus; UES closes behind it [BOARD-CORE]

**Three Layers of Airway Protection:**
(1) Epiglottic inversion over the laryngeal inlet, (2) True vocal fold adduction, (3) False vocal fold and arytenoid approximation. If any single layer fails, the others may still prevent aspiration. When multiple layers fail (as in severe brainstem stroke), aspiration risk is extremely high. Laryngeal elevation is the driving force. [BOARD-CORE]

#### Phase 4: Esophageal Phase
- Reflex phase, longest phase lasting 6-20 seconds [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 Table 1-12)
- Peristaltic waves propel bolus through esophagus to stomach
- Requires cricopharyngeal muscle contraction, coordinated peristalsis, and LES relaxation
- Rarely affected by stroke directly; deficits suggest structural/motility disorder [BOARD-CORE]
- Problems: heartburn, food sticking [BOARD-CORE]

## 3. CORTICAL VS BRAINSTEM DYSPHAGIA PATTERNS

### Cortical (Hemispheric) Dysphagia
- Swallowing is asymmetrically represented in the cortex; the swallowing-dominant hemisphere is NOT related to hand dominance [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Hamdy et al.; PMC7844337)
- Unilateral cortical lesions in either hemisphere can cause dysphagia [BOARD-CORE]
- Dysphagic patients with hemispheric stroke show strong bilateral reduction of cortical swallowing activation [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: PMC3061896)
- Recovery depends on cortical reorganization in the unaffected hemisphere [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Hamdy et al.)
- Predominantly affects oral phase and initiation of pharyngeal swallow [BOARD-CORE]
- Generally favorable prognosis with resolution within 2-4 weeks [BOARD-CORE] (Source: DeLisa Ch.18)

### Brainstem Dysphagia
- Damage to the brainstem usually leads to more severe dysphagia than cortical lesions [BOARD-CORE] (Source: MDPI 2039-4349/15/6/156)
- Lateral medulla (Wallenberg syndrome) produces the most severe dysphagia [BOARD-CORE]
- Lesions in the pons and medial/lateral medulla are highly associated with dysphagia; highest incidence in lateral medulla, followed by pons, medial medulla, then midbrain [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Meta-analysis; MDPI review 2024)
- Predominantly affects pharyngeal phase -- impaired pharyngeal constriction, laryngeal elevation, UES opening [BOARD-CORE]
- Acute cortical compensation occurs (right hemispheric lateralization observed) [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: PMC3061896)
- Recovery may be prolonged or incomplete [BOARD-CORE]

### Lesion-Specific Dysphagia Characteristics (2024 Research)
- Specific lesion locations correlate with distinct dysphagia characteristics [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: ScienceDirect S1052305724001277, 2024)
- Insular cortex lesions associated with pharyngeal phase dysfunction [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Internal capsule lesions associated with oral phase dysfunction [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 4. DIAGNOSTIC EVALUATION

### Bedside Swallowing Evaluation (BSE)
- First-line screening performed by SLP at the bedside [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38; DeLisa Ch.18)
- Includes medical history review, oral motor examination, trial swallows with various consistencies
- Observes for cough, wet/gurgly voice, delayed swallow, laryngeal elevation by palpation
- 3-oz water swallow test is a common screening protocol [BOARD-CORE]
- Sensitivity for aspiration: only 40-60% -- misses silent aspiration [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38)
- Cannot visualize pharyngeal phase directly [BOARD-CORE]
- Patients who are drowsy should be considered unsafe for oral intake [BOARD-CORE] (Source: DeLisa Ch.18)

### Predictors of Aspiration
**On VFSS:** Delayed initiation of the swallow reflex, decreased pharyngeal peristalsis [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.39)

**On Bedside Exam:** Abnormal cough, cough after swallow, dysphonia, dysarthria, abnormal gag reflex, voice change after swallow (wet voice) [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.39; Aronson, 1990)

### Dysphagia Screening Tools

#### TOR-BSST (Toronto Bedside Swallowing Screening Test)
- Sensitivity: 91.3-100% (CI 0.75-1.00); Specificity: 64% (CI 0.31-0.89) [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Martino et al., 2009; Cochrane review)
- Simple, accurate tool to identify stroke patients with dysphagia regardless of severity and setting [BOARD-CORE]
- Water-only screening protocol [BOARD-CORE]
- Can be administered by trained nurses [BOARD-CORE]

#### GUSS (Gugging Swallowing Screen)
- Sensitivity: 100% (CI 0.77-1.00); Specificity: 50-69% (CI 0.41-0.89) [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Trapl et al., 2007; Cochrane review)
- Uses water PLUS other consistencies (semisolid, solid) [BOARD-CORE]
- Stepwise approach: indirect swallowing test first, then direct testing with semisolid, liquid, solid [BOARD-CORE]
- Negative predictive value of 100% [BOARD-CORE]

#### Comparative Performance
- Both TOR-BSST and GUSS are among the best-performing screening tools [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cochrane systematic review)
- ESO-ESSD 2021 guideline recommends formal dysphagia screening using water swallow test or multiple consistency test as fast as possible after admission [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Dziewas et al., 2021, European Stroke Journal)
- Until screening is completed and swallowing judged safe, NO food, liquid, or oral medication should be given [BOARD-CORE] (Source: ESO-ESSD 2021)

#### Network Meta-Analysis of Screening Tools (2024)
- A comprehensive network meta-analysis in Frontiers in Neurology (2024) compared multiple dysphagia screening tools for stroke patients [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Front Neur 2024;1380287)
- Emphasized the need for personalized screening approaches based on patient characteristics [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Videofluoroscopic Swallow Study (VFSS / Modified Barium Swallow)
- GOLD STANDARD for dysphagia evaluation [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38; DeLisa Ch.18 p.26)
- Visualizes all 4 phases of swallowing in real time [BOARD-CORE]
- Detects aspiration and penetration with timing [BOARD-CORE]
- Tests compensatory strategies in real time (chin tuck, head turn) [BOARD-CORE]
- Evaluates multiple consistencies (thin liquid, nectar, puree, solid) [BOARD-CORE]
- Uses Penetration-Aspiration Scale (PAS) for grading [BOARD-CORE]
- Limitations: radiation exposure, requires patient transport to radiology [BOARD-CORE]
- Aspiration found in 40-70% of stroke patients on VFSS [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38)

### Fiberoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES)
- Portable -- can be done at bedside (ICU, nursing home) [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38; DeLisa Ch.18)
- No radiation exposure [BOARD-CORE]
- Excellent for laryngeal examination and secretion assessment [BOARD-CORE]
- Detects aspiration and penetration [BOARD-CORE]
- CANNOT visualize oral phase or exact moment of swallow ("white-out" period) [BOARD-CORE]
- Complements VFSS -- each has unique advantages [BOARD-CORE]

### VFSS vs FEES Comparison (Updated with 2023 Narrative Review)
| Feature | VFSS | FEES |
|---|---|---|
| Gold standard | Yes | No (complementary) |
| Oral phase | Visualized | Not visualized |
| Pharyngeal phase | Visualized | Partially (white-out) |
| Aspiration detection | Excellent | Good (sensitivity 0.80, specificity 0.92 for aspiration) |
| Penetration detection | Excellent | Excellent (sensitivity 1.00, specificity 0.83) |
| Radiation | Yes | No |
| Portability | Requires radiology suite | Bedside capable |
| Secretion assessment | Limited | Excellent |
| Repeat testing | Radiation concern | No limit |

(Source: App content; Cuccurullo; DeLisa; PubMed 36640583, 2023 narrative review) [BOARD-CORE]

FEES may be preferred as first-line examination due to limited invasiveness and no ionizing radiation. VFSS could be useful following FEES for full visualization when aspiration events might be missed. [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: 2023 narrative review, Radiography)

### Penetration-Aspiration Scale (Rosenbek PAS)
| Score | Description |
|---|---|
| 1 | Material does not enter the airway |
| 2 | Material enters airway, remains above vocal folds, ejected |
| 3 | Material enters airway, remains above vocal folds, NOT ejected |
| 4 | Material contacts vocal folds, ejected |
| 5 | Material contacts vocal folds, NOT ejected |
| 6 | Material passes below vocal folds, ejected |
| 7 | Material passes below vocal folds, NOT ejected despite effort |
| 8 | Material passes below vocal folds, NO effort made to eject (SILENT ASPIRATION) |

- PAS 1 = normal [BOARD-CORE]
- PAS 2-5 = penetration (material above or at vocal folds) [BOARD-CORE]
- PAS 6-8 = aspiration (material below vocal folds) [BOARD-CORE]
- PAS 8 = silent aspiration -- most dangerous, no cough reflex, highest risk of aspiration pneumonia [BOARD-CORE]

### Vallecular and Pyriform Sinus Residue Patterns
- Vallecular residue: indicates reduced tongue base retraction or poor bolus propulsion. Treatment: effortful swallow, chin tuck, tongue base exercises [BOARD-CORE]
- Pyriform sinus residue: indicates reduced pharyngeal contraction, impaired laryngeal elevation, or cricopharyngeal dysfunction [BOARD-CORE]
- Unilateral pyriform residue suggests unilateral pharyngeal weakness -- head turn toward weak side [BOARD-CORE]
- Bilateral pyriform residue suggests global pharyngeal weakness or UES dysfunction [BOARD-CORE]
- Treatment: Mendelsohn maneuver, head rotation, Shaker exercise [BOARD-CORE]

## 5. SILENT ASPIRATION

### Definition and Prevalence
- Aspiration MISSED in 40-60% of patients on bedside clinical examination alone [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.38)
- Food or liquid enters airway below vocal cords WITHOUT triggering a cough reflex [BOARD-CORE]
- More common with brainstem and bilateral strokes [BOARD-CORE]
- No consensus on which clinical components best detect silent aspiration [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Frontiers in Neurology 2025 systematic review)

### Detection Methods
- VFSS and FEES are the gold standard methods for detecting silent aspiration [BOARD-CORE]
- Cough reflex testing (CRT): supplementary screening but accuracy lacks consensus [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Trimble 2023, Int J Lang Commun Disord)
- Simplified cough test: sensitivity 87.1%, specificity 66.7% for detecting silent aspiration [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Frontiers in Neurology 2025 meta-analysis)
- Clinical swallow exam may have higher sensitivity than CRT alone for silent aspiration identification [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Trimble 2023)

### Emerging Technology
- Real-time dynamic monitoring using multisource signals (sound, nasal airflow, EMG, pressure, acceleration) under investigation with semisupervised deep learning [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: PMC10331777, 2023)
- Machine learning predictive models for aspiration screening in hospitalized stroke patients [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: Scientific Reports 2023)
- Voxel-based symptom-lesion mapping identifies infratentorial lesion locations associated with silent aspiration [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: PMC10957678, 2024)

## 6. ASPIRATION PNEUMONIA

### Aspiration Pneumonia vs Aspiration Pneumonitis
| Feature | Aspiration Pneumonia | Aspiration Pneumonitis |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Infection from aspiration of colonized oropharyngeal secretions | Chemical injury from sterile gastric contents (acid) |
| Onset | Hours to days | Acute (within hours) |
| Presentation | Fever, productive cough, infiltrate on imaging | Inflammatory response, NOT initially infectious |
| Organisms | Anaerobes, gram-negative rods, Staphylococcus | None initially |
| Treatment | Antibiotics | Supportive; antibiotics NOT initially needed |
| Classic form | -- | Mendelson syndrome |
| Progression | Infection | May resolve or progress to ARDS |

[BOARD-CORE] (Source: App content)

### Risk Factors for Aspiration Pneumonia
- Decreased level of consciousness [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.39)
- Poor oral hygiene (colonized oral secretions) -- KEY modifiable factor [BOARD-CORE]
- Tracheostomy [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.39)
- Tube feeding (does NOT eliminate aspiration risk) [BOARD-CORE]
- Emesis, reflux [BOARD-CORE]
- Reduced cough reflex [BOARD-CORE]
- Immune compromise [BOARD-CORE]
- Prolonged pharyngeal transit time [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.39)
- Volume and chronicity of aspiration [BOARD-CORE]

### Prevention Evidence (Recent 2020-2025)
- Oral hygiene conditions are significantly associated with aspiration pneumonia incidence in elderly stroke patients [BOARD-CORE] (Source: PubMed 40966864, 2025; ScienceDirect)
- Structured oral care protocols reduce risk of aspiration pneumonia in acute stroke [BOARD-CORE] (Source: AHA Stroke 2012; PMC7607750 2020 pilot RCT)
- Oral hygiene is more closely associated with initial aspiration pneumonia onset, while oral function plays a greater role in recurrence [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: PubMed 40966864, 2025)
- Standardized nursing assistant education programs for oral hygiene decrease non-ventilator hospital-acquired pneumonia rates [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: PMC11931980, Kozub 2025)
- Aspiration of oropharyngeal secretions is the primary mechanism for stroke-associated pneumonia [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Frontiers Rehab Sciences 2024)

### The NPO Paradox (2025 Research)
- NPO patients were 2.5 times MORE likely to develop stroke-associated pneumonia (SAP) than those without dietary restrictions (p = 0.008) [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Scientific Reports 2025, s41598-025-22020-3)
- On admission, 63% of SAP patients were already on NPO, 33.3% received texture-modified food, only 3.7% had unrestricted oral intake [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Most SAP cases were diagnosed before any oral intake, with majority occurring by day 3 [BOARD-LIKELY]
- This challenges the assumption that strict dietary restrictions prevent aspiration pneumonia [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Key insight: aspiration of ORAL SECRETIONS (not food) is the primary driver of SAP, so NPO does not address the root cause [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 7. MANAGEMENT

### Compensatory Strategies
| Swallowing Deficit | Recommended Strategy | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Delayed pharyngeal trigger | Chin tuck, thermal-tactile stimulation | Chin tuck narrows laryngeal vestibule, widens valleculae [BOARD-CORE] |
| Reduced tongue base retraction | Chin tuck, effortful swallow | Pushes tongue base posteriorly [BOARD-CORE] |
| Unilateral pharyngeal weakness | Head rotation toward weak side | Closes affected pyriform sinus, directs bolus to stronger side [BOARD-CORE] |
| Reduced laryngeal closure | Supraglottic swallow, chin tuck | Voluntary breath hold before/during swallow, cough after [BOARD-CORE] |
| Reduced UES opening | Mendelsohn maneuver, Shaker exercise | Prolongs laryngeal elevation, opens UES [BOARD-CORE] |
| Oral phase dysfunction | Head tilt toward strong side, modified diet | Uses gravity to direct bolus [BOARD-CORE] |

(Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.41; DeLisa Ch.18 p.27; App content) [BOARD-CORE]

#### Chin Tuck -- Most Commonly Tested Strategy
- Pushes tongue base posteriorly, narrows laryngeal vestibule, widens valleculae [BOARD-CORE]
- Most effective for delayed pharyngeal swallow trigger and reduced tongue base retraction [BOARD-CORE]
- NOT effective for all patients -- should be tested under VFSS [BOARD-CORE]
- Provides airway protection by facilitating forward motion of larynx and decreasing space between tongue base and posterior pharyngeal wall [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.41)

### Diet Modification: IDDSI Framework

#### IDDSI Replacing NDD
- IDDSI (International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative) replaced the National Dysphagia Diet (NDD) as the international standard [BOARD-CORE] (Source: ASHA; Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics)
- In October 2021, IDDSI became the ONLY texture-modified diet recognized by the Academy's Nutrition Care Manual (NCM) [BOARD-CORE] (Source: iddsi.org; Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics)
- NDD and its associated resources are no longer included in the NCM after October 2021 [BOARD-CORE]
- Uses numbered levels (0-7) with color coding for drinks (levels 0-4) and foods (levels 3-7) [BOARD-CORE]
- Standardized testing methods (fork drip test, spoon tilt test) provide objective measurement rather than subjective descriptions [BOARD-CORE]
- Levels 3-4 overlap between drinks and foods [BOARD-CORE]
- Research from East Asian countries (2021-2022) supports universal applicability regardless of food culture [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: PMC9592427)

#### IDDSI Levels
| Level | Drinks | Foods | Testing Method |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (Thin) | Water, tea, juice | -- | Flows through prongs of fork |
| 1 (Slightly Thick) | Thicker than water | -- | Flows through fork prongs slowly |
| 2 (Mildly Thick) | Sippable, drips slowly | -- | Drips slowly off fork in drizzle |
| 3 (Moderately Thick / Liquidized) | Cannot drink from cup, needs spoon | Smooth puree, no lumps | Does not drip through fork; cannot be sucked through straw |
| 4 (Extremely Thick / Pureed) | Cannot be sucked through straw | No lumps, not sticky | Sits in a pile on fork, does not flow off |
| 5 (Minced & Moist) | -- | 4mm pieces, can be mashed with fork | Pieces visible, easily mashed |
| 6 (Soft & Bite-Sized) | -- | 1.5cm pieces, soft enough to mash | Can be scooped and shaped on plate |
| 7 (Regular) | -- | Normal everyday foods | No specific test |

[BOARD-CORE] (Source: iddsi.org; App content)

- Thickened liquids slow bolus transit but may reduce compliance and hydration [BOARD-CORE]
- Implementation is voluntary but increasingly adopted globally [BOARD-CORE]

### Rehabilitative Exercises

#### Mendelsohn Maneuver
- Patient voluntarily holds larynx at highest point during swallow [BOARD-CORE]
- Prolongs UES opening duration and laryngeal elevation [BOARD-CORE]
- Target: reduced UES opening, reduced laryngeal elevation [BOARD-CORE]
- Evidence: Moderate -- improves UES opening duration and hyoid excursion on VFSS [BOARD-CORE]
(Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.41; App content)

#### Effortful Swallow
- Patient squeezes all swallowing muscles as hard as possible during swallow [BOARD-CORE]
- Increases tongue base pressure against posterior pharyngeal wall [BOARD-CORE]
- Target: reduced tongue base retraction, vallecular residue [BOARD-CORE]
- Evidence: Moderate -- increases pharyngeal pressure and reduces residue [BOARD-CORE]

#### Shaker Exercise (Head Lift)
- Supine head raises (isometric hold + isotonic repetitions) [BOARD-CORE]
- Strengthens suprahyoid muscles that open the UES [BOARD-CORE]
- Target: reduced UES opening, pyriform sinus residue [BOARD-CORE]
- Evidence: Strong -- RCT showed significant improvement in UES opening and reduced aspiration [BOARD-CORE]

#### EMST (Expiratory Muscle Strength Training)
- Forceful exhalation against a pressure threshold device [BOARD-CORE]
- Strengthens expiratory and suprahyoid muscles [BOARD-CORE]
- Target: weak cough, reduced hyolaryngeal excursion [BOARD-CORE]
- Effective for developing suprahyoid muscle activity in stroke patients [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Park et al., 2016)
- One 4-week intervention improved penetration/aspiration scores by 41% and cough acceleration volume by 103% [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Pitts et al., 2016)
- Evidence is MIXED overall -- some studies show benefit, others show no difference, potentially due to differing training regimes and adherence [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Plowman 2020 systematic review; PMC7752042 2020)
- 2025 meta-analysis confirms resistance exercise improves swallowing safety, oral intake capacity, and psychological wellbeing in post-stroke dysphagia [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Frontiers Physiology 2025)

#### Masako Maneuver (Tongue Hold)
- Swallow while holding tongue tip between teeth [BOARD-CORE]
- Forces posterior pharyngeal wall to compensate [BOARD-CORE]
- Limited evidence; may cause fatigue; practice WITHOUT food [BOARD-CORE]

#### McNeill Dysphagia Therapy Program (MDTP)
- Systematic exercise-based therapy using exercise-physiology and motor learning principles [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Carnaby-Mann; PMC7067660)
- High-intensity program to safely advance oral intake [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2020 double-blind placebo-controlled trial: MDTP demonstrated greater positive change than NMES or usual care [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Carnaby 2020, J Oral Rehab; PMC7067660)
- MDTP patients 4.3x more likely to return to pre-stroke diet (95% CI: 1.08-17.2, p<0.03) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Increase in oral intake and improved functional outcome by 3 months post-stroke (RR = 1.72) [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Neuromuscular Electrical Stimulation (NMES / VitalStim)
- VitalStim: surface electrodes on anterior neck, FDA-cleared but CONTROVERSIAL [BOARD-CORE] (Source: App content; Cochrane review)
- Theory: electrical stimulation strengthens or re-educates swallowing muscles [BOARD-CORE]
- Electrode placement is CRITICAL -- incorrect placement may PULL larynx DOWN, worsening aspiration [BOARD-CORE]
- 2023 meta-analysis: NMES is more effective than treatment without NMES, but effect sizes are SMALL [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Frontiers Neurology 2023; 1163045)
- Cochrane review: insufficient evidence to support or refute NMES for dysphagia [BOARD-CORE]
- Combined NMES + conventional therapy is more effective than either alone [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Frontiers Neurology 2025; 1700317)
- Should NOT be used as standalone treatment -- best as adjunct to traditional therapy [BOARD-CORE]
- Contraindications: cardiac pacemaker, anterior neck malignancy, cervical hardware [BOARD-CORE]
- **Board answer: evidence is inconclusive; traditional swallowing therapy remains the standard of care** [BOARD-CORE]

## 8. NEUROSTIMULATION APPROACHES (EMERGING)

### Pharyngeal Electrical Stimulation (PES)
- Intraluminal catheter delivers electrical stimulation directly to pharyngeal mucosa [BOARD-EMERGING]
- PHAST-TRAC trial: decannulation possible in 75% of treatment vs 20% in sham group for tracheotomized patients [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Dziewas et al., 2018)
- PHADER cohort study (2020): PES safe and associated with reduced dysphagia and PAS measures [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Lancet eClinicalMedicine 2020)
- PhEED clinical trial (2025): active stimulation significantly reduced dysphagia severity at day 83 [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: J Rehab Med 2025)
- Transcutaneous electrical stimulation (2025): improvements act through neurophysiological/sensory pathways rather than direct muscle stimulation [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: Scientific Reports 2025)
- ESO-ESSD 2021 guideline: in tracheotomized stroke patients with severe dysphagia, PES is suggested to accelerate decannulation [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: Dziewas et al., 2021)

### Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS)
- Promising approach for improving swallowing function after stroke [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: Multiple meta-analyses 2024-2025)
- 2024 network meta-analysis (18 RCTs, 760 participants): HF rTMS to ipsilesional hemisphere, bilateral hemisphere, and cerebellar targets all significantly improved swallowing function [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: ScienceDirect S0003999324014229)
- Acute stroke: HF ipsilesional hemisphere rTMS most effective [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Subacute stroke: HF ipsilesional + LF contralesional rTMS best efficacy [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Chronic stroke: rTMS failed to improve swallowing function [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Cerebellar rTMS combined with conventional exercises surpasses exercises alone [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: Karger 2024)
- ESO-ESSD 2021: neurostimulation recommended preferably within clinical trial settings [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: ESO-ESSD guideline)

### Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS)
- 2023 network meta-analysis (20 studies, 838 patients): anodal tDCS at 1.4 mA for 20 minutes identified as optimal parameters [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: Frontiers Neurology 2023; 1098831)
- 2025 study: bilateral tDCS demonstrated significantly greater improvement than unilateral stimulation [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: Scientific Reports 2025)
- tDCS combined with conventional dysphagia therapy more effective than either alone [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: PubMed 37247074, 2024)
- ESO-ESSD 2021: recommended as adjunct, preferably in clinical trial settings [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Capsaicin and TRPV-1 Agonists
- Alter sensory perception in the pharynx [BOARD-EMERGING] (Source: Labeit et al., Lancet Neurology 2024)
- Under investigation as pharmaceutical approach to enhance swallowing reflex [BOARD-EMERGING]
- May particularly benefit patients with reduced pharyngeal sensation [BOARD-EMERGING]

## 9. TRACHEOSTOMY AND SWALLOWING

- Tracheostomy tethers the larynx, reducing hyolaryngeal elevation [BOARD-CORE]
- Inflated cuff does NOT reliably prevent aspiration (material can pass around the cuff) [BOARD-CORE]
- Inflated cuff eliminates subglottic air pressure, impairing cough and sensation [BOARD-CORE]
- Deflating the cuff restores subglottic air pressure and improves swallowing [BOARD-CORE]

### Passy-Muir Speaking Valve (PMV)
- One-way valve: air IN through trach, OUT through upper airway [BOARD-CORE]
- Restores subglottic pressure, improves swallow coordination, enables phonation [BOARD-CORE]
- REQUIRES cuff FULLY DEFLATED -- air must exit through upper airway [BOARD-CORE]
- NEVER place PMV with cuff inflated -- creates closed system and respiratory distress [BOARD-CORE]
- Fenestrated tube or cuff deflation allows some phonation without PMV [BOARD-CORE]

## 10. ALTERNATIVE FEEDING

### NG vs PEG vs PEJ
| Feature | NG Tube | PEG | PEJ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Placement | Bedside, no sedation | Endoscopic, mild sedation | Endoscopic or surgical |
| Timing | Immediate, short-term (<4 weeks) | When NPO expected >4 weeks | When gastric feeding fails |
| Aspiration risk | Present | Present | Lower (post-pyloric) |
| Complications | Nasal irritation, sinusitis, displacement | Wound infection (5-25%), buried bumper | Tube migration, clogging, dumping |
| Tolerance | Poor | Better | Similar to PEG |
| FOOD Trial | NG preferred first 2-4 weeks | No benefit over NG in early stroke | Limited stroke-specific evidence |

[BOARD-CORE] (Source: App content; FOOD Trial)

### Critical Points
- PEG does NOT prevent aspiration or aspiration pneumonia [BOARD-CORE]
- Patients still aspirate oral secretions and can aspirate refluxed gastric contents [BOARD-CORE]
- Some studies suggest PEG-fed patients may have HIGHER aspiration pneumonia rates [BOARD-CORE]
- Good oral hygiene is the single most important intervention regardless of feeding method [BOARD-CORE]
- PEG placement should be considered when unable to tolerate grade 3 thickened fluids or pureed diet 14 days post-stroke [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- Decision should be multidisciplinary and patient-centered [BOARD-CORE]

### Timing of Oral Feeding Initiation
- ESO-ESSD 2021: no food/liquid until formal screening completed and swallowing judged safe [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Dziewas et al., 2021)
- Clinical practice guidelines vary: some recommend within 24 hours, others within 3-7 days [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Frontiers in Stroke 2025)
- Early initiation of oral feeding after clinical stabilization may safely improve outcomes (survival, chest infection rates, length of stay, swallowing function) [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: BMC Neurology 2011; Frontiers 2025)
- NOT advisable to start feeding on the first day of stroke because condition may be unclear [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Frontiers Nutrition 2025)
- Factors associated with achieving oral intake: young age, starting oral intake early, no stroke recurrence, achieving early rehabilitation of daily activities [BOARD-LIKELY] (Source: PMC9730258, 2022)

## 11. ESO-ESSD 2021 GUIDELINE KEY RECOMMENDATIONS

The European Stroke Organisation and European Society for Swallowing Disorders published comprehensive evidence-based guidelines in 2021 (Dziewas et al., European Stroke Journal 2021). Key recommendations:

1. **Screening**: All stroke patients should receive formal dysphagia screening as fast as possible after admission [BOARD-CORE]
2. **NPO until screening**: No food, liquid, or oral medication until screening completed and swallowing judged safe [BOARD-CORE]
3. **Instrumental assessment**: VFSS or FEES recommended for patients who fail screening [BOARD-CORE]
4. **Diet modification**: Texture-modified food and thickened liquids may reduce pneumonia risk [BOARD-CORE]
5. **Behavioral therapy**: Exercises, compensatory maneuvers, and postural changes recommended [BOARD-CORE]
6. **Oral care**: Oral care interventions recommended to reduce pneumonia risk [BOARD-CORE]
7. **Neurostimulation**: rTMS, tDCS, PES recommended as adjunct, preferably within clinical trials [BOARD-LIKELY]
8. **PES for tracheotomized patients**: Suggested to accelerate decannulation [BOARD-LIKELY]
9. **Early rehabilitation**: Swallowing therapy should begin as soon as possible [BOARD-CORE]

## 12. RECOVERY PROGNOSIS

### Prognostic Indicators
- Ickenstein et al. (2012): Subjects 72 hours post-stroke rated at level 1-3 on Functional Communication Measure and PAS level 5-8 were 11.8 times LESS likely to be orally fed 90 days post-stroke [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- Wilkinson et al. (2002): Subjects tolerating grade 1 fluids or modified soft diet 7 days post-stroke -- none needed PEG [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- Subjects needing PEG 14 days post-stroke: 50% could not tolerate grade 3 thickened fluids; 52% could not tolerate pureed diet [BOARD-CORE]
- Of subjects tolerating grade 3 fluids at day 7, 36% tolerated a normal diet by day 28 [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- Gresham (1990): 85% on full oral nutrition at discharge; 17% could not drink thin liquids safely [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)
- Nasal speech: caused by partial or complete failure of soft palate closure; uplifting the soft palate prevents nasal speech [BOARD-CORE] (Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1 p.42)

## BOARD-READINESS TAG SUMMARY

### BOARD-CORE (Established, definitely tested)
- Dysphagia prevalence by stroke location (25-65%, brainstem 67%)
- Four phases of swallowing and cranial nerve involvement
- VFSS as gold standard; FEES as complement
- Penetration-Aspiration Scale
- Compensatory strategies (chin tuck, head rotation, Mendelsohn, etc.)
- Shaker exercise evidence (strongest RCT)
- NMES/VitalStim: inconclusive evidence, traditional therapy remains standard
- PEG does NOT prevent aspiration
- NG for <4 weeks, PEG for >4 weeks
- Silent aspiration missed in 40-60% at bedside
- Tracheostomy and PMV management
- IDDSI framework replacing NDD

### BOARD-LIKELY (Recent but adopted, probably appearing soon)
- ESO-ESSD 2021 guideline recommendations
- IDDSI as only NCM-recognized standard since Oct 2021
- FEES as preferred first-line in certain settings
- MDTP evidence (2020 RCT)
- NPO paradox (2025)
- PES for tracheotomized patients
- Oral hygiene phase-specific prevention
- TOR-BSST and GUSS screening tool performance

### BOARD-EMERGING (Too new for current exam cycles)
- rTMS and tDCS for post-stroke dysphagia
- Cerebellar rTMS protocols
- PES (PhEED trial)
- Capsaicin/TRPV-1 agonists
- Machine learning screening models
- Real-time monitoring with deep learning
- Lesion-specific dysphagia mapping
