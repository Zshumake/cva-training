## SECTION 1: CEREBRAL BLOOD SUPPLY OVERVIEW

### Core Concepts [BOARD-CORE]

The brain receives approximately 15-20% of total cardiac output despite comprising only about 2% of body weight. The cerebral circulation is divided into two systems:

- **Anterior circulation (carotid system)**: Supplies approximately 80% of cerebral blood flow
- **Posterior circulation (vertebrobasilar system)**: Supplies approximately 20% of cerebral blood flow

The two systems are interconnected via the Circle of Willis at the base of the brain.

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation Board Review, Chapter 1; DeLisa, Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation, 6th Ed, Chapter 18.

## SECTION 2: ANTERIOR CIRCULATION (CAROTID SYSTEM)

### Internal Carotid Artery (ICA) [BOARD-CORE]

- ICA supplies approximately 80% of cerebral blood flow
- Enters the skull through the carotid canal
- Gives off the ophthalmic artery (supplies retina and optic nerve)
- Terminates by bifurcating into the ACA and MCA
- ICA occlusion is the most variable syndrome -- from asymptomatic (good collateral circulation) to massive hemispheric infarction (poor collaterals)
- ICA occlusions are often asymptomatic (30-40% of cases) due to collateral flow via the Circle of Willis
- Amaurosis fugax (transient monocular blindness) occurs prior to stroke onset in approximately 25% of ICA occlusion cases

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 8-9; DeLisa, Chapter 18, Stroke Syndromes.

### Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) [BOARD-CORE]

The MCA is the largest cerebral artery and most commonly affected vessel in stroke.

**Territory**: Lateral surface of the frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes; deep structures via lenticulostriate branches.

**MCA Segments**:
- M1 (horizontal/sphenoidal): Gives rise to lenticulostriate arteries
- M2 (insular/sylvian): Divides into superior and inferior divisions
- M3 (opercular): Cortical branches
- M4 (cortical): Terminal branches

**Superior Division Occlusion**:
- Contralateral face and arm weakness greater than leg
- Broca aphasia (dominant hemisphere)
- Head/eye deviation toward lesion side
- Hemispatial neglect, constructional apraxia (nondominant hemisphere)
- Most common cause of superior division occlusion is embolism

**Inferior Division Occlusion**:
- Wernicke aphasia (dominant hemisphere)
- Severe hemispatial neglect (nondominant hemisphere)
- Superior quadrantanopia or homonymous hemianopia
- Minimal or absent motor deficit

**Complete MCA Occlusion**:
- Contralateral hemiplegia (face and arm greater than leg)
- Global aphasia (dominant hemisphere)
- Hemispatial neglect with anosognosia (nondominant hemisphere)
- Contralateral homonymous hemianopia
- Head and eye deviation toward lesion

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 9-10; DeLisa, Chapter 18, Middle Cerebral Artery Syndromes.

### Lenticulostriate Arteries -- "Arteries of Stroke" [BOARD-CORE]

- Small perforating branches arising from the M1 segment of the MCA
- Supply the putamen, globus pallidus, caudate head, and posterior limb of the internal capsule
- Named "arteries of stroke" by Charcot because they are the most common site of hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage and lacunar infarction
- Lack significant collateral supply -- occlusion results in predictable deep infarcts
- Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms form on these vessels in chronic hypertension, predisposing to rupture
- Lipohyalinosis of these vessels causes lacunar infarcts

**Lacunar Syndromes from Lenticulostriate Occlusion** [BOARD-CORE]:

| Syndrome | Location | Presentation |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| Pure motor hemiplegia | Posterior limb internal capsule, corona radiata, pons | Face, arm, leg weakness; no sensory/cortical deficits |
| Pure sensory stroke | Thalamus (ventrolateral) | Contralateral numbness/paresthesias |
| Dysarthria-clumsy hand | Basis pontis, anterior limb internal capsule | Dysarthria + hand clumsiness |
| Sensorimotor stroke | Junction internal capsule/thalamus | Combined motor + sensory |
| Ataxic hemiparesis | Pons, internal capsule, cerebellum | Ipsilateral ataxia + contralateral weakness |

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, Table 1-4; DeLisa, Chapter 18.

### Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) [BOARD-CORE]

**Territory**: Medial surface of the frontal and parietal lobes, including the leg area of the motor/sensory homunculus.

**ACA Occlusion Produces**:
- Contralateral leg weakness and sensory loss (leg greater than arm or face -- OPPOSITE of MCA pattern)
- Urinary incontinence (medial frontal micturition centers)
- Gait apraxia (frontal gait disorder)
- Grasp reflex and paratonic rigidity (gegenhalten)
- Transcortical motor aphasia (dominant hemisphere)
- Abulia/akinetic mutism (bilateral ACA infarcts)
- Alien hand syndrome (corpus callosum involvement)
- Personality changes (medial frontal lobe)

**Key Point**: Proximal A1 occlusion is usually well tolerated because collateral flow crosses via AComm from the contralateral ACA.

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 10-11; DeLisa, Chapter 18, Anterior Cerebral Artery Syndromes.

### Anterior Choroidal Artery [BOARD-CORE]

- Arises from the ICA just distal to the PComm origin
- Supplies posterior limb of internal capsule (lateral portion), optic tract, lateral geniculate body, medial temporal lobe
- Classic triad of occlusion: contralateral hemiplegia + hemisensory loss + homonymous hemianopia
- Can mimic MCA stroke but tends to have equal face/arm/leg involvement
- Full syndrome is uncommon due to collateral supply

**Source**: App content (vascular_anatomy_content.dart).

## SECTION 3: POSTERIOR CIRCULATION (VERTEBROBASILAR SYSTEM)

### Vertebral Arteries [BOARD-CORE]

- Arise from the subclavian arteries
- Ascend through the transverse foramina of C1-C6
- Enter the skull through the foramen magnum
- Give off PICA before joining
- Two vertebral arteries merge at the pontomedullary junction to form the basilar artery

### Basilar Artery [BOARD-CORE]

- Formed by the confluence of the two vertebral arteries
- Gives off AICA and SCA
- Terminates by bifurcating into the two posterior cerebral arteries (PCAs)
- Supplies pons, cerebellum, and midbrain

**Basilar Artery Occlusion**: Devastating -- produces bilateral motor/sensory deficits, cranial nerve palsies, coma. Locked-in syndrome occurs with bilateral ventral pons infarction (conscious patient who can only move eyes vertically and blink).

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 12-16; DeLisa, Chapter 18.

### Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA) [BOARD-CORE]

**Territory**: Occipital lobe, inferior temporal lobe, thalamus, midbrain.

**PCA Origin Variants** [BOARD-CORE]:
- 70% of cases: Both PCAs arise from basilar artery
- 20-25%: One PCA from basilar, one from ICA (fetal PCA)
- 5-10%: Both PCAs arise from the ICAs

**Fetal PCA** [BOARD-LIKELY]: Present in approximately 15-30% of the population. When present, the PCA territory depends on anterior (carotid) circulation rather than posterior. Clinical implication: carotid occlusion may produce visual field deficits (PCA territory stroke). A 2025 study found fetal PCA is a morphological risk factor for basilar artery plaque formation (OR 3.196), suggesting a mechanism for increased posterior circulation stroke risk.

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, p. 11; Frontiers in Neurology 2025.

**PCA Occlusion Produces** [BOARD-CORE]:
- Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing (dual blood supply to macula from MCA and PCA)
- Anton syndrome: cortical blindness with denial of visual loss (bilateral PCA)
- Alexia without agraphia: can write but cannot read (dominant PCA with splenium involvement)
- Visual agnosia and prosopagnosia
- Thalamic pain syndrome (Dejerine-Roussy): contralateral burning pain from thalamic infarction
- Memory impairment (medial temporal structures)
- Weber syndrome: ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral hemiplegia (midbrain interpeduncular branches)

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 11-12; DeLisa, Chapter 18.

### Cerebellar Arteries and Their Syndromes [BOARD-CORE]

| Artery | Origin | Territory | Syndrome |
|--------|--------|-----------|----------|
| PICA | Vertebral artery | Lateral medulla, inferior cerebellar surface | **Wallenberg (lateral medullary)**: ipsilateral Horner syndrome, facial pain/temp loss, ataxia; contralateral body pain/temp loss; dysphagia, hoarseness |
| AICA | Proximal basilar | Lateral pons, middle cerebellar peduncle, inner ear | **Lateral pontine**: ipsilateral facial paralysis (CN VII), deafness (CN VIII), ataxia; contralateral body pain/temp loss |
| SCA | Distal basilar | Superior cerebellar surface, rostral pons | Ipsilateral cerebellar ataxia, contralateral pain/temp loss, possible tremor/choreiform movements |

**Distinguishing the Three**: All produce ipsilateral cerebellar ataxia + contralateral pain/temperature loss. Key differentiators:
- PICA = lateral medullary signs (dysphagia, hoarseness, Horner)
- AICA = lateral pontine signs (CN VII facial palsy, CN VIII hearing loss)
- SCA = superior cerebellar signs, rarely tested in isolation

**Wallenberg Syndrome Details** [BOARD-CORE]:
Most commonly due to vertebral artery occlusion (8 out of 10 cases), not PICA directly.

| Symptom | Anatomic Basis |
|---------|---------------|
| Ipsilateral Horner syndrome | Pupillodilator (sympathetic) fibers |
| Ipsilateral facial pain/temp loss | Spinal trigeminal tract |
| Ipsilateral ataxia (UE greater than LE) | Spinocerebellar tract |
| Contralateral body pain/temp loss | Spinothalamic tract |
| Dysphagia, hoarseness | Nucleus ambiguus, CN IX/X |
| Vertigo, nausea, vomiting | Vestibular nuclei |

**Key Point**: No facial or extremity muscle weakness in Wallenberg syndrome.

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, Table 1-2A, pp. 13-14; DeLisa, Chapter 18.

### Brainstem Syndromes (Medial/Paramedian) [BOARD-CORE]

| Syndrome | Level | Artery | Features |
|----------|-------|--------|----------|
| Weber | Midbrain | PCA (interpeduncular branches) | Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral hemiplegia |
| Benedikt | Midbrain | PCA | Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral hyperkinesia (tremor, chorea) |
| Millard-Gubler | Pons | Basilar (circumferential branches) | Ipsilateral CN VI/VII palsy + contralateral hemiplegia |
| Medial medullary | Medulla | Vertebral/anterior spinal artery | Ipsilateral CN XII palsy + contralateral hemiplegia + lemniscal sensory loss |

**Mnemonic**: Paramedian syndromes follow the "Rule of 4s" -- medial structures (motor nuclei of CNs, corticospinal tract, medial lemniscus) are affected by paramedian perforator occlusion. Lateral syndromes spare motor tracts.

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, Tables 1-2B and 1-3.

## SECTION 4: CIRCLE OF WILLIS

### Anatomy [BOARD-CORE]

The Circle of Willis is an arterial anastomosis at the base of the brain surrounding the optic chiasm and pituitary infundibulum. Components:
- Anterior communicating artery (AComm) -- connects L and R ACAs
- Bilateral A1 segments of ACA
- Bilateral terminal ICA portions
- Bilateral posterior communicating arteries (PComm) -- connect ICA to PCA
- Bilateral P1 segments of PCA
- Basilar artery tip

### Completeness and Clinical Significance [BOARD-CORE]

Only 20-25% of the population has a complete, fully functional Circle of Willis. A 2023 systematic review confirmed this finding and noted that only 20.9% of subjects had complete anatomy on MRA, while only 45.2% had typical anatomy at autopsy.

**Impact on Stroke Outcomes** [BOARD-LIKELY]: An incomplete Circle of Willis is associated with significantly lower odds of good short-term outcomes after stroke, even after adjusting for age and stroke severity. However, in isolated M1 occlusion, Circle of Willis configuration may not play as significant a role.

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, Figure 1-2; PMC 2023 (PMID 9948548); JMCHEM Sci 2024 systematic review.

### Common Variants [BOARD-CORE]

| Variant | Prevalence | Clinical Implication |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| Hypoplastic/absent PComm (one or both) | 30-40% | Reduced collateral between anterior/posterior systems |
| Fetal PCA (PCA arises from ICA) | 15-30% | PCA territory depends on anterior circulation |
| Hypoplastic/absent A1 (ACA) | ~10% | Contralateral ACA must supply both medial hemispheres |
| Absent AComm | ~5% | No cross-filling between hemispheres |
| Complete circle | 20-25% | Full collateral reserve |

### Aneurysm Sites [BOARD-CORE]

| Location | Frequency | Clinical Significance |
|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| AComm artery | 30-35% | Most common saccular aneurysm site; may compress optic chiasm causing bitemporal hemianopia |
| PComm artery | 25-30% | Compresses CN III: ipsilateral ptosis, mydriasis, "down and out" eye |
| MCA bifurcation | ~20% | Focal deficits |
| Basilar tip | 5-10% | Brainstem compression |
| ICA | 5-10% | Cavernous sinus involvement |

90-95% of saccular aneurysms occur in the anterior part of the Circle of Willis. Risk of rupture increases at 10 mm or larger. Peak age for rupture is 5th-6th decade.

**PComm Aneurysm and CN III**: A pupil-involving CN III palsy (ptosis + mydriasis + "down and out" eye) is an aneurysm until proven otherwise and requires emergent CTA/angiography.

**Source**: Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 17-18, Figure 1-12; App content.

## SECTION 5: VASCULAR TERRITORIES AND PATTERN RECOGNITION

### Homunculus Mapping [BOARD-CORE]

The motor and sensory homunculus is arranged with leg medially (ACA territory) and face/arm laterally (MCA territory). The homunculus is "upside down" -- foot and genitalia are on the medial surface near the interhemispheric fissure.

**Territory Pattern Recognition** (High-Yield Board Pearl):
- Face and arm greater than leg = MCA territory
- Leg greater than arm and face = ACA territory
- Visual field cuts with macular sparing = PCA territory

### Visual Field Deficits by Lesion Location [BOARD-CORE]

| Lesion | Visual Field Deficit | Vascular Territory |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Optic nerve | Monocular blindness (ipsilateral) | Ophthalmic artery (ICA) |
| Optic chiasm (center) | Bitemporal hemianopia | AComm aneurysm, pituitary tumor |
| Optic tract | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia (incongruent) | Anterior choroidal, PCA |
| Temporal lobe (Meyer loop) | Contralateral superior quadrantanopia ("pie in the sky") | MCA inferior division |
| Parietal lobe (optic radiation) | Contralateral inferior quadrantanopia ("pie on the floor") | MCA superior division |
| Occipital cortex | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia WITH macular sparing | PCA |
| Bilateral occipital cortex | Cortical blindness (Anton syndrome if with denial) | Bilateral PCA |

**Source**: App content; Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, Figure 1-11.

## SECTION 6: WATERSHED (BORDER ZONE) INFARCTS

### Types and Mechanisms [BOARD-CORE]

Watershed infarcts occur at border zones between major arterial territories where perfusion pressure is lowest. They constitute approximately 10% of all brain infarcts.

**Two Types**:
1. **External (cortical) watershed**: Wedge-shaped infarcts at the cortical junction between territories. Primarily caused by embolism, sometimes with associated hypoperfusion. Generally benign clinical course.
2. **Internal (subcortical) watershed**: "String of pearls" or rosary-like appearance on MRI in the deep white matter (centrum semiovale). Primarily caused by hemodynamic compromise.

**Source**: PMC 2023 (Article 10478671); Radiographics 2011.

### Watershed Zones and Presentations [BOARD-CORE]

| Zone | Location | Presentation |
|------|----------|-------------|
| Anterior watershed | ACA-MCA border | Proximal arm weakness ("man in a barrel"), transcortical motor aphasia |
| Posterior watershed | MCA-PCA border | Visual processing deficits, transcortical sensory aphasia |
| Internal watershed | Deep white matter (perforator border zones) | Motor deficits, upper extremity predominant |

### Clinical Pearls [BOARD-CORE]

- **"Man in a barrel" syndrome**: Bilateral ACA-MCA watershed infarcts causing bilateral proximal arm weakness with preserved leg and facial function. Classic cause: systemic hypotension (cardiac arrest, surgical hypotension, massive hemorrhage).
- **Transcortical aphasias** (preserved repetition) are characteristic of watershed zone damage -- language cortex is spared but disconnected from surrounding areas.
- Severe bilateral carotid stenosis combined with hypotension is a common mechanism.

**Source**: App content; DeLisa, Chapter 18.

## SECTION 7: COLLATERAL CIRCULATION [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Leptomeningeal Collaterals

Leptomeningeal collaterals are pial anastomotic vessels that provide alternate pathways for cerebral blood flow when primary arterial supply is compromised. Recent research (2024) demonstrates that these collaterals actively regulate reperfusion in ischemic stroke and can rescue the brain from futile recanalization.

**Clinical Impact**:
- Good collateral status is independently associated with reduced hemorrhagic transformation risk and better 90-day functional outcomes after endovascular thrombectomy
- Patients with favorable collaterals exhibit smaller core infarcts, larger penumbra, and higher mismatch ratios
- Poor collaterals lead to distal arterial collapse, and rapid reperfusion can paradoxically cause hemorrhagic transformation and worse outcomes
- Collateral status is a key factor in thrombectomy candidate selection

**Source**: Neuron 2024 (PMID 38412858); PubMed 2022 (PMID 36195701).

### Multiphase CT Angiography for Collateral Assessment [BOARD-LIKELY]

Multiphase CTA (mCTA) acquires arterial and two venous phases, providing time-resolved assessment of collateral filling. Collateral score ranges from 0 (worst) to 5 (best). mCTA-based collateral assessment is superior to single-phase CTA for predicting clinical outcomes and guiding thrombectomy decisions. It is particularly valuable for late-window (6-24 hour) stroke patients.

**Source**: AJNR 2021; Nature Scientific Reports 2019.

## SECTION 8: CEREBRAL VENOUS SINUS THROMBOSIS (CVST) [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Diagnosis

- MRI/MRV or CT/CTV are the primary diagnostic modalities
- D-dimer testing alone cannot rule out CVT and is not recommended for routine screening
- Thrombophilia screening is not generally recommended in all CVT patients

### Treatment (AHA Scientific Statement 2024)

- Anticoagulation with body weight-adjusted subcutaneous LMWH or dose-adjusted IV heparin in the acute phase
- LMWH is preferred (easier application, no laboratory monitoring needed)
- Anticoagulation is indicated even in the presence of intracranial hemorrhage due to venous congestion
- Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) are emerging as alternatives for long-term management
- VITT (vaccine-induced immune thrombotic thrombocytopenia) was recognized as a new risk factor associated with adenoviral vector COVID-19 vaccines

**Source**: AHA Scientific Statement, Stroke 2024; German S2k Guideline 2024; PMC 2025 (Article 12664938).

## SECTION 9: CAROTID WEB AS STROKE ETIOLOGY [BOARD-EMERGING]

### Definition and Mechanism

Carotid web (CW) is a rare form of focal fibromuscular dysplasia -- an abnormal shelf-like projection of intimal fibrous tissue into the carotid bulb. It causes blood flow stasis and subsequent embolization, leading to ischemic stroke in younger patients without conventional risk factors.

### Epidemiology

- Prevalence of 8.9% in patients with ischemic stroke of undetermined etiology vs 1.9% in controls
- Predominant in young female patients
- 57% of symptomatic CW patients did not have traditional stroke risk factors

### Diagnosis

- CTA is the preferred noninvasive imaging method (correlation with DSA kappa = 0.92)
- Diagnostic delays remain common and are associated with increased stroke recurrence risk

### Management

- No evidence-based guidelines exist -- each case managed individually
- 56% of medically-managed symptomatic patients had recurrent stroke (median 12 months)
- Carotid revascularization (endarterectomy or stenting) effectively prevents recurrence
- 72% of symptomatic patients ultimately required revascularization

**Source**: Stroke (AHA) 2018; ESO 2020; JVSGBI 2023; Stroke 2024 (PMID 050238).

## SECTION 10: INTRACRANIAL ATHEROSCLEROSIS (ICAD) [BOARD-CORE]

### SAMMPRIS Trial and Current Management

The SAMMPRIS trial (2011) established that aggressive medical management (dual antiplatelet therapy + intensive risk factor management) is superior to intracranial stenting for symptomatic intracranial atherosclerotic disease with 70-99% stenosis.

**Current Standard of Care** [BOARD-CORE]:
- Dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel) for 90 days for patients with recent stroke/TIA attributed to severe intracranial stenosis (70-99%)
- Intensive risk factor management (SBP less than 140, LDL less than 70, statin therapy)

**Post-SAMMPRIS Data** [BOARD-LIKELY]:
- Analysis showed DAPT beyond 90 days had a numerically lower (44% reduction) risk of primary endpoint vs. those off clopidogrel, but this was not statistically significant (6.0% vs 10.8%)
- Intracranial stenting may still be considered for patients who fail maximal medical therapy

**Source**: NEJM 2011; Lancet 2014; PMC 2020 (PMID 32992190).

## SECTION 11: BASILAR ARTERY THROMBECTOMY -- LANDMARK TRIALS [BOARD-LIKELY]

### ATTENTION Trial (2022)

- Patients with moderate-severe stroke due to basilar artery occlusion within 12 hours
- Thrombectomy group: significantly higher rate of good functional outcome (mRS 0-3) at 90 days
- Mortality: 37% thrombectomy vs 55% medical care
- Symptomatic ICH: 5% thrombectomy vs 0% medical
- Benefits sustained at 1-year follow-up; enhanced for excellent outcomes (mRS 0-1)

### BAOCHE Trial (2022)

- Late-window (6-24 hours) basilar artery occlusion
- Thrombectomy led to higher percentage with good functional status at 90 days
- Associated with procedural complications and more cerebral hemorrhages

### Clinical Takeaway

These trials provided the first strong RCT evidence supporting thrombectomy for basilar artery occlusion, both in early and late time windows -- a major shift in practice that complements the established anterior circulation thrombectomy evidence.

**Source**: NEJM 2022 (PMID 36239644); NEJM 2022 (PMID 2207576).

## SECTION 12: VERTEBROBASILAR INSUFFICIENCY UPDATES [BOARD-CORE]

### VERiTAS Study Hemodynamic Findings [BOARD-LIKELY]

The VERiTAS study demonstrated that hemodynamic status in vertebrobasilar disease can both improve and worsen over time. Flow status at baseline AND changes over time are independent risk factors for subsequent stroke, serving as important prognostic markers. This supports individualized monitoring rather than empiric treatment.

### Key Clinical Points [BOARD-CORE]

- Posterior circulation strokes account for approximately 20% of all strokes
- Vertebrobasilar system symptoms include: vertigo, nystagmus, bilateral motor abnormalities, ipsilateral CN dysfunction, "crossed" signs (ipsilateral face + contralateral body)
- Absence of cortical signs (aphasia, cognitive deficits) is an important distinguishing feature from anterior circulation strokes
- Isolated vertigo can be the initial and only symptom of VBI -- episodes usually last less than 30 minutes without hearing loss or nystagmus; if no other symptoms, consider BPPV
- Endovascular thrombectomy is now supported by RCT evidence for acute basilar occlusion

**Source**: Stroke (AHA) 2020; StatPearls 2025; Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 12-13.

## SECTION 13: VASCULAR IMAGING ADVANCES [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Current Imaging Modalities

| Modality | Strengths | Limitations |
|----------|-----------|------------|
| Non-contrast CT (NCCT) | Fast, available, rules out hemorrhage | Poor sensitivity for early ischemia (20-75%), especially posterior fossa |
| CT Angiography (CTA) | Rapid vessel assessment, identifies LVO, collateral evaluation | Contrast needed, may overestimate stenosis |
| CT Perfusion (CTP) | CBV/CBF mapping, identifies penumbra | Radiation, contrast, processing time |
| Multiphase CTA (mCTA) | Time-resolved collateral assessment, no extra contrast | Requires specific protocol |
| DWI MRI | Most sensitive/specific for acute ischemia, detects within minutes | Time, availability, contraindications |
| MR Angiography (MRA) | No radiation, no contrast (TOF), detailed vessel anatomy | Slower, overestimates stenosis, flow artifacts |
| DSA (Conventional angiography) | Gold standard for vessel anatomy | Invasive, mainly reserved for intervention |

### Emerging Technologies [BOARD-EMERGING]

- Virtual non-contrast CT (VNCCT): Reconstructed from dual-energy CTA, combines vascular and parenchymal assessment in one acquisition
- AI/machine-learning automated LVO detection and collateral scoring
- Direct-to-angio suite (DTAS) approaches to reduce door-to-recanalization time
- Fractal-based quantitative collateral assessment tools for objective thrombectomy triage

**Source**: Neurological Sciences 2025; AJNR 2021; Diagnostics 2025.

## SECTION 14: CSF CIRCULATION AND HYDROCEPHALUS

### CSF Basics [BOARD-CORE]

- Produced primarily by choroid plexus (70%) + brain parenchyma/ependyma (30%)
- Total volume: 120-150 mL; Production rate: approximately 20 mL/hour (500 mL/day)
- Turned over 3-4 times per day
- Normal opening pressure: 70-180 mm H2O

### CSF Flow Pathway [BOARD-CORE]

Lateral ventricles --> Foramen of Monro --> Third ventricle --> Cerebral aqueduct (of Sylvius) --> Fourth ventricle --> Foramina of Luschka (lateral) and Magendie (median) --> Subarachnoid space --> Arachnoid granulations --> Superior sagittal sinus

The cerebral aqueduct is the narrowest point and most common site of obstructive hydrocephalus.

### Hydrocephalus After Stroke [BOARD-CORE]

| Type | Mechanism | Stroke-Related Causes |
|------|-----------|----------------------|
| Obstructive (non-communicating) | Blockage within ventricular system | Cerebellar hemorrhage compressing 4th ventricle, IVH |
| Communicating | Impaired absorption at arachnoid granulations | SAH -- blood products clog arachnoid granulations |
| NPH | Chronic communicating with normal ICP | Late complication of SAH |

**NPH Triad**: "Wet, Wobbly, Wacky"
- Wet = urinary incontinence
- Wobbly = gait disturbance (magnetic gait) -- presents first, improves most with treatment
- Wacky = dementia/cognitive changes -- presents last

**Source**: App content; Cuccurullo, Chapter 1, pp. 6.

## SECTION 15: HIGH-YIELD BOARD PEARLS SUMMARY

1. **MCA is the most commonly affected vessel in stroke** and carries the majority of anterior circulation flow [BOARD-CORE]
2. **Face/arm > leg = MCA; Leg > face/arm = ACA; Visual fields with macular sparing = PCA** [BOARD-CORE]
3. **Only 20-25% have a complete Circle of Willis** -- PComm hypoplasia is the most common variant [BOARD-CORE]
4. **Pupil-involving CN III palsy = aneurysm until proven otherwise** (PComm aneurysm compresses CN III) [BOARD-CORE]
5. **AComm is the most common site for saccular aneurysms** (~30-35%) [BOARD-CORE]
6. **Wallenberg syndrome is most commonly from vertebral artery occlusion**, not PICA directly [BOARD-CORE]
7. **No facial/extremity weakness in Wallenberg** -- distinguishes from other brainstem syndromes [BOARD-CORE]
8. **Locked-in syndrome = bilateral ventral pons** (basilar artery), patient is conscious, can only blink/look up [BOARD-CORE]
9. **"Man in a barrel" = bilateral watershed infarcts** after hypotensive episode [BOARD-CORE]
10. **Transcortical aphasias (preserved repetition) = watershed infarcts** [BOARD-CORE]
11. **SAMMPRIS: Medical management superior to stenting** for symptomatic intracranial atherosclerosis [BOARD-CORE]
12. **ATTENTION/BAOCHE trials: Thrombectomy for basilar occlusion** now supported by RCT evidence [BOARD-LIKELY]
13. **Leptomeningeal collateral status predicts thrombectomy outcomes** -- good collaterals = better outcomes [BOARD-LIKELY]
14. **Carotid web: underrecognized cause of cryptogenic stroke in young patients** -- high recurrence on medical therapy alone [BOARD-EMERGING]
15. **Fetal PCA (15-30%)**: PCA territory depends on carotid system; carotid occlusion can cause visual deficits [BOARD-CORE]
