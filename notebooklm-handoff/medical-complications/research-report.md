# Medical Complications After Stroke: Comprehensive Review for PM&R Board Preparation

## Purpose
This document integrates content from the CVA Training app's Medical Complications module with relevant excerpts from Cuccurullo's Physical Medicine Board Review (Stroke Chapter) and DeLisa's Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation (Chapter 18: Stroke Rehabilitation), enriched with current evidence (2020-2026) from peer-reviewed literature and clinical guidelines. Every claim is source-tagged and rated for board readiness.

## 1. VENOUS THROMBOEMBOLISM (DVT/PE) AFTER STROKE

### Epidemiology
- DVT occurs in 20-75% of untreated hemiplegic stroke patients [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1; DeLisa Ch.18]
- 60-75% occur in affected extremity; 25% are proximal DVT; 1-2% result in PE [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1]
- PE is the most common cause of death between 2-4 weeks poststroke [BOARD-CORE] [Source: App content; Cuccurullo Ch.1]

### Risk Factors (Virchow Triad in Stroke)
- Hemiplegia/immobility (stasis) [BOARD-CORE]
- DVT most common in paretic leg [BOARD-CORE]
- Older age, obesity, prior DVT/PE, AF, cardiac disease, dehydration [BOARD-CORE]
- Hemorrhagic stroke patients cannot receive early anticoagulant prophylaxis [BOARD-CORE]

### DVT Prophylaxis -- Ischemic Stroke
- LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg SQ daily) -- preferred pharmacologic agent [BOARD-CORE] [Source: AHA/ASA Guidelines; Cuccurullo Ch.1]
- UFH 5000 units SQ BID-TID -- alternative [BOARD-CORE]
- IPC devices -- recommended for all immobile stroke patients [BOARD-CORE] [Source: CLOTS 3 trial]
- Early mobilization -- most important nonpharmacologic intervention [BOARD-CORE]
- Graduated compression stockings (TED hose) -- NOT recommended alone [BOARD-CORE] [Source: CLOTS 1 trial]
- Combine pharmacologic + IPC for highest-risk patients [BOARD-CORE]

### DVT Prophylaxis -- Hemorrhagic Stroke
- IPC devices -- mainstay, start on admission [BOARD-CORE]
- Pharmacologic prophylaxis delayed until hematoma stable (typically 48-72 hours) [BOARD-CORE]
- Some centers wait 7-14 days [BOARD-LIKELY]
- IVC filter -- rarely indicated, only for proven DVT when anticoagulation contraindicated [BOARD-CORE]

### CLOTS Trials -- Landmark Board Content
| Trial | Intervention | Result | Implication |
|-------|-------------|--------|-------------|
| CLOTS 1 (2009) | Thigh-length GCS vs none | GCS did NOT reduce DVT; increased skin complications | Do NOT use compression stockings |
| CLOTS 2 (2010) | Thigh-length vs below-knee GCS | Neither effective | Neither length works |
| CLOTS 3 (2013) | IPC vs no IPC | IPC significantly reduced DVT (ARR ~3.6%, NNT=28) | IPC IS effective and recommended |
[BOARD-CORE] [Source: CLOTS trials; Dennis et al., Lancet 2009, 2010, 2013]

### DVT/PE Diagnosis
| Test | Use | Key Points |
|------|-----|-----------|
| Duplex ultrasound | First-line for DVT | High sensitivity for proximal DVT, lower for calf DVT |
| D-dimer | Screening | High NPV; often elevated poststroke (less useful) |
| CT pulmonary angiography | Gold standard for PE | Rapid, widely available |
| V/Q scan | Alternative for PE | When CTA contraindicated |
[BOARD-CORE]

### Wells Score for DVT
- Stroke patients inherently score high (paralysis +1, bedridden +1) making Wells score less useful [BOARD-CORE]
- Maintain high clinical suspicion in all immobile stroke patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Score >=3: HIGH probability (~75%) -- proceed to ultrasound [BOARD-CORE]
- Score 1-2: MODERATE (~17%) -- D-dimer, then ultrasound if positive [BOARD-CORE]
- Score <=0: LOW (~5%) -- D-dimer; if negative, DVT effectively ruled out [BOARD-CORE]

### DVT Treatment
- Full-dose anticoagulation with LMWH bridging to warfarin (INR 2-3) or DOAC (dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban) [BOARD-CORE] [Source: DeLisa Ch.18]
- DOACs increasingly preferred over warfarin for convenience [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Patients can continue activity as tolerated (bed rest no longer required) [BOARD-CORE]
- IVC filter for patients who cannot safely receive anticoagulation [BOARD-CORE]

### Recent Developments (2020-2026)
- ESO 2019 guidelines recommend IPC in immobile patients with acute ischemic stroke [BOARD-CORE] [Source: ESO Guidelines, Eur Stroke J 2019]
- AHA/ASA 2024 guidelines: benefit of prophylactic heparin (UFH or LMWH) to increase overall survival is not well established (Class IIb) [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: AHA/ASA 2024]
- LMWH reduces DVT but increases bleeding risk with no net survival benefit [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2024 VA/DoD CPG: IPC remains strongly recommended; pharmacologic prophylaxis weighed against bleeding risk [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: VA/DoD 2024 Stroke Rehab CPG]

## 2. BLADDER AND BOWEL DYSFUNCTION

### Urinary Incontinence
- Affects 50-70% in first month; 15% at 6 months [BOARD-CORE] [Source: App content; Cuccurullo Ch.1]
- Strong negative predictor of functional outcome and discharge disposition [BOARD-CORE]
- Persistent incontinence at 2 weeks -- one of the strongest negative prognostic indicators [BOARD-CORE]

### Bladder Types After Stroke
| Type | Mechanism | Urodynamics | Treatment |
|------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Uninhibited neurogenic (spastic) | Loss of cortical inhibition of pontine micturition center (UMN) | Detrusor hyperreflexia -- involuntary contractions at low volumes | Timed voiding, anticholinergics (oxybutynin), behavioral training |
| Areflexic (flaccid) | Acute stage -- spinal shock equivalent | Detrusor areflexia -- overflow incontinence | Intermittent catheterization |
| Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia | Incoordination of detrusor and sphincter | High pressures, incomplete emptying | Alpha-blocker (tamsulosin), intermittent cath |
[BOARD-CORE]

**Most common type: Uninhibited neurogenic bladder (detrusor hyperreflexia) because stroke is an UMN lesion** [BOARD-CORE]

### Bladder Management
- Remove indwelling catheter within 24-48 hours [BOARD-CORE]
- Intermittent catheterization preferred over indwelling [BOARD-CORE]
- PVR monitoring -- if >150 mL, consider intermittent cath [BOARD-CORE]
- Anticholinergics: oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin, darifenacin, fesoterodine [BOARD-CORE]
- Side effects: dry mouth, constipation, confusion (especially elderly) [BOARD-CORE]
- Botulinum toxin type A and bladder stimulator for refractory detrusor overactivity [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1]

### UTI Prevention -- Recent Evidence (2024-2025)
- CAUTI is the most common hospital-acquired infection in stroke patients [BOARD-CORE]
- 2025 APIC guide and 2024 ISID position paper emphasize: earliest possible catheter removal, proper aseptic insertion technique, closed sterile drainage, and daily reassessment of catheter necessity [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: APIC 2025; ISID 2024]
- Nurse-driven catheter removal protocols reduce CAUTI rates [BOARD-LIKELY]
- In 2023, acute care hospitals reported >17,000 CAUTI events over 23 million catheter-days [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: CDC/NHSN 2023]

### Bowel Dysfunction
- Incontinence: ~31% in acute stroke, improves to ~10% by discharge [BOARD-CORE]
- Constipation: ~60% -- more common than incontinence [BOARD-CORE]
- Causes: immobility, medications (opioids, anticholinergics), dehydration [BOARD-CORE]
- Bowel program: timed toileting, fiber (25-30g/day), fluids, stool softeners [BOARD-CORE]
- Digital stimulation and suppositories for neurogenic bowel [BOARD-CORE]

## 3. CARDIAC COMPLICATIONS

### Cardiac Disease and Stroke
- 75% of stroke patients have coexisting cardiac disease [BOARD-CORE] [Source: App content; DeLisa Ch.18]
- Leading cause of long-term mortality in stroke survivors [BOARD-CORE]
- AF in 15-25%, CAD in 20-40%, HF in 10-20% [BOARD-CORE]

### Stroke-Induced Cardiac Changes (Stroke-Heart Syndrome)
- ECG changes in up to 70% of acute stroke patients [BOARD-CORE]
- QT prolongation -- most common ECG abnormality [BOARD-CORE]
- T-wave inversions and ST changes (can mimic MI) [BOARD-CORE]
- Troponin elevation without primary coronary disease [BOARD-CORE]
- Takotsubo cardiomyopathy (stress cardiomyopathy) [BOARD-CORE]
- Autonomic storm -- catecholamine surge damages myocardium [BOARD-CORE]

### Insular Cortex and Cardiac Autonomic Control
- Insular cortex is the primary cortical center for autonomic cardiovascular regulation [BOARD-CORE]
- RIGHT insular lesions: sympathetic overactivity (tachycardia, HTN, arrhythmias, myocardial injury, higher risk sudden cardiac death) [BOARD-CORE]
- LEFT insular lesions: parasympathetic overactivity (bradycardia, hypotension) [BOARD-CORE]
- Right insular strokes carry worse cardiac outcomes [BOARD-CORE]

### Recent Developments -- Stroke-Heart Syndrome (2023-2026)
- The concept of "Stroke-Heart Syndrome" is now well-established in literature, describing how stroke leads to central autonomic network dysregulation, HPA axis activation, and inflammatory cascade causing cardiac damage [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Fan et al., Adv Sci 2024; J Neurol 2024]
- Dorsal anterior insular cortex has prominent role in parasympathetic cardiac control; damage leads to sympathetic upregulation and myocardial injury [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Clin Auton Res 2024]
- Larger insular infarct volumes correlate with more severe myocardial injury [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Front Neurosci 2026]

### Cardiac Monitoring
- Continuous telemetry for at least 24-72 hours minimum [BOARD-CORE]
- Extended cardiac monitoring (14-30 day Holter) for cryptogenic stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- CRYSTAL-AF trial: ICM detected AF in 30% of cryptogenic stroke patients at 3 years [BOARD-CORE]
- Implantable cardiac monitor (ICM/LINQ) -- up to 3 years monitoring [BOARD-CORE]

### Autonomic Dysfunction
- Cardiac arrhythmias most common in first 72 hours [BOARD-CORE]
- Blood pressure variability, orthostatic hypotension, loss of circadian BP dipping [BOARD-CORE]
- Sweating abnormalities (hyperhidrosis or anhidrosis on affected side) [BOARD-CORE]
- Thermoregulatory dysfunction [BOARD-CORE]
- GI dysmotility from vagal and sympathetic disruption [BOARD-CORE]

### Exercise After Stroke
- Peak VO2 typically 50-70% of age-matched controls [BOARD-CORE]
- Aerobic exercise: 20-60 min, 3-5 days/week at 40-70% HRR [BOARD-CORE]
- Beta-blockers blunt HR response -- use RPE for intensity monitoring [BOARD-CORE]

## 4. FALLS AND FRACTURE PREVENTION

### Falls Epidemiology
- 25% of stroke patients fall during inpatient rehabilitation [BOARD-CORE] [Source: App content; DeLisa Ch.18]
- Even more common after discharge [BOARD-CORE]
- Right hemisphere stroke patients -- higher fall risk (neglect, impulsivity) [BOARD-CORE]

### Falls Risk Factors
- Hemiplegia, neglect, cognitive impairment, impulsivity, medications [BOARD-CORE]
- Polypharmacy (sedatives, antihypertensives) [BOARD-CORE]
- Fear of falling limits activity [BOARD-CORE]

### Falls Prevention
- Multifactorial fall prevention program recommended [BOARD-CORE]
- Balance training, strength training, gait training [BOARD-CORE]
- Environmental modifications (grab bars, raised toilet, adequate lighting) [BOARD-CORE]
- Medication review [BOARD-CORE]
- Hip protectors for high-risk patients [BOARD-CORE]

### Recent Advances in Balance Training (2024)
- Balance training methods now include perturbation-based training, aquatic training, cognitive-motor dual-task training, and virtual reality-based balance training [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Front Neurol 2024]
- Perturbation-based training enhanced balance and reduced fall incidence in stroke patients [BOARD-EMERGING] [Source: Front Neurol 2024]
- 2024 VA/DoD CPG recommends formal fall prevention programs during hospitalization [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Osteoporosis on the Hemiplegic Side
- BMD decreases significantly on paretic side within weeks to months [BOARD-CORE]
- Up to 4x increased fracture risk on hemiplegic side [BOARD-CORE]
- Hemiplegic hip and proximal humerus most vulnerable [BOARD-CORE]
- Falls + osteoporosis = high fracture risk [BOARD-CORE]
- Hip fractures on hemiplegic side have worse outcomes and higher mortality [BOARD-CORE]

### Osteoporosis Management
- DEXA scan recommended for stroke patients with prolonged immobility [BOARD-CORE]
- Weight-bearing exercise and standing programs may slow bone loss [BOARD-CORE]
- Vitamin D and calcium supplementation recommended [BOARD-CORE]
- Bisphosphonates for documented osteoporosis [BOARD-CORE]
- Single dose IV zoledronate effectively prevented bone loss at the hip after stroke [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Poole et al., Stroke 2007; confirmed in subsequent studies]
- IV bisphosphonates may be preferable in acute stroke due to swallowing difficulties [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Oxidative stress now recognized as contributor to post-stroke osteoporosis pathogenesis [BOARD-EMERGING] [Source: Front Med 2023]

## 5. POST-STROKE SEIZURES AND EPILEPSY

### Classification
| Feature | Early Seizures (<7 days) | Late Seizures (>7 days) |
|---------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Mechanism | Acute cellular dysfunction: glutamate excitotoxicity, ionic shifts | Epileptogenesis: gliosis, neuronal reorganization, scar formation |
| Prevalence | 2-5% ischemic; higher in hemorrhagic | 3-5%; cumulative risk increases over years |
| Type | Often focal with secondary generalization | Focal onset, may generalize; more likely recurrent |
| Recurrence | Low (~30%) -- often isolated provoked seizures | High (~50-70%) -- represents true epilepsy |
| Treatment | Treat acutely if prolonged; short-term AED may suffice | Long-term AED typically indicated |
| Risk factors | Hemorrhagic, cortical involvement, large infarct, severe NIHSS | Hemorrhagic, cortical, early seizures |
[BOARD-CORE] [Source: App content; Cuccurullo Ch.1]

### Key Management Principles
- Prophylactic anticonvulsants NOT recommended [BOARD-CORE]
- Treat if recurrent seizures or status epilepticus [BOARD-CORE]
- Levetiracetam preferred -- fewer drug interactions than phenytoin [BOARD-CORE]
- Avoid phenytoin if possible (cognitive effects, drug interactions, non-linear pharmacokinetics) [BOARD-CORE]

### Recent Evidence (2023-2025)
- Network meta-analyses confirm levetiracetam and lamotrigine as preferred first-line ASMs for post-stroke seizures with better tolerability than carbamazepine [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Neurology 2024; Front Neurol 2024]
- Levetiracetam associated with lower seizure rehospitalization risk vs valproic acid [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Neurology 2024]
- Long-term ASM not recommended for single early provoked seizure [BOARD-CORE]
- European guidelines have not found any ASM with clear superiority for post-stroke epilepsy [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Lacosamide and brivaracetam emerging as alternatives with favorable cognitive profiles [BOARD-EMERGING]

## 6. POST-STROKE DEPRESSION

### Epidemiology
- Affects approximately one-third of stroke survivors at any point after stroke [BOARD-CORE] [Source: AHA/ASA Scientific Statement; DeLisa Ch.18]
- Some studies report up to 50% prevalence [BOARD-CORE]
- More common than expected from psychosocial stress alone -- neurobiological component [BOARD-CORE]
- Left frontal and bifrontal lesions associated with depression (though relationship debated) [BOARD-CORE] [Source: DeLisa Ch.18]

### Mechanisms
- Stroke-induced catecholamine depletion through damage to frontal noradrenergic, dopaminergic, and serotonergic projections [BOARD-CORE] [Source: DeLisa Ch.18]
- Stroke in prefrontal cortex, limbic area, and basal ganglia disrupts mood-related neurotransmitter pathways [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: 2023 literature review]
- Diagnosis complicated by presence of emotional lability, fatigue, sleep disturbance, altered appetite [BOARD-CORE]

### Screening
- PHQ-9 and PHQ-2 screening instruments recommended due to brevity [BOARD-CORE]
- Should be performed routinely in all stroke patients [BOARD-CORE]

### Treatment
- SSRIs are standard treatment -- sertraline, escitalopram, fluoxetine, citalopram [BOARD-CORE]
- SSRIs may also enhance motor recovery (FLAME trial: fluoxetine enhanced motor recovery at 3 months) [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Chollet et al., Lancet Neurol 2011]
- Preventive (prophylactic) use of antidepressants NOT supported [BOARD-CORE]
- Persisting depression correlates with delayed recovery and poorer outcome [BOARD-CORE]
- Some practitioners add stimulant (methylphenidate) to boost response [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Recent Evidence (2023-2025)
- 2024 RCT: sertraline (100 mg) and escitalopram (20 mg) both showed significant HAM-D score reduction after 8 weeks [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Early SSRI/SNRI use during subacute phase NOT associated with increased bleeding risk in most patients [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Caution: 29% higher hemorrhagic stroke risk with antidepressants PLUS dual antiplatelet therapy [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Emotional lability (pseudobulbar affect) -- distinct from depression; responds to dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta) or SSRIs [BOARD-CORE]

## 7. POST-STROKE PAIN SYNDROMES

### Comprehensive Pain Classification
| Pain Type | Mechanism | Characteristics | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| Central Post-Stroke Pain (CPSP) | Thalamic or spinothalamocortical pathway lesion | Burning, aching, lancinating contralateral pain; allodynia, hyperalgesia; delayed onset | Amitriptyline, gabapentin/pregabalin, lamotrigine; opioids generally ineffective |
| Hemiplegic Shoulder Pain | Subluxation, rotator cuff injury, adhesive capsulitis, spasticity | Most common pain complaint (up to 72%); limits rehab | Positioning, slings, ROM, suprascapular nerve block, botulinum toxin |
| Spasticity-Related Pain | Velocity-dependent hypertonicity from UMN lesion | Cramping, painful spasms | Stretching, baclofen, tizanidine, botulinum toxin, ITB |
| CRPS (Shoulder-Hand Syndrome) | Sympathetically mediated with autonomic changes | Burning pain, edema, skin color/temperature changes | Aggressive ROM, corticosteroids (early), stellate ganglion block, mirror therapy |
| Musculoskeletal Pain | Abnormal biomechanics, compensatory overuse | Non-paretic limb overuse; back pain from asymmetric gait | Biomechanical correction, bracing, NSAIDs, PT |
[BOARD-CORE] [Source: App content; Cuccurullo Ch.1; DeLisa Ch.18]

### Central Post-Stroke Pain (CPSP) -- Details
- Prevalence: 8-35% of stroke patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Only ~25% due to thalamic lesions; also brainstem and parietal lobe projections [BOARD-CORE] [Source: DeLisa Ch.18]
- Often delayed onset weeks to months after stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- First-line: tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline) -- most robust evidence [BOARD-CORE]
- Anticonvulsants: gabapentin, pregabalin, lamotrigine [BOARD-CORE]
- Opioids generally NOT effective [BOARD-CORE]

### CPSP Recent Advances (2024-2025)
- 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis: TCAs have more robust efficacy evidence than anticonvulsants for CPSP [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: J Pain 2024]
- M1-targeting rTMS has the most robust evidence among neuromodulation treatments [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: J Clin Med 2024]
- Deep brain stimulation (DBS) targeting posterior limb of internal capsule and sensorimotor thalamus showing efficacy in refractory cases [BOARD-EMERGING] [Source: PubMed 2025]
- Ketamine (IV or intranasal) and dextromethorphan show promise by reducing central sensitization [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Virtual reality and acupuncture have some evidence for CPSP pain alleviation [BOARD-EMERGING]

### CRPS/Shoulder-Hand Syndrome -- Details
- Prevalence: 12-25% of hemiplegic stroke patients; most common subtype is shoulder-hand syndrome [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1]
- Usually appears 2-36 weeks after stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- **Three stages**: Stage 1 (acute) -- burning pain, diffuse swelling, vasomotor changes; Stage 2 (dystrophic) -- skin/muscle atrophy, brawny edema; Stage 3 (atrophic) -- trophic changes, contractures [BOARD-CORE]
- Diagnosis: Budapest criteria (revised IASP criteria) [BOARD-CORE]
- Triple-phase bone scan most sensitive early diagnostic test (sensitivity 92%, specificity 56%) [BOARD-CORE]
- Corticosteroids effective early (prednisone 30-50 mg daily, taper over 2-3 weeks) [BOARD-CORE]
- Stellate ganglion block -- diagnostic for sympathetically mediated CRPS; successful when patient develops ipsilateral Horner syndrome [BOARD-CORE]

### CRPS Recent Evidence (2024)
- ESWT (extracorporeal shock wave therapy) showed significant pain reduction and motor improvement in systematic review of 18 RCTs [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Canadian Stroke Best Practices 2024]
- EMG biofeedback integrated with rehabilitation training identified as most effective physical therapy for post-stroke shoulder-hand syndrome [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2024 VA/DoD CPG recommends functional shoulder orthoses to prevent subluxation and reduce CRPS risk [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 8. PNEUMONIA AND ASPIRATION

### Epidemiology
- Aspiration pneumonia: 40% frequency during postacute rehabilitation [BOARD-CORE] [Source: DeLisa Ch.18, Table 18-5]
- Dysphagia prevalence: 25-65% (67% brainstem, 28% left hemisphere, 21% right hemisphere) [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1]
- Silent aspiration in 40-60% on bedside evaluation [BOARD-CORE]
- Aspiration found in 40-70% on VFSS [BOARD-CORE]

### Dysphagia Screening and Management
- Bedside swallowing evaluation before any oral intake [BOARD-CORE]
- VFSS (modified barium swallow) -- gold standard [BOARD-CORE]
- FEES -- alternative, especially when VFSS impractical [BOARD-CORE]
- Compensatory strategies: chin tuck, modified food consistencies, bolus size control [BOARD-CORE]

### Pneumonia Prevention -- Current Evidence (2024-2025)
- Dysphagia screening combined with intensified oral hygiene reduces pneumonia after stroke [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Stroke 2013; confirmed in 2024 reviews]
- Sites with formal dysphagia screening protocols had 78% adherence vs 57% without [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Mechanical toothbrushing reduced pneumonia mortality risk by 10% in dependent older persons [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Cochrane/systematic reviews]
- Oral care protocols reduce aspiration pneumonia in acute stroke [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Risk factors for pneumonia: decreased LOC, tracheostomy, emesis, reflux, NGT feeding, dysphagia, prolonged pharyngeal transit time [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Cuccurullo Ch.1]

## 9. POST-STROKE FATIGUE

### Epidemiology
- Prevalence: 25-85% of stroke survivors (meta-analyses report ~43-53%) [BOARD-CORE] [Source: Front Neurol 2024]
- Independent symptom not fully explained by depression or disability [BOARD-CORE]
- Defined as exhaustion not relieved by rest, affecting daily life [BOARD-CORE]

### Risk Factors
- Female sex, higher age, being unmarried [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Front Neurol 2024]
- Low pre-stroke physical activity, poor sleep quality, insomnia [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Depression (common comorbidity but distinct entity) [BOARD-CORE]

### Treatment -- Current Evidence (2024-2025)
- Very limited evidence for any specific interventions [BOARD-CORE]
- Physiotherapy/exercise and aerobic training recommended but evidence is modest [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: Stroke 2025 systematic review]
- Fatigue management education programs (e.g., Fatigue After Stroke Educational Recovery Program) show promise [BOARD-EMERGING] [Source: JAHA 2024]
- No pharmacologic treatment with strong evidence [BOARD-CORE]
- Stimulants (modafinil, methylphenidate) used clinically despite limited evidence [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 10. SLEEP DISORDERS AFTER STROKE

### Prevalence and Types
- OSA impacts approximately 70% of stroke patients [BOARD-CORE] [Source: J Stroke Cerebrovasc Dis 2025]
- Insomnia: ~24% [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Restless legs syndrome: ~35% [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Daytime sleepiness: ~17% [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Sleep disorders are both risk factors for AND consequences of stroke [BOARD-CORE]

### Screening
- STOP-BANG and Berlin Questionnaire assess OSA risk (limited specificity) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- PSG (polysomnography) -- diagnostic gold standard [BOARD-CORE]
- OSA classified by AHI: 5-14 mild, 15-29 moderate, >=30 severe [BOARD-CORE]
- Systematic OSA screening NOT yet standard in post-stroke care (clinical gap) [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Treatment
- CPAP for OSA -- reduces sleepiness, potential to improve stroke outcome (data still emerging) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2025 AASM guidelines: clonazepam and melatonin first-line for REM sleep behavior disorder; melatonin preferred in older adults [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: AASM 2025]
- Poor sleep quality independently associated with worse functional recovery after ischemic stroke [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: J Sleep Res 2024]

## 11. PRESSURE INJURY PREVENTION

### Epidemiology
- Prevalence in stroke rehabilitation: 3-14% [BOARD-CORE] [Source: App content; DeLisa Ch.18]
- Common sites: sacrum, ischial tuberosities, heels, greater trochanter [BOARD-CORE]

### Braden Scale
- Most widely used pressure injury risk assessment tool [BOARD-CORE]
- Six subscales: sensory perception, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, friction/shear [BOARD-CORE]
- Score range 6-23; LOWER score = HIGHER risk [BOARD-CORE]
- <=18: at risk; <=15: moderate; <=12: high; <=9: very high risk [BOARD-CORE]
- Stroke patients inherently high risk (low sensory perception, activity, mobility scores) [BOARD-CORE]

### Prevention Strategies
- Reposition every 2 hours in bed [BOARD-CORE]
- Pressure-relieving mattress for high-risk patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Adequate nutrition: albumin >3.5, vitamin C, zinc, protein 1.0-1.5 g/kg/day [BOARD-CORE]
- Skin clean and dry; manage incontinence [BOARD-CORE]
- Daily skin inspections, especially insensate areas [BOARD-CORE]
- Weight shifts every 15-30 minutes in wheelchair [BOARD-CORE]

### Current Guidelines (2023-2025)
- EPUAP/NPIAP/PPPIA 2019 guidelines remain current standard [BOARD-CORE]
- Structured risk assessment within 8 hours of admission recommended [BOARD-CORE]
- 2024 systematic review confirms continued clinical importance of Braden Scale in acute care [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: PMC 2025]
- Prevention bundles (combining repositioning, nutrition, skin care, surface optimization) show benefit [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 12. HETEROTOPIC OSSIFICATION AND OTHER COMPLICATIONS

### Heterotopic Ossification (HO)
- Prevalence in stroke: ~1-5% (much lower than TBI 10-20% or SCI 15-30%) [BOARD-CORE]
- Most common location: shoulder and elbow on hemiplegic side [BOARD-CORE]
- Presents as painful, warm, swollen joint with progressive ROM loss [BOARD-CORE]
- Diagnosis: elevated alkaline phosphatase; triple-phase bone scan (most sensitive early); plain X-ray (delayed 4-6 weeks) [BOARD-CORE]
- Treatment: NSAIDs (indomethacin), gentle ROM, etidronate [BOARD-CORE]
- Surgical excision only after maturation (12-18 months) [BOARD-CORE]

### Malnutrition
- 16% at admission, rising to 26-35% during rehabilitation [BOARD-CORE]
- MUST tool (Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool) for screening [BOARD-CORE]
- Dysphagia, depression, cognitive impairment, UE weakness all contribute to poor intake [BOARD-CORE]
- Adequate protein: 1.0-1.5 g/kg/day for tissue healing [BOARD-CORE]

### Sexuality After Stroke
- Decreased sexual activity: 50-75% [BOARD-CORE]
- Physical barriers: hemiparesis, spasticity, fatigue, incontinence [BOARD-CORE]
- Safe to resume when medically stable (cardiac demand similar to climbing 2 flights of stairs) [BOARD-CORE]
- PDE5 inhibitors generally safe but avoid with nitrates [BOARD-CORE]
- Counseling should be offered proactively [BOARD-CORE]

The July 2024 VA/DoD CPG update incorporated 24 new recommendations and revised 19 from the 2019 guideline. Key updates relevant to medical complications: [BOARD-LIKELY] [Source: VA/DoD 2024 CPG; Annals of Internal Medicine 2025;178(2):249-268]

- Formal dysphagia screening before oral intake with standardized protocols
- ESWT for hemiplegic shoulder pain (new recommendation)

|-------------|-----------|
| Pulmonary aspiration/pneumonia | 40% |
| UTI | 40% |
| Depression | 30% |
| Musculoskeletal pain/RSD | 30% |
| Falls | 25% |
| Malnutrition | 16% |
| Venous thromboembolism | 6% |
| Pressure ulcer | 3% |
| Seizure | 4% |
