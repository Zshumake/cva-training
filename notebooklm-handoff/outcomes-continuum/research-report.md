# SECTION 1: STROKE PROGNOSTICATION

## Overview
Accurate prediction of functional outcomes after stroke is essential for treatment planning, goal setting, family counseling, and discharge planning. Multiple clinical factors predict outcomes, though no single factor is sufficient. The most important predictors are stroke severity, age, and early motor recovery.

**[BOARD-CORE]** The challenge in developing accurate prognostic models is not surprising, given the myriad of complex functions performed by the brain, the variability of stroke lesion location and size, the limitations of our assessment techniques, and the impact of many baseline factors on stroke recovery, such as age, comorbid conditions, personality and coping abilities, and social factors. (Source: DeLisa Ch. 18)

## Positive Prognostic Factors
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Younger age
- Mild stroke severity (low NIHSS score)
- Lacunar stroke subtype — BEST prognosis among all stroke subtypes
- Early motor recovery (hand movement by 4 weeks)
- Preserved sitting balance by 2 weeks
- Preserved cognition and continence
- Strong social support system
- Prior independence in ADLs
- Motivation and participation in rehabilitation

## Negative Prognostic Factors
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Older age (>75 years)
- Severe stroke (high NIHSS)
- Large vessel territory infarct (large MCA)
- Persistent incontinence at 2 weeks
- Poor sitting balance
- Hemispatial neglect and anosognosia
- Global aphasia
- Prior stroke or significant comorbidities
- Complete absence of hand movement by 4 weeks
- Severe cognitive impairment

**[BOARD-CORE]** Factors predicting poor ADL outcome (DeLisa Box 18-6): Advanced age, comorbidities (MI, diabetes), severity of stroke, severe weakness, poor sitting balance, visuospatial deficits, mental changes, incontinence, low initial ADL scores, and time interval from onset to rehabilitation.

## Lacunar Strokes — Best Prognosis
**[BOARD-CORE]** Lacunar infarcts (small vessel disease, <1.5 cm) have the best functional prognosis of all stroke subtypes. Up to 80% of lacunar stroke patients achieve functional independence. This is because the infarct size is small, cortical function is preserved, and motor recovery is often complete. However, lacunar strokes have a high recurrence rate (up to 25% in 5 years) and are associated with vascular cognitive impairment over time.

Per Cuccurullo: Lacunar lesions constitute approximately 20% of all strokes, resulting from occlusions in the deep penetrating branches of the large vessels that perfuse the subcortical structures, including internal capsule, basal ganglia, thalamus, and brainstem.

## Key Predictive Milestones
**[BOARD-CORE]**

| Milestone | Timing | Prognostic Implication |
|-----------|--------|----------------------|
| Voluntary finger extension | 72 hours | 98% probability of some UE function (SAFE score) |
| Hand movement present | 4 weeks | ~70% chance of useful hand recovery |
| No hand movement | 4 weeks | Poor prognosis for UE functional use |
| Sitting balance | 2 weeks | Strong predictor of ambulation and ADL independence |
| Continence restored | 2 weeks | Favorable discharge disposition |
| Best neurological recovery | 11 weeks | 95% reach best neurologic level by this time |

**[BOARD-CORE]** From DeLisa: The severity of arm weakness at onset and the timing of the return of movement in the hand are both important predictors of eventual motor recovery in the arm. The prognosis for return of useful hand function is poor when there is complete arm paralysis at onset or no measurable grasp strength by 4 weeks. However, even among those patients with severe arm weakness at onset, as many as 11% may gain good recovery of hand function. For patients showing some motor recovery in the hand by 4 weeks, as many as 70% will make a full or good recovery.

## Copenhagen Stroke Study — 95% Best Neurological Level by 11 Weeks
**[BOARD-CORE]** The Copenhagen Stroke Study demonstrated that 95% of stroke patients reach their best neurological level by 11 weeks poststroke. This does NOT mean functional recovery stops — patients continue to make functional gains through compensation, skill acquisition, and adaptation well beyond 11 weeks. Neurological recovery is distinct from functional recovery.

From DeLisa: Ninety-five percent of stroke survivors reached their best neurological level within 11 weeks of onset. Individuals with milder stroke recovered more quickly, and those with severe strokes reached their best neurological level in 15 weeks on average. The course of motor recovery reaches a plateau after an early phase of progressive improvement, and only minor additional measurable improvement occurs after 6 months post onset.

## NIHSS Prognostic Value
**[BOARD-CORE]** The NIHSS is a strong predictor of both short and long-term outcomes:
- Scores <5: 80% of stroke survivors will be discharged to home
- Scores 6-13: typically require acute inpatient rehabilitation
- Scores >14: frequently require long-term skilled care
- Each 1-point increase in NIHSS reduces the odds of an excellent outcome by 24% at 7 days and 17% at 3 months
(Source: Physiopedia NIHSS; AHA Stroke Journal 2019)

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2025 study using machine learning methods validated NIHSS as one of the most significant predictors in comprehensive rehabilitation outcome models, confirming its role alongside age and admission FIM score as top predictors of functional status at discharge. (Source: Scientific Reports 2025)

# SECTION 2: FUNCTIONAL OUTCOME MEASURES

## Functional Independence Measure (FIM)
**[BOARD-CORE]** An 18-item ordinal scale measuring functional independence in self-care, sphincter control, transfers, locomotion, communication, and social cognition. Each item scored 1-7.

| Score | Level | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 7 | Complete Independence | Performs task safely without assistance, aids, or devices |
| 6 | Modified Independence | Requires assistive device or extra time, but no helper |
| 5 | Supervision/Setup | Requires standby assist, cueing, or setup only |
| 4 | Minimal Assistance | Patient performs >=75% of task effort |
| 3 | Moderate Assistance | Patient performs 50-74% of task effort |
| 2 | Maximal Assistance | Patient performs 25-49% of task effort |
| 1 | Total Assistance | Patient performs <25% of task effort |

### FIM Domain Structure
**[BOARD-CORE]**

| Domain | Items | Max Score |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| Self-Care | Eating, Grooming, Bathing, Dressing Upper, Dressing Lower, Toileting | 42 |
| Sphincter Control | Bladder, Bowel | 14 |
| Transfers | Bed/Chair, Toilet, Tub/Shower | 21 |
| Locomotion | Walk/Wheelchair, Stairs | 14 |
| Communication | Comprehension, Expression | 14 |
| Social Cognition | Social Interaction, Problem Solving, Memory | 21 |
| TOTAL | 18 items | 126 |

### FIM Key Numbers for Board Exam
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Total score range: 18 (total dependence) to 126 (complete independence)
- Motor subscale: 13 items, max 91
- Cognitive subscale: 5 items, max 35
- **FIM score >80 at discharge predicts home discharge**
- **FIM score <40 at admission predicts poor rehabilitation outcomes and possible need for SNF-level care**
- FIM efficiency = FIM gain / LOS (points per day)
- Average FIM efficiency in IRF for stroke: 1.5-2.0 points/day

From DeLisa: The single most useful predictor of functional outcome is the initial ADL assessment (most commonly the FIM score). Most patients with FIM scores of greater than 80 are discharged home, although many other factors influence discharge disposition.

### FIM Transition to Section GG
**[BOARD-LIKELY]** CMS mandated the use of Section GG items beginning October 2016 and officially replaced the FIM functional items in October 2019 under the IMPACT Act. Section GG self-care and mobility scores have strong positive correlations with total FIM scores (self-care: r=0.87 and 0.95; transfer: r=0.82 and 0.90 at admission and discharge, respectively). Data collection from Section GG codes began in October 2023, calculating a new discharge score including both self-care and mobility activities. (Source: PMC 2022; CMS Federal Register 2024)

### FIM Prediction Model Update
**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2024 study of 566 stroke rehabilitation patients developed the B-ADL model incorporating postural balance, albumin level, age, arm function, days since stroke onset, and level of ADL to predict motor FIM >61 at 90 days post-stroke onset, demonstrating clinical utility for optimizing rehabilitation programs and resource allocation. (Source: ScienceDirect 2024)

## Modified Rankin Scale (mRS)
**[BOARD-CORE]** The most widely used global outcome measure in stroke clinical trials. Measures degree of disability and dependence in daily activities. Simple 7-point scale (0-6).

| Score | Description | Functional Level |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 0 | No symptoms | Completely asymptomatic |
| 1 | No significant disability | Symptoms present but able to carry out all usual activities |
| 2 | Slight disability | Unable to carry out all previous activities but independent in personal affairs |
| 3 | Moderate disability | Requires some help but able to walk without assistance |
| 4 | Moderately severe disability | Unable to walk or attend to bodily needs without assistance |
| 5 | Severe disability | Bedridden, incontinent, requires constant care |
| 6 | Dead | Patient has died |

### mRS in Clinical Trials
**[BOARD-CORE]** The mRS is the PRIMARY outcome measure in most major stroke trials (NINDS tPA, MR CLEAN, DAWN, DEFUSE 3). "Good outcome" is typically defined as mRS 0-2 (functional independence). "Favorable outcome" in some trials includes mRS 0-3. The shift analysis (ordinal shift across all mRS levels) is increasingly used rather than a single cutpoint. Structured mRS interview improves inter-rater reliability.

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** The utility-weighted modified Rankin Scale (UW-mRS) has been developed and incorporated into several recent trials (2020-2025). It adds a health utility scale as a patient-centered outcome, addressing the limitation that the standard mRS does not reflect the broader impact on quality of life. A 2025 study examined population-specific utility values for the UW-mRS. (Source: Frontiers in Neurology 2025)

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2025 study found that both NIHSS and mRS significantly improved predictive models for 30-day non-elective readmission and mortality after ischemic stroke, suggesting these scales should be routinely incorporated into risk prediction models. (Source: JMIR Medical Informatics 2025)

## Barthel Index
**[BOARD-CORE]** A 10-item ordinal scale measuring independence in basic ADLs. Total score 0-100.
- Score >60 = modified independence (can manage with minimal assistance)
- Score of 100 does NOT mean the patient is normal — it means independent in BASIC ADLs only
- Limitations: ceiling effect, does not assess IADLs (cooking, finances), does not assess cognition or communication
- FIM is more sensitive than Barthel because of its 7-point scale vs Barthel's 3-point increments

## Other Outcome Scales Summary
**[BOARD-CORE]**

| Scale | Measures | Key Features |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Berg Balance Scale | Balance and fall risk | 0-56 score, 14 items, <45 = increased fall risk |
| Fugl-Meyer Assessment | Sensorimotor recovery | 0-226 total, Motor UE 0-66, Motor LE 0-34, based on Brunnstrom stages |
| 10-Meter Walk Test | Gait speed | <0.4 m/s = household ambulator, 0.4-0.8 = limited community, >0.8 = community |
| 6-Minute Walk Test | Functional walking endurance | Community ambulation threshold ~300-350 m |

### Gait Speed as the "Sixth Vital Sign"
**[BOARD-CORE]** Gait speed, measured by the 10-meter walk test, is considered the "sixth vital sign" in rehabilitation. Functional ambulation categories:
- <0.4 m/s = household ambulator (cannot safely navigate community)
- 0.4-0.8 m/s = limited community ambulator
- >0.8 m/s = full community ambulator
- Minimum gait speed to safely cross a street crosswalk: approximately 1.0-1.2 m/s

## Stroke Impact Scale (SIS)
**[BOARD-CORE]** A patient-reported outcome measure (PROM) that captures the patient's own perspective on how stroke has affected their life across 8 domains: Strength, Hand function, Mobility, ADL/IADL, Memory/thinking, Communication, Emotion, Social participation. 59 items, each scored 1-5. More sensitive to change in higher-functioning patients than FIM or Barthel (no ceiling effect). Limitation: requires adequate cognition and language to complete.

# SECTION 3: REHABILITATION SETTINGS & CONTINUUM OF CARE

## Stroke Rehabilitation Continuum
The stroke rehabilitation continuum spans from the acute hospital through inpatient rehabilitation, skilled nursing, home health, and outpatient services. Matching the patient to the appropriate setting based on medical stability, functional level, and therapy tolerance is critical for optimal outcomes.

## Inpatient Rehabilitation Facility (IRF)
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Requires tolerance of 3 hours of therapy per day, 5 days/week
- Must need at least 2 therapy disciplines (PT, OT, SLP)
- Must be medically stable
- Must have realistic, measurable functional goals
- Must require physician supervision (face-to-face 3x/week)
- CMS 60% rule: 60% of cases must meet qualifying diagnoses (stroke qualifies)
- Average length of stay for stroke: 14-17 days (trending downward)
- Better outcomes than SNF for moderate-severe stroke

From DeLisa (Box 18-4) — Criteria for admission to a comprehensive rehabilitation program: Stable neurological status, significant persisting neurological deficit, identified disability affecting at least two of the following: mobility, self-care activities, communication, bowel or bladder control, or swallowing. Plus sufficient cognitive function to learn, sufficient communicative ability to engage with therapists, physical ability to tolerate the active program (at least 3 h/d), and achievable therapeutic goals.

### CMS 60% Rule for IRF Admission
**[BOARD-CORE]** CMS compliance requirement that at least 60% of an IRF's total patient population must have one of 13 qualifying diagnostic categories. Stroke is one of the 13 qualifying diagnoses. Facilities falling below 60% risk reclassification as an acute care hospital. Pre-admission screening must document: qualifying diagnosis, medical stability, ability to tolerate 3 hours/day of therapy. Rehabilitation physician must conduct face-to-face visit within 24 hours of admission.

### IRF-PAI and Section GG
**[BOARD-LIKELY]** The IRF Patient Assessment Instrument (IRF-PAI) documents functional status using Section GG items (replacing FIM items as of October 2019). Case mix groups (CMGs) determine reimbursement — grouped by diagnosis, functional status, age, and comorbidities. CMS IMPACT Act mandated standardized patient assessment data across post-acute care settings. (Source: CMS Federal Register 2024)

### IRF Quality Measures
**[BOARD-LIKELY]** Current IRF quality measures include: discharge to community rate, improvement in self-care, improvement in mobility (Section GG), and risk-adjusted 30-day readmission rates. For FY 2025, CMS finalized the addition of new quality measures and removal of others, with the Hospital 30-Day Risk-Standardized Mortality Rate Following Acute Ischemic Stroke now incorporating claims-based risk adjustment for stroke severity. (Source: CMS 2024)

## Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF)
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Therapy 1-2 hours per day typical
- For patients who cannot tolerate 3 hours/day of therapy
- Medically complex patients requiring nursing care
- Cognitive limitations may prevent active therapy participation
- Medicare covers up to 100 days per benefit period (with qualifying stay)
- Days 1-20 fully covered, days 21-100 require copay
- Average LOS for stroke: 21-35 days

## Home Health
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Patient must be "homebound" (leaving home is a taxing effort)
- PT, OT, SLP, nursing, social work, and aide services available
- Physician must certify need and create plan of care
- Typically 2-3 visits per week per discipline
- Focus on functional independence in actual home environment
- Environmental assessment and modification
- Caregiver training in the home setting

## Outpatient Rehabilitation
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Day rehabilitation programs: intensive (3-6 hours/day)
- Standard outpatient: 1-3 sessions per week
- Medicare therapy caps have been repealed (MIPS threshold replaces)
- Community re-integration focus
- May continue for months to years after stroke

## Rehabilitation Setting Selection Guide
**[BOARD-CORE]**

| Criterion | IRF | SNF | Home Health |
|-----------|-----|-----|------------|
| Therapy tolerance | 3 hrs/day | 1-2 hrs/day | Variable |
| Medical stability | Required | More complex OK | Stable |
| Supervision need | 24-hour available | 24-hour nursing | Caregiver or independent |
| Therapy disciplines | >=2 required | >=1 | >=1 |
| MD supervision | 3x/week face-to-face | Monthly | Certifying physician |
| Avg LOS | 14-17 days | 21-35 days | Weeks to months |
| Best for | Moderate-severe, motivated | Cannot tolerate IRF intensity | Homebound, functional plateau |

## Discharge Destination Predictors
**[BOARD-CORE]**

| Predictor | Home Discharge | Institutional Care |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| FIM score | Discharge FIM >80 | Discharge FIM <60 |
| Social support | Spouse or family caregiver available | Lives alone, no caregiver |
| Age | Younger (<75) | Older (>85) |
| Cognition | Intact or mildly impaired | Moderate-severe cognitive impairment |
| Continence | Continent or managed | Persistent incontinence |
| Neglect/awareness | Absent or mild | Severe neglect with anosognosia |
| Prior living situation | Community dwelling | Assisted living or nursing home |

## Early Rehabilitation Timing
**[BOARD-CORE]** Multiple studies demonstrate that early rehabilitation (initiated within 24-48 hours of medical stabilization) leads to better functional outcomes, shorter hospital stays, and lower mortality. However, the AVERT trial showed that VERY early high-dose mobilization (<24 hours) may be harmful. Current recommendation: begin rehabilitation within 24-48 hours with graduated intensity, avoiding aggressive early high-dose mobilization.

From DeLisa: The Post-Stroke Rehabilitation Outcomes Project demonstrated that patients have better outcomes if they are admitted into a rehabilitation program early rather than later, regardless of severity of the stroke.

## Stroke Unit vs General Ward
**[BOARD-CORE]** Studies of stroke units have consistently found improved outcomes when compared with care on general medical units. Stroke unit care reduces length of stay and improves functional outcomes and independence, though the mortality benefit compared to general wards is less conclusive (OR 0.84, 95% CI 0.70-1.00, P=0.05). A 2024 study found that admission to the stroke unit was associated with a 43% reduction in the likelihood of prolonged hospitalization. Benefits appear to relate to prevention and/or earlier recognition of medical complications of stroke and earlier mobilization. (Sources: PMC 2013; PubMed 2024)

## Early Supported Discharge (ESD)
**[BOARD-LIKELY]** ESD programs aim to transition care from stroke units to patients' homes. Recent evidence (2020-2025):
- Similar clinical outcomes to conventional rehabilitation
- Significantly shorter length of hospital stay
- Greater improvement in depressive symptoms in some studies
- Encourages family involvement in early rehabilitation
- No significant difference in total healthcare costs vs conventional care
- A 2025 Korean RCT (KOMPACT study) found ESD feasible in patients with mild to moderate stroke
(Sources: Frontiers in Neurology 2022; ScienceDirect 2025; AHA Circulation CVQO 2020)

## Length of Stay Trends in Stroke Rehabilitation
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Average IRF LOS for stroke: approximately 14-15 days (trending downward)
- In the 1990s, average IRF LOS for stroke was 20-25 days
- Prospective payment system (PPS) implementation in 2002 drove significant LOS reductions
- Shorter LOS does not always mean worse outcomes — higher therapy intensity can compensate
- FIM efficiency has INCREASED as LOS has decreased — more efficient rehabilitation
- SNF average LOS for stroke: 21-35 days

# SECTION 4: COMMUNITY REINTEGRATION & LONG-TERM OUTCOMES

## Overview
The ultimate goal of stroke rehabilitation is maximal participation in life roles and community activities. Approximately 50-70% of stroke survivors regain functional independence, but many face ongoing challenges.

## Long-Term Functional Outcomes
**[BOARD-CORE]**

| Outcome | Percentage | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| Independent in basic ADLs | 50-70% | At 6 months poststroke |
| Community ambulation | 50-60% | With or without assistive device |
| Return to work | 25-50% | Highly variable, depends on job demands |
| Return to driving | 30-50% | Requires formal driving evaluation |
| Persistent depression | 30-50% | Ongoing screening needed |
| Recurrent stroke within 5 years | 25-30% | Secondary prevention is critical |
| Caregiver burden/burnout | 30-50% | Caregiver support programs essential |

From DeLisa: While many stroke survivors are initially unable to walk independently, 54% to 80% achieve this milestone within the first 3 months poststroke (although frequently requiring the assistance of a cane or AFO). The Framingham cohort indicates that long-term survivors of stroke show good recovery of functional mobility, with 80% being independent in mobility. Between 47% and 76% of survivors achieve partial or total independence in ADLs.

## Long-Term Survival and Recurrence
**[BOARD-CORE]** Recent data on 5-year outcomes:
- Overall 5-year survival: approximately 50-59% (varies by sex and stroke subtype)
- Highest survival: small-artery occlusion stroke (72-76%)
- Lowest survival: cardioembolic stroke (36-48%)
- 5-year recurrence rate: approximately 12-20% (improving with better secondary prevention)
- The cumulative risk of recurrence or death by 5 years has reduced from 65% (1995-1999) to 43% (2010-2015)
- Annual risk of a second stroke: approximately 5%
- Survival rates by age: under 45 years 85-90%, over 75 years 55-65%
(Sources: Stroke AHA 2020; PMC 2020; Aked et al. European Journal of Neurology 2021)

From Cuccurullo: ARIC study (1987-2011 in four U.S. cities) showed that the cumulative all-cause mortality rate after a stroke was 10.5% at 30 days, 21.2% at 1 year, 39.8% at 5 years, and 58.4% at the end of 24 years of follow-up. Between 32% and 58% of initial survivors are dead within 5 years after a first stroke, with survival varying based on age, sex, and race.

## Driving After Stroke
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- No driving for at least 1-3 months after stroke (varies by state); many countries recommend at least 4 weeks for mild stroke, 6 months for others
- Comprehensive driving evaluation (CDE) by driving rehabilitation specialist is the standard
- Assess: vision, cognition, reaction time, judgment, motor function
- Behind-the-wheel evaluation is the gold standard
- Visual field cuts (hemianopia) are NOT absolute contraindications in all states
- Vehicle modifications may be needed (left-foot accelerator, hand controls, spinner knob)
- Physician has duty to report if patient is unsafe — varies by state law
- Seizure-free interval required if poststroke seizures
- Screening tools include: Compass test, Road Sign Recognition Test, Trail Making Test B, Rey-Osterrieth Complex Figure
(Sources: NCBI Bookshelf 2022; American Stroke Association)

## Return to Work After Stroke
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- 25-50% of working-age stroke survivors return to work (68.8% in young adults ages 18-50 per 2025 JAHA study)
- Predictors of successful return: younger age, mild deficit, white-collar job, supportive employer, independence in ADLs at 3 months, contributing to household income
- Negative predictors: large-artery atherosclerosis, diabetes, NIHSS >15, hemorrhagic stroke, aphasia
- Cognitive and communication deficits often more limiting than motor deficits
- Vocational rehabilitation services, ADA reasonable accommodations, supported employment programs all improve rates
- Gradual return with modified duties recommended

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2024 systematic review identified that the rehabilitation process involves multiple predictor domains: physical factors (stroke severity, functional disability), social factors (ethnicity, income, gender, occupation), and cognitive/emotional factors (psychiatric disorders, fatigue, cognitive functioning). The RETAKE trial (2025) explored experiences during COVID-19 and found pandemic-related disruptions significantly impacted return-to-work outcomes. (Sources: Archives of PM&R 2023; JAHA 2025; Frontiers in Sociology 2025)

## Caregiver Burden
**[BOARD-CORE]** Caregiver burden affects 30-50% of stroke caregivers and includes depression, anxiety, physical health decline, social isolation, and financial strain. Caregiver well-being directly impacts patient outcomes. Rehabilitation teams should assess and support caregivers through education, skills training, respite services, and counseling referrals. The Caregiver Strain Index (CSI) is a validated screening tool — score >=7 indicates high strain (13-item tool).

### Caregiver Training Needs
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Transfer training: bed-to-wheelchair, wheelchair-to-toilet, car transfers
- Medication management: dosing, side effects, pill organization
- Skin inspection: daily checks of pressure-prone areas
- Signs of medical emergency: stroke recurrence (FAST), DVT, PE, seizure, aspiration
- Emotional support: managing behavioral changes, depression screening
- Communication strategies for aphasia: yes/no questions, visual cues, communication boards
- Fall prevention in the home: environmental hazards, safe mobility techniques
- Caregiver self-care: respite services, support groups, recognizing burnout

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2021 AHA systematic review of stroke family caregiver interventions found that of 18 studies, 7 caregiver and 4 dyad intervention studies reported significant improvements in caregiver outcomes. Effective interventions tended to incorporate educational components and comprised 7-9 sessions. Dyadic interventions showed a significant effect on stroke survivors' physical functioning, memory, and quality of life AND on caregivers' depression. However, meta-analyses of overall caregiver burden reduction showed nonsignificant effects, indicating a need for more targeted interventions. (Source: Stroke AHA 2022)

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2025 narrative review identified predictors of caregiver burden including: severity of stroke-related disability, caregiver's physical health, availability of social support, and financial resources. Structured educational programs were the most consistently effective intervention. (Source: PMC 2025)

## Community Reintegration Challenges
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Transportation: loss of driving privilege, reliance on paratransit, public transit barriers
- Social isolation: up to 50% of stroke survivors report significant social isolation
- Loss of leisure activities: require adaptation or substitution
- Role changes within families: from provider/parent to dependent
- Financial impact: lost income, medical costs, home modifications, caregiver lost wages
- Community Participation Indicators (CPI), Reintegration to Normal Living (RNL) Index, Community Integration Questionnaire (CIQ) — validated measures

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2024 JAMA Network Open RCT evaluated a rehabilitation transition program to improve community participation among stroke survivors, finding that structured transitional care programs significantly improved participation outcomes. Depression, fatigue, mobility, and environmental support showed moderate to strong associations with participation performance and satisfaction. Perceived social participation, physical function, and caregiver strain were good predictors of community reintegration. (Sources: JAMA Network Open 2024; PMC 2022)

**[BOARD-EMERGING]** Narrative and skills-building interventions for young stroke survivors (ages 18-64) are being studied in RCTs, reflecting the growing recognition that stroke in adults aged 18-64 is increasing significantly worldwide and this group encounters enormous difficulties reintegrating into social roles. (Source: BMC Neurology 2021)

## Telerehabilitation
**[BOARD-LIKELY]** The delivery of rehabilitation services via telecommunication technology. Expanded rapidly post-COVID with growing evidence:
- Non-inferior to in-person therapy for many stroke rehabilitation interventions
- Effective for: upper extremity training, speech-language therapy, cognitive rehabilitation, caregiver education
- Advantages: eliminates transportation barriers, increases access in rural areas, enables more frequent sessions
- Limitations: cannot perform hands-on manual therapy, limited for severe cognitive/communication impairment
- Hybrid models (combination of in-person and telehealth) may be optimal
- CMS expanded telehealth coverage during COVID-19 — many expansions made permanent
- Patient satisfaction is generally high when technology barriers are addressed

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs confirmed that telerehabilitation is an effective and acceptable substitute for conventional stroke rehabilitation across outcomes domains including motor function, balance, ADL, and quality of life. A 2025 RCT specifically showed that AI-driven cognitive telerehabilitation was effective for subacute stroke patients. The optimal hybrid model combines monthly or quarterly in-person visits with 2-5 weekly telerehabilitation sessions. (Sources: PubMed 2024; Frontiers in Neurology 2025; JCMR 2024)

**[BOARD-EMERGING]** Remote monitoring with wearable devices (activity sensors, gait sensors) for home exercise feedback is an expanding area with growing evidence. (Source: Frontiers 2023)

## Ongoing Care Needs
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Secondary prevention compliance (medications, lifestyle)
- Ongoing spasticity management
- Annual depression screening
- Fall prevention program
- Exercise and fitness (adapted programs)
- Caregiver support and respite services
- Community-based stroke support groups
- Annual review of functional status and equipment needs
- Telehealth follow-up expanding access

## Adaptive Equipment & Community Resources
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Adaptive equipment: reachers, sock aids, rocker knife, built-up handles
- Bathroom safety: grab bars, tub bench, raised toilet seat, handheld shower
- Mobility: AFO, cane (held in UNAFFECTED hand), rollator, wheelchair
- Home modifications: ramp, stair lift, widened doorways
- Communication: speech-generating devices, aphasia apps
- Transportation: paratransit services, adaptive driving

## Quality Metrics in Stroke Care
**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Joint Commission Primary Stroke Center (PSC) certification
- Joint Commission Comprehensive Stroke Center (CSC): includes thrombectomy capability and neurocritical care
- CMS Hospital Quality Measures for Stroke: VTE prophylaxis, antithrombotic therapy, AF anticoagulation, DVT screening, rehab assessment, dysphagia screening
- Get With The Guidelines (GWTG) — Stroke: AHA/ASA quality improvement program
- GWTG Gold Plus achievement: >=85% adherence to all applicable measures for >=24 consecutive months
- IRF quality measures: discharge to community rate, improvement in self-care and mobility (Section GG)
- CMS IMPACT Act: standardized patient assessment data across post-acute care settings
- Risk-adjusted 30-day readmission rates: publicly reported, affect reimbursement

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** CMS stroke quality measure updates for 2024-2025:
- STK-2: Discharged on Antithrombotic Therapy
- STK-3: Anticoagulation Therapy for Atrial Fibrillation/Flutter
- STK-5: Antithrombotic Therapy by End of Hospital Day 2
- For 2026-2027: The 30-Day Stroke Mortality Rate (MORT-30-STK) will use a two-year data window instead of three years; Hospital 30-Day Risk-Standardized Mortality Rate for AIS will incorporate claims-based risk adjustment for stroke severity
- For FY 2025, CMS finalized the addition of 7 new quality measures and removal of 10 measures
(Sources: CMS.gov 2024; Joint Commission 2025)

# SECTION 5: KEY BOARD-EXAM PEARLS (HIGH-YIELD SUMMARY)

1. **FIM >80 at discharge = home discharge predictor** [BOARD-CORE]
2. **FIM <40 at admission = poor rehab outcome, consider SNF** [BOARD-CORE]
3. **95% reach best neurological level by 11 weeks** (Copenhagen Stroke Study) [BOARD-CORE]
