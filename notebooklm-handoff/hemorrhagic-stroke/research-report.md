## SECTION 1: HEMORRHAGIC STROKE OVERVIEW

Hemorrhagic stroke accounts for approximately 13% of all strokes but is responsible for a disproportionately high percentage of stroke-related deaths and disability. It is caused by rupture of a blood vessel, leading to bleeding into the brain parenchyma (intracerebral hemorrhage, ICH) or the subarachnoid space (subarachnoid hemorrhage, SAH).

### Key Facts
- ICH accounts for ~10% of all strokes; SAH accounts for ~3%
- 30-day mortality for ICH is 30-50%; for SAH is 40-50%
- More common in younger patients compared to ischemic stroke
- Hypertension is the number one risk factor for ICH
- Anticoagulant use is an increasingly common cause
- Presentation: sudden onset of severe headache, vomiting, rapid neurological deterioration
- Often associated with elevated ICP and risk of herniation

### ICH vs SAH Comparison

| Feature | ICH | SAH |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Percentage of strokes | ~10% | ~3% |
| Location of bleeding | Within brain parenchyma | Between arachnoid and pia mater |
| Most common cause | Hypertension | Ruptured saccular (berry) aneurysm |
| Classic presentation | Sudden focal deficit + headache + vomiting | "Worst headache of my life" + neck stiffness |
| CT appearance | Hyperdense (bright white) mass within brain | Blood in cisterns and sulci |
| 30-day mortality | 30-50% | 40-50% |
| Key complication | Herniation from mass effect | Vasospasm (days 4-14) |

**Board Pearl:** Acute blood appears BRIGHT (hyperdense/white) on non-contrast CT. This is the opposite of ischemic stroke, which appears DARK (hypodense) and may not be visible in the first 6-12 hours. [BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 2: INTRACEREBRAL HEMORRHAGE (ICH)

### 2.1 Etiology

- **Hypertension** -- #1 cause overall (Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms of lenticulostriate arteries) [BOARD-CORE]
- **Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA)** -- #1 cause in elderly/normotensive patients; causes lobar hemorrhage [BOARD-CORE]
- **Anticoagulant therapy** -- warfarin, DOACs, heparin [BOARD-CORE]
- **Coagulopathy** -- thrombocytopenia, DIC, liver failure
- **Vascular malformations** -- AVM, cavernous malformation
- **Brain tumor** (hemorrhagic metastasis: melanoma, renal cell, choriocarcinoma, thyroid)
- **Drug use** -- cocaine and amphetamines (acute hypertensive surge) [BOARD-CORE]
- **Hemorrhagic transformation** of ischemic stroke

### 2.2 ICH Locations and Associations

| Location | Frequency | Usual Cause | Clinical Features |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Putamen/basal ganglia | 35-50% (most common) | Hypertension | Contralateral hemiparesis, hemisensory loss, hemianopia, aphasia or neglect |
| Thalamus | 10-15% | Hypertension | Contralateral sensory loss, upgaze palsy ("eyes look at the nose"), aphasia or neglect |
| Pons | 5-10% | Hypertension | Rapidly fatal; coma, quadriplegia, pinpoint reactive pupils, hyperthermia, posturing |
| Cerebellum | 5-10% | Hypertension | Sudden onset ataxia, vertigo, headache, vomiting; may compress brainstem -- SURGICAL EMERGENCY |
| Lobar (cortical) | 20-30% | Amyloid angiopathy, AVM | Varies by lobe; more common in elderly normotensive patients |

**Board Pearl:** The putamen is the most common site of hypertensive ICH due to rupture of the lenticulostriate arteries (branches of the MCA). Cerebellar hemorrhage is the most important to recognize because it is a surgical emergency -- posterior fossa decompression can be life-saving. Indications: hematoma >3 cm, brainstem compression, or hydrocephalus. [BOARD-CORE]

### 2.3 ICH Management -- 2022 AHA/ASA Guideline Update

The 2022 AHA/ASA guidelines replaced the 2015 guidelines and represent the current standard of care.

**Source:** Greenberg SM, et al. 2022 Guideline for the Management of Patients With Spontaneous Intracerebral Hemorrhage. Stroke. 2022;53:e282-e361. [BOARD-CORE]

#### Blood Pressure Management (Updated Recommendations)
- For patients with SBP 150-220 mmHg: acute lowering to target of 140 mmHg, maintaining in range 130-150 mmHg, is safe and may be reasonable to improve functional outcomes [BOARD-CORE]
- Goal: initiate treatment within 2 hours of ICH onset, reach target within 1 hour [BOARD-LIKELY]
- IV nicardipine or labetalol are preferred agents [BOARD-CORE]
- For SBP >220 mmHg: aggressive reduction with continuous IV infusion and close monitoring is reasonable [BOARD-CORE]

#### INTERACT Trial Series -- Blood Pressure Evidence
- **INTERACT2 (2013):** Intensive BP lowering (target SBP <140) vs standard (target SBP <180). Primary outcome (death or major disability) not significantly different, but ordinal analysis showed improved functional outcomes. [BOARD-CORE]
- **ATACH-2 (2016):** Intensive (110-139 mmHg) vs standard (140-179 mmHg). No benefit from intensive lowering; possible harm with renal adverse events. [BOARD-CORE]
- **INTERACT3 (2023):** Goal-directed care bundle (BP lowering + glucose, temperature, anticoagulation management) improved functional outcomes. This was a stepped-wedge cluster-randomized trial in low/middle-income countries. Supports bundled care approach. [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Board Pearl:** The key takeaway: target SBP <140 mmHg is reasonable for most ICH patients with presenting SBP 150-220 mmHg. Going below 110 mmHg (ATACH-2 intensive arm) showed no benefit and possible harm. [BOARD-CORE]

#### Anticoagulant Reversal (Updated Recommendations)
- Anticoagulation should be discontinued immediately upon ICH diagnosis [BOARD-CORE]
- Rapid reversal should be performed as soon as possible [BOARD-CORE]

| Anticoagulant | Reversal Agent | Key Details | Board Tag |
|---------------|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| Warfarin | 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) + IV Vitamin K | PCC preferred over FFP (faster, smaller volume); target INR <1.4 within 1 hour | BOARD-CORE |
| Dabigatran (Pradaxa) | Idarucizumab (Praxbind) | Monoclonal antibody fragment; 5g IV; immediate and complete reversal | BOARD-CORE |
| Rivaroxaban/Apixaban | Andexanet alfa (Andexxa) | Recombinant modified factor Xa; see ANNEXA-I trial results below | BOARD-CORE |
| Heparin (UFH) | Protamine sulfate | 1 mg protamine per 100 units heparin in prior 2-3 hours; max 50 mg | BOARD-CORE |
| LMWH (enoxaparin) | Protamine sulfate (partial) | Only ~60% reversal; 1 mg per 1 mg enoxaparin in prior 8 hours | BOARD-CORE |
| Antiplatelet agents | Platelet transfusion (controversial) | PATCH trial: platelet transfusion may WORSEN outcomes | BOARD-CORE |

#### ANNEXA-I Trial (NEJM 2024) -- Factor Xa Inhibitor Reversal
- Randomized 530 patients with factor Xa inhibitor-associated ICH to andexanet vs usual care
- Hemostatic efficacy: 67.0% with andexanet vs 53.1% with usual care (p=0.003)
- Anti-factor Xa activity reduction: 94.5% with andexanet vs 26.9% with usual care
- Safety concern: thrombotic events 10.3% with andexanet vs 5.6% usual care; ischemic stroke 6.5% vs 1.5%
- Conclusion: andexanet reduces hematoma expansion but increases thrombotic risk [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Board Pearl:** 4F-PCC is the preferred reversal for warfarin (over FFP). For factor Xa inhibitors, andexanet alfa is effective but carries significant thrombotic risk. The 2022 guidelines note 4F-PCC may also be considered for factor Xa inhibitor reversal when andexanet is unavailable. [BOARD-CORE]

#### Surgical Interventions (Major Updates)
- Cerebellar hemorrhage >3 cm with brainstem compression or hydrocephalus: surgical evacuation recommended [BOARD-CORE]
- EVD if intraventricular extension with hydrocephalus [BOARD-CORE]
- Supratentorial ICH: role of surgery has been historically controversial, but new evidence from ENRICH trial is changing practice [BOARD-LIKELY]

#### Hemostatic Therapy
- Platelet transfusion outside emergency surgery or severe thrombocytopenia: appears to WORSEN outcomes (PATCH trial) [BOARD-CORE]
- Recombinant factor VIIa: reduces hematoma growth but does NOT improve functional outcome or mortality -- NOT recommended [BOARD-CORE]

#### Other Key 2022 Guideline Recommendations
- Specialized stroke unit care with multidisciplinary team recommended [BOARD-CORE]
- Compression stockings (any length): NO benefit for DVT prevention [BOARD-CORE]
- Intermittent pneumatic compression devices: RECOMMENDED for DVT prevention [BOARD-CORE]
- Seizure prophylaxis: NOT routinely recommended; treat clinical seizures with anti-seizure medication [BOARD-CORE]
- Continuous EEG monitoring reasonable for altered consciousness disproportionate to neurological injury [BOARD-LIKELY]
- ICP management: osmotic therapy (mannitol 20%, hypertonic saline), HOB 30 degrees [BOARD-CORE]

### 2.4 Spot Sign and Hematoma Expansion Prediction

The CTA "spot sign" is a focal area of contrast enhancement within a hematoma, representing active contrast extravasation.

- Strong independent predictor of hematoma expansion [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Sensitivity: ~53%; Specificity: ~88% for predicting expansion
- Present in approximately 25-30% of ICH patients scanned within 6 hours
- Higher Hounsfield unit values and multiple spot signs increase predictive accuracy
- Hematoma expansion occurs in approximately 25-38% of patients within 24 hours
- 2022 AHA guidelines: CTA can identify patients at risk for hematoma expansion who may benefit from more aggressive management [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Newer approaches: multiphase CTA and dynamic CTA show improved sensitivity compared to single-phase CTA [BOARD-EMERGING]

### 2.5 ICH Score -- Prognosis Prediction

| Component | Criteria | Points |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| GCS 3-4 | GCS score 3-4 | 2 |
| GCS 5-12 | GCS score 5-12 | 1 |
| GCS 13-15 | GCS score 13-15 | 0 |
| ICH volume >= 30 mL | Hematoma volume >= 30 cm3 | 1 |
| ICH volume < 30 mL | Hematoma volume < 30 cm3 | 0 |
| IVH present | Intraventricular hemorrhage | 1 |
| Infratentorial origin | Brainstem or cerebellar | 1 |
| Age >= 80 | Patient age >= 80 years | 1 |

Score 0-6. Higher score = higher 30-day mortality.

| ICH Score | 30-Day Mortality |
|-----------|------------------|
| 0 | 0% |
| 1 | 13% |
| 2 | 26% |
| 3 | 72% |
| 4 | 97% |
| 5-6 | 100% |

**Board Pearl:** ICH Score should NOT be used in isolation for withdrawal of care decisions. The 2022 AHA guidelines emphasize that early aggressive, full care should be provided and early care-limitation orders should be avoided, as they become a self-fulfilling prophecy. [BOARD-CORE]

### 2.6 Minimally Invasive Surgery for ICH

#### MISTIE III Trial (Lancet 2019)
- Image-guided minimally invasive catheter-based approach + alteplase for ICH evacuation
- 506 patients, hematoma >= 30 mL
- Primary outcome (mRS 0-3 at 365 days): 45% surgery vs 41% medical (p=0.33) -- NOT significant
- However: when clot reduced to <= 15 mL, outcomes significantly better
- Key lesson: achieving adequate clot evacuation (>= 70% reduction or residual <= 15 mL) matters more than the procedure itself [BOARD-LIKELY]

#### ENRICH Trial (NEJM 2024) -- LANDMARK TRIAL
- First positive randomized trial for surgical ICH evacuation [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 300 patients, 37 US centers, 2017-2023
- Compared early (within 24 hours) minimally invasive parafascicular surgery (MIPS) using BrainPath device vs standard medical management
- Hematoma volume 30-80 mL; both lobar (208) and anterior basal ganglia (92) hemorrhages

**Results:**
- Utility-weighted mRS at 180 days: 0.458 surgery vs 0.374 medical (difference 0.084; posterior probability of superiority 0.981)
- Benefit primarily driven by lobar hemorrhages: utility-weighted mRS 0.51 surgery vs 0.37 medical
- 30-day survival: 90.7% surgery vs 82% medical
- ICU stay: 6.9 days surgery vs 9.7 days medical
- Hospital stay: 14.9 days surgery vs 18.1 days medical
- Decompressive hemicraniectomy needed: 3.3% surgery vs 20% medical
- Cost-effective: $8,850 less per patient in surgery group

**Clinical significance:** ENRICH is the first trial demonstrating functional benefit of surgical clot evacuation for supratentorial ICH. The benefit was most pronounced for lobar hemorrhages. This may change the standard of care. [BOARD-LIKELY]

#### MIND Trial (JAMA Neurology 2025)
- Terminated early after ENRICH publication
- 239 patients with supratentorial ICH; 70.8% deep hemorrhages, 29.2% lobar
- Achieved 80.7% median hematoma volume reduction
- Did NOT show improved clinical outcomes at 180 days
- Differences from ENRICH: higher proportion of deep hemorrhages, different surgical technique
- Suggests benefit of MIS may be location-dependent (lobar > deep) [BOARD-EMERGING]

### 2.7 Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA)

- Deposition of beta-amyloid protein in walls of small cortical and leptomeningeal vessels [BOARD-CORE]
- Leading cause of lobar ICH in elderly normotensive patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Recurrent lobar hemorrhages in different locations over time -- hallmark pattern [BOARD-CORE]
- Spares deep structures (basal ganglia, thalamus, brainstem) [BOARD-CORE]
- Associated with Alzheimer disease (same beta-amyloid pathology) [BOARD-CORE]
- MRI GRE/SWI shows multiple cortical microbleeds -- key diagnostic finding [BOARD-CORE]
- Boston criteria for diagnosis: probable CAA = multiple lobar hemorrhages in patient >= 55 with no other cause [BOARD-CORE]
- Updated Boston Criteria v2.0 (2022): includes cortical superficial siderosis and white matter hyperintensity patterns as supporting features [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Anticoagulation relatively contraindicated due to high rebleeding risk [BOARD-CORE]

**Board Pearl:** Hypertensive ICH affects DEEP structures (putamen, thalamus, pons, cerebellum). CAA causes LOBAR (cortical) hemorrhages. Normotensive elderly patient with recurrent lobar hemorrhages and cortical microbleeds on MRI = cerebral amyloid angiopathy. [BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 3: SUBARACHNOID HEMORRHAGE (SAH)

### 3.1 Overview and Clinical Features

- Bleeding into the subarachnoid space, most commonly from ruptured saccular (berry) aneurysm at Circle of Willis [BOARD-CORE]
- ~3% of all strokes; extremely high morbidity and mortality (40-50% at 30 days) [BOARD-CORE]

**Key Clinical Features:**
- "Worst headache of my life" -- sudden, severe, thunderclap headache [BOARD-CORE]
- Neck stiffness (meningismus) [BOARD-CORE]
- Nausea, vomiting, photophobia
- Loss of consciousness at onset (up to 50%)
- Sentinel headache -- warning leak days to weeks before major rupture (30-50%) [BOARD-CORE]
- CN III palsy from PComm aneurysm [BOARD-CORE]
- Subhyaloid (preretinal) hemorrhage -- Terson syndrome [BOARD-CORE]

**Diagnostic Workup:**
1. Non-contrast CT (sensitivity >95% within 6 hours) [BOARD-CORE]
2. If CT negative: lumbar puncture for xanthochromia [BOARD-CORE]
3. If SAH confirmed: CTA or conventional angiography to locate aneurysm [BOARD-CORE]

### 3.2 Saccular (Berry) Aneurysms

- Cause ~85% of non-traumatic SAH [BOARD-CORE]
- Located at arterial bifurcations of Circle of Willis
- Most common location: anterior communicating artery (AComm) ~30-35% [BOARD-CORE]
- Second most common: posterior communicating artery (PComm) ~25-30% [BOARD-CORE]
- ~90-95% of saccular aneurysms occur on anterior part of Circle of Willis [BOARD-CORE]
- Risk of rupture increases with size (especially >7 mm; more likely if >10 mm) [BOARD-CORE]
- Risk factors: hypertension, smoking, heavy alcohol use, family history, connective tissue disorders [BOARD-CORE]
- Associated conditions: ADPKD, Ehlers-Danlos type IV, coarctation of the aorta [BOARD-CORE]
- Multiple in 20% of patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Peak age for rupture: 5th and 6th decade [BOARD-CORE]
- Rupture usually occurs during activity (straining, coitus) rather than sleep [BOARD-CORE]

### 3.3 2023 AHA/ASA SAH Guidelines Update

**Source:** Hoh BL, et al. 2023 Guideline for the Management of Patients With Aneurysmal Subarachnoid Hemorrhage. Stroke. 2023;54:e314-e370. [BOARD-LIKELY]

This replaces the 2012 SAH guidelines and represents the first major update in over a decade.

#### Key Updated Recommendations:
- **Aneurysm treatment timing:** Prompt treatment preferably within 24 hours (previously "as early as feasible"). Both clipping and coiling remain options; selection depends on patient/aneurysm characteristics [BOARD-LIKELY]
- **Management setting:** Care in centers with dedicated neurocritical care units, high case volumes, and multidisciplinary teams is associated with lower mortality [BOARD-LIKELY]
- **Seizure management:** For new-onset seizures, treat with ASM for 7 days. Prophylactic ASM NOT routinely recommended but may be considered in high-risk patients (MCA aneurysm, intraparenchymal hemorrhage, high-grade SAH, hydrocephalus, cortical infarction) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- **Terminology update:** "Delayed cerebral ischemia" (DCI) preferred over "vasospasm" as the clinical concern [BOARD-LIKELY]
- **Rehabilitation:** Multidisciplinary approach recommended; physical, cognitive, behavioral, and QoL deficits are common and can persist long-term [BOARD-LIKELY]

### 3.4 Hunt and Hess Scale -- SAH Clinical Grading

| Grade | Criteria | Approximate Mortality |
|-------|----------|----------------------|
| I | Asymptomatic or mild headache, slight nuchal rigidity | ~1% |
| II | Moderate-severe headache, nuchal rigidity, CN palsy only | ~5% |
| III | Drowsiness, confusion, or mild focal deficit | ~19% |
| IV | Stupor, moderate-severe hemiparesis, early decerebrate posturing | ~42% |
| V | Deep coma, decerebrate posturing, moribund | ~77% |

**Board Pearl:** Grade I-III: favorable for early surgical intervention (within 72 hours). Grade IV-V: worse prognosis; surgery may be delayed. [BOARD-CORE]

### 3.5 Fisher Scale and Modified Fisher Scale

**Fisher Scale** -- Predicts vasospasm risk based on CT appearance:

| Grade | CT Appearance | Vasospasm Risk |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | No blood detected | Low (~21%) |
| 2 | Diffuse thin layer <1 mm | Low (~25%) |
| 3 | Thick clot >1 mm | HIGH (~37%) |
| 4 | Diffuse/no SAH but with IVH | Moderate (~31%) |

**Modified Fisher Scale** -- Better predictor, independently scores cisternal blood and IVH:

| Grade | Cisternal Blood | IVH | Vasospasm Risk |
|-------|-----------------|-----|----------------|
| 0 | No SAH or IVH | No | ~0% |
| 1 | Thin SAH | No | ~24% |
| 2 | Thin SAH | Yes | ~33% |
| 3 | Thick SAH | No | ~33% |
| 4 | Thick SAH | Yes | ~40% |

**Board Pearl:** Fisher Grade 3 (thick cisternal clot >1mm) carries highest vasospasm risk. Modified Fisher is increasingly used and treats IVH as an independent predictor. [BOARD-CORE]

### 3.6 SAH Complications by Timeframe

| Timeframe | Complication | Details |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Immediate (day 0) | Rebleeding | Greatest risk in first 24 hours; ~4% on first day; highest mortality |
| Days 1-3 | Acute hydrocephalus | Blood blocks CSF absorption at arachnoid granulations |
| Days 4-14 | Cerebral vasospasm/DCI | Most dangerous; peaks days 7-10; leading cause of morbidity |
| Days 4-14 | Hyponatremia | Cerebral salt wasting (NOT SIADH) |
| Weeks-months | Chronic hydrocephalus | Communicating type; may require VP shunt; presents as NPH |
| Weeks-months | Seizures | ~5-10% develop seizures |

### 3.7 Vasospasm and Delayed Cerebral Ischemia (DCI)

- Occurs in 30-70% angiographically; ~30% develop symptomatic DCI [BOARD-CORE]
- Timeline: begins day 3, peaks days 7-10, resolves by day 14-21 [BOARD-CORE]
- Monitoring: daily neuro exams, transcranial Doppler (TCD velocities >120 cm/s suggest spasm, >200 cm/s is severe) [BOARD-CORE]

**Pharmacological Prevention:**
- **Nimodipine 60 mg PO q4h x 21 days** -- given to ALL SAH patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Only medication proven to improve outcomes in SAH [BOARD-CORE]
- Must be given ORALLY -- IV administration not approved and has caused fatal hypotension [BOARD-CORE]
- Neuroprotective mechanism; does NOT reduce angiographic vasospasm [BOARD-CORE]
- Begin within 96 hours of SAH [BOARD-CORE]

**Treatment of Symptomatic Vasospasm:**
- **Current approach:** euvolemia + induced hypertension (phenylephrine or norepinephrine) [BOARD-CORE]
- **Triple-H therapy (Hypertension, Hypervolemia, Hemodilution):** LARGELY ABANDONED [BOARD-CORE]
  - Only induced hypertension component is still supported
  - Hypervolemia increases complications (pulmonary edema, electrolyte derangement) without benefit
  - 2023 guidelines: maintenance of euvolemia is recommended; prophylactic hypervolemia or hemodilution is NOT recommended [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Refractory vasospasm: intra-arterial verapamil or balloon angioplasty [BOARD-CORE]

**Board Pearl:** Classic teaching emphasized triple-H therapy but modern evidence supports ONLY induced hypertension with euvolemia. Older board questions may still reference triple-H as the answer. [BOARD-CORE]

### 3.8 Hyponatremia in SAH -- CSW vs SIADH

This distinction is high-yield for board exams. [BOARD-CORE]

| Feature | Cerebral Salt Wasting (CSW) | SIADH |
|---------|----------------------------|-------|
| Volume status | Hypovolemic | Euvolemic/hypervolemic |
| Urine sodium | High | High |
| Urine output | High | Low |
| Treatment | IV normal saline + fludrocortisone | Fluid restriction |
| In SAH | Primary cause (~most cases) | Less common |

**Critical Point:** Fluid restriction (SIADH treatment) is CONTRAINDICATED in SAH because it worsens vasospasm. When in doubt, give saline (treating as CSW is safer). [BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 4: ARTERIOVENOUS MALFORMATIONS (AVM)

### 4.1 Key Facts
- Congenital tangles of abnormal arteries and veins without intervening capillary bed [BOARD-CORE]
- Most common cause of hemorrhagic stroke in young adults and children [BOARD-CORE]
- Most common presentation: ICH (50%), followed by seizures (25%) [BOARD-CORE]
- Annual rupture risk: 2-4% per year [BOARD-CORE]
- Lifetime bleeding risk: ~40-50% (historical estimate: 105 minus age in years) [BOARD-CORE]
- Peak presentation: ages 20-40 [BOARD-CORE]
- High-flow shunting may cause vascular steal phenomenon [BOARD-CORE]

### 4.2 Spetzler-Martin AVM Grading Scale

| Component | Feature | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Size | Small (<3 cm) | 1 |
| Size | Medium (3-6 cm) | 2 |
| Size | Large (>6 cm) | 3 |
| Location | Non-eloquent cortex | 0 |
| Location | Eloquent cortex | 1 |
| Venous drainage | Superficial only | 0 |
| Venous drainage | Deep drainage present | 1 |

Grade I-II: usually amenable to surgical resection. Grade III: individualized. Grade IV-V: high surgical risk; consider radiosurgery or observation. [BOARD-CORE]

**Eloquent cortex** includes: motor, sensory, language, visual, thalamus, hypothalamus, brainstem, cerebellar peduncles. [BOARD-CORE]

### 4.3 Vascular Malformation Types

| Type | Bleeding Risk | Key Feature | Treatment |
|------|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| AVM | High (2-4%/yr) | High-flow arteriovenous shunt | Surgery, embolization, radiosurgery |
| Cavernous malformation | Low (~0.5-1%/yr) | "Popcorn" on MRI | Surgery if symptomatic |
| DVA | Extremely low | Most common vascular malformation | NO treatment -- sole venous drainage |
| Capillary telangiectasia | Extremely low | Often in pons | No treatment |
| Dural AV fistula | Variable | Acquired; within dura | Embolization or surgery |

**Board Pearl:** DVAs must NOT be resected -- they may be the sole venous drainage for that region. [BOARD-CORE]

### 4.4 AVM Treatment

- **Microsurgical resection** -- definitive, best for Spetzler-Martin I-II [BOARD-CORE]
- **Endovascular embolization** -- adjunct to reduce size before surgery or radiosurgery [BOARD-CORE]
- **Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife)** -- small, deep, or eloquent AVMs; takes 2-3 years for obliteration [BOARD-CORE]
- **Multimodality treatment** -- combination for complex AVMs [BOARD-CORE]

### 4.5 ARUBA Trial and Post-ARUBA Updates

**ARUBA Trial (Original, 2014):**
- Randomized 226 patients with unruptured AVMs to medical management vs intervention
- At 33 months: stroke/death in 30.7% intervention vs 10.1% medical management
- Conclusion: medical management alone was superior for unruptured AVMs [BOARD-CORE]

**Long-term Follow-up (Lancet Neurology 2020):**
- Medical management remained superior even after extended follow-up [BOARD-CORE]

**Post-ARUBA Controversy and Real-World Data (2020-2025):**
- ARUBA criticized for: high proportion of standalone embolization, predominantly low-grade lesions, short follow-up
- Concerning unintended consequences: ruptured AVM incidence increased from 13.3% to 34.4% in post-ARUBA era, with mortality rising from 2.0% to 7.6%
- Endovascular treatment for unruptured AVMs declined 34.7% (2016-2022)
- Real-world registry data (NVQI-QOD) shows much better outcomes than ARUBA intervention arm: death/stroke 8.7% vs 30.7% with careful patient selection
- Current consensus: treatment decisions should be individualized; Spetzler-Martin I-II may still benefit from surgery despite ARUBA results [BOARD-LIKELY]

### 4.6 Normal Perfusion Pressure Breakthrough

- Complication after AVM resection or embolization [BOARD-CORE]
- Surrounding brain loses autoregulation from chronic high-flow shunting
- After AVM removal, normal perfusion pressure is too high for previously "steal"-affected tissue
- Results in edema, hemorrhage, or both
- Risk higher with large, high-flow AVMs
- Staged embolization may reduce risk [BOARD-CORE]

## SECTION 5: REHABILITATION AFTER HEMORRHAGIC STROKE

### 5.1 General Principles

- Hemorrhagic stroke may recover to a greater degree than ischemic stroke of comparable initial deficit (some evidence suggests better neuroplastic potential) [BOARD-CORE]
- Early rehabilitation is critical but very early aggressive mobilization (within 24 hours, per AVERT trial) was associated with worse outcomes [BOARD-CORE]
- 2022 AHA ICH Guidelines: provision of care in specialized inpatient units with multidisciplinary teams recommended [BOARD-CORE]
- 2024 VA/DoD Stroke Rehabilitation Guidelines: comprehensive updated recommendations for stroke rehabilitation management [BOARD-LIKELY]

### 5.2 Acute Phase Rehabilitation Considerations

**DVT Prophylaxis:**
- Intermittent pneumatic compression devices: RECOMMENDED [BOARD-CORE]
- Compression stockings: NO benefit (2022 AHA guidelines) [BOARD-CORE]
- Prophylactic-dose heparin: does NOT significantly increase risk of recurrent intracranial bleeding (per 2024 VA/DoD guidelines), though evidence quality is low [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Dysphagia and Aspiration:**
- ~40% of acute stroke patients experience silent aspiration [BOARD-CORE]
- Bedside swallow assessment before oral intake [BOARD-CORE]
- VFSS or FEES if any doubt [BOARD-CORE]

**Positioning:**
- HOB elevated 30 degrees (also helps ICP management) [BOARD-CORE]
- Frequent turning for pressure injury prevention [BOARD-CORE]

**Bladder Management:**
- Initial hypotonic bladder with overflow incontinence common [BOARD-CORE]
- Remove Foley as soon as possible; intermittent catheterization preferred [BOARD-CORE]

### 5.3 Rehabilitation Admission Criteria

Per AHA guidelines, stroke survivors who qualify for and have access to inpatient rehabilitation facility (IRF) care should receive IRF care in preference to skilled nursing facility (SNF) care. [BOARD-CORE]

Criteria include:
- Stable neurological status
- Significant persisting neurological deficit
- Disability in at least 2 of: mobility, self-care, communication, bowel/bladder, swallowing
- Sufficient cognitive function to learn
- Physical ability to tolerate active program (at least 3 hours/day)
- Achievable therapeutic goals

### 5.4 Recovery Patterns

- 95% of stroke survivors reach best neurological level within 11 weeks (Copenhagen Stroke Study) [BOARD-CORE]
- Motor recovery reaches plateau after early phase of progressive improvement; minor additional measurable improvement after 6 months [BOARD-CORE]
- Arm weakness at onset and timing of motor recovery in the hand are key predictors [BOARD-CORE]
- Complete arm paralysis at onset with no measurable grasp by 4 weeks: poor prognosis for hand function [BOARD-CORE]
- Chronic stroke survivors can still improve motor function with intensive exercise programs (neuroplasticity) [BOARD-CORE]
- Aphasia recovery is slower and more prolonged than motor recovery, with improvement continuing >1 year [BOARD-CORE]

### 5.5 PM&R Specialist Role

Per AHA/ASA guidelines: physician consultation and follow-up with PM&R/physiatry specialists or neurologists with rehabilitation training is strongly recommended to address ongoing rehabilitation needs. This should begin during acute stroke hospitalization when possible. [BOARD-CORE]

### 5.6 Restarting Anticoagulation After ICH

- Highly individualized decision based on indication strength, ICH location, and rebleeding risk [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Generally considered after 4-8 weeks if strong indication exists (e.g., mechanical heart valve, high-risk AF) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Higher rebleeding risk with lobar ICH (especially CAA) vs deep ICH [BOARD-LIKELY]
- DOACs may be preferred over warfarin when restarting (lower ICH recurrence risk) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2022 AHA guidelines: insufficient evidence for firm recommendation on timing; shared decision-making recommended [BOARD-LIKELY]

## SECTION 6: DIAGNOSTIC STUDIES SUMMARY

### CT vs MRI in Hemorrhagic Stroke

| Study | Hemorrhage Appearance | Key Points |
|-------|----------------------|------------|
| Non-contrast CT | BRIGHT (white/hyperdense) | Study of choice; seen immediately; 100% sensitivity for ICH >= 1 cm |
| CT with contrast | Avoid in acute setting | Blood and contrast both appear white -- confusing |
| MRI T1 (acute) | Isointense to dark | Less sensitive acutely |
| MRI T2 (acute) | Dark (hypointense) | Deoxyhemoglobin |
| MRI GRE/SWI | Dark "blooming" | Most sensitive for microbleeds, old hemorrhage |
| CTA | Identifies spot sign | Predicts hematoma expansion |

**Board Pearl:** Non-contrast CT is the study of choice for suspected hemorrhagic stroke. MRI GRE/SWI is the best sequence for detecting old microbleeds (important for CAA diagnosis). [BOARD-CORE]

### Transcranial Doppler in SAH

- Used to monitor for vasospasm [BOARD-CORE]
- MCA velocities >120 cm/s suggest vasospasm [BOARD-CORE]
- MCA velocities >200 cm/s indicate severe vasospasm [BOARD-CORE]
- Lindegaard ratio (MCA/ICA velocity ratio) >3 suggests vasospasm; >6 indicates severe [BOARD-LIKELY]
