# SECTION 1: ANTIPLATELET THERAPY IN STROKE

## 1.1 Overview

Antiplatelet agents are the cornerstone of secondary prevention after ischemic stroke or TIA in patients without a cardioembolic source. Aspirin remains the first-line agent and should be started within 24-48 hours of stroke onset. For patients treated with tPA, aspirin administration is generally delayed until 24 hours later.

**[BOARD-CORE]** Aspirin 50-325 mg daily is equally effective across the dose range for secondary stroke prevention (AHA/ASA Class I). Higher doses increase bleeding risk without additional efficacy.

## 1.2 Individual Antiplatelet Agents

### Aspirin (ASA)
- **Class:** COX-1 inhibitor
- **Mechanism:** Irreversibly inhibits cyclooxygenase-1, blocking thromboxane A2 synthesis and platelet aggregation
- **Dosing:** 50-325 mg daily; initiate within 24-48 hours (wait 24h if tPA given)
- **Side effects:** GI bleeding, peptic ulcer, tinnitus at high doses
- **Key trials:** IST and CAST established efficacy
- **[BOARD-CORE]** First-line antiplatelet for noncardioembolic ischemic stroke/TIA

### Clopidogrel (Plavix)
- **Class:** P2Y12 receptor antagonist
- **Mechanism:** Irreversibly blocks P2Y12 ADP receptor on platelets
- **Dosing:** 75 mg daily
- **Side effects:** Bleeding, TTP (rare), reduced efficacy in CYP2C19 poor metabolizers
- **Key trial:** CAPRIE -- slightly superior to aspirin for combined vascular endpoints
- **[BOARD-CORE]** Alternative to aspirin; preferred in aspirin allergy

### Aspirin + Extended-Release Dipyridamole (Aggrenox)
- **Class:** Combination antiplatelet
- **Mechanism:** Dipyridamole inhibits phosphodiesterase and adenosine uptake; combined with low-dose aspirin
- **Dosing:** 25 mg ASA / 200 mg ER dipyridamole BID
- **Side effects:** Headache (up to 40%, primary reason for discontinuation), GI upset
- **Key trials:** ESPS-2 and ESPRIT showed superiority over aspirin alone; PRoFESS showed equivalence to clopidogrel
- **[BOARD-CORE]**

## 1.3 Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT) After Minor Stroke/TIA

**[BOARD-CORE]** Short-term DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel for 21 days) started within 24 hours of minor stroke (NIHSS <=3) or high-risk TIA reduces 90-day stroke recurrence. Long-term DAPT beyond 21-90 days is NOT recommended due to increased hemorrhagic risk (SPS3 and MATCH trials).

### CHANCE Trial (2013)
- Chinese study; minor stroke (NIHSS <=3) or high-risk TIA within 24 hours
- Protocol: ASA 75 mg + clopidogrel 75 mg (300 mg load day 1) for 21 days, then clopidogrel alone
- Result: 90-day stroke recurrence 8.2% (DAPT) vs 11.7% (ASA alone) -- 32% relative risk reduction
- No significant increase in hemorrhagic events with 21-day DAPT
- Predominantly Chinese population -- CYP2C19 polymorphisms may affect generalizability
- Established 21 days as the optimal DAPT duration

### POINT Trial (2018)
- International trial; minor stroke or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 >=4) within 12 hours
- Protocol: ASA + clopidogrel (600 mg load) for 90 days vs ASA alone
- Reduced major ischemic events (5.0% vs 6.5%) at 90 days
- BUT: increased major hemorrhage (0.9% vs 0.4%) with 90-day DAPT
- Post-hoc: benefit concentrated in first 21 days; bleeding risk increased after 21 days
- Supports CHANCE protocol of 21-day DAPT over 90-day DAPT

### NEW: THALES Trial (2020) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 11,016 patients with minor stroke (NIHSS <=5) or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 >=6)
- Compared aspirin + ticagrelor vs aspirin alone for 30 days
- Ticagrelor-aspirin reduced stroke and death compared to aspirin alone
- Associated with increased severe bleeding
- Ticagrelor-aspirin benefit predominantly in the first week
- ESO guideline: weak recommendation for 30-day ASA + ticagrelor as alternative to ASA + clopidogrel

### NEW: CHANCE-2 Trial (2021) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- CYP2C19 loss-of-function (LOF) carriers with minor stroke/TIA
- Ticagrelor-aspirin vs clopidogrel-aspirin for 21 days, then monotherapy to 90 days
- Risk of stroke at 90 days was modestly lower with ticagrelor than clopidogrel in CYP2C19 LOF carriers
- No difference in severe/moderate bleeding
- Published in NEJM 2021
- Clinical relevance: pharmacogenomic-guided antiplatelet selection gaining traction

### Antiplatelet Agent Comparison Table

| Agent | Key Trial | Relative Efficacy |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|
| Aspirin 50-325 mg | IST/CAST | Baseline comparator |
| Clopidogrel 75 mg | CAPRIE | Slightly superior to ASA |
| Aggrenox (ASA+DP) | ESPS-2, ESPRIT | Superior to ASA alone |
| ASA + Clopidogrel (21d) | CHANCE, POINT | Superior short-term for minor stroke/TIA |
| ASA + Ticagrelor (30d) | THALES | Alternative DAPT for minor stroke/TIA |
| ASA + Clopidogrel (long) | SPS3, MATCH | NOT recommended -- increased bleeding |

## 1.4 ESUS and Antiplatelets vs Anticoagulants

**[BOARD-CORE]** NAVIGATE ESUS (2018): Rivaroxaban 15 mg vs aspirin 100 mg for Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source -- NO benefit of rivaroxaban, increased bleeding. RE-SPECT ESUS similarly showed no benefit of dabigatran over aspirin. Anticoagulation is NOT indicated for ESUS unless a specific indication (e.g., occult AF) is found.

## 1.5 COMPASS Trial (2017)

**[BOARD-LIKELY]** Low-dose rivaroxaban 2.5 mg BID + ASA 100 mg vs ASA alone in stable atherosclerotic disease. Reduced major CV events by 24% and ischemic stroke by 42%. Increased major bleeding. NOT first-line stroke secondary prevention -- niche population with polyvascular disease.

# SECTION 2: ANTICOAGULATION FOR STROKE PREVENTION

## 2.1 Indications for Anticoagulation

**[BOARD-CORE]**
- Atrial fibrillation (valvular or nonvalvular)
- Mechanical heart valve (warfarin ONLY -- DOACs contraindicated)
- Left ventricular thrombus
- Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis
- Hypercoagulable states (antiphospholipid syndrome -- warfarin preferred)
- Aortic arch atheroma (controversial)

## 2.2 Warfarin (Coumadin)

- **Class:** Vitamin K antagonist
- **Mechanism:** Inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase, blocking factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C and S
- **Dosing:** Target INR 2.0-3.0 for AF; INR 2.5-3.5 for mechanical valves
- **Side effects:** Bleeding, skin necrosis (protein C deficiency), teratogenicity
- **[BOARD-CORE]** Target INR 2.0-3.0 reduces stroke risk by approximately 67% in AF. Warfarin remains the ONLY approved anticoagulant for mechanical heart valves and antiphospholipid syndrome. Skin necrosis occurs in first 3-6 days due to rapid protein C depletion.

## 2.3 Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)

### Dabigatran (Pradaxa)
- **Class:** Direct thrombin inhibitor
- **Trial:** RE-LY -- 150 mg BID superior to warfarin for stroke prevention with similar major bleeding
- **Dosing:** 150 mg BID; 75 mg BID for CrCl 15-30 mL/min; contraindicated if CrCl <15
- **Side effects:** GI bleeding (higher than warfarin), dyspepsia
- **Reversal:** Idarucizumab (Praxbind)
- **[BOARD-CORE]**

### Rivaroxaban (Xarelto)
- **Class:** Factor Xa inhibitor
- **Trial:** ROCKET-AF -- noninferior to warfarin
- **Dosing:** 20 mg daily with evening meal; 15 mg for CrCl 15-50
- **Reversal:** Andexanet alfa (Andexxa)
- **[BOARD-CORE]**

### Apixaban (Eliquis)
- **Class:** Factor Xa inhibitor
- **Trial:** ARISTOTLE -- SUPERIOR to warfarin for stroke prevention AND major bleeding reduction AND mortality
- **Dosing:** 5 mg BID; reduce to 2.5 mg BID if >=2 of: age >=80, weight <=60 kg, Cr >=1.5
- **Side effects:** Lowest major bleeding rate among DOACs
- **[BOARD-CORE]** Often considered the preferred DOAC for AF

### Edoxaban (Savaysa)
- **Class:** Factor Xa inhibitor
- **Trial:** ENGAGE AF-TIMI 48 -- noninferior to warfarin
- **Dosing:** 60 mg daily
- **Reversal:** Andexanet alfa
- **[BOARD-CORE]**

### DOAC Comparison Table

| Drug | Trial | Dosing | vs Warfarin | Reversal |
|------|-------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Dabigatran | RE-LY | 150 mg BID | Superior efficacy | Idarucizumab |
| Rivaroxaban | ROCKET-AF | 20 mg daily | Noninferior | Andexanet alfa |
| Apixaban | ARISTOTLE | 5 mg BID | Superior efficacy + safety | Andexanet alfa |
| Edoxaban | ENGAGE AF | 60 mg daily | Noninferior | Andexanet alfa |

### NEW: Meta-Analysis of DOACs vs Warfarin (Updated Data) [BOARD-CORE]
- Patient-level network meta-analysis of 71,683 participants across all four DOAC trials
- DOACs reduce stroke/systemic embolism by 19% vs warfarin (RR 0.81)
- ALL DOACs significantly reduce hemorrhagic stroke vs warfarin
- Apixaban and edoxaban significantly reduce major bleeding vs warfarin
- In low-risk AF patients, DOACs associated with lower stroke, bleeding, ICH, and mortality vs warfarin
- Real-world data confirms trial findings across diverse populations

## 2.4 Timing of Anticoagulation After Stroke

### The "1-3-6-12 Day Rule" [BOARD-CORE]

| Infarct Size | Start Anticoagulation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| TIA (no infarct) | Day 1 | No hemorrhagic transformation risk |
| Small (<1.5 cm) | Day 3 | Low HT risk |
| Moderate | Day 6 | Balance HT and recurrence risk |
| Large (>1/3 MCA) | Day 12-14 | High HT risk; repeat imaging first |

### NEW: ELAN Trial (2023) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Randomized trial at 103 sites in 15 countries
- Early DOAC initiation (within 48h for minor/moderate stroke, day 6-7 for major) vs later initiation (day 3-4 for minor, day 6-7 for moderate, day 12-14 for major)
- Early anticoagulation showed similar safety and efficacy to later initiation
- Composite of stroke, embolism, hemorrhage, or death at 30 days: estimated 2.8 percentage points lower to 0.5 points higher with early DOACs
- Supports earlier DOAC initiation than traditional 1-3-6-12 rule

### NEW: OPTIMAS Trial (2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Complementary trial to ELAN examining early vs delayed anticoagulation
- Confirmed safety of early anticoagulation with DOACs after ischemic stroke with AF
- Together with ELAN, shifting practice toward earlier DOAC initiation

## 2.5 Anticoagulant Reversal Agents

### Reversal Agent Table [BOARD-CORE]

| Anticoagulant | Reversal Agent | Dose/Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Warfarin | 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) + Vitamin K | PCC: 25-50 units/kg based on INR; Vit K 10 mg IV for sustained reversal; FFP if PCC unavailable |
| Dabigatran | Idarucizumab (Praxbind) | 5 g IV (two 2.5 g vials); immediate complete reversal; does NOT reverse factor Xa inhibitors |
| Rivaroxaban/Apixaban/Edoxaban | Andexanet alfa (Andexxa) | Low-dose (400 mg bolus + 4 mg/min x 2h) or high-dose (800 mg bolus + 8 mg/min x 2h); very expensive |
| All DOACs (if no specific agent) | 4-factor PCC (off-label) | 50 units/kg IV; less effective but widely available |
| Heparin (UFH) | Protamine sulfate | 1 mg per 100 units heparin given in last 2-3h; max 50 mg |
| LMWH (enoxaparin) | Protamine sulfate (partial) | 1 mg per 1 mg enoxaparin; only approximately 60% reversal |

### Reversal Agent Specificity Pearl [BOARD-CORE]
Idarucizumab is SPECIFIC for dabigatran only -- monoclonal antibody fragment binding dabigatran with 350x greater affinity than thrombin. Andexanet alfa reverses ALL factor Xa inhibitors by acting as a decoy factor Xa. PCC is the universal backup.

### NEW: Updated Reversal Agent Evidence (2020-2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]

**ANNEXA-4 Trial Updates:**
- Good or excellent hemostasis achieved in 83% of patients within 12 hours
- Thrombotic events: 2.6% within 3 days, 11% within 30 days of andexanet alfa
- Thrombotic complication rate approximately 10-14% with andexanet alfa -- consistently higher than idarucizumab (4-5%) or PCC (3-8%)

**Comparative Meta-Analysis:**
- Hemostatic efficacy similar: 4PCC (80.1%), idarucizumab (76.7%), andexanet alfa (80.7%)
- Andexanet alfa has highest thrombotic risk (VTE 5.5%, ATE 5.0%) vs PCC (VTE 2.4%, ATE 2.2%) and idarucizumab (VTE 1.7%, ATE 3.3%)
- Cost barrier: andexanet alfa high dose approximately $50,000 in USA -- 5-10x more than PCC or idarucizumab
- Many hospitals do not stock andexanet alfa; PCC remains widely available backup

**ISTH 2024 Guidance:**
- Updated recommendations for DOAC reversal now published
- PCC increasingly recognized as reasonable first-line option when specific agents unavailable

# SECTION 3: STATIN THERAPY AFTER STROKE

## 3.1 Overview

**[BOARD-CORE]** High-intensity statin therapy is recommended for ALL patients with atherosclerotic ischemic stroke or TIA regardless of baseline LDL. Do NOT stop statins during acute stroke hospitalization -- discontinuation is associated with a 4.7-fold increased risk of death or dependency at 90 days.

## 3.2 Atorvastatin (Lipitor)

- **Class:** HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor
- **Mechanism:** Competitively inhibits HMG-CoA reductase, upregulates hepatic LDL receptors
- **Dosing:** 40-80 mg daily (high-intensity)
- **Side effects:** Myalgia, hepatotoxicity (rare), rhabdomyolysis (rare), new-onset diabetes
- **[BOARD-CORE]** SPARCL trial: Atorvastatin 80 mg reduced recurrent stroke by 16% and major CV events by 20%. Post-hoc analysis showed LDL <70 reduced recurrent stroke by 28% compared to LDL >=100.

## 3.3 Pleiotropic Effects of Statins [BOARD-CORE]

- Atherosclerotic plaque stabilization (increase fibrous cap, reduce lipid core)
- Improved endothelial function (upregulate eNOS, improve NO bioavailability)
- Anti-inflammatory (reduce CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha; inhibit ICAM-1, VCAM-1)
- Antithrombotic (reduce tissue factor, decrease platelet reactivity, lower PAI-1)
- Antioxidant (reduce LDL oxidation)
- Neuroprotective (promote BDNF, enhance angiogenesis in ischemic penumbra)
- These effects occur rapidly (days to weeks), before significant LDL reduction

## 3.4 Statin Intensity Classification

| Intensity | Agent/Dose | Expected LDL Reduction |
|---|---|---|
| High | Atorvastatin 40-80 mg | >=50% |
| High | Rosuvastatin 20-40 mg | >=50% |
| Moderate | Atorvastatin 10-20 mg | 30-49% |
| Moderate | Simvastatin 20-40 mg | 30-49% |
| Low | Simvastatin 10 mg | <30% |
| Low | Pravastatin 10-20 mg | <30% |

## 3.5 NEW: Treat Stroke to Target (TST) Trial (NEJM 2020) [BOARD-CORE]

- 2,860 patients with atherosclerotic ischemic stroke or TIA
- Randomized to LDL target <70 mg/dL vs 90-110 mg/dL
- Mean achieved LDL: 65 vs 96 mg/dL
- Primary composite endpoint: 8.5% in lower-target group vs 10.9% in higher-target group (HR 0.78, P=0.04)
- No increase in ICH or new-onset diabetes with lower target
- Statin plus ezetimibe used to achieve targets when needed
- **[BOARD-CORE]** AHA/ASA guidelines now support LDL target <70 mg/dL for atherosclerotic stroke

## 3.6 Do NOT Stop Statins After Stroke Pearl [BOARD-CORE]

Discontinuing statins during acute hospitalization is associated with 4.7-fold increased risk of death or dependency at 90 days. Acute withdrawal of pleiotropic effects (loss of endothelial protection, rebound inflammation) likely contributes. Continue statins throughout hospitalization. If not already on a statin, initiate high-intensity therapy during hospitalization.

# SECTION 4: BLOOD PRESSURE MANAGEMENT IN SECONDARY STROKE PREVENTION

## 4.1 Overview

**[BOARD-CORE]** Hypertension is the single most important modifiable risk factor for stroke. Target <130/80 mmHg for most stroke/TIA patients.

## 4.2 Key Trials and Evidence

### PROGRESS Trial [BOARD-CORE]
- Perindopril + indapamide reduced recurrent stroke by 43%
- ACE inhibitors and thiazide diuretics have best evidence

### SPS3 Trial (Lacunar Stroke) [BOARD-CORE]
- BP arm: target SBP <130 vs 130-149 mmHg in recent lacunar stroke
- Lower target: non-significant 19% reduction in recurrent stroke
- Significant 63% reduction in ICH with lower target
- Antiplatelet arm: dual antiplatelet did NOT reduce recurrence vs ASA alone and INCREASED bleeding/mortality
- **Pearl:** For lacunar stroke, aggressive BP control is key; long-term DAPT is harmful

### NEW: Meta-Analysis of 4 RCTs (RESPECT, PAST-BP, SPS3, PODCAST) [BOARD-CORE]
- Intensive BP control (SBP <120-130) vs standard (<140-150) in patients with prior cerebrovascular disease
- Significant 22% reduction in recurrent stroke (RR 0.78)
- J-shaped relationship: lowest risk at approximately SBP 124 mmHg, DBP 67 mmHg
- SBP <120 mmHg should be avoided

### NEW: 2021 AHA/ASA Guidelines Update [BOARD-CORE]
- Target BP <130/80 mmHg for secondary stroke prevention
- Resume antihypertensives after acute phase (typically 24-72 hours)
- Avoid aggressive lowering in first 24-48 hours

### NEW: ESPRIT Trial (2025) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Intensive SBP arm (mean 119 mmHg) vs standard (mean 135 mmHg)
- Stroke occurred in 4.7% (intensive) vs 5.4% (standard)
- Benefit mainly driven by reduction in hemorrhagic stroke
- Supports intensive BP control but caution against excessive lowering

### NEW: 2025 AHA/ACC Guideline Comparison [BOARD-EMERGING]
- 2023 ESH: target <130/80 mmHg, avoid <120 mmHg
- 2024 ESC: target SBP 120-129 mmHg
- 2025 AHA/ACC/multi-society: updated BP management guidance
- Convergence toward lower targets but floor effect recognized

# SECTION 5: MEDICATIONS FOR STROKE RECOVERY AND REHABILITATION

## 5.1 SSRIs for Motor Recovery [BOARD-CORE]

### FLAME Trial (2011)
- 118 patients with ischemic stroke; fluoxetine started days 5-10 post-onset
- Enhanced motor recovery and increased functional independence at 3 months
- Generated excitement for SSRIs as motor recovery agents

### NEW: FOCUS Trial (2019) [BOARD-CORE]
- Large pragmatic RCT
- Fluoxetine 20 mg daily for 6 months after acute stroke
- NO benefit on functional outcome at 6 months
- Reduced depression but increased risk of bone fracture

### NEW: AFFINITY Trial (2020) [BOARD-CORE]
- Fluoxetine 20 mg daily for 6 months
- NO improvement in functional outcome
- Increased risk of falls, bone fractures, and seizures
- Externally validates FOCUS results in ethnically diverse population

### NEW: EFFECTS Trial (2020) [BOARD-CORE]
- Fluoxetine 20 mg daily for 6 months
- NO effect on functional outcome up to 1 year
- Fewer new depression diagnoses but increased bone fractures

### NEW: Meta-Analysis of FOCUS/AFFINITY/EFFECTS (n=3,356) [BOARD-CORE]
- No effect of SSRIs on functional independence or disability score
- SSRIs should NOT be prescribed routinely to improve motor recovery -- they are ineffective and increase serious adverse events
- SSRIs remain appropriate for treating post-stroke depression specifically

## 5.2 Post-Stroke Depression Pharmacotherapy [BOARD-CORE]

- Affects approximately one third of stroke survivors
- Associated with poor recovery, recurrent vascular events, reduced quality of life, and increased mortality
- **First-line treatment:** SSRIs (most commonly fluoxetine, sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram)
- **Alternative:** SNRIs (especially when anxiety prominent), mirtazapine, milnacipran
- **Prevention:** RCTs show antidepressants significantly decrease PSD incidence vs placebo; 12-month regimens most effective (mirtazapine, milnacipran, sertraline)
- **Early treatment with antidepressants appears to enhance physical and cognitive recovery** and may increase survival up to 10 years
- **Emotional lability (pseudobulbar affect):** Responds to dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta) or SSRIs
- **[BOARD-CORE]** Safety note: SSRIs with dual antiplatelet therapy may increase hemorrhagic stroke risk by 29% -- use caution with concurrent DAPT

## 5.3 Anti-Spasticity Medications [BOARD-CORE]

### Overview
- Spasticity develops days to weeks after stroke, follows classic UE flexor and LE extensor patterns
- Treatment: stretching, splints/orthosis, serial casting, electrical stimulation, cold modalities, then medications

### Oral Medications (Limited Efficacy, Significant Side Effects)
- **Baclofen:** GABA-B agonist; sedation, weakness, cognitive impairment; limited evidence in stroke
- **Tizanidine:** Alpha-2 agonist; sedation, dry mouth, hepatotoxicity
- **Dantrolene:** Acts peripherally on skeletal muscle; hepatotoxicity risk
- **Benzodiazepines (diazepam):** GABA-A agonist; sedation, cognitive impairment -- generally avoided in stroke rehab due to impairment of neuroplasticity
- All oral agents: modest effects with limiting side effects (fatigue, somnolence, cognitive impairment)

### Chemodenervation
- **Botulinum toxin type A (BoNT-A):** First-line focal treatment for spasticity per guidelines
- Particularly useful for UE wrist/finger flexors, ankle invertors in LE
- Injections required every 3-4 months
- **Phenol/alcohol neurolysis:** For large muscle groups; limited by pain, dysesthesia

### Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB) Pump
- Limited experience in stroke; may improve walking speed and functional mobility
- Uncommonly used in stroke due to medical comorbidities

### NEW: Baclotox Trial (Ongoing 2025) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Multicenter RCT comparing early botulinum toxin vs baclofen within 2 months post-stroke
- Hypothesis: BoNT-A is more supportive of motor recovery than baclofen
- Results pending

## 5.4 Neuroprotective Agents [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Historical Context
- No effective neuroprotective agent has been established for clinical use
- Clinical trials of glutamate receptor antagonists and free radical scavengers have been disappointing in humans

### NEW: Nerinetide (NA-1) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- PSD-95 inhibitor preventing postsynaptic density protein 95 from coupling to NMDA receptors
- **ESCAPE-NA1 trial (2020):** In patients not receiving alteplase, nerinetide improved functional outcomes (59.3% vs 49.8% achieving mRS 0-2); no benefit in alteplase-treated patients (alteplase cleaves nerinetide)
- **2025 meta-analysis (3 RCTs, n=2,462):** Modest reduction in mortality (RR 0.86), some benefit in infarct size and stroke worsening; no clear effect on overall functional recovery
- **ACT-42 trial** currently in progress for PSD-95 inhibition
- Nerinetide remains investigational; may benefit patients treated with thrombectomy without alteplase

### Other Investigational Agents
- **Edaravone:** Free radical scavenger; limited stroke-specific evidence
- **Remote ischemic conditioning:** Non-pharmacological neuroprotective strategy under investigation

## 5.5 Medications That May Impair Stroke Recovery [BOARD-CORE]

Animal studies and clinical data suggest certain centrally acting drugs negatively affect motor recovery and neuroplasticity:

**Medications to AVOID or use with caution during stroke rehabilitation:**
- **Antihypertensives:** Clonidine, prazosin (central alpha-2 agonists)
- **Neuroleptics/antipsychotics:** Haloperidol and other dopamine receptor antagonists
- **Benzodiazepines:** Diazepam, lorazepam (impair neuroplasticity; use alternatives when possible)
- **Anticonvulsants:** Phenytoin, phenobarbital (if seizure prophylaxis needed, consider levetiracetam)

**Medications generally SAFE during stroke rehabilitation:**
- SSRIs (for depression, not routine motor recovery)
- Levetiracetam (if anticonvulsant needed)
- Modafinil, methylphenidate (for post-stroke fatigue/arousal)

## 5.6 Central Post-Stroke Pain [BOARD-CORE]

- Most commonly from thalamic lesions (thalamic pain syndrome)
- Burning, unremitting pain affecting contralateral side
- Poorly responsive to opioid analgesics
- **Treatment:** Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline -- best studied), gabapentin, lamotrigine, pregabalin
- **[BOARD-CORE]** Anticonvulsants (gabapentin, lamotrigine) show some efficacy as alternatives to TCAs

# SECTION 6: SECONDARY PREVENTION -- SURGICAL AND PROCEDURAL

## 6.1 Carotid Endarterectomy (CEA) [BOARD-CORE]

- Symptomatic stenosis >70%: Strong benefit (NASCET NNT = 6)
- Symptomatic stenosis 50-69%: Moderate benefit (NNT = 22)
- Symptomatic stenosis <50%: No benefit
- Asymptomatic stenosis >60%: Modest benefit if perioperative risk <3%
- Best performed within 2 weeks of index event
- Perioperative stroke/death rate must be <6% for symptomatic, <3% for asymptomatic

## 6.2 Carotid Artery Stenting (CAS) [BOARD-CORE]

- Alternative to CEA when surgical risk is high
- CREST trial: Similar long-term outcomes overall
- CAS: Higher periprocedural stroke risk
- CEA: Higher periprocedural MI risk, cranial nerve injury (4.7%)
- Age <70: CAS may be preferred; Age >70: CEA preferred

## 6.3 PFO Closure [BOARD-CORE]

- RESPECT, CLOSE, REDUCE trials: PFO closure superior to medical therapy
- Indicated for age <60 with cryptogenic stroke and moderate-to-large shunt
- Atrial septal aneurysm increases benefit
- RoPE score identifies patients most likely to benefit
- Post-procedure: new-onset AF is most common complication

# SECTION 7: DVT PROPHYLAXIS IN STROKE [BOARD-CORE]

- DVT occurs in 20-75% of untreated stroke survivors
- 60-75% in affected extremity; 25% proximal DVT; 1-2% PE
- **Ischemic stroke:** Prophylactic-dose subcutaneous heparin (LMWH preferred over UFH) for duration of acute and rehab stay or until mobilized
- **ICH:** LMWH started between days 2-4 over no prophylaxis
- **Both:** Intermittent pneumatic compression during acute hospitalization
- Elastic compression stockings are NOT useful

# SECTION 8: ACUTE STROKE PHARMACOTHERAPY [BOARD-CORE]

## 8.1 Thrombolysis
- IV tPA (alteplase) within 4.5 hours of onset
- NINDS trial: 30% more likely to have minimal/no disability at 3 months
- Extended window criteria (3-4.5h): age <80, no diabetes+prior stroke, NIHSS <=25, not on anticoagulants

## 8.2 BP Management in Acute Stroke
- Ischemic: Treat if SBP >220 or DBP >120 (non-thrombolytic candidates)
- Thrombolytic candidates: Treat if SBP >185 or DBP >110
- IV labetalol, nicardipine, clevidipine preferred
- Hemorrhagic: Treat if SBP >180 or DBP >105; IV labetalol preferred (no cerebral vasodilation/ICP increase)

## 8.3 Nimodipine for SAH [BOARD-CORE]
- Calcium channel blocker decreasing cerebral vasospasm after SAH
- Initiated within 96 hours of hemorrhage onset
- Dosing: 60 mg PO every 4 hours for 21 days
- Reduces incidence of permanent neurologic damage and death

## 8.4 Corticosteroids
- No utility in ischemic stroke
- May worsen prognosis due to hyperglycemia
