## PART 1: CORE APP CONTENT (Current)

### 1.1 Ischemic Stroke Overview
- Ischemic stroke accounts for approximately 87% of all strokes
- Caused by occlusion of a cerebral artery resulting in insufficient blood flow
- Central core of irreversible injury surrounded by potentially salvageable penumbra

### 1.2 Pathophysiology of Ischemia
- Normal CBF: 50-60 mL/100g/min
- Ischemic penumbra: CBF 10-20 mL/100g/min (electrically silent but viable)
- Infarct core: CBF <10 mL/100g/min (irreversible injury within minutes)
- Ischemic cascade: energy failure, glutamate excitotoxicity, calcium influx, free radical damage, apoptosis
- Penumbra can survive hours depending on collateral flow ("time is brain")
- ~1.9 million neurons die every minute during untreated large vessel occlusion
- "Mismatch" between infarct core and penumbra guides treatment beyond standard time windows

### 1.3 TOAST Classification of Ischemic Stroke [BOARD-CORE]
| Subtype | Percentage | Description |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| Large artery atherosclerosis | ~20-25% | Stenosis >50% of major cerebral artery |
| Cardioembolism | ~20-25% | Cardiac source (AFib, valvular disease, etc.) |
| Small vessel occlusion (lacunar) | ~20-25% | Small deep infarct <15 mm |
| Other determined etiology | ~5% | Dissection, vasculitis, hypercoagulable state |
| Undetermined (cryptogenic) | ~25-30% | No cause identified or multiple causes |

Board Pearl: TOAST guides treatment -- large artery gets antiplatelet + statin + possible CEA/stenting; cardioembolism gets anticoagulation; small vessel gets antiplatelet + aggressive BP control; cryptogenic requires extended cardiac monitoring.

### 1.4 Thrombotic Stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- 48% of ischemic strokes
- Classically occurs during sleep (~48% onset during sleep -- patient wakes with deficit)
- Stuttering or stepwise progression over hours to days ("stroke-in-evolution")
- Often preceded by TIA(s) in same vascular territory
- Most commonly involves MCA, ICA bifurcation, or vertebrobasilar system
- Pathophysiology: atherosclerotic plaque rupture/erosion at bifurcations/curves, platelet adhesion, thrombus formation, progressive luminal narrowing

### 1.5 Embolic Stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- 26% of ischemic strokes
- Sudden onset with MAXIMAL deficit at presentation (no stuttering)
- Occurs during activity (vs. thrombotic during sleep)
- Higher risk of hemorrhagic transformation
- 75% of cardiac emboli go to the brain (preferentially via left carotid/MCA)
- MCA is most commonly affected vessel

### 1.6 Thrombotic vs. Embolic -- High-Yield Comparison [BOARD-CORE]
| Feature | Thrombotic | Embolic |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Onset | Gradual, stuttering, during sleep | Sudden, maximal at onset, during activity |
| Preceding TIAs | Common, same territory | Less common |
| Deficit | Stepwise worsening | Maximal at onset |
| Hemorrhagic transformation | Less common | More common |
| Multiple territories | Usually single | May affect multiple |
| Underlying cause | Local atherosclerosis | Distant source |

### 1.7 Sources of Emboli [BOARD-CORE]
**Cardiac sources (~20-30% of all ischemic strokes):**
- Atrial fibrillation (most common cardiac source; risk by CHA2DS2-VASc; DOACs preferred)
- Left ventricular thrombus (post-MI, especially anterior wall; risk highest first 3 months)
- Rheumatic mitral stenosis (most common valvular cause; warfarin required, DOACs not validated)
- Prosthetic mechanical valve (lifelong warfarin; DOACs contraindicated)
- Infective endocarditis (anticoagulation may worsen mycotic aneurysm bleeding)
- PFO with atrial septal aneurysm (consider device closure in cryptogenic stroke age <60)
- Atrial myxoma (most common primary cardiac tumor)
- Dilated cardiomyopathy (anticoagulation if EF <20% or documented thrombus)

**Arterial sources (artery-to-artery embolism):**
- Carotid atherosclerotic plaque
- Aortic arch atheromas
- Arterial dissection

### 1.8 Lacunar Stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- Small deep infarcts <15 mm from occlusion of single penetrating arteriole
- 13-20% of ischemic strokes
- Strong association with chronic HTN and diabetes
- Located in: basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, pons, corona radiata
- HALLMARK: NO cortical signs (no aphasia, neglect, visual field cuts, seizures)
- Generally better prognosis, lower mortality (~2% at 30 days)

### 1.9 Classic Lacunar Syndromes [BOARD-CORE]
| Syndrome | Location | Presentation |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| Pure motor hemiparesis (MOST COMMON) | Posterior limb internal capsule or basis pontis | Face/arm/leg weakness equally, no sensory loss |
| Pure sensory stroke | VPL thalamus | Numbness/paresthesias face/arm/leg, no motor deficit |
| Ataxic hemiparesis | Posterior limb IC or pons | Cerebellar ataxia + hemiparesis same side |
| Dysarthria-clumsy hand | Basis pontis or genu IC | Dysarthria, dysphagia, hand weakness/clumsiness |
| Sensorimotor stroke | Thalamocapsular region | Combined motor + sensory; largest lacunar type |

Board Pearl: Pure motor hemiparesis is #1 tested lacunar syndrome. Face/arm/leg affected EQUALLY (unlike MCA where face/arm > leg). NO sensory loss.

### 1.10 Carotid Artery Dissection [BOARD-CORE]
- ~20% of strokes in patients <45 years
- Causes: trauma (even minor -- chiropractic manipulation), connective tissue disorders (Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan, FMD), spontaneous
- Classic triad: (1) ipsilateral head/neck/face pain, (2) ipsilateral partial Horner syndrome (miosis, ptosis WITHOUT anhidrosis), (3) contralateral hemiparesis
- Diagnosis: CTA, MRA, or MRI with fat-saturated T1 (intramural hematoma = bright crescent)
- Treatment: anticoagulation or antiplatelet for 3-6 months; most heal spontaneously

### 1.11 Embolic Shower [BOARD-CORE]
- Simultaneous infarcts in multiple vascular territories
- Most common cause: cardiac embolism (AF, endocarditis, mural thrombus)
- Multiple territory involvement rules out single-vessel thrombotic etiology
- Endocarditis clue: multiple small infarcts + fever + new murmur = septic emboli

### 1.12 Cryptogenic Stroke Workup [BOARD-CORE]
- 25-30% of ischemic strokes
- ESUS = non-lacunar infarct with no source after standard workup
- Extended cardiac monitoring (30-day or ILR) detects occult AF in 10-25%
- TEE for PFO, atrial septal aneurysm, aortic arch atheroma
- Hypercoagulable workup in young patients: antiphospholipid syndrome, Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency
- Bubble study for right-to-left shunt (PFO)

## PART 2: TEXTBOOK CONTENT (Cuccurullo & DeLisa)

### 2.1 Epidemiology (Cuccurullo)
- AHA estimates 795,000 strokes annually: 610,000 new + 185,000 recurrent
- 5th leading cause of death in US
- ~6.8 million stroke survivors in US
- 10.5% cumulative mortality at 30 days; 21.2% at 1 year; 58.4% at 24 years

### 2.2 Risk Factors (Cuccurullo) [BOARD-CORE]
**Nonmodifiable:** Age (doubles each decade after 55), Sex (male > female), Race (African Americans 2x), Family history

**Modifiable:**
- HTN: most important modifiable risk factor (7-fold increased risk for both ischemic and hemorrhagic); SBP <130 reduces stroke by 20% and ICH by 2/3
- History TIA/prior stroke: 5% stroke within 1 month, 14% within 1 year if untreated
- Heart disease: CHF and CAD increase risk 2-fold; valvular disease and arrhythmias increase embolic risk
- Atrial fibrillation: independently increases risk 5-fold throughout all ages
- Diabetes: >3 years duration increases ischemic stroke rate
- Smoking: 2-4x increased risk
- Carotid stenosis: CEA beneficial 50-69% symptomatic (ARR 4.6%) and 70-99% symptomatic (ARR 16%)
- Hyperlipidemia: statins reduce stroke risk ~30%

### 2.3 Neuroanatomic Localization (Cuccurullo) [BOARD-CORE]

**Internal Carotid Artery:**
- Most variable syndrome; 30-40% of ICA occlusions are asymptomatic
- Amaurosis fugax (transient monocular blindness) in ~25% before stroke
- Variable presentation depending on collateral circulation

**Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA):**
- Supplies lateral aspect of hemisphere
- Lenticulostriate arteries supply basal ganglia and internal capsule
- Superior division: Rolandic/pre-Rolandic areas; face/arm > leg; Broca's aphasia (dominant); neglect/apraxia (nondominant)
- Inferior division: lateral temporal/inferior parietal; Wernicke's aphasia (left); visual neglect (right)
- Most common cause of superior division occlusion is embolus

**Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA):**
- Contralateral leg weakness and sensory loss (leg > arm, opposite of MCA)
- Urinary incontinence with grasp reflex and paratonic rigidity (Gegenhalten)
- Transcortical motor aphasia if left side
- Gait apraxia
- Bilateral ACA occlusion: abulia, akinetic mutism, paraplegia

**Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA):**
- Contralateral homonymous hemianopia (visual field cuts)
- Bilateral: cortical blindness with denial = Anton syndrome
- Alexia without agraphia, prosopagnosia
- Weber syndrome (midbrain): ipsilateral CN3 palsy + contralateral hemiplegia

### 2.4 Vertebrobasilar Syndromes (Cuccurullo) [BOARD-CORE]

**Wallenberg Syndrome (Lateral Medullary):**
- PICA/vertebral artery occlusion
- Ipsilateral: Horner's (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis), facial pain/temperature loss, cerebellar ataxia
- Contralateral: body pain/temperature loss
- Dysphagia, dysarthria, hoarseness, vertigo, nystagmus
- NO facial or extremity weakness

**Benedikt Syndrome (Red Nucleus/Tegmentum of Midbrain):**
- Ipsilateral CN3 palsy with mydriasis
- Contralateral hyperkinesia (ataxia, tremor, chorea, athetosis)

**Weber Syndrome (Base of Midbrain):**
- Ipsilateral CN3 palsy
- Contralateral hemiplegia

**Millard-Gubler Syndrome (Base of Pons):**
- Ipsilateral CN6 and CN7 palsies
- Contralateral hemiplegia

**Locked-in Syndrome:**
- Bilateral ventral pons lesion (basilar artery occlusion)
- Tetraparesis, preserved consciousness
- Can only move eyes vertically and blink

**Basilar Artery Occlusion:**
- May cause internuclear ophthalmoplegia, conjugate gaze palsy, ocular bobbing
- Can be fatal; thrombectomy now standard of care

### 2.5 Lacunar Syndromes -- Detailed (Cuccurullo Table 1-4) [BOARD-CORE]
| Syndrome | Anatomical Location |
|----------|-------------------|
| Pure motor hemiparesis | Posterior limb IC (lenticulostriate), corona radiata, pons |
| Pure sensory stroke | Thalamus (ventrolateral), parietal white matter |
| Dysarthria-clumsy hand | Basis pontis, internal capsule (anterior limb) |
| Sensorimotor stroke | Junction IC and thalamus |
| Ataxia and leg paralysis | Pons, midbrain, IC, cerebellum, corona radiata |
| Hemichorea-hemiballismus | Head of caudate, thalamus, subthalamic nucleus |

### 2.6 Diagnostic Studies (Cuccurullo) [BOARD-CORE]
| Study | Acute Infarct | Hemorrhage |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| CT | DARK (hypodense); may be normal first 6-12h; best 3-4 days post | BRIGHT (hyperdense); seen immediately |
| MRI DWI | Most sensitive; detects within minutes | Hypointense on T1 and T2 acutely |

- Head CT without contrast is study of choice when hemorrhage suspected
- MRI DWI is most sensitive and specific for acute ischemic infarct
- Carotid ultrasound for screening; CTA/MRA for detailed vessel imaging

### 2.7 Acute Stroke Management (DeLisa) [BOARD-CORE]
- IV tPA (alteplase) most effective when given as early as possible; efficacy up to 4.5 hours
- Mechanical thrombectomy effective within first 6 hours for proximal anterior circulation LVOs
- Neuroprotection agents have not been successful in clinical trials to date
- Control of blood pressure, fever, and hyperglycemia improves outcome

### 2.8 Secondary Prevention (DeLisa) [BOARD-CORE]
- Aspirin 50-325 mg reduces stroke ~25%
- Clopidogrel: comparable efficacy to aspirin; different mechanism
- Dual antiplatelet (aspirin + clopidogrel) benefits early after stroke but increases hemorrhage long-term
- DOACs (dabigatran, apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban) for AF -- effective alternatives to warfarin
- Statins reduce recurrent stroke regardless of lipid levels
- CEA for symptomatic carotid stenosis >=70%; consider for 50-69% if symptomatic
- CEA for asymptomatic stenosis 60-99% (less compelling evidence)
- CAS: higher periprocedural risk in patients >70; similar outcomes in younger patients

### 2.9 Rehabilitation (DeLisa) [BOARD-CORE]
- Stroke units improve outcomes vs general medical units
- AVERT trial: very early aggressive mobilization (<24h) associated with worse outcomes
- DVT prophylaxis essential for all hemiplegic patients
- Dysphagia screening before oral intake (40% have silent aspiration)
- 88% have hemiparesis; arm more involved than leg (MCA territory predominance)
- 95% reach best neurologic level within 11 weeks (Copenhagen Stroke Study)
- Arm: complete paralysis at onset with no grasp by 4 weeks = poor prognosis
- Language recovery slower than motor; may continue >1 year
- IRF recommended over SNF for qualified patients (AHA guideline)

## PART 3: NEW DEVELOPMENTS (2020-2026) -- VERIFIED RESEARCH

### 3.1 Tenecteplase: FDA Approval and Paradigm Shift [BOARD-LIKELY]

**FDA Approval (March 2025):** Tenecteplase (TNKase) received FDA approval for acute ischemic stroke -- the first new stroke thrombolytic approved in nearly 30 years.

**Key Advantages Over Alteplase:**
- Single 5-second IV bolus vs. alteplase's 1-hour infusion
- Easier to administer in emergency/prehospital settings
- Higher fibrin specificity and longer half-life

**Supporting Trials:**
- AcT Trial: Large multi-center non-inferiority study; tenecteplase non-inferior to alteplase
- ATTEST-2 Trial: Tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg non-inferior to alteplase 0.9 mg/kg within 4.5h
- ORIGINAL Trial: Confirmed non-inferiority in Chinese population

**2026 AHA/ASA Guidelines:** Tenecteplase now recommended alongside alteplase as an acceptable IV thrombolytic agent.

Sources: FDA approval press release (Genentech, March 2025); ATTEST-2 (Lancet Neurology 2024); ORIGINAL (JAMA 2024); 2026 AHA/ASA Acute Ischemic Stroke Guideline (Stroke 2026)

### 3.2 2026 AHA/ASA Acute Ischemic Stroke Guideline [BOARD-LIKELY]

The 2026 Guideline replaces the 2018/2019 guidelines. Key updates:

**Mobile Stroke Units (NEW Class 1 recommendation):**
- Use MSUs for suspected AIS patients eligible for thrombolytics
- Based on B_PROUD and BEST-MSU trials
- Improves time-to-treatment and functional outcomes

**Extended Thrombectomy Window (NEW Class 1):**
- Thrombectomy for anterior LVO 6-24h after onset: age <80, NIHSS >=6, ASPECTS 3-5 without significant mass effect
- Tissue-based (imaging) selection replaces time-based paradigm

**Posterior Circulation Thrombectomy (NEW Class 1):**
- Basilar artery occlusion with NIHSS >=10 and mild ischemic damage, within 24h

**Thrombolytic Choice:**
- Both alteplase AND tenecteplase now acceptable agents

Sources: 2026 AHA/ASA Guideline for Early Management of AIS (Stroke, 2026; DOI: 10.1161/STR.0000000000000513)

### 3.3 Mechanical Thrombectomy -- Expanded Indications [BOARD-CORE to BOARD-LIKELY]

**Extended Time Window (established -- now BOARD-CORE):**
- DAWN Trial: Thrombectomy 6-24h with clinical-imaging mismatch; 49% achieved functional independence vs 13% control
- DEFUSE-3 Trial: Thrombectomy 6-16h with perfusion mismatch; 45% good outcome vs 17% control
- 1-year follow-up shows sustained and even enhanced benefit

**Posterior Circulation -- Basilar Artery (BOARD-LIKELY):**
- BAOCHE Trial (NEJM 2022): Thrombectomy 6-24h for basilar occlusion; 46% good outcome vs 23% control; mortality 37% vs 55%
- ATTENTION Trial: Similar results within 12h
- 1-year follow-up: excellent outcome (mRS 0-1) increased from 20% to 28% in thrombectomy group

**Emerging Concept -- Tissue Clock vs. Time Clock (BOARD-LIKELY):**
- Individual infarct growth rate varies based on collaterals, clot characteristics, comorbidities
- Imaging-guided patient selection is replacing fixed time cutoffs
- Some patients benefit beyond 24h with favorable imaging

Sources: DAWN (NEJM 2018); DEFUSE-3 (NEJM 2018); BAOCHE (NEJM 2022); ATTENTION (NEJM 2022); 2026 AHA/ASA Guidelines

### 3.4 Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT) Updates [BOARD-CORE]

**Aspirin + Clopidogrel (CHANCE/POINT -- now established):**
- CHANCE Trial: Aspirin + clopidogrel within 24h of minor stroke/TIA for 21 days; 32% relative risk reduction in recurrent stroke
- POINT Trial: Aspirin + clopidogrel within 12h for 90 days; reduced major ischemic events but increased major hemorrhage
- Current recommendation: DAPT for 21 days, then single antiplatelet

**Aspirin + Ticagrelor:**
- THALES Trial (NEJM 2020): Aspirin + ticagrelor vs aspirin alone in mild-moderate AIS/TIA within 24h for 30 days; stroke/death 5.5% vs 6.6% (HR 0.83); BUT increased severe bleeding 0.5% vs 0.1% [BOARD-LIKELY]
- CHANCE-2 Trial: In CYP2C19 loss-of-function allele carriers, ticagrelor-aspirin reduced stroke recurrence 33% vs clopidogrel-aspirin [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Clinical Application:**
- For minor non-cardioembolic ischemic stroke or high-risk TIA: start DAPT within 24h
- Aspirin + clopidogrel is standard; consider aspirin + ticagrelor for CYP2C19 poor metabolizers
- Limit DAPT to 21-30 days to minimize bleeding risk
- Then transition to single antiplatelet long-term

Sources: CHANCE (NEJM 2013); POINT (NEJM 2018); THALES (NEJM 2020); CHANCE-2 (Lancet 2022); 2025 meta-analysis (J Int Med Res)

### 3.5 Patent Foramen Ovale (PFO) Closure [BOARD-CORE]

**Landmark Trials (now established):**
- CLOSE Trial: PFO closure superior to antiplatelet alone; HR 0.03 for stroke recurrence
- RESPECT Extended: PFO closure reduced recurrent stroke (HR 0.55)
- DEFENSE-PFO: Confirmed benefit of closure

**Current Recommendations:**
- PFO closure recommended for patients aged 18-60 with cryptogenic stroke and high-risk PFO anatomy (large shunt, atrial septal aneurysm)
- 2024 ESO Guidelines: reference guideline using GRADE methodology
- Pascal Classification: closure + antiplatelet for large R-to-L shunt or ASA

**Age Considerations (BOARD-EMERGING):**
- Traditional cutoff: age <60
- Emerging evidence (2025) suggests PFO closure may be safe and effective in patients >=60, though evidence remains limited

**Post-Closure Complication:**
- New-onset atrial fibrillation can occur after PFO closure device implantation, which may limit overall efficacy

Sources: CLOSE (NEJM 2017); RESPECT Extended (NEJM 2017); DEFENSE-PFO (JACC 2018); 2024 ESO PFO Guidelines (ESJ 2024)

### 3.6 Cryptogenic Stroke & Implantable Loop Recorders [BOARD-CORE]

**ILR AF Detection Rates:**
- 12.2% at 3 months
- 16.0% at 6 months
- 18.7% at 12 months
- 22.8% at 24 months
- 28.5% at 36 months
- Higher detection in ESUS vs other cryptogenic (22.0% vs 14.2% at 6 months)

**Predictors of AF Detection:**
- Older age
- Higher CHA2DS2-VASc score
- Left atrial enlargement
- Prolonged P wave duration
- Prolonged PR interval

**Clinical Controversy:**
- Despite higher AF detection with ILRs, stroke recurrence rates are similar regardless of whether AF is detected -- raises question of whether detected asymptomatic AF is truly the cause of the index stroke
- Cost: ILR ~$13,000 more at baseline vs external monitoring
- Wearable devices (smartwatches, patches) may be comparable for AF detection at lower cost

Sources: CRYSTAL-AF (NEJM 2014); Reveal LINQ Registry (JAHA 2024); 2024 systematic review of wearable technology

### 3.7 Large Vessel Occlusion (LVO) Detection Scales [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Prehospital Scale Performance (2025 pooled analysis of 2,358 patients):**
- 63 scales identified; 14 reconstructed for analysis
- RACE scale: highest clinical benefit across harm-benefit trade-offs
- Top AUROC (0.79-0.81): RACE, LAMS, G-FAST, mG-FAST

**Key Scales:**
| Scale | Components | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| RACE | Facial palsy, arm motor, leg motor, gaze, aphasia/agnosia | Simplification of NIHSS; best overall |
| LAMS | Facial droop, arm drift, grip strength | Simple, fast |
| G-FAST | Gaze, face, arm, speech, time | Incorporates gaze deviation |
| Cincinnati (CPSS) | Face, arm, speech + gaze (adjusted) | High specificity (94%) but low sensitivity (35%) |

**Clinical Importance:** These scales are valuable for prehospital triage to direct LVO patients to thrombectomy-capable centers, but imaging confirmation remains essential -- scales miss a sizable number of LVOs.

Sources: 2025 Neurology pooled analysis; RACE validation studies

### 3.8 Stroke Mimics [BOARD-CORE]

**Prevalence:** 20-40% of emergency stroke activations are stroke mimics.

**Common Mimics (by frequency):**
1. Seizures (27% of neurological mimics, 14% of all mimics)
2. Migraine with aura
3. Hypoglycemia (important -- readily correctable)
4. Conversion disorder/functional neurological disorder (12.8% have functional etiology)
5. Brain tumors
6. Peripheral vertigo
7. Toxic-metabolic encephalopathy
8. CNS infections

**Features Favoring Mimic Over Stroke:**
- Dizziness, altered mental status, seizure-like movements, memory disturbance
- Younger age, absence of vascular risk factors

**Features Favoring Stroke:**
- Dysarthria, hemiparesis, facial droop
- Sudden onset, focal deficit matching vascular territory
- Older age, vascular risk factors

**Board Relevance:** Always check glucose before assuming stroke. Risk of treating mimics with tPA is low but not zero.

Sources: StatPearls (updated 2024); 2024-2025 ED mimic studies; Stroke Mimics Score (SMS) 2025

### 3.9 Carotid Intervention Updates [BOARD-LIKELY]

**CREST-2 Trial (2025 -- Major Update):**
- CAS + medical therapy: primary outcome 2.8% vs 6.0% medical therapy alone (STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT)
- CEA + medical therapy: 3.7% vs 5.3% medical therapy alone (NOT statistically significant)
- This is the first RCT to show superiority of CAS over medical management alone for asymptomatic stenosis

**Current Recommendations:**
- Symptomatic stenosis >=70%: CEA or CAS recommended (Class 1)
- Symptomatic stenosis 50-69%: CEA may be considered
- Asymptomatic stenosis >=60-70%: consider intervention IF perioperative risk <3% AND life expectancy >3-5 years
- CEA preferred in patients >70 (lower periprocedural risk); CAS equivalent in younger patients
- TCAR (Transcarotid Artery Revascularization): emerging alternative with potentially lower embolic risk

**Key Point for Boards:** CREST-2 challenges the primacy of CEA -- stenting showed statistical benefit while surgery did not reach significance in asymptomatic disease.

Sources: CREST-2 (2025); SVS 2022 Guidelines; ESVS 2023 Carotid Guidelines

### 3.10 Emerging Neuroprotection [BOARD-EMERGING]

**Nerinetide (NA-1):**
- Mechanism: disrupts PSD-95, blocking NMDA receptor-mediated excitotoxicity
- ESCAPE-NA1: overall neutral; BUT tPA degrades nerinetide (plasmin cleaves the peptide)
- ESCAPE-NEXT (2025): neutral overall but suggested benefit within 3h of onset (without tPA)
- FRONTIER (2025): patients receiving tPA/EVT + nerinetide showed improved functional outcome at 90 days

**Other Emerging Agents:**
- Edaravone: free radical scavenger; approved in Japan; limited Western evidence
- Uric acid: endogenous antioxidant; URICO-ICTUS showed trend toward benefit with alteplase
- Scp776 (IGF-1-based): promising phase 2 (ARPEGGIO trial)

**Non-Pharmacological Neuroprotection:**
- Remote ischemic conditioning: intermittent limb ischemia may activate endogenous protective pathways
- Normobaric hyperoxia: under investigation
- Therapeutic hypothermia: technically challenging; no convincing RCT data for AIS

**Board Relevance:** No neuroprotective agent has yet achieved definitive clinical benefit for AIS. This remains a major unmet need. The nerinetide story illustrates the complexity of drug-drug interactions in acute stroke (tPA degrades nerinetide).

Sources: ESCAPE-NA1 (Lancet 2020); ESCAPE-NEXT & FRONTIER (2025); ARPEGGIO Phase 2; Neuroprotection review (Brain Circulation 2024)
