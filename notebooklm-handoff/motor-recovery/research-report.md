## SECTION 1: PATTERNS OF MOTOR RECOVERY

### 1.1 Twitchell's Classic Recovery Pattern (1951)

Motor recovery after stroke follows a predictable sequence first described by Twitchell (1951) based on 121 patients with MCA territory infarctions. [BOARD-CORE]

**Timeline of Recovery Phases:**

| Phase | Timeframe | Key Features |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| Flaccidity | Days to weeks | Total loss of voluntary movement, absent or decreased DTRs |
| Spasticity emergence | Weeks 1-4 | DTRs return within 48 hours, increased tone develops, especially UE flexors and LE extensors |
| Synergy patterns | Weeks 2-8 | Voluntary movement only within stereotyped synergy patterns |
| Out-of-synergy movement | Months 2-6 | Isolated voluntary movements emerge, spasticity diminishes |
| Selective motor control | Months 3-12+ | Near-normal movement with isolated joint control |

*Sources: Twitchell (1951); Cuccurullo Ch. 1, pp. 27-28; DeLisa Ch. 18, Recovery from Impairments section*

### 1.2 UE Flexor Synergy Pattern [BOARD-CORE]

The upper extremity flexor synergy is the dominant pattern during Brunnstrom Stage 3:
- Scapular retraction and elevation
- Shoulder abduction and external rotation
- Elbow flexion
- Forearm supination
- Wrist and finger flexion

*Source: Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 28; App content Tab 1*

### 1.3 LE Extensor Synergy Pattern [BOARD-CORE]

The lower extremity extensor synergy dominates:
- Hip extension, adduction, and internal rotation
- Knee extension
- Ankle plantarflexion and inversion
- Toe flexion

*Source: Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 28; App content Tab 1*

### 1.4 Copenhagen Stroke Study Data [BOARD-CORE]

The Copenhagen Stroke Study (1995) was a landmark prospective study of 947 consecutive stroke patients defining the natural history of neurological and functional recovery:

- 95% of patients reached their best neurologic recovery level by 11 weeks
- Mild strokes (initial NIHSS <5): best recovery reached by 3-5 weeks
- Moderate strokes: best recovery reached by 6-11 weeks
- Severe strokes: best recovery reached by 13-17 weeks
- Functional recovery (ADLs) continued 2-4 weeks beyond neurologic plateau due to compensatory strategies
- Initial stroke severity was the strongest predictor of ultimate outcome
- Walking function: 80% achieved independent walking; most within 6 weeks
- Upper extremity: only 33% achieved useful hand function

*Sources: Jorgensen et al. (1995); DeLisa Ch. 18, Recovery from Impairments section; App content Tab 1*

### 1.5 Proportional Recovery Rule [BOARD-CORE]

Most stroke patients recover approximately 70% of their potential recovery (the deficit between initial impairment and full recovery):

- Proposed by Prabhakaran et al.: patients recover approximately 70% of their maximum possible improvement
- Example: if initial Fugl-Meyer UE = 20/66, deficit = 46, expected recovery = 46 x 0.7 = 32 points, predicted FM = 52
- "Fitters" (approximately 70% of patients): follow the proportional recovery rule closely
- "Non-fitters" (approximately 30% of patients): recover far less than predicted, often have severe initial deficits
- Non-fitters typically have greater corticospinal tract damage (disrupted PLIC on DWI)
- The rule applies primarily to UE motor recovery measured by Fugl-Meyer
- Physiological support: patients with motor evoked potentials (MEP+) from paretic wrist extensors 5 days post-stroke recovered approximately 70% of maximum possible improvement by 12 weeks (Byblow et al.)
- MEP-negative patients do NOT demonstrate proportional recovery

*Sources: Prabhakaran et al. (2008); Stinear et al.; App content Tab 1; Stroke 2016;47(12):3024-3031*

### 1.6 Corticospinal Tract Integrity and Prediction [BOARD-CORE]

- PLIC carries the majority of corticospinal fibers controlling hand and arm function
- DWI signal in the PLIC within 72 hours predicts motor outcome at 3-6 months
- Asymmetry in fractional anisotropy (FA) on DTI correlates with motor recovery
- FA ratio <0.5 predicts poor UE motor recovery
- TMS: presence of motor evoked potentials (MEPs) from affected hemisphere predicts good recovery
- Absent MEPs at 1-2 weeks post-stroke associated with "non-fitter" status

*Sources: DeLisa Ch. 18, references 48-51; App content Tab 1*

### 1.7 SAFE Score [BOARD-CORE]

A simple bedside assessment at 72 hours post-stroke predicting UE motor recovery:

- SAFE = Shoulder Abduction + Finger Extension
- Score each on MRC scale: 0-5; total 0-10
- SAFE score >= 5: 98% probability of achieving some useful UE function
- SAFE score = 0 at 72 hours: poor prognosis for UE recovery
- Quick, requires no equipment, highly predictive
- Forms the first step in the PREP2 prediction algorithm

*Sources: Stinear et al.; App content Tab 1*

### 1.8 PREP2 Algorithm [BOARD-LIKELY]

The Predict REcovery Potential (PREP2) algorithm sequentially combines clinical assessment and TMS testing in the first week post-stroke to predict UE functional outcomes at 3 months:

1. Start with SAFE score at 72 hours
2. If SAFE >= 5 and age <80: Excellent prognosis
3. If SAFE >= 5 and age >= 80: Good prognosis
4. If SAFE < 5: proceed to TMS testing for MEPs
5. MEP+: Good prognosis
6. MEP-: proceed to MRI (PLIC asymmetry index)
7. Asymmetry index < 0.125: Limited prognosis
8. Asymmetry index >= 0.125: Poor prognosis

- Refined algorithm improved accuracy from 59% to 75%
- 2024 external validation (Millot et al.): accuracy varies by setting; decreased from 75% in acute stage to 60% in subacute stage
- Cognitive syndromes may influence prediction accuracy

*Sources: Stinear et al. (2017); Lundquist et al. (2021) Neurorehabil Neural Repair; Millot et al. (2024) Neurorehabil Neural Repair; App content Tab 1*

### 1.9 Recovery Predictors from Textbooks [BOARD-CORE]

From Cuccurullo (p. 27-28):
- Severity of UE weakness at onset is a critical predictor
- Complete arm paralysis at onset: only 9% gain good recovery of hand function
- If motor recovery of the hand appears by 4 weeks: up to 70% chance of full or good recovery
- Poor prognosis with: no measurable grasp by 4 weeks, severe proximal spasticity, prolonged flaccidity, late return of proprioceptive facilitation (tapping response >9 days), late proximal traction response (>13 days)

From DeLisa (pp. 17-18):
- 88% of acute stroke patients have hemiparesis
- Arm more involved than leg in MCA strokes; pattern reversed in ACA strokes
- Lesion load to corticospinal tracts predicts recovery (imaging studies)
- TMS-evoked motor responses predict recovery
- Most recovery occurs in first 3 months; minor additional improvement after 6 months
- Chronic stroke survivors CAN experience improved motor function with intensive exercise programs

## SECTION 2: BRUNNSTROM STAGES OF MOTOR RECOVERY

### 2.1 The Seven Stages [BOARD-CORE]

| Stage | Description | Motor Characteristics |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Flaccidity | No voluntary movement. Absent reflexes. Complete paralysis |
| 2 | Spasticity developing | Minimal voluntary movement appears. Basic limb synergies may begin. Spasticity starts to develop |
| 3 | Spasticity marked | Voluntary movement ONLY within synergy patterns. Spasticity at peak. Flexor synergy (UE), extensor synergy (LE) |
| 4 | Spasticity declining | Movement combinations OUT of synergy begin. Spasticity decreasing |
| 5 | Synergies less dominant | Relatively independent (isolated) joint movements. Synergies no longer dominate |
| 6 | Coordination approaches normal | Isolated joint movements freely performed. Coordination near normal. Spasticity minimal |
| 7 | Normal | Normal motor function restored |

*Sources: Brunnstrom (1966); Sawner and LaVigne (1992); Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 28; DeLisa Ch. 18 Table 18-4; App content Tab 2*

### 2.2 Board-Critical Stage 3 Pearl [BOARD-CORE]

At Brunnstrom Stage 3:
- Patient asked to flex the elbow demonstrates ENTIRE flexor synergy pattern (shoulder abduction, elbow flexion, forearm supination, wrist flexion)
- Cannot isolate individual joint movements
- LE effort produces the full extensor synergy
- This is the hallmark finding of Stage 3
- MOST COMMONLY TESTED stage on board exams
- Most patients plateau at Stage 3-4

*Source: App content Tab 2; Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 28*

### 2.3 Key Stage 4 Movements (Out of Synergy) [BOARD-CORE]

These movements define the transition to Stage 4:
- UE: Hand behind back (shoulder extension + internal rotation)
- UE: Shoulder flexion to 90 degrees with elbow extended
- UE: Forearm pronation/supination with elbow at 90 degrees
- LE: Knee flexion beyond 90 degrees in sitting
- LE: Ankle dorsiflexion with knee flexed

*Source: App content Tab 2*

### 2.4 DeLisa's 6-Stage Brunnstrom Classification [BOARD-CORE]

DeLisa presents 6 stages (vs. 7 in some descriptions):
1. No activation of the limb
2. Spasticity appears, weak basic flexor and extensor synergies present
3. Spasticity prominent; patient voluntarily moves limb but ALL within synergy patterns
4. Patient begins to activate muscles selectively OUTSIDE the flexor and extensor synergies
5. Spasticity decreases; most muscle activation is selective and independent from synergies
6. Isolated movements performed in smooth, phasic, well-coordinated manner

Note: Not all patients progress sequentially; some skip stages. Scale has not achieved widespread clinical or research usage due to broad categories.

*Source: DeLisa Ch. 18, Table 18-4, p. 30*

### 2.5 Modern Assessment of Brunnstrom Stages [BOARD-EMERGING]

Recent developments (2022-2025) include:
- Wearable sensor-based objective assessment of Brunnstrom stages using machine learning algorithms
- Improved interpretability of lower limb recovery staging through sensor data
- Brunnstrom stages moderately correlated with neurophysiological measures and highly correlated with Modified Motor Assessment Scale (MMAS)
- Traditional observation method limited by observer skill and experience; cannot provide real-time recording

*Sources: Frontiers in Neuroinformatics 2022; PMC validation studies*

## SECTION 3: MOTOR ASSESSMENT SCALES

### 3.1 Fugl-Meyer Assessment (FMA) [BOARD-CORE]

The most widely used quantitative measure of motor impairment after stroke:

| Domain | Score Range | Details |
|--------|------------|---------|
| Upper Extremity Motor | 0-66 | Shoulder/elbow/forearm (36), wrist (10), hand/fingers (14), coordination/speed (6) |
| Lower Extremity Motor | 0-34 | Hip/knee/ankle movements within and out of synergy, coordination |
| Total Motor Score | 0-100 | Sum of UE (66) + LE (34) |
| Sensation | 0-24 | Light touch and proprioception |
| Balance | 0-14 | Sitting and standing |
| Joint ROM | 0-44 | Passive ROM |
| Joint Pain | 0-44 | Pain during passive ROM |
| Grand Total | 0-226 | All domains combined (rarely used) |

**Severity Categories (UE):**
- 0-19 = Severe
- 20-47 = Moderate
- 48-52 = Mild-moderate
- 53-66 = Mild

**Scoring:** 3-point ordinal scale (0 = cannot perform, 1 = partial, 2 = full performance)

**MCID Values (Updated):**
- UE Motor MCID: 5.25 points (general); 13 points for moderate-to-severe impairment at 1-6 weeks post-stroke
- LE Motor MCID: 6 points
- Motor and sensory FMA scores 5 days post-stroke are the strongest predictors of motor recovery at 6 months

*Sources: Fugl-Meyer et al. (1975); DeLisa Ch. 18, pp. 29-30; PMC 2019 MCID study; App content Tab 2*

### 3.2 Wolf Motor Function Test (WMFT) [BOARD-CORE]

- 17 tasks progressing from simple (forearm to table) to complex (lifting basket)
- Tasks timed with 120-second maximum per task
- Functional ability scale: 0-5 (0 = no attempt, 5 = normal)
- Primary outcome measure in the EXCITE trial for CIMT
- Sensitive to change in patients with mild-to-moderate UE impairment

*Source: Wolf et al.; App content Tab 2*

### 3.3 Action Research Arm Test (ARAT) [BOARD-LIKELY]

- 19-item observational assessment of UE function
- 4 subscales: grasp (6), grip (4), pinch (6), gross movement (3)
- Total score: 0-57; each item scored 0-3
- Hierarchical design: score 3 on hardest item gives full subscale score
- MCID = 5.7 points
- Takes 8-10 minutes

*Source: App content Tab 2; DeLisa Ch. 18*

## SECTION 4: REHABILITATION APPROACHES

### 4.1 Traditional Approaches [BOARD-CORE]

**Bobath / Neurodevelopmental Treatment (NDT) (Bobath, 1978):**
- Goal: normalize tone, inhibit primitive patterns, facilitate automatic voluntary reactions
- Most commonly used approach worldwide
- Uses key points of control (shoulder/pelvic girdle) and reflex-inhibiting postures (RIPs)
- Views synergies as pathological; avoids training them
- OPPOSITE philosophy to Brunnstrom approach
- Limited evidence of superiority over other approaches

**Brunnstrom Movement Therapy (Sawner and LaVigne, 1992):**
- Uses primitive synergistic patterns in training to improve motor control through central facilitation
- Views synergies as normal recovery stages, not pathological
- Trains patients to use available motor patterns at their current recovery stage
- Progressively trains out-of-synergy movements
- OPPOSITE to Bobath, which inhibits abnormal patterns

**PNF (Knott and Voss, 1968):**
- Uses spiral and diagonal movement patterns (D1 and D2 flexion/extension)
- Manual resistance with irradiation to facilitate weaker muscle groups
- Contract-relax and hold-relax techniques for ROM
- Beevor's axiom: the brain knows movements, not individual muscles

**Rood Approach / Sensorimotor Approach (Schultz-Krohn, 2013):**
- Modification of muscle tone via cutaneous sensorimotor stimulation
- Facilitatory: quick stretch, icing, fast brushing, tapping, vibration
- Inhibitory: slow stroking, neutral warmth, prolonged pressure, joint compression
- Follows developmental sequence (mobility -> stability -> controlled mobility -> skill)

**Motor Relearning Program (Carr and Shepherd, 1985):**
- Based on cognitive motor relearning theory
- Emphasizes functional training of specific tasks
- Problem-solving approach rather than repetitive specific movements

*Sources: Cuccurullo Ch. 1, pp. 28-30; DeLisa Ch. 18, Therapy for Motor Weakness section; App content Tab 3*

### 4.2 Comparison of Approaches [BOARD-CORE]

| Approach | Philosophy | Key Technique |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| Bobath/NDT | Inhibit abnormal, facilitate normal | Handling, RIPs |
| Brunnstrom | Use synergies as building blocks | Synergy facilitation |
| PNF | Diagonal spiral patterns | Manual resistance, irradiation |
| Rood | Sensory stimulation drives motor | Icing, brushing, tapping |
| Task-Oriented | Practice the actual task | Repetitive task-specific training |

**Critical evidence:** No single traditional approach is clearly superior. Systematic reviews (Pollock et al., 2014) show the amount and intensity of practice matters more than the specific approach. Task-specific, repetitive, high-intensity practice has the strongest evidence base.

*Sources: Pollock et al. (2014); Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 29; App content Tab 3*

### 4.3 Neuroplasticity Principles [BOARD-CORE]

Ten principles governing rehabilitation-driven neuroplasticity:

1. **Use it or lose it** -- failure to drive brain functions leads to degradation
2. **Use it and improve it** -- training drives cortical reorganization
3. **Specificity** -- nature of training determines nature of plasticity
4. **Repetition matters** -- hundreds to thousands of reps required
5. **Intensity matters** -- sufficient intensity needed to drive plasticity
6. **Time matters** -- earlier intervention drives more recovery; sensitive window approximately 60-90 days post-stroke
7. **Salience matters** -- training must be meaningful and motivating
8. **Age matters** -- plasticity occurs across lifespan but may be slower in older adults
9. **Transference** -- plasticity in one neural circuit can enhance related behaviors
10. **Interference** -- plasticity in one pathway can impede others (maladaptive plasticity, e.g., learned nonuse)

**Mechanisms of cortical reorganization:**
- Cortical remapping: shifting functional representations to perilesional or contralesional regions
- Axonal sprouting, dendritic arborization, synaptic reorganization
- Resolution of ischemic penumbra and edema (early recovery)
- Recovery most active during subacute phase; greatest responsiveness in sensitive window of approximately 60-90 days

*Sources: Kleim and Jones (2008); DeLisa Ch. 18, Mechanisms of Neurologic Recovery section; Frontiers in Neurology 2020; PMC 2023 narrative review; App content Tab 3*

## SECTION 5: ADVANCED THERAPIES

### 5.1 Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy (CIMT) [BOARD-CORE]

**Original Protocol:**
- Mitt or sling on unaffected hand for 90% of waking hours
- Intensive structured practice 6 hours/day for 2 weeks
- Based on learned nonuse theory (Taub)

**Eligibility Criteria (EXCITE Trial requirements):** [BOARD-CORE -- frequently tested]
- At least 10 degrees of active wrist extension
- At least 10 degrees of active finger extension
- At least 10 degrees of thumb abduction/extension
- Only approximately 20-25% of stroke patients meet criteria

**EXCITE Trial Results:** [BOARD-CORE]
- Patients with mild-moderate UE impairment 3-9 months post-stroke
- Significantly improved WMFT Performance Time and MAL scores
- Improvements maintained at 2-year follow-up with no regression
- Both early (3-9 months) and delayed (12+ months) groups benefited
- Earlier CIMT showed greater improvement than delayed group
- Cortical reorganization demonstrated via TMS mapping and fMRI

**Modified CIMT (mCIMT):**
- 30 min-3 hours/day for extended period
- More practical and similarly effective
- Home-based CIMT (HOMECIMT trial): cluster-randomized, showed feasibility

**Recent Evidence (2020-2025):** [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2025 meta-analysis: modified CIMT improves UE function with courses >2 months, intervention periods >= 2 weeks, sessions >= 30 minutes
- 2023 meta-regression: better effects in chronic stroke (>6 months); increased efficacy with constraint >3 hours
- Telerehabilitation-based CIMT shows comparable effectiveness to traditional CIMT
- CIMT effects on balance and functional mobility: standardized mean difference 0.51 (P = 0.01)
- CIMT remains the only motor rehabilitation approach with Level I evidence for UE recovery

*Sources: Wolf et al. (2006, 2008) EXCITE trial; Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 29; DeLisa Ch. 18, Motor Recovery section; Frontiers in Human Neuroscience 2022; JCM 2023; App content Tab 4*

### 5.2 Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES) [BOARD-CORE]

- Stimulates peripheral nerves to produce functional muscle contraction during tasks
- Used for dorsiflexion (foot drop), wrist/finger extension, grasp
- EMG-triggered FES requires active patient participation
- NMES is cyclic and non-task-based; FES is applied during functional tasks
- Bioness L300 and WalkAide: FDA-cleared FES foot drop devices
- Evidence supports improved motor recovery when combined with task practice

From Cuccurullo (p. 30): FES may improve ability to voluntarily move/use affected limb; small effect on some aspects of function vs. no treatment; insufficient data to support or refute routine clinical use for neuromuscular retraining.

*Sources: Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 30; DeLisa Ch. 18; App content Tab 4*

### 5.3 Body-Weight-Supported Treadmill Training (BWSTT) [BOARD-CORE]

- Patient walks on treadmill while partially suspended (20-40% body weight reduction initially)
- Based on central pattern generator (CPG) theory
- **LEAPS trial (Duncan et al., 2011):** BWSTT NOT superior to progressive home exercise at 1 year [BOARD-CORE]
- Subjects receiving BWSTT within 2 months post-stroke were at higher risk to fall
- Still useful for early mobility and patients with significant weakness

*Sources: Duncan et al. (2011) LEAPS trial; Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 30; DeLisa Ch. 18; App content Tab 4*

### 5.4 Robotic-Assisted Therapy [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Established devices:**
- MIT-Manus / InMotion ARM for UE
- Lokomat for LE gait training
- Enables high repetitions (hundreds to thousands per session)

**VA ROBOTICS trial:** Similar outcomes to intensive conventional therapy [BOARD-CORE]

**Recent Evidence (2022-2025):** [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2025 Stroke umbrella review: combined 396 RCTs from 16 meta-analyses; robotic therapy provides clinically meaningful improvements but is NOT superior to dose-matched conventional therapy
- 2025 meta-analysis (13 RCTs, 424 patients): moderate statistically significant effect over conventional rehab; strongest effects in subacute and acute phases; chronic phase showed limited improvement
- Network meta-analysis (31 RCTs, 1,537 patients): robot-assisted training combined with conventional rehab improves UE motor dysfunction
- Optimal parameters: younger age and intervention >6 weeks associated with enhanced outcomes
- 2025 Neurology systematic review: Upper limb robots improve motor arm function; moderate quality evidence
- Combined robotic + NMES therapy showing promise in recent RCTs

*Sources: Lo et al. (2010) VA ROBOTICS; Stroke 2024 umbrella review; Frontiers in Neurology 2025; Scientific Reports; App content Tab 4*

### 5.5 Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS/rTMS) [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Interhemispheric Competition Model:**
- After stroke, reduced excitability in ipsilesional hemisphere
- Increased inhibition from contralesional hemisphere
- Goal: rebalance interhemispheric inhibition

**Stimulation Protocols:**
- High-frequency (HF) rTMS (>= 5 Hz): applied to ipsilesional motor cortex to facilitate excitability
- Low-frequency (LF) rTMS (<= 1 Hz): applied to contralesional motor cortex to reduce inhibition
- Intermittent theta-burst stimulation (iTBS): shorter protocol, rebalances bilateral excitability

**Current Evidence (2021-2025):** [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 2024 Stroke review: evidence hampered by unexplained variability in effect sizes and insufficient understanding of mechanisms
- 2025 meta-analysis (low risk-of-bias RCTs only): rTMS shows benefit for motor impairment, but nuances regarding type and site of stimulation not established
- rTMS improves motor impairment, dysphagia, depression, cognitive function, and central post-stroke pain
- Current evidence does NOT support routine clinical use for motor recovery
- TMS remains primarily a DIAGNOSTIC/PROGNOSTIC tool (MEP assessment) rather than a validated therapeutic intervention for motor recovery

*Sources: Stroke 2024 review; Archives PMR meta-analysis 2025; Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 31; App content Tab 4*

### 5.6 Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS) [BOARD-EMERGING]

**Recent Evidence (2022-2025):**
- 2025 meta-analysis: NO significant difference in UE motor function improvement between tDCS and control (MD = 1.133, P = 0.056)
- However, LOWER LIMB motor function significantly improved (MD = 1.628, P < 0.001)
- tDCS alone is INEFFECTIVE for motor recovery; beneficial only when combined with other therapies
- TRANSPORT2 trial (Lancet Neurology 2025): tDCS (2 mA or 4 mA) + modified CIMT did NOT lead to further reduction in motor impairment vs. sham + mCIMT at 1-6 months post-stroke; but was safe and well-tolerated
- tDCS + CIMT combination showed most significant effects in network meta-analysis

*Sources: Brain Network and Modulation 2025; Lancet Neurology 2025 TRANSPORT2; Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience 2022; App content Tab 4*

### 5.7 Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) [BOARD-EMERGING]

**Mechanism:** Decode motor intent from EEG/ECoG signals; provide feedback through robotic orthosis, FES, or virtual display.

**Recent Evidence (2024-2025):**
- 2024 multicenter RCT (17 centers, 296 ischemic stroke patients, China): BCI group showed significantly greater FMA-UE improvement than control (13.17 vs. 9.83 points, P = 0.0045)
- 2025 ReHand-BCI trial: RCT using robotic hand orthosis with BCI feedback; both groups performed 30 therapy sessions; results demonstrate feasibility and potential benefit
- 2025 overview of systematic reviews: BCIs yield significant immediate motor function improvements; safe and viable option
- BCI + FES combination: notable improvements in UE function and induction of neuroplasticity
- Limitation: gains may plateau without ongoing intervention; shorter intensive regimens may be more effective; maintenance sessions may be critical

*Sources: ScienceDirect 2024 multicenter RCT; Frontiers in Neuroscience 2025 ReHand-BCI; Frontiers in Human Neuroscience 2025 overview; App content Tab 4*

### 5.8 Mirror Therapy [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Mechanism:** Mirror placed in patient's midsagittal plane reflects movements of nonparetic side, creating visual illusion of movement in paretic limb. Activates mirror neuron system and promotes cortical reorganization.

**Recent Evidence (2020-2025):**
- 2025 meta-analysis (15 studies, 546 participants): significant effect on motor impairment, motor function, and ADL compared to controls
- Bimanual mirror therapy showed significant benefits; unimanual MT may have MORE significant effect than bimanual
- 2025 PM&R systematic review: beneficial for improving UE motor function in subacute stroke
- Mirror + robotic therapy combination: 2025/2026 meta-analysis (16 RCTs, n=736) shows promise
- Limitations: small sample sizes in most studies, methodological limitations; not routinely used in stroke rehabilitation

*Sources: Cuccurullo Ch. 1, p. 30; Thieme et al. (2012); ScienceDirect 2021; PM&R 2025; App content Tab 4*

### 5.9 Virtual Reality Rehabilitation [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Recent Evidence (2022-2025):**
- 2025 Cochrane Review update: VR as part of comprehensive rehabilitation can be beneficial; more utility after 6+ months post-stroke
- 2025 meta-analysis (55 RCTs, 2,142 patients): VR confers benefits over conventional therapy for UE motor function, functional independence, quality of life, spasticity, and dexterity
- Fully immersive VR: greatest gains in gross motor function
- Non-immersive VR: enhanced fine dexterity
- FMA-UE improvement: MD = 7.47 points (exceeds MCID)
- Home-based VR: positive impact on UE rehabilitation, particularly in patients with varying dysfunction levels
- Safety: no significant adverse events (motion sickness, nausea, headache) reported
- VR NOT more beneficial than conventional therapy when used as sole intervention; best as ADJUNCT

*Sources: Cochrane 2025 CD008349.pub5; Frontiers in Neurology 2025; BMC Medical Informatics 2024; JMIR 2025; App content Tab 4*

### 5.10 Fluoxetine / SSRIs for Motor Recovery [BOARD-CORE]

**FLAME Trial (2011):** Fluoxetine 20 mg improved motor recovery at 3 months vs. placebo in ischemic stroke.

**Subsequent Large Trials (2020-2021) -- ALL NEGATIVE:** [BOARD-CORE]
- **FOCUS (UK):** Fluoxetine 20 mg daily x 6 months did NOT improve functional outcome; decreased depression but INCREASED fracture risk
- **AFFINITY (Australia/NZ):** No functional outcome improvement; INCREASED falls, fractures, and seizures
- **EFFECTS (Sweden):** No effect on functional outcome up to 1 year; modest depression reduction (11% to 7%) but similar antidepressant use at 12 months

**Board Pearl:** SSRIs are NOT routinely recommended solely for motor recovery enhancement after stroke. The three large confirmatory trials definitively refuted the FLAME trial's positive findings.

*Sources: Chollet et al. (2011) FLAME; FOCUS (Lancet 2019); AFFINITY (Lancet Neurology 2020); EFFECTS (Lancet Neurology 2020); Stroke 2021; App content Tab 4*

## SECTION 6: TIMING OF REHABILITATION

### 6.1 Early Mobilization [BOARD-CORE]

**AVERT Trial (A Very Early Rehabilitation Trial):**
- Very early aggressive mobilization within 24 hours post-stroke was associated with WORSE outcomes at 3 months
- High-dose intensive early mobilization INCREASED mortality within 14 days
- This finding changed clinical practice guidelines

**Optimal Mobilization (Dose-Response Analysis):** [BOARD-CORE]
- Initiation: 24-48 hours after stroke (not within first 24 hours)
- Activities: bed/chair transfer, sitting out of bed, standing, walking
- Frequency: 2-3 times per day
- Duration: determined by patient condition
- Shorter, more frequent sessions superior to long intensive sessions

**VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guidelines (2024):** [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Updated stroke rehabilitation guidelines available
- Emphasize judicious early mobilization timing

*Sources: AVERT trial (Lancet 2015); Bernhardt et al. dose-response (Neurology 2016); DeLisa Ch. 18 (AVERT reference 39); VA/DoD 2024 guidelines*

### 6.2 Task-Specific Training Dose and Intensity [BOARD-LIKELY]

**Recent Evidence (2020-2025):**
- 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis: insufficient evidence that HIGHER-intensity task-specific training leads to better functional outcomes
- However, interventions <= 60 minutes and >3 sessions/week were most effective for balance
- Feasibility: patients with moderate-severe impairment can achieve 157 repetitions/visit + 52 reps/day self-practice (target: 100-300 reps/day)
- Possible solutions for increasing training dose: CIMT, robotics, circuit therapy, gaming technologies, goal-oriented instructions, cardiovascular exercise
- Stepping practice: high step count (3000+/session) recommended for locomotor training

*Sources: SAGE 2025 systematic review; ScienceDirect 2022 dose review; App content Tab 3*

## SECTION 7: LOCOMOTOR TRAINING AND AFO PRESCRIBING

### 7.1 Locomotor Training Approaches [BOARD-CORE]

| Approach | Description | Evidence Level |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| Overground gait training | Walking with manual assist or device | Standard of care |
| BWSTT | Harness-supported treadmill with therapist assist | LEAPS: not superior to progressive overground exercise at 1 year |
| Robotic gait training (Lokomat, Ekso) | Robotic exoskeleton guides leg movement | May benefit non-ambulatory patients; not superior for ambulatory patients |
| FES-assisted gait | E-stim to dorsiflexors/plantarflexors during walking | Improves gait speed, reduces foot drop |
| Task-specific stepping | Repetitive stepping with varied speed/direction/terrain | Strongest evidence; 3000+ steps/session recommended |

*Source: App content Tab 3*

### 7.2 AFO Prescribing [BOARD-CORE]

| AFO Type | Best Indication | Key Feature |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Solid AFO | Severe spasticity, fixed equinovarus | Blocks all ankle motion; limits push-off |
| Posterior Leaf Spring (PLS) | Isolated foot drop, minimal spasticity | Thin posterior strut; assists dorsiflexion |
| Articulated AFO | Moderate spasticity with some active dorsiflexion | Hinged joint allows DF, blocks PF |
| Ground Reaction AFO (GRAFO) | Genu recurvatum | Anterior shell to tibial tuberosity |

- Too rigid too early may limit motor learning
- Too flexible may not provide adequate control
- FES to dorsiflexors is an alternative/adjunct to AFO for foot drop
