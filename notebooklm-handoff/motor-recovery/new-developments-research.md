# Motor Recovery After Stroke: New Developments Research Report (2020-2026)

## Purpose
This report summarizes research developments from 2020-2026 that update, refine, or challenge content in the CVA Training app's Motor Recovery module. Each finding is source-tagged with board-readiness level. This report identifies gaps in current app content and new evidence that should inform updates.

---

## 1. CONSTRAINT-INDUCED MOVEMENT THERAPY (CIMT) -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
The app covers the original CIMT protocol, eligibility criteria, EXCITE trial results with 2-year follow-up, and modified CIMT.

### New Developments

**1.1 Modified CIMT Dosing Optimization** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of modified CIMT (mCIMT) found that interventions are effective when:
- Course duration exceeds 2 months
- Intervention periods are at least 2 weeks
- Each training session lasts at least 30 minutes
- Effective in both chronic stroke patients and those with shorter duration symptoms

*Source: PMC 2025 (Interventional effects of modified CIMT on UL function)*

**1.2 Telerehabilitation CIMT** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis found CIMT delivered via telerehabilitation was comparable in effectiveness to traditional in-person CIMT for UE dysfunction in chronic stroke.

*Source: PubMed 38685678 (2024)*

**1.3 CIMT Effect on Balance** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2022 meta-analysis demonstrated CIMT improved balance and functional mobility with a standardized mean difference of 0.51 (P = 0.01), expanding the known benefits beyond UE function alone.

*Source: PubMed 35326973 (2022)*

**1.4 Timing Dose-Response** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2023 meta-regression analysis showed:
- Better effects on functional ability in chronic stroke (>6 months)
- Increased efficacy with constraint time >3 hours
- Timing and dose should guide clinical decision-making

*Source: JCM 2023;12(6):2267*

### Gap Identified
App does not cover telerehabilitation-based CIMT or the balance/mobility benefits. Consider adding mCIMT dosing parameters.

## 2. ROBOTIC-ASSISTED THERAPY -- MAJOR UPDATES

### Current App Content
Covers MIT-Manus/InMotion ARM, Lokomat, VA ROBOTICS trial results. States similar outcomes to intensive conventional therapy.

### New Developments

**2.1 Umbrella Review -- Largest Evidence Synthesis** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2024 Stroke journal umbrella review combined results from 396 RCTs across 16 meta-analyses plus 6 additional RCTs published after 2024. Key finding: robotic therapy provides clinically meaningful improvements but remains NOT superior to dose-matched conventional therapy.

*Source: Stroke 2024 (doi: 10.1161/STROKEAHA.124.048183)*

**2.2 Phase-Dependent Effectiveness** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2025 meta-analysis (13 RCTs, 424 patients) found:
- Moderate but statistically significant effect over conventional rehab overall
- STRONGEST effects in subacute and acute phases
- CHRONIC phase showed LIMITED improvement
- Younger age and intervention >6 weeks associated with better outcomes

*Source: PMC 2025 systematic review; Frontiers in Neurology 2025*

**2.3 Combined Robotic + NMES** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2025 systematic review of RCTs found that combining robot-assisted therapy with neuromuscular electrical stimulation showed enhanced UE rehabilitation outcomes compared to either intervention alone.

*Source: ScienceDirect 2025 (S0894113025000535)*

**2.4 Network Meta-Analysis of UE Robotic Training** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Analysis of 31 eligible RCTs (1,537 patients) confirmed efficacy of robot-assisted training combined with conventional rehabilitation for UE motor dysfunction post-stroke.

*Source: ScienceDirect 2024 (S2590109524001009)*

### Gap Identified
App should update to reflect phase-dependent effectiveness (subacute > chronic) and the consistent finding that robotics is NOT superior to dose-matched conventional therapy.

## 3. BRAIN-COMPUTER INTERFACES -- NEW AREA

### Current App Content
Listed as a bullet point under "Emerging Therapies" with minimal detail.

### New Developments

**3.1 First Large Multicenter RCT** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2024 multicenter RCT across 17 centers in China enrolled 296 ischemic stroke patients:
- BCI group: FMA-UE change of 13.17 points
- Control group: FMA-UE change of 9.83 points
- Difference statistically significant (P = 0.0045)
- Both groups exceeded MCID for FMA-UE
- This is the largest BCI stroke rehabilitation RCT to date

*Source: ScienceDirect 2024 (S2666634024000862)*

**3.2 ReHand-BCI Trial** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2025 RCT using a robotic hand orthosis controlled by BCI showed:
- Both experimental and control groups performed 30 sessions
- Primary outcomes: FMA-UE and ARAT
- Demonstrated feasibility and safety of BCI-controlled robotic rehabilitation

*Source: Frontiers in Neuroscience 2025*

**3.3 BCI Safety and Efficacy Overview** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2025 overview of systematic reviews concluded:
- BCIs yield significant immediate improvements in motor functions
- Safe and viable rehabilitation option
- BCI + FES combination shows notable improvements in UE function and neuroplasticity induction
- Gains may plateau without ongoing intervention
- Maintenance/booster sessions likely critical for long-term benefit

*Source: Frontiers in Human Neuroscience 2025*

### Gap Identified
App needs significant expansion of BCI content, including the landmark 2024 multicenter RCT results. Current single bullet point is insufficient for this rapidly advancing field.

## 4. TRANSCRANIAL MAGNETIC STIMULATION (TMS/rTMS) -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
Listed as an emerging therapy bullet point. TMS mentioned in diagnostic context for MEP assessment.

### New Developments

**4.1 Current Evidence Summary** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2024 Stroke review article concluded that evidence for rTMS efficacy is hampered by unexplained variability of reported effect sizes and insufficient understanding of mechanisms. Benefits seen in motor impairment, dysphagia, depression, cognitive function, and central post-stroke pain.

*Source: Stroke 2024 (doi: 10.1161/STROKEAHA.123.043159)*

**4.2 Protocol Optimization** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2025 meta-analysis of optimized rTMS protocols found:
- High-frequency (>= 5 Hz) rTMS on ipsilesional M1: facilitates excitability
- Low-frequency (<= 1 Hz) rTMS on contralesional M1: reduces inhibition
- Intermittent theta-burst stimulation (iTBS): superior for rebalancing bilateral excitability
- Based on interhemispheric competition model
- Current evidence does NOT support routine clinical use for motor recovery

*Source: SAGE 2025 meta-analysis; Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience 2023*

**4.3 Combination with Rehabilitation** [BOARD-LIKELY]
rTMS combining inhibitory and facilitatory stimulation improves motor function by modulating GABA in acute ischemic stroke patients.

*Source: Multiple 2023-2025 studies referenced in PMC reviews*

### Gap Identified
App should expand TMS content beyond a single bullet point, particularly the interhemispheric competition model and stimulation protocol types, as these are increasingly appearing in PM&R review materials.

## 5. TRANSCRANIAL DIRECT CURRENT STIMULATION (tDCS) -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
Listed as a single bullet point under "Emerging Therapies."

### New Developments

**5.1 Upper Limb: No Significant Benefit** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2025 meta-analysis found NO significant difference in UE motor function improvement between tDCS and control groups (MD = 1.133, P = 0.056).

*Source: Brain Network and Modulation 2025*

**5.2 Lower Limb: Significant Benefit** [BOARD-EMERGING]
The same meta-analysis found SIGNIFICANT improvement in LE motor function with tDCS (MD = 1.628, P < 0.001).

*Source: Brain Network and Modulation 2025*

**5.3 TRANSPORT2 Trial** [BOARD-EMERGING]
Phase 2, multicenter, sham-controlled, triple-blind trial (Lancet Neurology, March 2025):
- tDCS (2 mA or 4 mA) + modified CIMT vs. sham + mCIMT
- 1-6 months post-stroke
- Did NOT lead to further reduction in motor impairment
- Safe, well-tolerated, and feasible
- This was a definitive negative trial for tDCS augmentation of CIMT

*Source: Lancet Neurology 2025 (S1474-4422(25)00044-4)*

**5.4 tDCS as Combination Therapy** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2022 systematic review found tDCS alone is ineffective; only beneficial when combined with other therapies. A network meta-analysis found tDCS + CIMT showed the most significant combined effects.

*Source: Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience 2022*

### Gap Identified
App should differentiate UE (no benefit) vs. LE (benefit) findings and reference TRANSPORT2 as a key negative trial.

## 6. MIRROR THERAPY -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
Listed as a single bullet point under "Emerging Therapies."

### New Developments

**6.1 Strong Meta-Analytic Support** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2025 meta-analysis (15 studies, 546 participants) found significant effects on motor impairment, motor function, AND activities of daily living compared to controls.

*Source: PubMed 39834285 (2025)*

**6.2 Unimanual vs. Bimanual** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Cochrane review update analysis found both unimanual and bimanual mirror therapy improve motor outcomes, with unimanual MT showing a MORE significant effect than bimanual.

*Source: ScienceDirect 2021 (S0361923021002951)*

**6.3 Mirror + Robot Combination** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2025-2026 meta-analysis (16 RCTs, n=736) examined mirror therapy combined with robot-assisted therapy, showing promising combination effects.

*Source: Springer Nature 2026 (s12984-026-01877-z)*

### Gap Identified
Mirror therapy has substantially more evidence now than implied by a single bullet point. Should be elevated to its own ComparisonCardBlock with evidence summary.

## 7. VIRTUAL REALITY REHABILITATION -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
Listed as a single bullet point under "Emerging Therapies."

### New Developments

**7.1 Cochrane Review Update (2025)** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Updated Cochrane review found VR can be beneficial as part of a comprehensive rehabilitation program. More utility after 6+ months post-stroke.

*Source: Cochrane 2025 (CD008349.pub5)*

**7.2 Large Meta-Analysis** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2024-2025 meta-analysis (55 RCTs, 2,142 patients) found VR superior to conventional therapy for:
- UE motor function (FMA-UE improvement MD = 7.47, exceeding MCID)
- Functional independence
- Quality of life
- Spasticity reduction
- Dexterity improvement

Key nuance:
- Fully immersive VR: greatest gains in GROSS motor function
- Non-immersive VR: enhanced FINE dexterity

*Source: BMC Medical Informatics and Decision Making 2024*

**7.3 Home-Based VR** [BOARD-EMERGING]
A 2025 systematic review confirmed positive impact of home-based VR training on UE rehabilitation, particularly for patients with varying dysfunction levels.

*Source: JMIR 2025 (e69003)*

**7.4 Safety Confirmed** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Multiple reviews confirm no significant adverse events (motion sickness, nausea, headache) with VR rehabilitation.

### Gap Identified
VR should be expanded from a single bullet point to a full section. The FMA-UE improvement of 7.47 points (exceeding MCID of 5.25) is clinically meaningful evidence.

## 8. SSRIs FOR MOTOR RECOVERY -- CRITICAL UPDATE

### Current App Content
App covers FLAME trial and the negative FOCUS/AFFINITY/EFFECTS results. This content is accurate and up-to-date.

### Confirmation of Current Content
The three large negative trials (FOCUS, AFFINITY, EFFECTS) definitively settled this question:
- Fluoxetine 20 mg daily x 6 months: NO functional outcome benefit
- INCREASED adverse events: falls, fractures, hyponatremia, seizures
- Modest depression reduction (approximately 4% absolute reduction)
- Individual patient data meta-analysis of all three trials planned

**Board Pearl (unchanged):** SSRIs are NOT routinely recommended solely for motor recovery enhancement. This is well-established and remains BOARD-CORE.

### Gap Identified
None -- app content is current and accurate on this topic.

## 9. TIMING OF REHABILITATION -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
Not extensively covered in the Motor Recovery module.

### New Developments

**9.1 AVERT Trial Dose-Response Refinement** [BOARD-CORE]
Prespecified dose-response analysis of AVERT found optimal early mobilization parameters:
- Timing: 24-48 hours post-stroke (NOT within first 24 hours)
- Intensity: bed/chair transfer, sitting, standing, walking
- Frequency: 2-3 times per day
- Duration: based on patient condition
- Shorter, more frequent sessions superior to prolonged intensive sessions

*Source: Neurology 2016 (Bernhardt et al. dose-response)*

**9.2 Safety Concern with Very Early Mobilization** [BOARD-CORE]
Tertiary AVERT analysis confirmed increased mortality within 14 days with high-dose very early mobilization.

*Source: AVERT tertiary analysis*

**9.3 VA/DoD Updated Guidelines (2024)** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Updated VA/DoD Clinical Practice Guidelines for Management of Stroke Rehabilitation published in 2024.

*Source: healthquality.va.gov/guidelines/rehab/stroke/*

### Gap Identified
AVERT trial findings and optimal mobilization timing should be added to the Motor Recovery module, as this is board-tested content.

## 10. PROPORTIONAL RECOVERY RULE AND PREP2 -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
App covers the proportional recovery rule, fitters vs. non-fitters, and PREP2 algorithm with good detail.

### New Developments

**10.1 PREP2 External Validation** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2024 external validation study (Millot et al.) found:
- PREP2 accuracy varies significantly by clinical setting
- Accuracy decreased from 75% in acute stage to 60% in subacute stage
- Cognitive syndromes may influence prediction accuracy
- Highlights need for cautious clinical application

*Source: Neurorehabilitation and Neural Repair 2024 (Millot et al.)*

**10.2 PREP2 Implementation Barriers** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2021 qualitative study on implementing PREP2 in clinical practice found:
- Clinicians valued the structured prediction approach
- Barriers included access to TMS equipment and MRI timing
- Clinical adoption remains limited despite evidence

*Source: Physical Therapy 2021 (pzab040)*

**10.3 Physiological Basis Refined** [BOARD-LIKELY]
MEP+ survivors (intact CST) follow the 70% proportional recovery rule; MEP- survivors do NOT demonstrate proportional recovery. This provides physiological validation for the fitter/non-fitter distinction.

*Source: Stroke 2016;47(12):3024-3031; PMC 2023 review*

### Gap Identified
App should note PREP2's variable accuracy in external validation and the implementation barriers.

## 11. FUGL-MEYER ASSESSMENT -- MCID UPDATES

### Current App Content
App lists MCID = 5.25 points for UE motor. This is correct for the general population.

### New Developments

**11.1 Severity-Specific MCID** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2019 PMC study established that the MCID for FMA-UE in patients with moderate-to-severe hemiparesis during the convalescent phase is 13 points -- substantially higher than the commonly cited 5.25.

*Source: PMC 6879402 (2019)*

**11.2 LE MCID Established** [BOARD-LIKELY]
The MCID for FMA-LE has been established at 6 points.

*Source: PubMed 27086865; PMC 12445363 scoping review*

**11.3 Predictive Value at Day 5** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Motor and sensory FMA scores at 5 days post-stroke are the strongest predictors of motor recovery at 6 months, reinforcing the value of early assessment.

### Gap Identified
App should add the severity-specific MCID of 13 points for moderate-severe patients, the LE MCID of 6 points, and the predictive value at day 5.

## 12. TASK-SPECIFIC TRAINING DOSE -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
App states task-specific repetitive practice has strongest evidence base and recommends 3000+ steps/session for locomotor training.

### New Developments

**12.1 Dose-Response Uncertainty** [BOARD-LIKELY]
A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis found insufficient evidence that higher-intensity task-specific training leads to improved functional outcomes compared to standard-intensity training.

*Source: SAGE 2025 (02692155251351906)*

**12.2 Optimal Session Parameters** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Sessions of 60 minutes or less with >3 sessions/week were most effective for balance recovery.

*Source: Same 2025 systematic review*

**12.3 Feasibility of High Repetitions** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Feasibility data shows patients with moderate-severe impairment CAN achieve 157 repetitions per therapy visit plus 52 reps/day of self-practice, meeting targets of 100-300 reps/day.

*Source: Frontiers in Neurology 2023 (Task-AT Home protocol)*

### Gap Identified
App should temper the "more is better" message with current evidence showing the dose-response relationship is not clearly established for task-specific training.

## 13. NEUROPLASTICITY MECHANISMS -- UPDATES

### Current App Content
App covers 10 neuroplasticity principles comprehensively.

### New Developments

**13.1 Sensitive Window Quantified** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Research has more precisely defined the post-stroke sensitive window for neuroplasticity at approximately 60-90 days post-stroke, with a graded capacity for plastic change persisting into the chronic phase.

*Source: PMC 10608684 (2023); Frontiers in Neurology 2020*

**13.2 Molecular Mechanisms** [BOARD-EMERGING]
Recent studies have clarified mechanisms including:
- Synaptogenesis increases substantially after experimental lesioning
- Dendrite number and shape are altered following infarction
- Both synaptogenesis and dendrite remodeling associated with increased neurological activity in motor maps in both ipsilesional and contralesional cortices
- BDNF, GABA modulation, and glutamate receptor plasticity are key molecular pathways

*Source: PMC 10239808 (2023); Multiple 2020-2025 reviews*

**13.3 Maladaptive Plasticity Clarified** [BOARD-LIKELY]
Interference principle further refined: contralesional overactivation can become maladaptive, supporting the interhemispheric competition model used in TMS/tDCS protocols.

### Gap Identified
App content on neuroplasticity is comprehensive. Consider adding the 60-90 day sensitive window timeframe.
