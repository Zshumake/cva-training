# SECTION 1: DEFINITION AND CLASSIFICATION OF STROKE

## 1.1 What Is a Stroke?

A stroke, or cerebrovascular accident (CVA), is a sudden interruption of blood flow to the brain resulting in neurological deficits that persist for more than 24 hours or result in death. The WHO defines stroke as rapidly developing clinical signs of focal or global disturbance of cerebral function, lasting more than 24 hours or leading to death, with no apparent cause other than vascular origin (Aho et al., 1980). [BOARD-CORE]

Key distinction: the term "reversible ischemic neurologic deficit" (RIND), which historically described deficits lasting 24-72 hours, is no longer used in modern classification. [BOARD-CORE]

## 1.2 Two Major Categories

- **Ischemic stroke (87%)**: caused by arterial occlusion from thrombus or embolus [BOARD-CORE]
- **Hemorrhagic stroke (13%)**: caused by rupture of a blood vessel (intracerebral hemorrhage or subarachnoid hemorrhage) [BOARD-CORE]

## 1.3 Stroke vs. TIA: Modern Definition

The critical distinction is duration AND imaging. Historically, a TIA was defined as deficits lasting less than 24 hours. The modern tissue-based definition (AHA/ASA) requires no evidence of infarction on MRI DWI -- a TIA shows no infarct on MRI diffusion-weighted imaging, whereas a stroke shows restricted diffusion regardless of symptom duration. This is a paradigm shift: if imaging shows an infarct, it is a stroke even if symptoms resolved in minutes. [BOARD-CORE]

## 1.4 Stroke Classification: TOAST Criteria

The Trial of ORG 10172 in Acute Stroke Treatment (TOAST) classification is the most commonly used ischemic stroke subtype system worldwide and is a required field in the US National Get With The Guidelines-Stroke (GWTG-Stroke) registry. [BOARD-CORE]

### Ischemic Stroke Subtypes (TOAST)

| Type | Percentage of All Ischemic Strokes | Mechanism | Key Features |
|------|------|-----------|--------------|
| Large artery atherosclerosis (thrombotic) | ~48% | In-situ clot formation on atherosclerotic plaque; perfusion failure distal to stenosis/occlusion | Usually occurs during sleep; "stuttering" onset over 24-48 hours; 50% with preceding TIA in same vascular territory |
| Cardioembolism (embolic) | ~26% | Clot travels from distant cardiac source | Immediate onset; occurs while awake; seizures may occur; 75% of cardiogenic emboli go to brain; 11% with preceding TIA |
| Small vessel occlusion (lacunar) | ~13% | Lipohyalinosis of small penetrating arteries; infarcts less than 15 mm | Seen in putamen, pons, thalamus, caudate, internal capsule, corona radiata; strong HTN correlation (up to 81%); absence of cortical signs |
| Stroke of other determined etiology | Variable | Dissection, vasculitis, hypercoagulable states, sickle cell, moyamoya | More common in young adults |
| Stroke of undetermined etiology (cryptogenic) | ~25-30% | No etiology identified despite complete workup | Active area of research; occult AF, PFO being investigated |

**2024 Update**: Recent research highlights limitations of TOAST classification, with accuracy of subtyping at approximately 61%, with most errors in large artery atherosclerosis and undetermined categories. Alternative systems (CCS, A-S-C-O classification) and AI-assisted approaches are being explored. (Frontiers in Neurology, 2024) [BOARD-EMERGING]

### Hemorrhagic Stroke Subtypes

| Type | Percentage of All Strokes | Mechanism | Key Features |
|------|---------------------------|-----------|--------------|
| Intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH) | ~10% | Vessel rupture into brain parenchyma | 90% associated with HTN; occurs when patient is calm/unstressed; Blacks > Whites; most common location: putamen |
| Subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) | ~3% | Bleeding into subarachnoid space from ruptured aneurysm | Occurs during activity/straining; peak age 5th-6th decade; "worst headache of my life"; 90-95% from anterior Circle of Willis |

## 1.5 Key Terminology

- **Penumbra**: ischemic tissue surrounding the infarct core that is electrically silent but structurally intact and potentially salvageable. This is the target of acute reperfusion therapy. [BOARD-CORE]
- **Infarct core**: irreversibly damaged tissue with cerebral blood flow less than 10 mL/100g/min [BOARD-CORE]
- **Hemorrhagic transformation**: bleeding into an ischemic infarct; can occur spontaneously or after reperfusion therapy [BOARD-CORE]
- **Cerebral autoregulation**: the brain's ability to maintain constant cerebral blood flow despite changes in systemic blood pressure (MAP 60-150 mmHg). Lost in the acute setting around the infarct zone. [BOARD-CORE]
- **Excitotoxicity**: dying cells in the penumbra release glutamate, which activates calcium channels in adjacent cells, leading to toxic calcium accumulation, free radical generation, and further cell death. Clinical trials of glutamate receptor antagonists have been disappointing. [BOARD-CORE]

## 1.6 Neuroanatomical Locations of Ischemic Stroke

### Anterior Circulation

**Internal Carotid Artery (ICA):**
- Most variable syndrome; occlusion often asymptomatic (30-40%) if good collateral circulation exists
- Can produce massive infarction in ACA and MCA distributions
- Transient monocular blindness (amaurosis fugax) occurs prior to stroke in ~25% of ICA occlusions
- Embolic occlusion of retinal branch = ocular infarction [BOARD-CORE]

**Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA):**
- Supplies lateral aspect of hemisphere; most common stroke territory
- Superior division: face and arm greater than leg weakness; Broca's aphasia (dominant hemisphere) or constructional apraxia/hemineglect (nondominant)
- Inferior division: superior quadrantanopia or homonymous hemianopia; Wernicke's aphasia (dominant) or left visual neglect (nondominant)
- Lenticulostriate arteries (deep penetrating branches): supply basal ganglia and internal capsule [BOARD-CORE]

**Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA):**
- Supplies medial aspect of hemisphere
- Contralateral weakness and sensory loss, leg greater than arm (opposite of MCA pattern)
- Urinary incontinence, grasp reflex, paratonic rigidity (gegenhalten)
- Gait apraxia
- Bilateral ACA infarction: akinetic mutism, abulia [BOARD-CORE]

### Posterior Circulation

**Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA):**
- Visual field cuts (contralateral homonymous hemianopia); bilateral = Anton syndrome (cortical blindness with denial)
- Prosopagnosia, alexia without agraphia
- Weber syndrome (midbrain): ipsilateral CN3 palsy + contralateral hemiplegia [BOARD-CORE]

**Vertebrobasilar System Key Features:**
- Vertigo, nystagmus, bilateral motor abnormalities
- Crossed signs: ipsilateral face + contralateral body deficits
- ABSENCE of cortical signs (no aphasia or cognitive deficits) -- this distinguishes posterior from anterior circulation strokes [BOARD-CORE]

### Brainstem Stroke Syndromes

| Syndrome | Location | Artery | Key Features |
|----------|----------|--------|--------------|
| Wallenberg (lateral medullary) | Lateral medulla | PICA/vertebral | Ipsilateral Horner's, ipsilateral facial pain/temperature loss, contralateral body pain/temperature loss, dysphagia, ataxia, NO motor weakness |
| Weber | Midbrain | PCA (interpeduncular branches) | Ipsilateral CN3 palsy + contralateral hemiplegia |
| Millard-Gubler | Pons | Basilar (circumferential branches) | Ipsilateral CN6/CN7 palsy + contralateral hemiplegia |
| Medial medullary | Medial medulla | Vertebral/anterior spinal artery | Ipsilateral CN12 palsy + contralateral hemiplegia + contralateral proprioception loss |
| Locked-in | Ventral pons (bilateral) | Basilar artery | Tetraparesis, preserved consciousness, only vertical eye movements and blinking preserved |

[ALL BOARD-CORE]

### Lacunar Syndromes

| Syndrome | Location |
|----------|----------|
| Pure motor hemiplegia | Posterior limb of internal capsule, corona radiata, pons |
| Pure sensory stroke | Thalamus (ventrolateral), parietal white matter |
| Dysarthria-clumsy hand | Basis pontis, anterior internal capsule |
| Sensorimotor stroke | Junction of internal capsule and thalamus |
| Ataxic hemiparesis | Pons, midbrain, internal capsule, cerebellum |

[ALL BOARD-CORE]

## 1.7 Assessment Scales

### NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS)

Standardized assessment of neurological deficit severity. Score 0-42. Used for tPA eligibility, prognosis, and serial monitoring.

| Domain | What Is Tested | Score Range |
|--------|----------------|-------------|
| 1a. Level of consciousness | Alert, drowsy, stupor, coma | 0-3 |
| 1b. LOC questions | Age and current month | 0-2 |
| 1c. LOC commands | Open/close eyes, grip/release | 0-2 |
| 2. Best gaze | Horizontal eye movements | 0-2 |
| 3. Visual fields | Confrontation visual fields | 0-3 |
| 4. Facial palsy | Symmetry of facial movements | 0-3 |
| 5a/5b. Motor arm (L/R) | Arm drift over 10 seconds | 0-4 each |
| 6a/6b. Motor leg (L/R) | Leg drift over 5 seconds | 0-4 each |
| 7. Limb ataxia | Finger-nose and heel-shin | 0-2 |
| 8. Sensory | Pinprick in face, arm, leg | 0-2 |
| 9. Best language | Naming, reading, describing | 0-3 |
| 10. Dysarthria | Clarity of speech | 0-2 |
| 11. Extinction/inattention | Double simultaneous stimulation | 0-2 |

**Severity Interpretation:**
- NIHSS 1-4 = minor stroke
- NIHSS 5-15 = moderate stroke
- NIHSS 16-20 = moderate-severe
- NIHSS 21-42 = severe stroke
- NIHSS >= 6 strongly supports consideration of IV tPA
- NIHSS > 25 associated with high risk of hemorrhagic transformation after thrombolysis [BOARD-CORE]

**Critical NIHSS Limitation**: The NIHSS is weighted toward anterior circulation and dominant hemisphere deficits. It underestimates posterior circulation strokes (a devastating basilar occlusion may score low) and non-dominant hemisphere strokes (neglect is scored but not heavily weighted). [BOARD-CORE]

### Modified Rankin Scale (mRS)

Most widely used outcome measure in stroke clinical trials. Measures global disability/dependence.

| Grade | Description | Functional Status |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| 0 | No symptoms at all | Fully independent |
| 1 | No significant disability | Able to carry out all usual duties |
| 2 | Slight disability | Unable to carry out all previous activities but independent in ADLs |
| 3 | Moderate disability | Requires some help but walks without assistance |
| 4 | Moderately severe | Unable to walk unassisted, unable to attend to bodily needs without help |
| 5 | Severe disability | Bedridden, incontinent, constant nursing care |
| 6 | Dead | N/A |

**Key point**: mRS 0-2 = "good outcome" in clinical trials. mRS 0-1 = "excellent outcome." Primary endpoint in most tPA and thrombectomy trials is mRS 0-2 at 90 days. [BOARD-CORE]

### Hunt and Hess Scale (SAH Grading)

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Asymptomatic, mild headache, slight nuchal rigidity |
| 2 | Moderate-severe headache, nuchal rigidity, no neurologic deficit other than cranial nerve palsy |
| 3 | Drowsiness/confusion, mild focal neurologic deficit |
| 4 | Stupor, moderate-severe hemiparesis |
| 5 | Coma, decerebrate posturing |

[BOARD-CORE]

# SECTION 2: EPIDEMIOLOGY

## 2.1 Incidence and Prevalence (Updated 2025)

### United States Statistics
- Approximately 795,000 people in the U.S. experience a stroke each year (610,000 first-time; 185,000 recurrent) [BOARD-CORE]
- Someone has a stroke every 40 seconds; someone dies from stroke every 3.5 minutes [BOARD-CORE]
- Approximately 7.2 million Americans over age 20 have had a stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- Stroke is the 5th leading cause of death in the U.S. [BOARD-CORE]
- Stroke is the #1 leading cause of long-term adult disability [BOARD-CORE]

### Global Statistics (GBD 2021 Data, published 2024)
- In 2021, there were 11.9 million incident strokes worldwide (AHA/Lancet Neurology, 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 93.8 million people living with effects of stroke globally [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 7.25 million stroke deaths globally in 2021: 3.59 million from ischemic stroke, 3.31 million from ICH, 0.35 million from SAH (GBD 2021, Lancet Neurology 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Stroke is the 2nd leading cause of death worldwide and 3rd leading cause of death and disability combined (DALYs) (WSO Global Stroke Fact Sheet 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 87.2% of stroke deaths and 89.4% of DALYs occur in low- and lower-middle-income countries (GBD 2021) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- From 1990 to 2021: incident strokes increased by 70%, stroke deaths increased by 44%, DALYs increased by 32% globally (GBD 2021) [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Mortality Data
- Approximately 1/4 of stroke patients die within the first year [BOARD-CORE]
- In the first 30 days after stroke, 90% of deaths are from direct brain lesion effects or immobility complications [BOARD-CORE]
- After 6 months, most deaths are cardiovascular (nonstroke) or sudden death [BOARD-CORE]
- Cumulative mortality: 10.5% at 30 days, 21.2% at 1 year, 39.8% at 5 years, 58.4% at 24 years (ARIC study) [BOARD-CORE]
- Mortality from stroke has declined since the 1950s, with a sharp decline in the 1970s related to better HTN treatment [BOARD-CORE]

### Recovery and Disability Outcomes
- 50-70% of stroke survivors regain functional independence [BOARD-CORE]
- Only 10% achieve near-complete recovery [BOARD-CORE]
- 25% recover with minor impairment [BOARD-CORE]
- 40% experience moderate to severe disability [BOARD-CORE]
- 15-30% are permanently disabled [BOARD-CORE]
- 88% of acute stroke patients have hemiparesis; arm is more involved than leg in MCA territory strokes [BOARD-CORE]
- 95% of survivors reach best neurological level within 11 weeks of onset (Copenhagen Stroke Study) [BOARD-CORE]

## 2.2 Economic Burden

- Estimated annual cost of stroke in the U.S.: $34 billion (direct medical costs + lost productivity) [BOARD-CORE]
- Average lifetime cost per ischemic stroke patient: $140,000-$225,000 [BOARD-CORE]
- Global cost of stroke: over US$890 billion per year (0.66% of global GDP), projected to nearly double by 2050 (WSO 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Cost is projected to triple in the U.S. by 2030 as the population ages [BOARD-CORE]

## 2.3 Demographic Risk Patterns

### Age
- Risk doubles each decade after age 55 [BOARD-CORE]
- About 10% of strokes occur in individuals aged 18-50 [BOARD-CORE]

### Sex
- Higher incidence in men overall [BOARD-CORE]
- Women have higher lifetime risk (longer lifespan), higher mortality, and worse outcomes [BOARD-CORE]
- Stroke is more common among women over age 85 [BOARD-CORE]

### Race/Ethnicity Disparities

- African Americans have approximately 2x the risk of first-ever stroke compared to White Americans [BOARD-CORE]
- African Americans have higher stroke mortality at every age and are more likely to be disabled after stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- The Black-White stroke disparity is now WORSENING after decades of steady disparity -- this is driven by higher incidence, not higher case fatality (PMC, 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Hispanic Americans have increasing stroke incidence, driven by rising rates of diabetes and metabolic syndrome; disparities vary by region and Hispanic country of origin (Springer Nature, 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Asian Americans/Pacific Islanders have higher rates of hemorrhagic stroke relative to ischemic stroke compared to White Americans [BOARD-CORE]
- Disparities persist even after controlling for traditional risk factors, suggesting roles of systemic inequities, social determinants of health, and chronic stress [BOARD-CORE]
- Over the last 20 years, stroke deaths and their racial disparities have INCREASED, with nearly 3 in 10 deaths occurring at home, increases particularly among racial minorities and rural residents (ScienceDaily, 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Geographic Disparities

- **Stroke Belt**: Southeastern U.S. region with 30% higher stroke mortality. Includes: NC, SC, GA, AL, MS, TN, AR, LA [BOARD-CORE]
- **Stroke Buckle**: Coastal NC, SC, and GA with up to 40% higher rates [BOARD-CORE]
- Contributing factors: higher HTN/DM/obesity/smoking prevalence, dietary patterns, socioeconomic disparities, limited stroke center access [BOARD-CORE]

## 2.4 Stroke in Young Adults (Increasing Trend)

- From 2011-2013 to 2020-2022, stroke prevalence increased 14.6% among adults aged 18-44 and 15.7% among adults aged 45-64 (CDC MMWR, 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Stroke rates in adults younger than 65 increased by 15% over the last decade (CDC, 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Global trends show rising incidence among ages 15-39, with the trend particularly notable in high-income countries (Frontiers in Neurology, 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Contributing factors: rising obesity, diabetes, hypertension prevalence; opioid epidemic; non-traditional risk factors (PMC, 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- In 40-50% of young adult strokes, no obvious traditional risk factors are found; coagulopathies may account for up to 20% (DeLisa Ch. 18) [BOARD-CORE]

### Causes of Stroke in Young Adults (Board-Relevant List)
- Cerebral embolism (cardiac sources)
- Arterial dissection (carotid/vertebral -- may occur with minimal or no trauma, or after chiropractic manipulation)
- Hypercoagulable states: protein C, protein S, antithrombin III deficiency
- Sickle cell disease
- Vasculopathy: moyamoya disease, SLE, drug-induced vasculitis
- Oral contraceptives (especially combined with smoking or migraine with aura)
- Postpartum period
- Homocystinuria
- AVM/aneurysm rupture
[BOARD-CORE]

# SECTION 3: RISK FACTORS

## 3.1 Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Age | Single most important risk factor worldwide; risk more than doubles each decade after age 55 |
| Sex | Higher incidence in men; higher mortality in women |
| Race | African Americans: 2x first-stroke risk; order: African Americans > Caucasians > Asians |
| Family history/Genetics | Increased risk with first-degree relative history; polygenic risk scores increasingly studied |
| Prior stroke or TIA | Strongest predictor of future stroke; annual recurrence risk ~5%; 5-year cumulative recurrence ~25% |

[ALL BOARD-CORE]

## 3.2 Modifiable Risk Factors

### Hypertension -- #1 Modifiable Risk Factor

- Present in approximately 70% of stroke patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Increases risk 3-5x (sevenfold for both ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke) [BOARD-CORE]
- Population attributable risk: ~35-50% [BOARD-CORE]
- For every 10 mmHg reduction in systolic BP, stroke risk decreases by approximately 33% [BOARD-CORE]
- Lower rates of recurrent stroke with lower blood pressures [BOARD-CORE]
- SPS3 trial: targeting SBP less than 130 mmHg (vs. 130-149) reduced stroke by 20% and ICH by two-thirds [BOARD-CORE]

**2024 Updated BP Targets:**
- SPRINT trial: intensive target (SBP less than 120 mmHg vs. less than 140) reduced cardiovascular events by 25% and death by 27% in high-risk patients without prior stroke or diabetes. Stroke reduction was 11% (not statistically significant as standalone endpoint). (NEJM, 2015; updated analyses 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- ESPRIT trial (2025 analysis): targeting SBP less than 120 mmHg halved the risk of hemorrhagic stroke (JACC, 2025) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- AHA/ASA 2024: Class 1 recommendation for BP target less than 160/110 in pregnant/postpartum patients with severe hypertension to reduce fatal maternal ICH (AHA/ASA 2024 Primary Prevention Guideline) [BOARD-LIKELY]

### Atrial Fibrillation -- #1 Cardiac Cause of Embolic Stroke

- Increases stroke risk 5-fold throughout all ages [BOARD-CORE]
- Population attributable risk: ~15-20% [BOARD-CORE]
- Percentage of strokes attributable to AF increases with age: 1.5% at age 50-59, 23.5% at age 80-89 [BOARD-CORE]
- AF prevalence increases with age: 2% under 50, 15% in 70s, 28% in 80s, 40% over 90 [BOARD-CORE]

**CHA2DS2-VASc Score (Stroke Risk in AF):**

| Component | Points |
|-----------|--------|
| C = CHF | 1 |
| H = Hypertension | 1 |
| A2 = Age >= 75 | 2 |
| D = Diabetes | 1 |
| S2 = Prior Stroke/TIA | 2 |
| V = Vascular disease | 1 |
| A = Age 65-74 | 1 |
| Sc = Sex category (female) | 1 |

- Score >= 2 in men or >= 3 in women: anticoagulate [BOARD-CORE]
- Score 0: no therapy needed [BOARD-CORE]
- Score 1: consider anticoagulation [BOARD-CORE]
- DOACs (dabigatran, rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban) are effective alternatives to warfarin; do not require INR monitoring [BOARD-CORE]

### Diabetes Mellitus

- 2-4x increased stroke risk [BOARD-CORE]
- Risk increases further after 3+ years of disease duration [BOARD-CORE]
- Accelerates micro- and macrovascular disease [BOARD-CORE]
- Population attributable risk: ~5-10% [BOARD-CORE]

### Hyperlipidemia

- LDL-C associated with increased risk, particularly for large artery atherosclerotic subtype [BOARD-CORE]
- Statins reduce recurrent stroke risk and are included in secondary prevention irrespective of lipid levels [BOARD-CORE]
- HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors reduce stroke risk by approximately 30% [BOARD-CORE]

### Smoking

- Current smokers: 2-4x increased risk vs. nonsmokers or those who quit more than 10 years [BOARD-CORE]
- Risk factor for both ischemic stroke and SAH [BOARD-CORE]
- Smoking cessation reduces risk to near-baseline within 5 years [BOARD-CORE]

### Carotid Artery Stenosis

- Greater than 70% symptomatic stenosis: ~13% annual stroke risk without intervention [BOARD-CORE]
- Carotid endarterectomy (CEA) reduces this to ~2% [BOARD-CORE]
- CEA beneficial for 50-69% symptomatic stenosis (absolute risk reduction 4.6%) and highly beneficial for 70-99% stenosis (absolute risk reduction 16%) [BOARD-CORE]
- Carotid stenting: alternative to CEA; somewhat higher periprocedural risk in patients over 70, but comparable in those under 70 [BOARD-CORE]
- Asymptomatic stenosis 60-99%: CEA may be considered but evidence less compelling [BOARD-CORE]

### Other Modifiable Risk Factors

- **Obesity and physical inactivity**: physical inactivity alone has a relative risk of 2.7x and population attributable risk of ~30% [BOARD-CORE]
- **Excessive alcohol**: greater than 2 drinks/day = relative risk 0.51 (protective); greater than 7 drinks/day = relative risk 2.96 (Sacco et al., 1999) [BOARD-CORE]
- **Obstructive sleep apnea**: independent risk factor; intermittent hypoxia promotes atherosclerosis and AF [BOARD-CORE]
- **Migraine with aura**: doubles stroke risk in young women, especially combined with OCPs and smoking [BOARD-CORE]
- **Sickle cell disease**: major risk factor especially in children [BOARD-CORE]
- **Patent foramen ovale (PFO)**: associated with cryptogenic stroke via paradoxical embolism [BOARD-CORE]
- **Oral contraceptives**: increased risk when combined with smoking or migraine with aura [BOARD-CORE]

### Hypercoagulable States Associated with Stroke

- Elevated RBC count, hematocrit, fibrinogen
- Protein S and C deficiencies
- Antithrombin III deficiency
- Sickle cell anemia
- Cancer-associated hypercoagulability
- Factor V Leiden mutation
[BOARD-CORE]

# SECTION 4: NEW DEVELOPMENTS IN RISK FACTOR MANAGEMENT (2020-2026)

## 4.1 The 2024 AHA/ASA Primary Prevention of Stroke Guideline

This guideline replaces the 2014 version and covers 7 major updated domains (AHA/ASA, Stroke, 2024): [BOARD-LIKELY]

1. **Dietary Modification**: Class 1 (strong) recommendation for Mediterranean diet; formal discussion of potassium salt substitution for stroke prevention
2. **GLP-1 Receptor Agonists**: Class 1A recommendation for use in patients with type 2 diabetes with high-risk or established cardiovascular disease to reduce stroke risk (based on SUSTAIN-6, REWIND trials)
3. **Blood Pressure Targets**: Updated intensive targets; Life's Essential 8 framework
4. **Lipid-Lowering Medications**: Expanded indications; earlier initiation of residual risk-lowering therapies
5. **Antithrombotic Agents**: Refined guidance on aspirin use for primary prevention
6. **Colchicine Therapy**: New anti-inflammatory approach for ASCVD patients
7. **Sex-Specific Preventive Risk Assessment**: Screening for premature ovarian failure, early-onset menopause; pregnancy-specific BP targets

**Life's Essential 8**: Updated from Life's Simple 7, now includes sleep as a core component alongside diet, physical activity, tobacco avoidance, healthy weight, blood glucose, cholesterol, and blood pressure. [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 4.2 GLP-1 Receptor Agonists: A Paradigm Shift

- Meta-analysis of 11 cardiovascular outcome trials (82,140 participants): GLP-1 receptor agonists achieved a 16% relative reduction in stroke risk vs. placebo (PMC, 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Semaglutide received EU approval in 2025 as first stroke management therapy based on SOUL trial data: 14% reduction in cardiovascular death, MI, and stroke vs. placebo when added to standard care in T2DM patients (EMA, 2025) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- SELECT trial (NEJM, 2023): semaglutide reduced MACE in obese patients WITHOUT diabetes [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Post-hoc REWIND analysis: dulaglutide reduced ischemic stroke risk (HR 0.75) in T2DM (AHA Journals) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Stroke survivors on GLP-1 RAs or SGLT2i had lower risk of subsequent stroke, MI, or death vs. non-users over 3-year follow-up (AHA 2024 Scientific Sessions) [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 4.3 Colchicine: Anti-Inflammatory Stroke Prevention

- Colchicine 0.5 mg daily (Lodoco) received FDA approval June 2023 to reduce MI, stroke, revascularization, and CV death in patients with established ASCVD (FDA, 2023) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- COLCOT trial: 23% overall relative risk reduction; stroke specifically reduced by 74% (HR 0.26, 95% CI 0.10-0.70) (NEJM, 2019; meta-analysis 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- LoDoCo2 trial: 31% relative risk reduction in composite cardiovascular endpoint in stable CAD [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Meta-analysis: 46% stroke risk reduction (HR 0.54, 95% CI 0.34-0.86) across trials (European Heart Journal, 2025) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- AHA/ASA 2024: adding low-dose colchicine to intensive statin therapy may be reasonable to reduce ischemic stroke risk in patients with recent MI [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 4.4 Emerging Biomarkers for Stroke Risk

**Predictive biomarkers** (vascular risk/inflammatory priming): [BOARD-EMERGING]
- IL-6, ADMA, endothelial microparticles (EMP), homocysteine
- Lipoprotein(a) -- promotes thrombosis via platelet and coagulation effects
- Elevated RDW (red blood cell distribution width) -- indicates chronic inflammation/oxidative stress
- MR-proANP -- significantly associated with cardioembolic stroke subtype

**Diagnostic biomarkers** (acute phase 0-3 hours): [BOARD-EMERGING]
- GFAP, microRNAs (miR-124), D-dimer -- enable stroke subtype differentiation
- NSE (neuron-specific enolase) -- estimates lesion burden

**Prognostic biomarkers**: [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Serum neurofilament light chain (sNfL) -- correlates with infarct size and long-term outcomes
- NT-pro-BNP -- associated with cardioembolism and prognosis
- GDF-15, tau protein -- correlate with chronic damage

(Sources: PMC 2025; OAE Publish 2025; multiple systematic reviews)

## 4.5 Genetic Risk Factors and Polygenic Risk Scores

- GIGASTROKE Consortium identified 18 genetic loci for stroke recurrence across African and European ancestries, including PPARGC1B, CCDC3, OPRL1, MYH11 (Frontiers in Stroke, 2024) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Chinese cohort study: comparing top vs. bottom 5% of polygenic risk score showed hazard ratio of 3.01 for stroke (Springer Nature, 2023) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Japanese metaGRS predicted stroke recurrence, particularly in patients without hypertension (PMC, 2025) [BOARD-EMERGING]
- Current limitation: PRS performance varies by ancestry; not yet validated for clinical use [BOARD-EMERGING]

## 4.6 Environmental Risk Factors

- Air pollution is now recognized as a top 5 global risk factor for stroke: high SBP, particulate matter air pollution, smoking, high LDL cholesterol, and household air pollution (GBD 2021, Lancet Neurology 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Ambient particulate matter air pollution contributes to 14% of death and disability from SAH specifically (IHME/Lancet 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- 84% of global stroke burden in 2021 was attributable to 23 modifiable risk factors, including air pollution [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Short-term hourly exposure to PM2.5, PM10, NO2, and SO2 associated with increased stroke hospital admissions (Stroke, AHA Journals 2024) [BOARD-LIKELY]

## 4.7 Lifestyle Interventions: Updated Evidence

- Up to 90% of strokes may be preventable by addressing 10 modifiable risk factors, half of which are lifestyle-related (INTERSTROKE study reaffirmed) [BOARD-CORE]
- Mediterranean diet: Class 1 (strong) recommendation for primary stroke prevention (AHA/ASA 2024). Long-term study shows significantly lower stroke risk in women closely following Mediterranean diet (ScienceDaily, 2026) [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Potassium salt substitution: formally discussed in 2024 guidelines as a stroke prevention strategy [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Sleep: added to AHA Life's Essential 8; both short and long sleep duration associated with increased stroke risk [BOARD-LIKELY]
- Physical activity: 150+ min/week of moderate-intensity aerobic exercise recommended; population attributable risk of inactivity is ~30% [BOARD-CORE]

# SECTION 5: TRANSIENT ISCHEMIC ATTACK (TIA)

## 5.1 Definition and Key Facts

- **Classic definition**: focal neurological deficits lasting less than 24 hours (most resolve within 1 hour) [BOARD-CORE]
- **Modern tissue-based definition**: transient symptoms WITHOUT evidence of infarction on MRI DWI [BOARD-CORE]
- TIA is a medical emergency: 10-15% of TIA patients will have a stroke within 90 days [BOARD-CORE]
- Greatest risk of stroke is within the first 48 hours after TIA [BOARD-CORE]
- Up to 1/3 of untreated TIA patients will eventually have a stroke [BOARD-CORE]
- Approximately 12% of all strokes are heralded by a TIA [BOARD-CORE]
- TIA prevalence: 2.7% in men 65-69, 3.6% in men 75-79 [BOARD-CORE]
- 5% of TIA patients will have a stroke within 1 month; 14% within 1 year if untreated [BOARD-CORE]
- Amaurosis fugax (transient monocular blindness): a type of TIA involving the ophthalmic/retinal artery [BOARD-CORE]

## 5.2 ABCD2 Score -- TIA Risk Stratification

| Criteria | Points |
|----------|--------|
| A -- Age >= 60 years | 1 |
| B -- Blood pressure >= 140/90 at presentation | 1 |
| C -- Clinical: unilateral weakness | 2 |
| C -- Clinical: speech impairment without weakness | 1 |
| D -- Duration >= 60 minutes | 2 |
| D -- Duration 10-59 minutes | 1 |
| D2 -- Diabetes present | 1 |

**Interpretation:**
- Score 0-3 = low risk (less than 1% 2-day stroke risk) [BOARD-CORE]
- Score 4-5 = moderate risk (~4% 2-day risk) [BOARD-CORE]
- Score 6-7 = high risk (~8% 2-day risk) [BOARD-CORE]
- Score >= 4: admit for observation and urgent workup [BOARD-CORE]

## 5.3 TIA Workup

- Emergent non-contrast CT to rule out hemorrhage [BOARD-CORE]
- MRI with DWI to confirm absence of infarct (defines TIA vs. stroke) [BOARD-CORE]
- Vascular imaging: CTA, MRA, or carotid ultrasound [BOARD-CORE]
- Cardiac evaluation: ECG, telemetry, echocardiography [BOARD-CORE]
- Labs: CBC, BMP, coagulation studies, lipid panel, HbA1c [BOARD-CORE]

## 5.4 TIA Management

- Antiplatelet therapy: aspirin 325 mg acutely, then 81 mg daily [BOARD-CORE]
- Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT: aspirin + clopidogrel) for 21 days may reduce early stroke risk in high-risk TIA (CHANCE and POINT trials) [BOARD-CORE]
- Statin therapy for all patients with atherosclerotic disease [BOARD-CORE]
- Blood pressure management to target less than 130/80 mmHg [BOARD-CORE]
- Carotid endarterectomy if ipsilateral carotid stenosis greater than 70% [BOARD-CORE]
- Anticoagulation if atrial fibrillation identified [BOARD-CORE]

## 5.5 TIA Mimics vs True TIA

| Feature | True TIA | TIA Mimic |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Onset | Sudden, maximal at onset | Gradual, migratory, or fluctuating |
| Symptoms | Negative (loss of function) | Positive (tingling, flashing lights) |
| Duration | Usually less than 1 hour | Variable, may last hours |
| Risk factors | Vascular risk factors present | Younger, history of migraine or seizure |
| Common mimics | N/A | Migraine aura, focal seizure, hypoglycemia, peripheral vertigo, anxiety |

[BOARD-CORE]

# SECTION 6: HEMORRHAGIC STROKE DETAILS

## 6.1 Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH)

- 13% of all strokes; linked to chronic HTN (>1/3 occur in normotensives) [BOARD-CORE]
- Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms: "false" aneurysms from arterial wall dilations secondary to HTN [BOARD-CORE]
- Symptoms: sudden headache, vomiting (22-44%), seizures (10% in first few days), LOC [BOARD-CORE]
- Nuchal rigidity common if blood extends to ventricles/subarachnoid space [BOARD-CORE]

**ICH by Location:**

| Location | Features |
|----------|----------|
| Putamen (most common) | Hemiplegia from internal capsule compression; vomiting in ~50% |
| Thalamus | Hemiplegia, contralateral sensory loss; aphasia if dominant side; hemineglect if nondominant |
| Pons | Deep coma in minutes; pinpoint pupils; decerebrate rigidity leads to death; may survive if less than 1 cm |
| Cerebellum | Hours to develop; occipital headache, vomiting, vertigo; CN6 palsy; urgent surgical evacuation may be lifesaving |
| Lobar (cerebral) | Variable by lobe involved; associated with cerebral amyloid angiopathy in elderly |

[ALL BOARD-CORE]

- **Cerebral amyloid angiopathy**: 5-20% of ICH; most common after age 65; lobar (not deep) hemorrhages; hemosiderin on MRI; no specific treatment available [BOARD-CORE]

## 6.2 Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)

- ~3% of all strokes; typically from ruptured saccular (berry) aneurysm [BOARD-CORE]
- 90-95% of saccular aneurysms on anterior Circle of Willis [BOARD-CORE]
- Rupture threshold: generally >= 10 mm; peak age 5th-6th decade [BOARD-CORE]
- Rupture occurs during activity (not sleep) [BOARD-CORE]
- "Sentinel headache": sudden, intense headache preceding SAH by days to weeks in ~50% of patients [BOARD-CORE]
- Mortality: 25% in first 24 hours; risk of rebleeding 25% in first month, 2.2% per year after [BOARD-CORE]
- **Vasospasm**: occurs in ~25% of cases; days 3-12 post-rupture (peak day 7); treated with nimodipine (calcium channel blocker) [BOARD-CORE]
- Hydrocephalus: may develop immediately (intraventricular hemorrhage) or weeks later (arachnoiditis from blood in CSF) [BOARD-CORE]

## 6.3 Arteriovenous Malformations (AVMs)

- Congenital tangled mass of dilated vessels; abnormal arterial-venous communication [BOARD-CORE]
- 40-50% lifetime hemorrhage risk; annual bleed risk ~4% [BOARD-CORE]
- Hemorrhage is initial presentation in ~50% of cases [BOARD-CORE]
- Treatment: surgical excision, proton beam therapy, or embolization [BOARD-CORE]

# SECTION 7: DIAGNOSTIC STUDIES

## 7.1 Imaging in Acute Stroke

| Study | Acute Infarct | Hemorrhage |
|-------|--------------|------------|
| CT | Dark (hypodense); NOT seen immediately (may be normal first 24-48 hours); best at 3-4 days | White (hyperdense); seen immediately in 100% of cases |
| MRI DWI | Most sensitive and specific for acute infarct; detects within minutes of onset | Acute: dark/isointense on T1 and T2 |
| MRI T2 | Bright (hyperintense) due to edema; more pronounced at 24 hours to 7 days | Signal changes depend on chronicity |

[ALL BOARD-CORE]

**Key board points:**
- Non-contrast CT is the initial study of choice to RULE OUT hemorrhage (do not use contrast -- blood and contrast both appear white) [BOARD-CORE]
- MRI DWI is the most sensitive and specific imaging for acute ischemic infarct [BOARD-CORE]
- CT is less sensitive than MRI for detecting lacunar infarcts, especially infratentorial [BOARD-CORE]
- SAH: CT positive in 90% within 4-5 days; if negative, lumbar puncture is indicated [BOARD-CORE]

## 7.2 Vascular Imaging

- Carotid ultrasound: screening for carotid stenosis; useful for follow-up [BOARD-CORE]
- Transcranial Doppler: evaluates basal cranial vessels; detects vasospasm and intracranial collateral pathways [BOARD-CORE]
- CTA/MRA: evaluates extracranial and intracranial circulation [BOARD-CORE]
- Conventional angiography: gold standard but increasingly reserved for interventional procedures [BOARD-CORE]

## 7.3 Cardiac Evaluation

- ECG and telemetry: detect AF and other arrhythmias [BOARD-CORE]
- Transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE): initial cardiac evaluation [BOARD-CORE]
- Transesophageal echocardiogram (TEE): superior for left atrium, mitral valve, aortic arch; "bubble study" for PFO [BOARD-CORE]
- Implantable loop recorder: for detecting occult/paroxysmal AF in cryptogenic stroke [BOARD-CORE]

# SECTION 8: RECOVERY PRINCIPLES (Board-Relevant Fundamentals)

## 8.1 Recovery Timeline

- Motor recovery: 95% of stroke survivors reach best neurological level within 11 weeks (Copenhagen Stroke Study) [BOARD-CORE]
- Mild strokes recover faster; severe strokes reach plateau at ~15 weeks [BOARD-CORE]
- Only minor additional measurable motor improvement after 6 months post-onset [BOARD-CORE]
- Language recovery is slower and more prolonged than motor recovery (improvements may continue more than 1 year) [BOARD-CORE]
- Hemispatial neglect: substantial but not necessarily complete recovery; follows pattern of proportional recovery [BOARD-CORE]
- Visual field defects: if persistent beyond a few weeks, late recovery is unlikely [BOARD-CORE]

## 8.2 Prognostic Factors for Motor Recovery

- Severity of arm weakness at onset and timing of hand recovery are key predictors [BOARD-CORE]
- Complete arm paralysis at onset with no grasp by 4 weeks: poor prognosis for useful hand function [BOARD-CORE]
- Some hand motor recovery by 4 weeks: up to 70% will make good recovery [BOARD-CORE]
- Corticospinal tract lesion load on imaging predicts recovery [BOARD-CORE]
- MCA territory strokes: arm more impaired than leg due to somatotopic organization [BOARD-CORE]
- ACA territory strokes: leg more impaired than arm (pattern reversed from MCA) [BOARD-CORE]

## 8.3 Neuroplasticity Mechanisms

- Early improvement (days to weeks): resolution of penumbra ischemia, edema, diaschisis [BOARD-CORE]
- Later improvement (weeks to months): cortical reorganization, synaptogenesis, axonal sprouting [BOARD-CORE]
- Use-dependent plasticity: intensive training drives cortical map reorganization [BOARD-CORE]
- Chronic stroke survivors can still improve motor function with intensive exercise programs -- this represents motor learning rather than neurological recovery per se [BOARD-CORE]
