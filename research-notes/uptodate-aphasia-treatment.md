# Aphasia Treatment and Recovery — UpToDate Summary

## Epidemiology
- 15-38% of ischemic stroke patients develop aphasia

## Recovery Timeline
- Most improvement occurs in the first few months
- Recovery typically plateaus after 1 year
- Early recovery driven by reperfusion of ischemic penumbra
- Later recovery driven by cortical reorganization and neuroplasticity

## Prognostic Factors
- Severity at onset: strongest predictor of long-term deficit
- Younger age: better prognosis
- Higher Barthel index (less overall disability): better prognosis
- Higher education level: better prognosis

## Speech-Language Therapy
- Intensive therapy (>2 hours/week) is more effective than less intensive therapy
- **CIAT (Constraint-Induced Aphasia Therapy)**: shows benefit, analogous to CIMT for motor recovery
  - CIAT combined with memantine has shown additional benefit

## Pharmacologic Interventions
- No pharmacologic intervention has proven long-term benefit
- **FLAME trial**: fluoxetine initially appeared to help aphasia recovery
- **FOCUS, AFFINITY, EFFECTS trials**: SSRIs showed NO benefit for post-stroke recovery (including aphasia)
  - These large RCTs effectively refuted the FLAME findings

## Neuromodulation
- **TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation)**: promising but not standard clinical practice
- **tDCS (transcranial direct current stimulation)**: improves naming ability but NOT functional communication (per Cochrane review)

## Depression Monitoring
- Depression is common post-stroke and directly interferes with aphasia recovery
- Screen and treat depression in all aphasia patients

## Key Board Pearls
- Severity at onset is the strongest predictor of aphasia outcome
- Intensive speech therapy (>2h/week) is superior to less intensive therapy
- SSRIs do NOT improve aphasia or stroke recovery (FOCUS/AFFINITY/EFFECTS)
- CIAT is the best-studied specific aphasia therapy approach
- tDCS improves naming but not functional communication
- Always screen for and treat comorbid depression
