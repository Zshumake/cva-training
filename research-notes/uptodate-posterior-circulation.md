# Posterior Circulation Stroke — UpToDate Summary

## Epidemiology
- 20% of all ischemic events involve posterior circulation

## Wallenberg Syndrome (Lateral Medullary)
- Most common syndrome from intracranial vertebral artery (ICVA) occlusion
- Vestibulocerebellar symptoms nearly always present
- Ipsilateral face pain/temperature loss + contralateral body pain/temperature loss (crossed sensory findings)
- Dysphagia and hoarseness (nucleus ambiguus involvement)
- Ipsilateral Horner syndrome
- Ondine curse: failure of automatic respiration (central hypoventilation)

## Medial Medullary Syndrome
- Contralateral hemiparesis (corticospinal tract)
- Ipsilateral tongue paralysis (hypoglossal nucleus/nerve)

## Cerebellar Syndromes
- **PICA infarction**: can cause pseudotumoral mass effect with brainstem compression — may require surgical decompression
- **AICA syndrome**: vertigo, ipsilateral hearing loss (internal auditory artery), facial weakness

## Basilar Artery Occlusion
- Motor deficits + oculomotor deficits
- Locked-in syndrome: quadriplegia + anarthria with preserved consciousness and vertical eye movements

## Top of the Basilar Syndrome
- Altered alertness, behavioral changes, memory deficits
- Oculomotor and pupillary abnormalities
- Peduncular hallucinations (vivid visual hallucinations)

## Subclavian Steal Syndrome
- Retrograde flow through vertebral artery due to proximal subclavian stenosis
- Neurologic symptoms are uncommon unless concurrent carotid disease exists

## PCA Territory Infarction
- Hemianopia: most common finding
- Left PCA: alexia without agraphia, Gerstmann syndrome (acalculia, agraphia, finger agnosia, right-left confusion)
- Right PCA: prosopagnosia (inability to recognize faces)
- Fetal PCA variant: PCA supplied by carotid — carotid disease can cause PCA territory infarction

## Special Vascular Anatomy
- **Artery of Percheron**: single artery supplying bilateral thalami — occlusion causes bilateral thalamic infarction

## Diagnostic Pearls
- **HINTS test** (Head Impulse-Nystagmus-Test of Skew): more sensitive than MRI for detecting acute posterior fossa stroke in the first 48 hours
  - Central pattern: normal head impulse, direction-changing nystagmus, skew deviation present

## Key Board Pearls
- Wallenberg = crossed sensory loss + Horner + dysphagia + vertigo/ataxia
- HINTS exam outperforms early MRI for posterior fossa stroke
- Locked-in syndrome: patient is conscious — vertical eye movements and blinking preserved
- Artery of Percheron occlusion = bilateral thalamic infarcts from single vessel
- Cerebellar PICA infarct can present as surgical emergency due to mass effect
- Fetal PCA variant means carotid disease can cause occipital lobe stroke
