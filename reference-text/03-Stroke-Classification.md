## Section 1: Overview of Stroke Classification

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The fundamental division in stroke classification is between ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke:

| Category | Percentage | Mechanism | Subtypes |
|----------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Ischemic stroke | 87% | Arterial occlusion depriving brain tissue of blood flow | Thrombotic, embolic, lacunar, cryptogenic |
| Hemorrhagic stroke | 13% | Rupture of a blood vessel with bleeding into or around the brain | ICH (~10%), SAH (~3%) |

Note that globally the proportions differ somewhat: approximately 65% ischemic, 29% ICH, and 6% SAH. The higher proportion of ischemic strokes in the US reflects differences in risk factor profiles and population demographics.

**Ischemic Stroke Subtypes by Frequency:**

| Subtype | Approximate Percentage of Ischemic Strokes | Key Feature |
|---------|----------------------------------------------|-------------|
| Thrombotic | ~48% | In-situ clot formation on atherosclerotic plaque; gradual onset, often during sleep |
| Embolic | ~26% | Clot travels from a distant source (usually cardiac); sudden onset during activity |
| Lacunar | ~15-26% | Small vessel disease; small deep infarcts <15 mm; no cortical signs |
| Cryptogenic/Other | ~13-25% | No identifiable cause despite complete workup |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The most commonly tested distinction is between thrombotic and embolic stroke. Thrombotic = gradual onset, stuttering progression, occurs during sleep, preceded by TIAs. Embolic = sudden onset, maximal deficit at presentation, occurs during activity, no preceding TIAs. This distinction is tested repeatedly on board examinations.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**The Ischemic Cascade:**

When cerebral blood flow drops below the threshold for neuronal survival, a complex series of biochemical events unfolds:

1. **Energy failure:** ATP depletion within minutes of severe ischemia leads to failure of the Na+/K+-ATPase pump
2. **Ionic imbalance:** Potassium efflux and sodium/calcium influx cause neuronal depolarization
3. **Excitotoxicity:** Uncontrolled release of glutamate overstimulates NMDA and AMPA receptors, causing massive calcium influx
4. **Calcium overload:** Intracellular calcium activates destructive enzymes (proteases, lipases, endonucleases)
5. **Free radical damage:** Reactive oxygen species damage cell membranes, proteins, and DNA
6. **Inflammation:** Activated microglia and infiltrating leukocytes propagate tissue injury
7. **Apoptosis:** Programmed cell death extends the zone of injury beyond the initial infarct core

The penumbra (tissue with CBF between 10-20 mL/100g/min) remains viable for a period of hours depending on the degree of residual blood flow and the robustness of collateral circulation. This is the physiological basis for the "time is brain" concept: an estimated 1.9 million neurons are lost every minute during an untreated large vessel occlusion.

## Section 2: TOAST Classification System

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The TOAST (Trial of Org 10172 in Acute Stroke Treatment) classification system, published by Adams et al. in 1993, remains the most widely used framework for categorizing ischemic stroke etiology. It guides treatment selection and prognostication.

| TOAST Category | Description | Required Findings | Treatment Implications |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Large artery atherosclerosis | Stenosis >50% of a major cerebral artery or its branches with infarct in the territory of that artery | Vascular imaging showing stenosis or occlusion; cortical, cerebellar, or subcortical infarct >15 mm | Antiplatelet + statin + possible CEA/stenting |
| Cardioembolism | Cardiac source of embolism identified | At least one high-risk cardiac source (AF, mechanical valve, recent MI, etc.) | Anticoagulation |
| Small vessel occlusion (lacunar) | Small deep infarct compatible with a lacunar syndrome | Subcortical infarct <15 mm; no cortical signs; no high-risk cardiac source | Single antiplatelet + BP control |
| Stroke of other determined etiology | Non-atherosclerotic vasculopathy, hypercoagulable state, dissection, etc. | Specific etiology identified | Etiology-specific treatment |
| Stroke of undetermined etiology (cryptogenic) | No etiology identified despite complete workup; or multiple potential etiologies | Complete workup inconclusive | Extended cardiac monitoring; antiplatelet therapy |

**High-Risk Cardiac Sources of Embolism:**

| Source | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Atrial fibrillation | Most common source; 5-fold risk increase |
| Rheumatic mitral valve disease | Classic in developing countries |
| Prosthetic heart valves | Mechanical > bioprosthetic risk |
| Recent myocardial infarction | Particularly anterior wall MI with wall motion abnormality |
| Left ventricular thrombus | Seen with EF <28% or dilated cardiomyopathy |
| Infective endocarditis | Septic emboli; multiple small infarcts with fever and new murmur |
| Atrial myxoma | Rare; classically produces embolic stroke in young patient |
| Patent foramen ovale with atrial septal aneurysm | Paradoxical embolism; important in young cryptogenic stroke |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Atrial fibrillation is the most common cardiac source of embolism. Approximately 75% of cardiac emboli travel to the brain (rather than to other organs). When you see multiple infarcts in different vascular territories on imaging, think cardiac embolism first. The presence of simultaneous anterior and posterior circulation infarcts essentially rules out a single-vessel thrombotic mechanism.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**SSS-TOAST and CCS Refinements:**

The original TOAST classification has been refined through the SSS-TOAST (Stop Stroke Study TOAST) and CCS (Causative Classification System) frameworks. These refinements add subcategories of "evident," "probable," and "possible" for each TOAST category, improving interrater reliability and reducing the proportion of strokes classified as "undetermined."

**ISPS25 (Ischemic Stroke Phenotyping System, Yaghi et al. 2025):**

The ISPS25 represents the most recent evolution in stroke classification. Published by Yaghi et al. in Stroke in 2025, it updates the TOAST framework with a more granular phenotyping approach.

Key features of ISPS25:
- Emphasizes a standardized minimum evaluation that all ischemic stroke patients should undergo
- Requires brain imaging, vascular imaging, 12-lead ECG plus 24-hour cardiac monitoring, transthoracic echocardiography with agitated saline (bubble study), and standard laboratory workup
- Provides a more structured approach to integrating findings from the complete evaluation
- Aims to reduce the proportion of strokes classified as "cryptogenic" through systematic and thorough evaluation

## Section 3: Thrombotic Stroke

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Thrombotic stroke is the most common subtype of ischemic stroke, accounting for approximately 48% of ischemic strokes. It results from in-situ thrombus formation on a pre-existing atherosclerotic plaque within a cerebral or cervical artery.

**Key Clinical Features:**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Onset | Gradual; stuttering or stepwise progression over hours to days |
| Timing | Classically occurs during sleep or periods of rest (~48% onset during sleep) |
| Deficit progression | Waxing and waning ("stroke-in-evolution"); symptoms may fluctuate before stabilizing |
| Preceding TIAs | Common; TIAs typically occur in the same vascular territory |
| Mechanism | In-situ clot formation on atherosclerotic plaque in a large or medium-sized artery |
| Typical territory | Single vascular territory |

**Pathophysiology of Thrombotic Stroke:**

1. Atherosclerotic plaque develops over years at arterial bifurcations, curves, and branch points
2. Plaque rupture or endothelial erosion exposes subendothelial collagen and tissue factor
3. Platelet adhesion and activation initiate the formation of a platelet-rich "white thrombus"
4. Activation of the coagulation cascade generates thrombin and fibrin, forming a "red thrombus"
5. Progressive luminal narrowing leads to hemodynamically significant stenosis
6. Complete occlusion produces ischemia in the territory supplied by that artery
7. Collateral circulation may partially compensate, explaining the gradual and fluctuating onset

**Common Sites of Thrombosis:**

| Location | Clinical Significance |
|----------|---------------------|
| ICA bifurcation (extracranial) | Most common extracranial site; source of artery-to-artery embolism |
| MCA mainstem or branches | Most common intracranial site |
| Vertebrobasilar junction | Posterior circulation thrombosis |
| Origin of the vertebral arteries | Common atherosclerotic site |
| Intracranial ICA | Associated with higher recurrence risk |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Thrombotic strokes characteristically occur during sleep (approximately 48% of cases) because decreased blood pressure and cardiac output during nocturnal hours create a low-flow state that promotes thrombosis in an already stenotic artery. The patient classically "wakes up with a deficit." This is one of the most commonly tested distinctions between thrombotic and embolic stroke.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Large Artery Atherosclerosis vs. Small Vessel Disease:**

Thrombotic stroke encompasses two distinct entities that share the common mechanism of in-situ thrombosis but differ in vessel size and clinical presentation:

1. **Large vessel thrombosis:** Affects the extracranial and intracranial large arteries (ICA, MCA main stem, vertebral arteries, basilar artery). Produces cortical and large subcortical infarcts. The mechanism is atherosclerosis with plaque rupture and superimposed thrombosis, or artery-to-artery embolism from an unstable plaque.

2. **Small vessel thrombosis (lacunar):** Affects the small penetrating arteries (lenticulostriate arteries, thalamoperforating arteries, pontine perforators). Produces small (<15 mm), deep infarcts. The mechanism is lipohyalinosis or branch atheromatous disease from chronic hypertension.

These two subtypes have different risk factor profiles, treatment strategies, and prognoses, which is why the TOAST classification separates them.

**Intracranial vs. Extracranial Atherosclerosis:**

An important racial disparity exists in the distribution of atherosclerosis:
- White Americans more commonly have extracranial carotid disease
- African Americans, Hispanic Americans, and Asian Americans more commonly have intracranial atherosclerosis
- The Northern Manhattan Study demonstrated that the relative rate of intracranial atherosclerosis in Black versus White individuals is approximately 5.85 times higher

This distinction has treatment implications: extracranial carotid stenosis is amenable to CEA or stenting, while intracranial atherosclerosis is primarily managed medically (the SAMMPRIS trial showed medical therapy was superior to intracranial stenting for most patients with intracranial stenosis).

## Section 4: Embolic Stroke

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Embolic stroke accounts for approximately 26% of ischemic strokes and is caused by a clot (or other material) that forms at a distant site, travels through the bloodstream, and lodges in a cerebral artery, causing acute occlusion.

**Key Clinical Features:**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Onset | Sudden; maximal deficit at onset |
| Timing | Typically occurs during activity (not during sleep) |
| Deficit progression | Deficits are maximal immediately; may improve as embolus lyses or migrates |
| Preceding TIAs | Less common than with thrombotic stroke |
| Mechanism | Clot or material travels from distant source to occlude cerebral artery |
| Typical territory | May affect multiple vascular territories simultaneously (embolic shower) |
| Hemorrhagic transformation | More common than with thrombotic stroke |

**Sources of Embolism:**

Approximately 75% of cardiac emboli travel to the brain rather than to other organs, reflecting the high proportion of cardiac output directed to the cerebral circulation.

| Source Category | Specific Sources |
|----------------|-----------------|
| Cardiac (most common) | AF, valvular disease, recent MI, left ventricular thrombus, dilated cardiomyopathy, endocarditis, atrial myxoma |
| Arterial (artery-to-artery) | Unstable carotid plaque, aortic arch atheroma |
| Paradoxical | Venous thrombus crossing through PFO or ASD to reach arterial circulation |
| Other | Fat embolism, air embolism, tumor embolism |

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Thrombotic vs. Embolic Stroke: Complete Comparison**

| Feature | Thrombotic | Embolic |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Onset | Gradual, stuttering, during sleep | Sudden, maximal deficit at onset, during activity |
| Preceding TIAs | Common, same territory | Less common |
| Deficit progression | Stepwise worsening over hours to days | Maximal at onset, may improve as embolus migrates |
| Hemorrhagic transformation | Less common | More common (reperfusion of damaged tissue after embolus lyses) |
| Multiple territories | Usually single territory | May affect multiple territories (embolic shower) |
| Underlying cause | Local atherosclerosis | Distant source (cardiac, aortic, arterial) |
| Typical vessel affected | ICA, MCA (at atherosclerotic sites) | MCA (most common destination of emboli) |
| CT/MRI pattern | Wedge-shaped infarct in single territory | May show multiple acute infarcts in different territories |

**Hemorrhagic Transformation:**

Embolic strokes have a higher rate of hemorrhagic transformation than thrombotic strokes. This occurs because the embolus initially causes ischemia and vascular endothelial damage in its territory. When the embolus lyses or migrates (spontaneously or after reperfusion therapy), blood flow is restored through damaged vessels, leading to extravasation of blood into the infarcted tissue. Hemorrhagic transformation ranges from petechial hemorrhagic infarction (usually clinically silent) to parenchymal hematoma (potentially symptomatic and life-threatening).

**Embolic Shower and Multiple Territory Infarcts:**

When imaging reveals simultaneous acute infarcts in multiple vascular territories (both anterior and posterior circulation, or bilateral hemispheres), this pattern strongly suggests a cardiac source of embolism. A single embolic event from the heart can shower multiple emboli to different cerebral vessels simultaneously. This finding should prompt urgent echocardiography and extended cardiac monitoring.

The combination of multiple small infarcts + fever + new cardiac murmur should raise immediate concern for infective endocarditis with septic emboli, requiring blood cultures and TEE.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Multiple acute infarcts in different vascular territories on DWI MRI = think cardiac embolism. This pattern effectively rules out a single-vessel thrombotic etiology and demands thorough cardiac evaluation (TTE, TEE, extended monitoring). If accompanied by fever and new murmur, think infective endocarditis until proven otherwise.

## Section 5: Lacunar Stroke

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Lacunar infarcts are small (2-15 mm), deep, non-cortical infarcts caused by occlusion of a single penetrating artery. They account for approximately 15-26% of all ischemic strokes. The term "lacune" derives from the French word for "lake," describing the small, fluid-filled cavities that remain after the infarcted tissue is resorbed.

**Key Characteristics:**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Size | 2-15 mm in diameter |
| Location | Deep structures: basal ganglia, thalamus, internal capsule, corona radiata, pons |
| Mechanism | Lipohyalinosis (most common, hypertension-related) or branch atheromatous disease |
| Cortical signs | ABSENT -- no aphasia, neglect, apraxia, or hemianopia |
| Imaging | CT sensitivity only 30-44%; MRI DWI is much more sensitive |

**Pathophysiology:**

Lipohyalinosis is the most common mechanism of lacunar infarction. Chronic hypertension causes fibrinoid necrosis and hyaline deposition in the walls of small penetrating arteries, leading to progressive luminal narrowing and eventual occlusion. Branch atheromatous disease (atherosclerotic plaque at the ostium of a penetrating artery) is another mechanism. Embolism can rarely cause a lacunar-appearing infarct, but this is uncommon.

**The Five Classic Lacunar Syndromes:**

| Syndrome | Frequency | Deficits | Typical Lesion Location |
|----------|-----------|---------|------------------------|
| **Pure motor hemiparesis** | 45-57% (most common) | Contralateral face, arm, and leg weakness (equal involvement); no sensory loss, no cortical signs | Posterior limb of internal capsule; basis pontis; corona radiata |
| **Pure sensory stroke** | 5-7% | Contralateral face, arm, and leg numbness/paresthesias; no motor deficit | Ventral posterolateral (VPL) thalamus |
| **Ataxic hemiparesis** | 3-18% | Ipsilateral cerebellar-type ataxia with contralateral hemiparesis (ataxia and weakness on same side) | Posterior limb of internal capsule; basis pontis; corona radiata |
| **Sensorimotor stroke** | 5-20% | Combined hemiparesis and hemisensory loss (face, arm, leg) | Thalamus and adjacent posterior limb of internal capsule |
| **Dysarthria-clumsy hand syndrome** | 2-6% | Dysarthria, dysphagia, and clumsiness/weakness of contralateral hand | Basis pontis; genu of internal capsule; corona radiata |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The hallmark of lacunar stroke is the ABSENCE of cortical signs. If a patient presents with aphasia, neglect, visual field cut, or gaze deviation, it is NOT a lacunar syndrome even if the infarct is small. Pure motor hemiparesis is the most common lacunar syndrome (approximately 50% of all lacunar strokes). The posterior limb of the internal capsule is the most commonly tested location for pure motor hemiparesis.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Capsular Warning Syndrome:**

Capsular warning syndrome is a pattern of repetitive, stereotyped hemiplegic TIAs that predict an impending capsular (internal capsule) lacunar infarct. Patients experience multiple brief episodes of hemiparesis over hours to days, each resolving, until a completed stroke occurs. The syndrome carries a high risk (approximately 40-60%) of progression to completed infarction and represents a neurological urgency requiring close monitoring and intervention.

**CT vs. MRI Sensitivity:**

A critical imaging limitation in lacunar stroke is the poor sensitivity of CT. CT detects only 30-44% of lacunar infarcts, meaning that the majority of lacunar strokes are missed on CT. MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) is far more sensitive and should be obtained when lacunar stroke is suspected and the CT is unrevealing. DWI overestimates lacunar infarct volume by approximately 40%, so a lesion that appears slightly larger than 15 mm on acute DWI may still be a true lacunar infarct.

**Prognosis of Lacunar Stroke:**

Lacunar strokes have the best short-term prognosis of any ischemic stroke subtype, with approximately 91% of patients achieving a favorable outcome at 3 months. However, long-term prognosis (recurrent stroke, vascular death, cognitive decline) is similar to that of other ischemic stroke subtypes. Patients with lacunar stroke are at risk for progressive small vessel disease, cognitive impairment (vascular dementia), and recurrent lacunar events.

**Landmark Trial -- SPS3:**

The SPS3 (Secondary Prevention of Small Subcortical Strokes) trial demonstrated that long-term dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin plus clopidogrel) is HARMFUL in lacunar stroke. The dual antiplatelet group had increased major hemorrhage and all-cause mortality compared with aspirin alone, with no significant reduction in recurrent stroke. This result changed clinical practice: lacunar stroke should be treated with single antiplatelet therapy (aspirin or clopidogrel alone), not long-term DAPT.

> **Clinical Pearl:** SPS3 trial results: do NOT use long-term dual antiplatelet therapy for lacunar stroke. It increases bleeding and mortality without reducing recurrent stroke. This is distinct from the short-term DAPT benefit shown in the CHANCE and POINT trials for minor stroke/TIA within 21-90 days of onset. The key distinction: short-term DAPT (21 days) after minor stroke/TIA is beneficial; long-term DAPT for lacunar stroke is harmful.

**CADASIL:**

CADASIL (Cerebral Autosomal Dominant Arteriopathy with Subcortical Infarcts and Leukoencephalopathy) is the most common monogenic (single-gene) cause of cerebral small vessel disease. It is caused by mutations in the NOTCH3 gene on chromosome 19.

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Genetics | Autosomal dominant; NOTCH3 gene mutation (chromosome 19) |
| Onset | Symptoms typically begin in the 3rd-5th decade |
| Clinical features | Migraine with aura (often the earliest symptom), recurrent subcortical lacunar infarcts, progressive cognitive decline leading to vascular dementia, psychiatric symptoms (depression, apathy) |
| Imaging | Extensive white matter hyperintensities (leukoaraiosis) on MRI, particularly in the anterior temporal poles and external capsules |
| Diagnosis | Genetic testing for NOTCH3 mutations; skin biopsy showing granular osmiophilic material (GOM) in vessel walls |
| Treatment | No specific disease-modifying therapy; standard stroke prevention measures |

> **Clinical Pearl:** CADASIL should be suspected in a young or middle-aged patient with recurrent lacunar strokes, migraines with aura, and prominent white matter changes on MRI, especially if there is a family history of early-onset stroke or dementia. Anterior temporal pole involvement on MRI is considered somewhat characteristic. The answer to "most common monogenic cause of small vessel disease" is always CADASIL.

## Section 6: Cryptogenic Stroke and ESUS

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Cryptogenic stroke accounts for approximately 25-30% of all ischemic strokes. It is defined as ischemic stroke for which no etiology is identified despite a complete standard evaluation. A subset of cryptogenic strokes has been further classified as embolic stroke of undetermined source (ESUS).

**ESUS Criteria:**
- Non-lacunar infarct (not a small deep infarct compatible with lacunar syndrome)
- Absence of extracranial or intracranial atherosclerosis causing >=50% stenosis in the territory of the infarct
- No major-risk cardioembolic source identified
- No other specific cause identified (dissection, vasculitis, etc.)

**Cryptogenic Stroke Workup:**

| Investigation | Purpose | Key Findings |
|--------------|---------|-------------|
| Extended cardiac monitoring (30-day event monitor or ICM) | Detect occult paroxysmal AF | Detects AF in ~10-25% of cryptogenic strokes |
| TEE (transesophageal echocardiography) | Evaluate PFO, atrial septal aneurysm, aortic arch atheroma, left atrial appendage thrombus | Better than TTE for detecting these pathologies |
| Bubble study (agitated saline echocardiography) | Detect right-to-left shunt (PFO) | Positive in 40-50% of young cryptogenic stroke patients |
| Hypercoagulable workup | Antiphospholipid syndrome, Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency, prothrombin gene mutation | Indicated in young patients, those with no identified cause, family history of thrombosis |
| Vessel wall MRI | Detect arterial dissection, vasculitis, non-stenotic atherosclerotic plaque | Fat-saturated T1 for dissection; enhancement pattern for vasculitis |

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Patent Foramen Ovale (PFO) and Stroke:**

The PFO is a remnant of the fetal foramen ovale that persists as a flap-like opening between the right and left atria. It is present in approximately 25% of the general population but is found in up to 40-50% of patients with cryptogenic stroke, particularly in younger adults. The proposed mechanism is paradoxical embolism: a venous thrombus (often from the lower extremities or pelvis) crosses through the PFO during a transient right-to-left pressure gradient (such as during Valsalva maneuver) and enters the arterial circulation, ultimately reaching the brain.

Multiple randomized trials (CLOSE, RESPECT, REDUCE, DEFENSE-PFO) have demonstrated that PFO closure (typically with a transcatheter device) reduces the risk of recurrent stroke in selected patients with cryptogenic stroke and PFO, particularly those with a large shunt or atrial septal aneurysm, and those under age 60.

**Hypercoagulable States:**

Routine hypercoagulable testing is low-yield in unselected stroke patients but is indicated in specific populations:
- Patients under age 50 with no identified cause
- Patients with features suggestive of antiphospholipid syndrome (history of venous thromboembolism, recurrent miscarriages, livedo reticularis)
- Family history of thrombophilia or early thrombosis

Key hypercoagulable findings in ischemic stroke:

| Condition | Prevalence in Stroke | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| Lupus anticoagulant | ~3% (8% if age <=50) | Most clinically significant antiphospholipid antibody |
| Anticardiolipin antibodies | ~17% (21% if age <=50) | Must be confirmed on repeat testing 12 weeks later |
| Factor V Leiden | ~7% of stroke patients | Most common inherited thrombophilia |
| Prothrombin G20210A mutation | ~5% of stroke patients | Second most common inherited thrombophilia |

## Section 7: Carotid Artery Dissection

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Carotid artery dissection is an important cause of ischemic stroke in young and middle-aged adults, accounting for approximately 20% of strokes in patients under age 45.

**Mechanism:** An intimal tear allows blood to enter the vessel wall, creating an intramural hematoma. This hematoma can narrow the arterial lumen (causing hemodynamic compromise) or serve as a nidus for thrombus formation and subsequent embolism to the brain.

**Causes:**
- Trauma (even minor): chiropractic cervical manipulation, motor vehicle accident, sports injury, forceful coughing/sneezing
- Connective tissue disorders: Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (vascular type), Marfan syndrome, fibromuscular dysplasia
- Spontaneous (no identifiable precipitant)

**Classic Clinical Triad:**
1. Ipsilateral head, neck, or face pain (often unilateral headache or neck pain preceding neurological symptoms)
2. Ipsilateral partial Horner syndrome: miosis (pupil constriction) and ptosis (eyelid drooping) WITHOUT anhidrosis. The reason anhidrosis is absent is that the third-order sympathetic fibers to the face (controlling facial sweating) separate from the ICA before the typical dissection location, while the fibers to the pupil and eyelid travel along the ICA.
3. Contralateral cerebral ischemic symptoms (hemiparesis, aphasia, etc.) from embolic or hemodynamic stroke

**Diagnosis:**
- CTA (CT angiography): Shows luminal narrowing, intimal flap, or "flame-shaped" tapering
- MRA (MR angiography): Similar findings
- MRI with fat-saturated T1 sequences: Shows the intramural hematoma as a bright crescent ("crescent sign") surrounding the narrowed lumen

**Treatment:**
- Anticoagulation (heparin followed by warfarin) or antiplatelet therapy for 3-6 months
- The CADISS trial found no significant difference between anticoagulation and antiplatelet therapy for dissection-related stroke prevention
- Most dissections heal spontaneously with recanalization of the vessel

> **Clinical Pearl:** Think of carotid dissection in any young patient (under 45-50) with stroke preceded by neck pain or recent trauma. The classic triad is ipsilateral pain + ipsilateral partial Horner (miosis + ptosis, NO anhidrosis) + contralateral stroke symptoms. The partial Horner (without anhidrosis) is a key distinguishing feature because the sympathetic fibers run along the surface of the ICA, and compression by the intramural hematoma disrupts the fibers to the eye but spares those to the facial sweat glands.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Vertebral Artery Dissection:**

Vertebral artery dissection is less common than carotid dissection but shares many features. It presents with occipital or posterior neck pain, and may cause posterior circulation stroke (most commonly lateral medullary syndrome/Wallenberg syndrome from extension into the PICA territory). Vertebral dissection is associated with cervical manipulation, yoga, and rotational neck trauma. It is diagnosed by CTA, MRA, or MRI with fat-saturated T1 imaging of the neck.

**Fibromuscular Dysplasia (FMD):**

FMD is a non-atherosclerotic, non-inflammatory arterial disease that predisposes to dissection, aneurysm formation, and stenosis. It predominantly affects women (90% female) and most commonly involves the renal arteries (causing renovascular hypertension) and the cervical ICA (predisposing to dissection and stroke). Angiography classically shows a "string of beads" appearance.

## Section 8: Stroke Subtype Prognosis

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Stroke subtype influences prognosis in both the short and long term:

| Subtype | Short-Term Prognosis | Long-Term Prognosis | Key Prognostic Points |
|---------|---------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Lacunar | Best (91% favorable at 3 months) | Similar to other subtypes | Low acute mortality but ongoing small vessel disease risk |
| Large artery atherosclerosis | Intermediate | Moderate recurrence risk | Depends on degree of stenosis and treatment |
| Cardioembolic | Worst short-term | High recurrence without anticoagulation | Larger infarcts, higher hemorrhagic transformation risk |
| Cryptogenic | Variable | Depends on underlying etiology | Extended monitoring may reveal treatable cause |

**Key Prognostic Predictors:**
- NIHSS score is the single most important prognostic tool for ischemic stroke outcomes
- NIHSS >16 predicts high probability of death or severe disability
- NIHSS <=6 predicts good recovery
- Each additional NIHSS point decreases the odds of an excellent outcome by approximately 17%
- Age is the second strongest predictor after stroke severity

**Recovery Timeline:**
- Greatest neurological recovery occurs in the first 3-6 months
- The Copenhagen Stroke Study found that 95% of patients reach their best neurological recovery level by 11 weeks
- Functional recovery can continue beyond the neurological recovery window through compensatory strategies and rehabilitation

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Early Physical Exam Predictors:**

Simple bedside examination findings within the first 72 hours can reliably predict long-term motor recovery:

| Exam Finding | Timing | Predictive Value |
|-------------|--------|-----------------|
| Finger extension + shoulder abduction present | Day 2 | 0.98 probability of hand dexterity recovery at 6 months |
| Sitting balance 30 seconds + leg muscle contraction present | Within 72 hours | 98% probability of independent ambulation |

These simple bedside tests are among the most reliable early predictors of motor recovery and are frequently tested on board examinations.

**Landmark Rehabilitation Trials:**

| Trial | Intervention | Key Finding |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| EXCITE | Constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT) in chronic stroke | Improved upper extremity motor function compared with usual care |
| VNS-REHAB | Vagus nerve stimulation paired with rehabilitation | Improved upper extremity motor recovery in chronic stroke |
| AVERT | Very early mobilization (<24 hours post-stroke) | HARMFUL -- associated with worse functional outcomes |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The AVERT trial is critically important for rehabilitation physicians: very early mobilization (within 24 hours of stroke onset) is HARMFUL and associated with worse outcomes at 3 months. The current recommendation is to avoid intensive out-of-bed activity in the first 24 hours after stroke. This is counter to the intuition that "earlier is better" for mobilization and is a frequently tested concept.

## Section 9: Cardiac Evaluation in Ischemic Stroke

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Cardiac evaluation is essential in all ischemic stroke patients because cardioembolism accounts for approximately 20-25% of ischemic strokes and because many strokes initially classified as cryptogenic ultimately prove to have a cardiac source.

**Minimum Cardiac Workup:**

| Test | Indication | What It Detects |
|------|-----------|----------------|
| 12-lead ECG | All stroke patients | AF, prior MI, LVH |
| Continuous cardiac monitoring (>=24 hours) | All stroke patients | Paroxysmal AF, other arrhythmias |
| TTE (transthoracic echocardiography) | First-line for most patients | LV thrombus, reduced EF, valvular disease, wall motion abnormalities |
| TEE (transesophageal echocardiography) | Patients <45, suspected aortic pathology, AF, prosthetic valves, PFO evaluation | PFO, atrial septal aneurysm, left atrial appendage thrombus, aortic arch atheroma |
| Extended cardiac monitoring (30-day or ICM) | Cryptogenic stroke | Occult paroxysmal AF (detected in 10-25%) |

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**TTE vs. TEE:**

TTE is the first-line echocardiographic study for most stroke patients and is adequate for detecting left ventricular thrombus, valvular disease, and wall motion abnormalities. TEE provides superior visualization of the left atrium, left atrial appendage, interatrial septum (PFO), and aortic arch, and is indicated when these structures need to be evaluated more thoroughly.

Specific indications for TEE over TTE:
- Age under 45 (higher suspicion for PFO/paradoxical embolism)
- Suspected aortic arch atheroma
- Known or suspected AF (to evaluate left atrial appendage for thrombus)
- Prosthetic heart valves (TTE provides limited visualization)
- Suspected endocarditis with non-diagnostic TTE

## Section 10: Acute Ischemic Stroke Management Overview

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

While acute stroke management is primarily the domain of neurology and emergency medicine, PM&R physicians must understand the fundamentals of acute treatment, as this knowledge appears on board examinations and affects rehabilitation planning.

**Acute Imaging:**
- Non-contrast CT of the head is the first imaging study in acute stroke -- the primary purpose is to exclude hemorrhage
- Acute blood appears bright (hyperdense/white) on CT
- Ischemic infarction may not be visible on CT for the first 6-12 hours
- MRI DWI is more sensitive for early ischemic stroke
- CT angiography (CTA) identifies large vessel occlusion for thrombectomy decision-making

**Intravenous Thrombolysis (tPA/Alteplase/Tenecteplase):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Time window | Within 4.5 hours of symptom onset (standard); extended windows with imaging selection |
| Key exclusion | Any evidence of intracranial hemorrhage on CT |
| Other exclusions | INR >1.7, platelet count <100,000, recent surgery, active bleeding |
| Blood pressure limits | SBP must be <185 before and <180 after administration |
| Dose | Alteplase 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg); 10% bolus, remainder over 60 minutes |
| Major complication | Symptomatic hemorrhagic transformation (~6%) |

**Mechanical Thrombectomy:**
- Indicated for large vessel occlusion (LVO) of the anterior circulation
- Standard time window: within 24 hours with appropriate imaging selection
- Dramatically effective: five landmark trials in 2015 (MR CLEAN, ESCAPE, EXTEND-IA, SWIFT PRIME, REVASCAT) established the benefit
- Can produce dramatic neurological improvement through clot retrieval

**Blood Pressure Management in Acute Ischemic Stroke:**
- Before thrombolysis: SBP must be <185/DBP <110
- After thrombolysis: maintain SBP <180/DBP <105 for 24 hours
- Without thrombolysis: permissive hypertension (allow SBP up to 220); do not acutely lower unless severe end-organ damage
- IV labetalol and nicardipine are first-line agents

> **Clinical Pearl:** Permissive hypertension is the standard approach in acute ischemic stroke (without thrombolysis): do NOT acutely lower blood pressure unless SBP exceeds 220 mmHg. Aggressively lowering BP in an acutely ischemic brain can worsen the stroke by reducing perfusion to the penumbra. In hemorrhagic stroke, the BP target is lower (SBP <140 per INTERACT2). This distinction between ischemic and hemorrhagic BP goals is commonly tested.
