## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** The classic hemiplegic posture is UE FLEXOR synergy (shoulder adduction/internal rotation, elbow flexion, forearm pronation, wrist flexion, finger flexion, thumb-in-palm) and LE EXTENSOR synergy (hip extension/adduction/internal rotation, knee extension, ankle plantarflexion/inversion, toe flexion).

> **Board Pearl:** The Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS) grades resistance to passive stretch from 0 to 4 with a "1+" grade (total of 6 grades). It does NOT distinguish between neural (spasticity) and non-neural (contracture) components. The Tardieu Scale is more specific for true spasticity because it tests at multiple velocities.

> **Board Pearl:** Phenol to the obturator nerve is the classic treatment for hip adductor spasticity (scissoring gait). The obturator nerve is a PURE motor nerve to the adductors, so dysesthesia risk is minimal. Phenol to mixed motor/sensory nerves (e.g., tibial nerve) carries significant dysesthesia risk.

> **Board Pearl:** Shoulder pain occurs in 70-84% of hemiplegic patients. The Basmajian principle states that the supraspinatus and posterior deltoid (not gravity) are primarily responsible for maintaining the glenohumeral joint in the socket. FES to these muscles reduces subluxation.

> **Board Pearl:** Central post-stroke pain (Dejerine-Roussy syndrome) has DELAYED onset (weeks to months after thalamic stroke), is characterized by burning/lancinating pain with allodynia, and is treated with amitriptyline, gabapentin, or pregabalin. It is NOT responsive to opioids.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 10.1 Spasticity: Pathophysiology and Patterns

**[BOARD]**

Spasticity is defined as a velocity-dependent increase in tonic stretch reflexes resulting from hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex arc due to loss of upper motor neuron (UMN) inhibitory input. It is a component of the UMN syndrome that develops in 20-40% of stroke survivors.

**UMN Syndrome Components:**

| Positive Phenomena (Increased Activity) | Negative Phenomena (Decreased Activity) |
|----------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Spasticity (velocity-dependent tone) | Weakness (paresis/plegia) |
| Hyperreflexia | Loss of dexterity |
| Clonus | Fatigue |
| Babinski sign | Loss of selective motor control |
| Flexor/extensor spasms | |
| Associated reactions | |
| Co-contraction | |
| Synergistic movement patterns | |

**Note:** The negative phenomena (weakness, loss of dexterity) are the PRIMARY cause of functional limitation. Spasticity is often a secondary contributor, and treatment of spasticity without addressing weakness may not improve function.

**Classic Hemiplegic Spasticity Patterns:**

**Upper Extremity (Flexor Pattern):**

| Joint | Spastic Position | Muscles Involved |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Shoulder | Adduction, internal rotation | Pectoralis major, subscapularis, latissimus dorsi, teres major |
| Elbow | Flexion | Biceps brachii, brachialis, brachioradialis |
| Forearm | Pronation | Pronator teres, pronator quadratus |
| Wrist | Flexion, ulnar deviation | Flexor carpi radialis (FCR), flexor carpi ulnaris (FCU) |
| Fingers | Flexion (clenched fist) | Flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS), flexor digitorum profundus (FDP) |
| Thumb | Flexion, adduction (thumb-in-palm) | Flexor pollicis longus, adductor pollicis |

**Lower Extremity (Extensor Pattern):**

| Joint | Spastic Position | Muscles Involved |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Hip | Extension, adduction, internal rotation | Gluteus maximus, hip adductors (adductor longus, magnus, brevis) |
| Knee | Extension | Quadriceps (especially rectus femoris) |
| Ankle | Plantarflexion (equinus) | Gastrocnemius, soleus |
| Ankle (inversion) | Varus | Tibialis posterior |
| Toes | Flexion, curling | Flexor digitorum longus, flexor hallucis longus |

> **Board Pearl:** Some patients USE their lower extremity extensor spasticity functionally. The stiff extended leg acts as an internal splint for standing and transfers. Reducing LE extensor tone (e.g., with botox to quadriceps or gastrocsoleus) may paradoxically WORSEN function by removing this support. Always assess functional impact before treating spasticity.

**Spasticity Timeline:**
- Onset: Days to weeks after stroke (during transition from Brunnstrom Stage 1 to Stage 2)
- Peak: 1-3 months post-stroke (Brunnstrom Stage 3)
- Plateau or decline: 3-6 months (Brunnstrom Stages 4-5)
- Chronic spasticity: May persist indefinitely if recovery is incomplete

### 10.2 Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS)

**[BOARD]**

The MAS is the most widely used clinical measure of spasticity. It grades resistance to passive stretch on a 6-point ordinal scale.

**Modified Ashworth Scale:**

| Grade | Description | Key Distinguishing Feature |
|-------|-------------|---------------------------|
| 0 | No increase in muscle tone | Normal tone |
| 1 | Slight increase in tone; catch and release OR minimal resistance at END of range | Catch at the very end of ROM |
| 1+ | Slight increase in tone; catch followed by minimal resistance through LESS THAN half of ROM | Catch with resistance through <50% of ROM |
| 2 | More marked increase in tone through MOST of ROM, but limb easily moved | Resistance through majority of ROM; limb still mobile |
| 3 | Considerable increase in tone; passive movement DIFFICULT | Hard to move the limb passively |
| 4 | Limb RIGID in flexion or extension | Essentially no passive movement possible |

**MAS Limitations:**
- Does NOT distinguish neural (spasticity) from non-neural (contracture) components of increased tone
- Tests at only one speed (not velocity-dependent)
- Poor inter-rater reliability, especially at the 1 and 1+ levels
- Ordinal scale (differences between grades are not equal)
- Does not capture dynamic spasticity during functional tasks

### 10.3 Tardieu Scale

**[BOARD]**

The Tardieu Scale is more specific than the MAS for true spasticity because it assesses the muscle reaction at multiple velocities, capturing the velocity-dependent nature of spasticity.

**Tardieu Scale Methodology:**

| Test Speed | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| V1 | As slow as possible (speed of gravity) |
| V2 | Speed of limb falling under gravity |
| V3 | As fast as possible |

**Key Measurements:**
- **R1 (angle of catch at fast speed V3):** The joint angle where the examiner first feels the spastic catch during rapid passive stretch
- **R2 (full passive ROM at slow speed V1):** The maximum passive ROM achieved at slow speed
- **R2 minus R1 = Dynamic component** (neural/spastic component amenable to treatment)

**Interpreting Tardieu Results:**

| Finding | Interpretation | Clinical Implication |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| Large R2-R1 gap | Significant dynamic (neural) spasticity | Good candidate for botulinum toxin, oral antispasmodics |
| Small R2-R1 gap with limited R2 | Fixed contracture (non-neural) | Requires serial casting, surgical release, or splinting; botox alone insufficient |
| R1 near R2 (small gap) with good R2 | Minimal true spasticity | May not benefit from antispasticity treatment |

> **Board Pearl:** The Tardieu Scale's key advantage over the MAS is differentiating true (neural) spasticity from fixed (non-neural) contracture. A large R2-R1 difference = treatable spasticity. A small R2-R1 difference with limited R2 = fixed contracture requiring mechanical intervention. This distinction is critical for treatment planning.

### 10.4 Spasticity Treatment Ladder

**[BOARD]**

Spasticity management follows a stepwise approach from least invasive to most invasive interventions. Treatment should be guided by functional goals: improving active function, facilitating passive care (hygiene, positioning), or reducing pain.

**Treatment Ladder:**

| Step | Intervention | Mechanism | Indications | Key Notes |
|------|-------------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Stretching and positioning | Maintains muscle length; prevents contracture; modulates tonic stretch reflex | All patients with spasticity | Foundation of all spasticity management; daily ROM exercises |
| 2 | Splinting and orthotics | Prolonged low-load stretch; maintains joint position | Contracture prevention; functional positioning | Static splints (resting hand splint); dynamic splints; AFOs |
| 3 | Oral medications | Systemic reduction of spasticity | Generalized spasticity; multiple limb involvement | Sedation is primary limitation; limited efficacy for focal spasticity |
| 4 | Botulinum toxin injections | Blocks presynaptic ACh release at NMJ | Focal spasticity in specific muscles | Most common interventional spasticity treatment |
| 5 | Phenol/alcohol nerve blocks | Chemical neurolysis; protein denaturation | Large muscles; pure motor nerves; cost-sensitive settings | Immediate onset; risk of dysesthesia with mixed nerves |
| 6 | Intrathecal baclofen (ITB) pump | Delivers baclofen directly to intrathecal space | Severe, generalized, bilateral spasticity refractory to other treatments | Trial injection required before pump implantation |
| 7 | Surgical interventions | Tendon lengthening, release, or transfer | Fixed contractures not responding to conservative measures | Last resort; requires careful patient selection |

**Oral Antispasticity Medications:**

| Medication | Mechanism | Dose | Side Effects | Board-Relevant Points |
|-----------|-----------|------|-------------|----------------------|
| Baclofen | GABA-B agonist (presynaptic inhibition in spinal cord) | 5 mg TID, titrate to max 80 mg/day | Sedation, weakness, seizures if abruptly discontinued | Most commonly used oral antispastic; abrupt withdrawal can cause seizures, hyperthermia, death |
| Tizanidine | Alpha-2 adrenergic agonist (presynaptic inhibition in spinal cord) | 4 mg TID, titrate to max 36 mg/day | Sedation, dry mouth, hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs) | Less weakness than baclofen; check LFTs at baseline and periodically |
| Dantrolene | Acts PERIPHERALLY on ryanodine receptor in muscle (reduces Ca2+ release from SR) | 25 mg daily, titrate to max 400 mg/day | Hepatotoxicity (potentially fatal), weakness, diarrhea | ONLY oral agent acting peripherally; monitor LFTs; contraindicated in liver disease |
| Diazepam | GABA-A agonist (enhances presynaptic inhibition) | 2-10 mg BID-QID | Sedation, dependence, cognitive impairment | Limited role due to sedation and dependence; avoid in elderly stroke patients |

> **Board Pearl:** Dantrolene is the only oral antispasticity agent that acts peripherally (directly on skeletal muscle via the ryanodine receptor). Baclofen, tizanidine, and diazepam all act centrally. Dantrolene's major risk is fatal hepatotoxicity requiring LFT monitoring. Baclofen must NEVER be abruptly discontinued (withdrawal syndrome includes seizures, hallucinations, hyperthermia, and death).

### 10.5 Botulinum Toxin Injections

**[BOARD]**

Botulinum toxin type A (BoNT-A) is the most widely used interventional treatment for focal spasticity after stroke. It works by blocking presynaptic acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction, causing temporary chemical denervation.

**Botulinum Toxin Formulations:**

| Product | Generic Name | Unit Equivalence | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Botox | OnabotulinumtoxinA | 1 unit | Reference standard; most studied |
| Dysport | AbobotulinumtoxinA | 2.5-3 units per 1 Botox unit | Approximately 3:1 conversion ratio |
| Xeomin | IncobotulinumtoxinA | 1 unit (approximately equivalent to Botox) | No complexing proteins; may have lower immunogenicity |
| Myobloc | RimabotulinumtoxinB | 40-100 units per 1 Botox unit | Type B; different receptor; may work when resistance to type A develops |

**Note:** Units are NOT interchangeable between products. Each formulation has its own dosing system.

**Pharmacokinetics:**

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Onset | 2-3 days to 2 weeks |
| Peak effect | 2-6 weeks |
| Duration | 3-6 months |
| Mechanism of wearing off | Axonal sprouting and new synaptic junction formation |
| Repeated dosing | Possible every 3-4 months; antibody formation can reduce efficacy with repeated use |

**Upper Extremity Injection Targets:**

| Spastic Pattern | Target Muscles | Typical Botox Dose (per muscle) | Injection Guidance |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| Shoulder adduction/IR | Pectoralis major, subscapularis | 50-100 units | Ultrasound for subscapularis |
| Elbow flexion | Biceps brachii, brachialis, brachioradialis | 50-100 units each | EMG, ultrasound, or electrical stimulation |
| Forearm pronation | Pronator teres, pronator quadratus | 25-75 units each | EMG or ultrasound |
| Wrist flexion | Flexor carpi radialis (FCR), flexor carpi ulnaris (FCU) | 25-100 units each | Palpation or ultrasound; FCR is most common wrist target |
| Finger flexion (clenched fist) | FDS, FDP | 25-50 units per muscle belly | Ultrasound preferred; treat both superficial and deep flexors |
| Thumb-in-palm | Flexor pollicis longus, adductor pollicis, first dorsal interosseous | 10-25 units each | Small muscles; low doses |

**Lower Extremity Injection Targets:**

| Spastic Pattern | Target Muscles | Typical Botox Dose (per muscle) | Injection Guidance |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| Equinus (plantarflexion) | Gastrocnemius (medial + lateral heads), soleus | 50-200 units total | Most common LE target; inject both gastroc and soleus |
| Varus (inversion) | Tibialis posterior | 50-100 units | Deep muscle; ultrasound or electrical stimulation essential |
| Equinovarus | Gastrocsoleus + tibialis posterior | Combined dosing | Treat both contributors |
| Stiff-knee gait | Rectus femoris | 75-200 units | Delayed knee flexion in swing; palpation usually sufficient |
| Hip adductor spasticity | Adductor longus, adductor magnus | 75-200 units each | Phenol often preferred here (large muscles, pure motor nerve available) |
| Toe curling/clawing | Flexor digitorum longus, flexor hallucis longus | 25-50 units each | Ultrasound guided; small doses |

> **Board Pearl:** The most common upper extremity botox target is the FCR (for wrist flexion spasticity). The most common lower extremity target is the gastrocsoleus complex (for equinus/plantarflexion spasticity). Always combine botox injections with therapy (stretching, splinting, functional training) to maximize benefit.

### 10.6 Phenol vs. Botulinum Toxin

**[BOARD]**

| Feature | Botulinum Toxin | Phenol (5-7%) / Alcohol (45-100%) |
|---------|----------------|----------------------------------|
| Mechanism | Blocks presynaptic ACh release at NMJ | Chemical neurolysis; protein denaturation causing Wallerian degeneration |
| Onset | Gradual (2-3 days to 2 weeks) | Immediate (within minutes) |
| Peak effect | 2-6 weeks | Immediate to days |
| Duration | 3-6 months (requires re-injection) | 3-6 months (variable; can be longer) |
| Cost | Expensive ($600-2,000+ per session) | Very inexpensive ($5-20 per vial) |
| Best targets | Small muscles; precise focal targeting | Large muscles; pure motor nerves |
| Key risk | Diffusion to adjacent muscles; antibody formation | Painful dysesthesia if sensory nerve fibers affected |
| Guidance method | EMG, ultrasound, or electrical stimulation | Electrical stimulation essential to locate motor point or nerve |
| Sensory effects | NONE | Dysesthesia risk with mixed motor/sensory nerves |
| Reversibility | Self-limited (wears off) | Partially reversible as nerve regenerates |
| Contraindications | Myasthenia gravis, NMJ disorders | Injection near critical sensory nerves |

**Classic Phenol Targets:**

| Nerve/Location | Rationale |
|---------------|-----------|
| Obturator nerve (for hip adductors) | PURE motor nerve to adductors; minimal dysesthesia risk; classic board answer |
| Musculocutaneous nerve (for elbow flexion) | Motor to biceps and brachialis; some sensory risk but low |
| Motor branch of tibial nerve | Motor branches to gastrocsoleus; must avoid sensory branches |
| Motor points (intramuscular phenol) | Direct injection into motor point of muscle; less precise but avoids nerve proximity |

> **Board Pearl:** Phenol nerve block to the obturator nerve for hip adductor spasticity is a classic PM&R board answer. The obturator nerve is a pure motor nerve to the adductors, making dysesthesia risk minimal. This makes it an ideal phenol target. In contrast, phenol to the tibial nerve (a mixed motor/sensory nerve) carries significant dysesthesia risk and is generally avoided in favor of botulinum toxin for ankle/foot spasticity.

### 10.7 Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB) Pump

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

ITB delivers baclofen directly to the intrathecal space via a surgically implanted pump, bypassing the blood-brain barrier and achieving 100- to 1,000-fold greater concentrations at the spinal cord with 1/100th the oral dose.

**ITB Trial and Implantation:**

| Step | Details |
|------|---------|
| Patient selection | Severe, generalized spasticity refractory to oral medications and focal treatments |
| Trial injection | 50-100 mcg intrathecal bolus via lumbar puncture |
| Positive response | Significant reduction in MAS (typically 1-2 grades) within 4-8 hours |
| Pump implantation | Subcutaneous abdominal pocket; catheter tunneled to intrathecal space |
| Dose range | Typical starting dose 50-100 mcg/day; titrated over weeks to months |
| Refill schedule | Every 1-6 months depending on dose and reservoir size |

**ITB Complications:**

| Complication | Mechanism | Management |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Withdrawal syndrome | Abrupt cessation (pump malfunction, catheter kink, empty reservoir) | Medical EMERGENCY: seizures, hyperthermia, rhabdomyolysis, death; IV benzodiazepines; oral/enteral baclofen; restore ITB delivery |
| Overdose | Programming error; catheter malfunction | Respiratory depression, hypotension, coma; supportive care; may require intubation |
| Catheter complications | Kink, migration, disconnection, fracture | Imaging (CT myelogram, X-ray); surgical revision |
| Infection | Wound infection; meningitis | Antibiotics; may require pump removal |
| CSF leak | Post-implantation | Conservative management; blood patch |

> **Board Pearl:** ITB withdrawal is a medical emergency that can be FATAL. Symptoms include rebound spasticity, hyperthermia, seizures, rhabdomyolysis, and death. It resembles malignant hyperthermia or neuroleptic malignant syndrome. Treatment includes IV benzodiazepines, oral/enteral baclofen, and urgent restoration of intrathecal baclofen delivery. Always check pump function and reservoir volume in any ITB patient presenting with acute spasticity increase.

### 10.8 Hemiplegic Shoulder Pain

**[BOARD]**

Shoulder pain is the most common pain complaint after stroke, occurring in 70-84% of hemiplegic patients. It is associated with worse functional outcomes, prolonged hospital stays, and decreased quality of life.

**Causes of Hemiplegic Shoulder Pain:**

| Cause | Frequency | Mechanism | Key Features |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Glenohumeral subluxation | Very common (up to 80%) | Loss of rotator cuff tone (supraspinatus, deltoid) allowing inferior subluxation | Palpable sulcus; pain with ROM; may be painless if sensory loss present |
| Rotator cuff injury/impingement | Common | Improper ROM techniques; forced overhead movement of flaccid shoulder | Pain with overhead activities; positive impingement signs |
| Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) | Common (chronically) | Capsular contracture from immobility; spastic internal rotators | Progressive loss of ROM, especially external rotation; pain at end-range |
| Spasticity-related pain | Common | Muscle shortening and joint position from spastic pattern | Correlates with MAS grade; internal rotation/adduction contracture |
| CRPS Type I | 12-48% (variable reports) | Sympathetically maintained pain; neurogenic inflammation | Allodynia, edema, color/temperature changes, trophic changes |
| Central post-stroke pain | 2-8% | Thalamic or brainstem lesion affecting spinothalamic pathway | Burning, lancinating; delayed onset; allodynia; poor response to opioids |
| Heterotopic ossification | 1-5% | Ectopic bone formation in periarticular soft tissues | Loss of ROM; palpable mass; elevated alkaline phosphatase |
| Brachial plexopathy | Rare | Traction injury from positioning or falls | Lower motor neuron pattern; electrodiagnostic confirmation |

**Subluxation and the Basmajian Principle:**

Basmajian and Bazant (1959) demonstrated through EMG studies that the glenohumeral joint at rest is maintained NOT by gravity but by the passive locking mechanism of the supraspinatus and posterior deltoid. In the normal shoulder, these muscles provide a "sling" effect that keeps the humeral head in the glenoid fossa. After stroke, flaccidity of these muscles allows the weight of the arm to cause inferior subluxation.

**Subluxation Assessment:**
- Palpation of the acromiohumeral gap: measured in finger-breadths (1-3 finger-breadths below acromion)
- Radiographic measurement: distance between inferior acromion and superior humeral head

**Subluxation Management:**

| Intervention | Evidence | Notes |
|-------------|---------|-------|
| Functional electrical stimulation (FES) | LEVEL I evidence for reducing subluxation | Applied to supraspinatus and posterior deltoid; 6-8 weeks of treatment |
| Proper positioning | Universal recommendation | Affected arm supported on pillows, armboard, or lap tray; avoid dependent hanging |
| Arm slings | Controversial | May reduce subluxation but can promote flexion synergy, restrict use of hand, and increase edema |
| Range of motion exercises | Universal | Gentle PROM; AVOID forceful overhead motion (causes impingement and pain) |
| Taping (kinesiotaping) | Limited evidence | May provide short-term support and proprioceptive feedback |

**Sling Controversies:**

| Sling Type | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Hemisling | Simple, widely available | Promotes flexor synergy; does not reduce subluxation well; restricts hand use |
| GivMohr sling | Better biomechanical support | Complex fitting; still restricts hand |
| Harris hemisling | Inhibits flexor synergy (positions arm in extension) | Uncomfortable for some patients |
| Arm trough (wheelchair) | Supports arm during wheelchair use | Not portable; only for seated position |

> **Board Pearl:** FES to the supraspinatus and posterior deltoid is the best evidence-based intervention for reducing glenohumeral subluxation. Arm slings are controversial because they may promote flexor synergy, restrict hand use, and increase dependent edema. NEVER forcefully range a hemiplegic shoulder overhead because this causes impingement, rotator cuff injury, and pain.

### 10.9 Complex Regional Pain Syndrome Type I (CRPS-I)

**[BOARD]**

CRPS Type I (formerly reflex sympathetic dystrophy or shoulder-hand syndrome in the context of stroke) occurs in 12-48% of hemiplegic patients, depending on diagnostic criteria used.

**Three Classical Stages (Steinbrocker):**

| Stage | Name | Duration | Features |
|-------|------|----------|----------|
| 1 | Acute (hot) | 0-3 months | Burning pain, edema, warmth, erythema, increased sweating, increased hair growth, allodynia |
| 2 | Dystrophic (cold) | 3-6 months | Cool, cyanotic skin; brawny edema; skin atrophy; decreased hair growth; nail changes; early osteoporosis; pain persists |
| 3 | Atrophic | 6-12+ months | Severe atrophy (skin, muscle, bone); contractures; pale, cool skin; minimal edema; pain may decrease but function is severely impaired |

**Note:** Progression through all three stages is NOT inevitable. Many patients are diagnosed and treated before advancing. The staging system has been largely replaced by the Budapest criteria for diagnosis but remains commonly tested.

**Budapest Criteria for CRPS (2003):**

Clinical diagnosis requires ALL of the following:

1. **Continuing pain disproportionate to any inciting event**
2. **At least ONE sign in TWO or more categories** (at time of evaluation):
   - Sensory: hyperalgesia or allodynia
   - Vasomotor: temperature asymmetry, skin color changes
   - Sudomotor/edema: edema, sweating changes
   - Motor/trophic: decreased ROM, motor dysfunction (weakness, tremor), trophic changes (hair, nails, skin)
3. **At least ONE symptom in THREE or more categories** (patient-reported):
   - Sensory: hyperesthesia, allodynia
   - Vasomotor: temperature asymmetry, skin color changes
   - Sudomotor/edema: edema, sweating changes
   - Motor/trophic: decreased ROM, motor dysfunction, trophic changes
4. **No other diagnosis better explains the signs and symptoms**

**Diagnostic Studies:**

| Test | Finding | Utility |
|------|---------|---------|
| Triple-phase bone scan | Increased uptake in delayed phase (Stage 1-2) | Most commonly used confirmatory test; sensitivity 73%, specificity 86% |
| Plain radiographs | Periarticular osteoporosis (patchy demineralization) | Late finding; seen in Stages 2-3 |
| MRI | Bone marrow edema, soft tissue edema | May show early changes; not specific |
| Skin temperature measurement | Asymmetry of 1 degree C or greater | Objective vasomotor finding |
| Quantitative sudomotor axon reflex test (QSART) | Asymmetric sweating | Specialized test for sudomotor dysfunction |

**CRPS-I Treatment:**

| Modality | Details |
|----------|---------|
| Physical and occupational therapy | Most important intervention; desensitization, ROM, edema management, functional use |
| Corticosteroids | Short course prednisone (30-40 mg/day, taper over 2-3 weeks); most effective in acute stage |
| Bisphosphonates | Alendronate, pamidronate; may reduce bone turnover and pain |
| Gabapentin/pregabalin | Neuropathic pain management |
| Sympathetic nerve blocks | Stellate ganglion block (UE) or lumbar sympathetic block (LE); diagnostic and therapeutic |
| Mirror therapy | Visual feedback technique; emerging evidence for pain reduction |
| Calcitonin | Reduces bone resorption; variable evidence |
| Vitamin C | 500 mg daily may prevent CRPS after distal radius fracture (preventive, not treatment) |

> **Board Pearl:** The triple-phase bone scan showing increased uptake in the delayed phase is the most commonly used confirmatory test for CRPS. The Budapest criteria are the current diagnostic standard. Remember: CRPS-I has no demonstrable nerve lesion (unlike CRPS-II which requires a definable nerve injury). In the stroke population, CRPS-I is far more common.

### 10.10 Central Post-Stroke Pain (Dejerine-Roussy Syndrome)

**[BOARD]**

Central post-stroke pain (CPSP), historically known as Dejerine-Roussy syndrome or thalamic pain syndrome, is a neuropathic pain condition resulting from damage to the spinothalamocortical pathway (most commonly the ventroposterolateral [VPL] thalamic nucleus).

**Key Clinical Features:**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Onset timing | DELAYED: weeks to months after stroke (typically 1-6 months) |
| Pain character | Burning, lancinating, aching, squeezing; may be constant or intermittent |
| Allodynia | Pain from normally non-painful stimuli (light touch, temperature changes, clothing) |
| Hyperalgesia | Exaggerated pain response to mildly painful stimuli |
| Location | Contralateral to lesion; often involves the hand, foot, or face |
| Stroke location | Thalamic (VPL nucleus) most classic; also lateral medullary, brainstem, parietal cortex |
| Associated deficits | Contralateral sensory loss (decreased pain/temperature sensation) with paradoxical pain |
| Response to opioids | POOR; opioids are generally ineffective |

**Treatment:**

| Agent | Evidence Level | Dose | Notes |
|-------|---------------|------|-------|
| Amitriptyline | First-line | 10-75 mg at bedtime | Tricyclic antidepressant; NNT approximately 2-3 for neuropathic pain |
| Gabapentin | First-line | 300-3600 mg/day in divided doses | Titrate slowly; sedation and dizziness |
| Pregabalin | First-line | 75-600 mg/day in divided doses | Similar to gabapentin; may have faster onset |
| Lamotrigine | Second-line | 25-200 mg/day | Slow titration required (Stevens-Johnson risk) |
| Duloxetine/venlafaxine | Second-line | 60-120 mg/day (duloxetine) | SNRI; may be effective for burning pain |
| Carbamazepine | Third-line | 200-1200 mg/day | Better for lancinating component |
| Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) | Adjunct | Variable settings | Non-pharmacological; may help some patients |
| Motor cortex stimulation | Refractory cases | Surgical implantation | Investigational; for intractable CPSP |

> **Board Pearl:** Central post-stroke pain (Dejerine-Roussy) has three distinguishing features: (1) DELAYED onset (weeks to months after thalamic stroke), (2) burning/lancinating pain with allodynia in a contralateral distribution, and (3) POOR response to opioids. First-line treatment is amitriptyline or gabapentin/pregabalin. The paradox is that the patient has sensory LOSS (decreased pinprick, temperature) but experiences severe spontaneous and evoked PAIN.

### 10.11 Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

**[BOARD]**

Heterotopic ossification is the formation of mature lamellar bone in periarticular soft tissues. In stroke, it occurs in 1-5% of patients, most commonly at the elbow.

**Key Features in Stroke:**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Most common location | Elbow (EXTENSOR side/medial aspect) |
| Second most common | Hip, shoulder |
| Side affected | Hemiplegic/paretic side |
| Risk factors | Severe spasticity, prolonged immobility, aggressive ROM, coma |
| Clinical presentation | Loss of ROM (especially extension), warmth, swelling, palpable mass |
| Time to development | Usually 1-4 months post-stroke |

**Diagnosis:**

| Test | Timing | Finding |
|------|--------|---------|
| Serum alkaline phosphatase | Early marker (elevated) | Rises before radiographic changes; nonspecific |
| Triple-phase bone scan | Early (most sensitive) | Increased uptake in all three phases; positive before X-ray |
| Plain radiographs | Late (2-4 weeks after symptoms) | Mature ossification visible; used to confirm maturity before surgery |
| CT scan | When surgery planned | Delineates extent and relationship to neurovascular structures |

**Treatment:**

| Stage | Intervention |
|-------|-------------|
| Prevention | Gentle ROM (avoid aggressive stretching); etidronate (200 mg BID prophylaxis in high-risk patients) |
| Acute/active HO | NSAIDs (indomethacin 75 mg/day); etidronate (20 mg/kg/day for 2 weeks, then 10 mg/kg/day); maintain ROM |
| Mature HO with functional limitation | Surgical resection (only when HO is mature: normal alkaline phosphatase, cold on bone scan, typically 12-18 months) |

> **Board Pearl:** In stroke-related HO, the ELBOW on the EXTENSOR side is the most common location. This differs from TBI-related HO (which more commonly affects the hip). Alkaline phosphatase and triple-phase bone scan are elevated/positive early. Surgery for HO removal should wait until the bone is mature (normal alkaline phosphatase, cold bone scan) to reduce recurrence risk; this typically takes 12-18 months.

### 10.12 Hemiplegic Gait Deviations and AFO Prescribing

**[BOARD]**

Hemiplegic gait deviations result from the combination of weakness, spasticity, and impaired selective motor control. The lower extremity extensor synergy creates a characteristic pattern of stiff-legged ambulation with compensatory mechanisms.

**Comprehensive Gait Deviation Table:**

| Gait Phase | Deviation | Cause | Compensatory Mechanism | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|-------|----------------------|-----------|
| Swing | Foot drop (equinus) | Weak tibialis anterior; spastic plantarflexors | Circumduction, hip hiking, vaulting | AFO (PLS, hinged, or rigid); FES to common peroneal nerve |
| Swing | Equinovarus | Spastic tibialis posterior + gastrocsoleus | Circumduction | Rigid AFO with mediolateral control; botox to tibialis posterior |
| Swing | Inadequate knee flexion | Spastic rectus femoris | Circumduction, hip hiking | Botox to rectus femoris; consider rectus femoris transfer |
| Swing | Circumduction | Combined foot drop + inadequate knee/hip flexion | None (IS the compensation) | Treat underlying cause (equinus, stiff knee) |
| Swing | Hip hiking | Compensating for functional limb length discrepancy | None (IS the compensation) | AFO to correct foot position; address hip/knee flexion |
| Swing | Vaulting | Rising on toes of unaffected side | None (IS the compensation) | Address affected-side length discrepancy |
| Stance | Genu recurvatum (hyperextension) | Weak quadriceps (compensation) OR spastic plantarflexors pushing knee back | Patient locks knee for stability | AFO set in 5 degrees dorsiflexion; GRAFO |
| Stance | Knee buckling | Weak quadriceps | Excessive forward lean; hand on thigh | Quadriceps strengthening; KAFO if severe |
| Stance | Trendelenburg | Weak hip abductors | Lateral trunk lean over affected side | Hip abductor strengthening |
| Stance | Scissoring | Hip adductor spasticity | Narrow base; crossover steps | Botox or phenol to adductors/obturator nerve |
| Entire cycle | Asymmetric step length | Hemiplegic pattern; avoidance of weight-bearing | Decreased time on affected side | Address all contributing factors; gait training |
| Entire cycle | Slow gait velocity | Combined deficits | N/A | Comprehensive rehabilitation; AFO; assistive device |

### 10.13 Contracture Prevention Protocols

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Contracture development is a major preventable complication in stroke patients with persistent paresis or spasticity. Once established, contractures significantly limit function and are difficult to reverse.

**High-Risk Contractures in Stroke:**

| Joint | Common Contracture | Muscles Involved | Prevention Strategy |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Shoulder | Adduction + internal rotation | Pectoralis major, subscapularis | Position in abduction and external rotation; gentle ROM |
| Elbow | Flexion | Biceps, brachialis | Extended positioning; resting splint in extension; ROM |
| Forearm | Pronation | Pronator teres | Position in supination during rest; supination splint |
| Wrist | Flexion | Wrist flexors (FCR, FCU) | Resting hand splint in 20-30 degrees extension; dorsal wrist cock-up splint |
| Fingers | Flexion (clenched fist) | FDS, FDP, intrinsic muscles | Hand splint with fingers extended; cone or roll in palm; hygiene attention |
| Hip | Flexion + adduction | Iliopsoas, adductors | Prone positioning (if tolerated); avoid prolonged sitting; abduction pillow |
| Knee | Flexion OR extension | Hamstrings (flexion) or quadriceps (extension) | Positioning with knee supported in extension; ROM |
| Ankle | Plantarflexion (equinus) | Gastrocsoleus | Night splint or AFO; standing frame; serial casting if developing |

**Contracture Management Hierarchy:**

| Severity | Intervention |
|----------|-------------|
| Risk prevention | Daily ROM, positioning, splinting programs |
| Early contracture (<15 degrees limitation) | Aggressive stretching, dynamic splinting, botox to spastic muscles + serial casting |
| Moderate contracture (15-30 degrees) | Serial casting (progressive plaster or fiberglass casts changed weekly); turnbuckle splints |
| Severe fixed contracture (>30 degrees) | Surgical release (tendon lengthening, capsulotomy); consult orthopedic surgery |

**Serial Casting Protocol:**
- Apply cast at maximum comfortable stretch position
- Change every 5-7 days, progressively increasing range
- Continue until desired ROM achieved or plateau reached
- Often combined with botox injection (inject 1-2 weeks before casting to reduce spasticity)
- Maintain gains with splinting and ongoing stretching program after cast removal

> **Board Pearl:** The ankle equinus contracture is the most clinically significant contracture in hemiplegic patients because it directly impacts ambulation and AFO fitting. Prevention begins immediately with positioning, AFO use, and daily stretching. Serial casting combined with botox injection is the most effective non-surgical treatment for established contractures. Always combine botox with stretching/casting; botox alone without a stretching program is insufficient.

## Board-Style Review Questions

**Question 1:** A 60-year-old man is 3 months post-left MCA stroke. He has a spastic right upper extremity in the classic flexor pattern. The physical therapist reports that on rapid passive stretch of the elbow, there is a catch followed by resistance through approximately 40% of the available ROM. How would you score this on the Modified Ashworth Scale?

*Answer:* MAS grade 1+. The description matches the 1+ definition: "slight increase in tone; catch followed by minimal resistance through LESS THAN half of ROM." Grade 1 would show only a catch and release at end-range. Grade 2 would show increased tone through MOST of the ROM.

**Question 2:** A patient presents 4 months after a right thalamic stroke with burning pain in the left hand, allodynia to light touch, and poor response to oxycodone. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the most appropriate first-line treatment?

*Answer:* Central post-stroke pain (Dejerine-Roussy syndrome). This is characterized by delayed onset (months after thalamic stroke), burning neuropathic pain with allodynia, and poor opioid response. First-line treatment is amitriptyline 10-25 mg at bedtime (titrated up) or gabapentin/pregabalin.

**Question 3:** A hemiplegic patient has severe hip adductor spasticity causing scissoring gait and difficulty with perineal hygiene. The team wants to use a chemical nerve block. Which nerve should be targeted, and which agent is preferred?

*Answer:* The obturator nerve should be targeted with phenol (5-7%). The obturator nerve is a pure motor nerve to the hip adductors, making phenol an excellent choice because the risk of dysesthesia (which occurs when sensory nerve fibers are damaged) is minimal. The immediate onset of phenol (vs. days-to-weeks for botox) is an additional advantage for this large muscle group.
