## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** The FIM has 18 items: 13 motor + 5 cognitive. Each item scored 1-7 (1 = total assistance, 7 = complete independence). Total score range: 18-126. Motor subscale max: 91. Cognitive subscale max: 35. FIM score >80 at discharge predicts home discharge.

> **Board Pearl:** Modified Rankin Scale (mRS) is the most widely used global outcome measure in stroke clinical trials. Score 0-6: 0 = no symptoms, 1 = no significant disability, 2 = slight disability, 3 = moderate disability (walks without assistance), 4 = moderately severe disability (cannot walk without assistance), 5 = severe disability (bedridden), 6 = dead.

> **Board Pearl:** IRF admission criteria: medically stable, can tolerate 3 hours/day of therapy (or 15 hours/week), needs 2 or more therapy disciplines, requires close physician supervision, reasonable expectation of functional improvement. CMS 60% rule: at least 60% of IRF patients must have one of 13 qualifying diagnoses (stroke is #1).

> **Board Pearl:** Lacunar strokes have the BEST short-term functional prognosis (up to 80% achieve independence). Cardioembolic strokes (large MCA territory) have the WORST prognosis.

> **Board Pearl:** The Copenhagen Stroke Study showed that 95% of patients reach their best neurologic recovery level by 11 weeks. However, FUNCTIONAL recovery can continue well beyond this through compensation and skill acquisition.

### 15.1 Functional Outcome Measures

#### 15.1.1 Functional Independence Measure (FIM)

[BOARD] The FIM is the most widely used functional outcome measure in rehabilitation settings and is the foundation of the Inpatient Rehabilitation Facility Patient Assessment Instrument (IRF-PAI) used for Medicare reimbursement.

**7-Level Scoring Scale:**

| Score | Level | Helper Required? | Definition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **7** | Complete Independence | No | Performs task safely, in reasonable time, without modification, assistive device, or helper |
| **6** | Modified Independence | No | Requires assistive device, extra time, or safety concern, but no helper |
| **5** | Supervision/Setup | Yes | Requires standby assist, cueing, coaxing, or setup only; patient performs ≥100% of physical effort |
| **4** | Minimal Assistance | Yes | Patient performs ≥75% of the effort; helper provides ≤25% |
| **3** | Moderate Assistance | Yes | Patient performs 50-74% of the effort; helper provides 26-49% |
| **2** | Maximal Assistance | Yes | Patient performs 25-49% of the effort; helper provides 51-75% |
| **1** | Total Assistance | Yes | Patient performs <25% of the effort; helper provides >75% |

> **Board Pearl:** FIM levels 5-7 = "Modified Independent" — No helper needed (though supervision may be required at level 5). FIM levels 1-4 = "Dependent" — Helper required. The key threshold for discharge planning: Can the patient function at FIM level 5 or above in critical activities? If so, they may be safe with supervision at home.

**FIM Domain Structure:**

| Domain | Items | Maximum Score |
|---|---|---|
| **MOTOR SUBSCALE** | | **91** |
| Self-Care | Eating, Grooming, Bathing, Dressing Upper Body, Dressing Lower Body, Toileting | 42 |
| Sphincter Control | Bladder Management, Bowel Management | 14 |
| Transfers | Bed/Chair/Wheelchair, Toilet, Tub/Shower | 21 |
| Locomotion | Walk/Wheelchair, Stairs | 14 |
| **COGNITIVE SUBSCALE** | | **35** |
| Communication | Comprehension, Expression | 14 |
| Social Cognition | Social Interaction, Problem Solving, Memory | 21 |
| **TOTAL** | **18 items** | **126** |

**Key FIM-Based Metrics:**

| Metric | Definition | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Admission FIM** | FIM score at IRF admission | Baseline; lower admission FIM = more impaired; FIM <40 predicts poor rehab outcomes |
| **Discharge FIM** | FIM score at IRF discharge | >80 predicts home discharge; used for discharge planning |
| **FIM Gain** | Discharge FIM minus Admission FIM | Measures improvement during rehabilitation; average stroke FIM gain = 20-30 points in IRF |
| **FIM Efficiency** | FIM Gain ÷ Length of Stay (points/day) | Measures rate of improvement; average stroke FIM efficiency in IRF = 1.5-2.0 points/day |
| **FIM Motor Subscale** | Sum of 13 motor items (max 91) | Strongest single predictor of discharge destination and care needs |

> **Board Pearl:** FIM score >80 at discharge predicts home discharge. FIM score <40 at admission predicts poor rehabilitation outcomes and possible need for skilled nursing level of care. Average stroke FIM gain in IRF is 20-30 points. Average FIM efficiency is 1.5-2.0 points/day.

#### 15.1.2 Modified Rankin Scale (mRS)

[BOARD] The mRS is the most widely used global disability outcome measure in stroke clinical trials. It is a clinician-rated ordinal scale that captures overall degree of disability and dependence.

| Score | Description | Functional Level |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | No symptoms at all | Normal |
| **1** | No significant disability despite symptoms; able to carry out all usual duties and activities | Symptoms but no disability |
| **2** | Slight disability; unable to carry out all previous activities but able to look after own affairs without assistance | Independent but with limitations |
| **3** | Moderate disability; requires some help but able to walk without assistance | Walks independently; needs some help with ADLs |
| **4** | Moderately severe disability; unable to walk without assistance and unable to attend to own bodily needs without assistance | Cannot walk or manage ADLs alone |
| **5** | Severe disability; bedridden, incontinent, and requires constant nursing care and attention | Bedridden, dependent |
| **6** | Dead | — |

**Key Points:**
- mRS 0-2 is typically considered a "favorable outcome" in clinical trials
- mRS 0-1 is considered "excellent outcome"
- Two analytical approaches in trials: dichotomous (favorable vs. unfavorable at a cutpoint) vs. shift analysis (ordinal shift across the full scale — more statistically powerful)
- **Limitations:** Heavily weighted toward ambulation and physical function; does not capture cognitive, communication, or social participation domains well

> **Board Pearl:** The mRS is the primary endpoint in most stroke treatment trials. mRS 0-2 = favorable outcome (independent). mRS 3-5 = unfavorable outcome (dependent). The scale is weighted toward physical function and may not capture communication or cognitive deficits.

#### 15.1.3 Barthel Index

[BOARD]

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| **Items** | 10 activities (feeding, bathing, grooming, dressing, bowel control, bladder control, toilet use, transfers, mobility, stairs) |
| **Score range** | 0-100 |
| **Scoring** | 0, 5, 10, or 15 points per item depending on independence level |
| **Key thresholds** | >60 = assisted independence (likely safe with some help); <40 = severe dependence; 100 = fully independent in basic ADLs |
| **Strengths** | Simple, quick (5-10 min), widely used, well-validated |
| **Limitations** | Ceiling effect (many stroke patients score 100 despite residual deficits); does not capture instrumental ADLs; limited sensitivity to change in mild-moderate impairment |

> **Board Pearl:** Barthel Index >60 = assisted independence (key threshold). Barthel Index <40 = severe dependence. Ceiling effect is a significant limitation — a patient can score 100 on the Barthel yet still have significant cognitive, communication, or instrumental ADL deficits.

#### 15.1.4 Stroke Impact Scale (SIS)

[COMPREHENSIVE] The SIS is a patient-reported outcome measure designed specifically for stroke, capturing the patient's perspective on the multidimensional impact of stroke on their life.

**8 Domains:**

| Domain | Items | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| Strength | 4 | Perceived strength in affected limbs |
| Hand Function | 5 | Ability to use the affected hand for functional tasks |
| ADL/IADL | 10 | Difficulty with basic and instrumental activities of daily living |
| Mobility | 9 | Walking, balance, stairs, transfers |
| Communication | 7 | Understanding, speaking, reading, writing |
| Emotion | 9 | Emotional well-being, depression, anxiety, sense of burden |
| Memory/Thinking | 7 | Memory, attention, problem-solving, processing speed |
| Social Participation | 8 | Work, social activities, family role, leisure |

**Additional item:** Stroke recovery VAS (0-100): patient rates their overall recovery on a visual analog scale.

**Key Points:**
- Each domain scored 0-100 (higher = better)
- Patient-reported (captures subjective experience)
- More comprehensive than clinician-rated scales
- Captures domains often missed by mRS and Barthel (communication, emotion, social participation)
- Used in clinical trials and clinical practice
- Available in multiple languages

### 15.2 Prognostic Assessment Tools

#### 15.2.1 Orpington Prognostic Scale

[BOARD] The Orpington Prognostic Scale is a simple bedside tool that estimates the probability of independence after stroke rehabilitation. It combines four clinical assessments:

| Component | Assessment | Scoring |
|---|---|---|
| **Motor deficit (arm)** | Arm strength on affected side | 0 = MRC grade 5 (normal); 0.4 = MRC 4; 0.8 = MRC 3; 1.2 = MRC 1-2; 1.6 = MRC 0 |
| **Proprioception** | Position sense in affected thumb | 0 = Intact; 0.4 = Impaired; 0.8 = Absent |
| **Balance** | Sitting balance | 0 = Sits independently and reaches forward; 0.4 = Sits independently but cannot reach; 0.8 = Cannot sit without support |
| **Cognition** | Mental test score (orientation, attention) | 0 = Scores ≥8/10; 0.4 = Scores 5-7; 0.8 = Scores <5 |

**Total Score = 1.6 + sum of component scores**

| Orpington Score | Prediction |
|---|---|
| **<3.2** | Mild stroke; high probability of independence; early discharge likely |
| **3.2-5.2** | Moderate stroke; benefits from inpatient rehabilitation; variable outcomes |
| **>5.2** | Severe stroke; poor prognosis for independence; likely needs institutional care |

#### 15.2.2 Reding Three-Factor Approach

[BOARD] Reding and colleagues identified three factors at 2 weeks poststroke that strongly predict rehabilitation outcomes:

| Factor | Assessment | Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Sitting balance** | Can the patient maintain independent sitting for 30 seconds? | Sitting balance at 2 weeks is one of the strongest predictors of ambulation and ADL independence |
| **Bladder continence** | Is the patient continent of urine? | Persistent incontinence at 2 weeks predicts poor functional outcomes and institutional placement |
| **Motor function** | Is voluntary movement present in the affected limbs? | Absence of voluntary movement at 2 weeks has significant negative prognostic implications |

**Prediction Categories:**
- **0 factors absent (all three present):** Best prognosis — high probability of discharge to home and functional independence
- **1 factor absent:** Intermediate prognosis — likely benefits from intensive rehabilitation
- **2-3 factors absent:** Poor prognosis — may not benefit from intensive rehabilitation; SNF or long-term care more likely

> **Board Pearl:** The Reding three factors at 2 weeks are: (1) sitting balance, (2) continence, and (3) motor function. All three present = good prognosis. All three absent = poor prognosis. These are simple bedside assessments that powerfully predict rehabilitation outcomes.

#### 15.2.3 Motor Recovery Predictors

[BOARD]

| Predictor | Timing | Prognostic Value |
|---|---|---|
| **Voluntary finger extension + shoulder abduction (SAFE score)** | 72 hours | If both present: 98% probability of some dextrous upper extremity function at 6 months |
| **No hand movement** | 4 weeks | Poor prognosis for useful hand recovery |
| **Hand movement present** | 4 weeks | ~70% chance of useful hand recovery |
| **Sitting balance present** | 2 weeks | Strong predictor of ambulation and ADL independence |
| **Sitting balance + leg muscle contraction** | 72 hours | 98% probability of independent ambulation |
| **Best neurological recovery** | 11 weeks | 95% reach best neurologic level by this time (Copenhagen Stroke Study) |

> **Board Pearl:** The SAFE score (Shoulder Abduction + Finger Extension) at 72 hours is a powerful early predictor: if both are present, 98% probability of functional upper extremity recovery. If neither is present at 72 hours, the prognosis for UE function is poor. This can be assessed at the bedside with a simple motor examination.

### 15.3 Stroke Prognosis by Subtype

[BOARD]

| Stroke Subtype | Short-Term Functional Prognosis | Long-Term Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **Lacunar (small vessel)** | **BEST** — Up to 80% achieve functional independence; small infarct size; cortical function preserved | High recurrence rate (~25% in 5 years); cumulative white matter disease leads to vascular cognitive impairment over time |
| **Large artery atherosclerotic** | Moderate to poor — depends on collateral circulation and infarct size | Risk of recurrent stroke; may benefit most from aggressive secondary prevention |
| **Cardioembolic (large MCA)** | **WORST** — Large territory involvement; higher initial severity; more global deficits | Anticoagulation for AF reduces recurrence; significant long-term disability |
| **Watershed (hemodynamic)** | Variable — depends on degree and duration of hypoperfusion | Often bilateral; may cause mixed transcortical aphasia; cognitive impairment common |
| **Posterior circulation** | Variable — brainstem: severe acutely but some recovery possible; PCA: visual deficits may be permanent | Brainstem strokes may have prolonged recovery course; cerebellar strokes often have good motor recovery |

> **Board Pearl:** Lacunar strokes = best short-term prognosis (80% independence). Cardioembolic (large MCA) = worst prognosis. Each additional NIHSS point at presentation decreases odds of excellent outcome by 17%. NIHSS ≤6: good recovery likely. NIHSS >16: high probability of death or severe disability.

**Recovery Milestones (Copenhagen Stroke Study and Others):**
- Most recovery occurs in the first 3-6 months
- 95% of patients reach best neurologic level by 11 weeks
- Neurologic recovery is distinct from functional recovery — patients continue to make functional gains through compensation and skill acquisition well beyond 11 weeks
- Aphasia recovery: mild ~2 weeks, moderate ~6 weeks, severe ~10 weeks to maximum improvement
- Motor recovery follows a predictable sequence (Brunnstrom stages): flaccidity → spasticity with synergy patterns → voluntary movement out of synergy → near-normal movement

### 15.4 The Rehabilitation Continuum

[BOARD] Stroke rehabilitation occurs along a continuum of settings, with the appropriate level of care determined by the patient's medical status, functional abilities, rehabilitation needs, and goals.

#### 15.4.1 Settings Along the Continuum

| Setting | Criteria | Therapy Intensity | Average LOS | Typical Patient |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Acute Care Hospital** | Medically unstable; acute treatment needs; diagnostic workup | Minimal; bedside mobilization; prevention of complications | 4-7 days | Acute stroke; medical complications; surgical intervention |
| **Inpatient Rehabilitation Facility (IRF)** | Medically stable; tolerates 3h/day therapy; 2+ disciplines; physician oversight; reasonable expectation of improvement | High: 3 hours/day (or 15 hours/week) minimum | ~15 days (stroke average) | Moderate-severe stroke with rehabilitation potential |
| **Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF)** | Cannot tolerate IRF intensity; medical complexity; lower rehabilitation potential | 1-2 hours/day typical | Variable (weeks to months) | Patients too debilitated for IRF; those awaiting placement; complex medical needs |
| **Long-Term Acute Care Hospital (LTACH)** | Medically complex; prolonged ventilator weaning; complex wounds | Variable | 25+ days | Ventilator-dependent; severe medical complexity |
| **Home Health** | Homebound; needs skilled therapy; can manage in home environment | 2-5 sessions/week (PT, OT, SLP as needed) | 30-60 day episodes | Mild-moderate stroke; post-IRF transition; home environment challenges |
| **Outpatient Rehabilitation** | Can travel to clinic; ongoing therapy needs; motivated | 2-5 sessions/week | Months to years | Post-IRF or post-home health; chronic stroke; maintenance |
| **Day Rehabilitation** | Structure and intensity between outpatient and IRF | 3-5 hours/day | Weeks to months | Alternative to IRF for some patients; ongoing intensive rehabilitation |
| **Community-Based Programs** | Chronic stroke; maintenance; fitness | Variable | Ongoing | Long-term exercise, social support, community reintegration |

#### 15.4.2 IRF Admission Criteria

[BOARD] Admission to an IRF requires meeting specific Medicare criteria:

**Clinical Criteria:**
1. **Medical stability** — Patient is medically stable enough to participate in rehabilitation
2. **Therapy tolerance** — Can tolerate a minimum of **3 hours/day of therapy** (or 15 hours/week). Therapy can be a combination of PT, OT, and SLP
3. **Multiple therapy disciplines** — Requires **2 or more therapy disciplines** (PT, OT, SLP, rehabilitation psychology, recreation therapy, orthotics/prosthetics)
4. **Physician supervision** — Requires close medical supervision by a rehabilitation physician (physiatrist or designated attending)
5. **Realistic goals** — Reasonable expectation of meaningful functional improvement within a reasonable timeframe
6. **Active participation** — Patient is cognitively and physically able to actively participate in the rehabilitation program

**CMS 60% Rule:**

[BOARD] The CMS 60% rule requires that at least 60% of an IRF's total patient population must have a primary diagnosis or comorbidity that falls into one of 13 qualifying conditions:

| # | Qualifying Condition |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Stroke** (most common qualifying diagnosis) |
| 2 | Spinal cord injury |
| 3 | Congenital deformity |
| 4 | Amputation |
| 5 | Major multiple trauma |
| 6 | Hip fracture |
| 7 | Brain injury |
| 8 | Burns |
| 9 | Active polyarthritis (including rheumatoid arthritis) |
| 10 | Systemic vasculitis with joint involvement |
| 11 | Specified neurological disorders (MS, motor neuron disease, polyneuropathy, muscular dystrophy, Parkinson disease) |
| 12 | Severe or advanced osteoarthritis (involving 2+ weight-bearing joints with BMI ≥50) |
| 13 | Knee or hip joint replacement (if bilateral, BMI ≥50, or age ≥85) |

> **Board Pearl:** Stroke is the #1 qualifying diagnosis for IRF admission under the CMS 60% rule. The 60% compliance threshold means IRFs must maintain at least 60% of their census with patients having one of these 13 conditions — this distinguishes IRFs from SNFs.

**3-Hour Rule:**
- Patients must be able to tolerate at least 3 hours of therapy per day (or 15 hours per week)
- Therapy can be a combination of different disciplines in a single day
- Can be delivered in individual or group sessions
- Recent evidence suggests the 3-hour rule does not independently predict better outcomes, but it remains a regulatory requirement

#### 15.4.3 Length of Stay and Efficiency

[COMPREHENSIVE]

| Metric | Stroke Average (IRF) | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Average LOS** | ~15 days | Has decreased over time due to payment pressures; shorter LOS associated with lower costs but potentially less recovery |
| **FIM gain** | 20-30 points | Average improvement during IRF stay |
| **FIM efficiency** | 1.5-2.0 points/day | Rate of functional improvement; used for program benchmarking |
| **Discharge to community** | 65-75% | Varies by facility, patient population, and social support |

### 15.5 Discharge Destination Predictors

[BOARD] Predicting discharge destination is essential for planning and family counseling.

| Factor | Predicts HOME Discharge | Predicts INSTITUTIONAL Placement |
|---|---|---|
| **FIM score** | Discharge FIM >80 | Admission FIM <40 |
| **Motor function** | Good motor recovery; independent mobility | Severe hemiplegia; wheelchair-dependent |
| **Cognition** | Intact or mild impairment | Severe cognitive impairment; safety concerns |
| **Continence** | Continent | Persistent incontinence |
| **Age** | Younger | Older (>80) |
| **Social support** | Strong family/caregiver; accessible home | Lives alone; no caregiver; inaccessible home |
| **Aphasia** | Mild or absent | Severe (global); limits safety and communication |
| **Neglect** | Absent | Present (especially with anosognosia) |
| **Depression** | Absent or treated | Untreated; apathy |
| **Comorbidities** | Few | Multiple; complex medical needs |
| **Pre-stroke function** | Independent | Already dependent |

> **Board Pearl:** The strongest predictors of discharge destination are: FIM score (especially motor subscale), social support, cognition, and continence. FIM >80 at discharge = home. FIM <40 at admission = poor rehab potential. Social support availability may be the most modifiable factor — social work involvement is critical.

### 15.6 Telerehabilitation

[COMPREHENSIVE] Telerehabilitation has emerged as an important modality for extending stroke rehabilitation services, particularly since the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated adoption.

**Evidence Base:**
- Systematic reviews and RCTs demonstrate that telerehabilitation for stroke is non-inferior to conventional face-to-face rehabilitation for upper extremity function, ADL outcomes, balance, and mobility
- Strong evidence for telerehabilitation-delivered speech-language therapy for aphasia
- Growing evidence for tele-neuropsychological assessment and cognitive rehabilitation
- Particularly valuable for rural and underserved populations with limited access to specialized rehabilitation services

**Modalities:**

| Modality | Application | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Synchronous video therapy** | PT, OT, SLP sessions via videoconference | Strong — multiple RCTs show non-inferiority |
| **Asynchronous monitoring** | Home exercise programs with sensor-based tracking; automated reminders | Moderate — improves adherence to home programs |
| **Remote cognitive rehabilitation** | Computer-based cognitive training with therapist oversight | Moderate |
| **Tele-psychology** | CBT for depression, anxiety; caregiver support | Strong (consistent with general teletherapy evidence) |
| **Remote monitoring** | Blood pressure, glucose, medication adherence; fall detection | Emerging — improves secondary prevention adherence |
| **Virtual reality rehabilitation** | Gamified exercise and motor training via VR platforms at home | Emerging — engaging; may improve adherence |

**Barriers:**
- Technology access and literacy (digital divide disproportionately affects elderly, low-income, rural patients)
- Internet connectivity requirements
- Privacy and security concerns (HIPAA compliance)
- Inability to provide hands-on assistance (cueing, guarding, manual techniques)
- Reimbursement variability across payers and states
- Patient preference — some patients prefer in-person therapy

**Best Applications:**
- Continuation of therapy after IRF discharge (home-based)
- Rural and remote populations
- Aphasia therapy (strong evidence for intensive remote SLP)
- Caregiver training and support
- Secondary prevention monitoring
- Long-term maintenance programs

### 15.7 Quality Metrics and Ongoing Surveillance

[COMPREHENSIVE] Stroke care quality is measured and reported through several frameworks:

#### 15.7.1 CMS Quality Measures for Stroke

| Measure | Description | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Dysphagia screening** | Screening performed before any oral intake | 100% compliance |
| **DVT prophylaxis** | Pharmacologic or mechanical prophylaxis by hospital day 2 | >95% compliance |
| **Antithrombotic therapy at discharge** | Antiplatelet or anticoagulant prescribed | >95% compliance |
| **Statin therapy at discharge** | Statin prescribed for appropriate patients | High compliance |
| **Rehabilitation assessment** | Assessment of rehabilitation needs documented | 100% compliance |
| **Stroke education** | Patient/family education on stroke warning signs, follow-up, and risk factor management | High compliance |

#### 15.7.2 Joint Commission Stroke Center Certification

[COMPREHENSIVE] The Joint Commission certifies Primary Stroke Centers (PSC) and Comprehensive Stroke Centers (CSC) based on adherence to quality measures:

**Key metrics:**
- Door-to-needle time for tPA (target ≤60 minutes)
- Door-to-groin puncture for thrombectomy (target ≤90 minutes)
- Dysphagia screening completion
- DVT prophylaxis implementation
- Antithrombotic therapy at discharge
- Anticoagulation for AF at discharge
- Statin therapy at discharge
- Rehabilitation assessment
- Stroke education

#### 15.7.3 Ongoing Surveillance After Stroke

[BOARD] Stroke survivors require ongoing medical surveillance for multiple conditions:

| Domain | Surveillance Need | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **Secondary stroke prevention** | Blood pressure, lipids, glucose, AF screening, medication adherence | Every 3-6 months initially; then annually |
| **Spasticity** | Clinical assessment; functional impact; contracture development | Every visit; Ashworth/Tardieu scales |
| **Depression** | PHQ-9 or equivalent screening | 3, 6, 12 months minimum; then annually |
| **Cognition** | MoCA or equivalent screening; functional cognitive assessment | 3-6 months; then annually or as indicated |
| **Osteoporosis** | DEXA scan; calcium/vitamin D supplementation | Baseline within 1 year; repeat per guidelines |
| **Falls** | Fall risk assessment; fall history; medication review | Every visit |
| **Contractures** | ROM assessment; splinting/stretching program adherence | Every visit in first year; then as indicated |
| **Driving** | Cognitive and visual screening; CDRS evaluation when appropriate | As clinically indicated |
| **Return to work** | Vocational assessment; workplace accommodation needs | During rehabilitation and follow-up |
| **Sexuality** | Proactive screening and counseling | At least once during rehabilitation; revisit at follow-up |
| **Caregiver burden** | Caregiver screening; resource provision | At every major transition of care |
| **Nutritional status** | Weight, BMI, nutritional screening (MUST) | Every visit |
| **Pain** | Pain assessment (visual analog scale, numeric rating); specific pain syndromes (shoulder, central pain, spasticity) | Every visit |

### 15.8 Landmark Trials in Stroke Rehabilitation

[BOARD]

| Trial | Year | Key Finding | Board Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **EXCITE** | 2006 | Constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT) in chronic stroke (3-9 months) improves motor function in patients with some preserved wrist/finger extension | First large RCT proving CIMT efficacy; establishes that motor recovery is possible beyond the traditional "plateau" |
| **AVERT** | 2015 | Very early mobilization (<24 hours poststroke) is HARMFUL — associated with worse 3-month outcomes | Do NOT rush mobilization. Wait at least 24 hours. Early does not mean immediate |
| **FOCUS/AFFINITY/EFFECTS** | 2019-2020 | Routine SSRI (fluoxetine) does NOT improve overall stroke recovery outcomes | SSRIs are for DEPRESSION treatment, not for general stroke recovery |
| **VNS-REHAB** | 2021 | Vagus nerve stimulation paired with intensive rehabilitation improves upper extremity motor recovery in chronic stroke | FDA-approved for stroke rehab; represents a new paradigm in neuromodulation-enhanced rehabilitation |
| **FLAME** | 2011 | Fluoxetine appeared to improve motor recovery — later refuted by FOCUS/AFFINITY/EFFECTS | Important as a cautionary tale about small, positive trials being overturned by large RCTs |
| **FOOD** | 2005 | Early PEG (within 2-3 weeks) associated with trend toward worse functional outcomes vs. NG tube; no significant mortality difference. Clinical consensus supports PEG for dysphagia expected to persist >4 weeks | NG for <4 weeks; PEG for dysphagia >4 weeks (not as early intervention) |
| **CLOTS 1/3** | 2009/2013 | GCS does not prevent DVT (CLOTS 1); IPC does (CLOTS 3) | IPC for all immobile stroke patients |
| **Copenhagen Stroke Study** | 1995 | 95% reach best neurologic recovery by 11 weeks; recovery patterns vary by severity | Foundation for understanding stroke recovery trajectory |

> **Board Pearl:** AVERT trial: Very early mobilization (<24 hours) is HARMFUL in acute stroke. EXCITE trial: CIMT is effective for chronic stroke with some preserved UE function. VNS-REHAB: Vagus nerve stimulation + rehab improves UE recovery. FOCUS/AFFINITY/EFFECTS: SSRIs do NOT improve general stroke recovery.

### 15.9 Community Reintegration and Long-Term Outcomes

[COMPREHENSIVE]

#### 15.9.1 Long-Term Residual Deficits (6 Months Post-Stroke)

| Deficit | Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Hemiparesis | 50% |
| Cognitive deficits | 46% |
| Depression | 35% |
| Unable to walk independently | 31% |
| Institutionalized | 26% |
| Hemianopia | 20% |
| Aphasia | 19% |

#### 15.9.2 Return to Work

- Approximately 50% of working-age stroke survivors return to work within 6-12 months
- Job retention at 1 year is lower than initial return
- Positive predictors: younger age, white-collar job, mild stroke, preserved cognition, employer flexibility
- Negative predictors: severe motor deficits, aphasia, cognitive impairment, physically demanding work, depression

#### 15.9.3 Ongoing Rehabilitation Needs

[COMPREHENSIVE] Stroke is a chronic condition requiring lifelong management:

**Spasticity Management:**
- Develops in 20-40% of stroke survivors, typically 1-6 months poststroke
- Assessment: Modified Ashworth Scale, Tardieu Scale
- Treatment hierarchy: stretching/ROM → orthotic management → oral medications (baclofen, tizanidine) → focal chemodenervation (botulinum toxin) → intrathecal baclofen → surgical intervention
- Goal-oriented treatment: reduce pain, improve function, facilitate care, prevent contractures

**Contracture Prevention:**
- Daily ROM exercises (active or passive)
- Positioning programs
- Serial casting or dynamic splinting for established contractures
- Night splints for ankle equinus prevention

**Depression Surveillance:**
- Ongoing screening at follow-up visits (PHQ-9)
- Medication management and therapy referral
- Monitor for suicidal ideation (suicide risk is elevated poststroke)

**Osteoporosis Management:**
- Calcium + Vitamin D supplementation
- DEXA screening
- Bisphosphonates or denosumab if indicated
- Weight-bearing exercise

**Secondary Stroke Prevention:**
- Blood pressure control (<130/80 for most; medication adherence)
- Statin therapy (high-intensity for atherosclerotic mechanism)
- Antiplatelet therapy (aspirin, clopidogrel, or aspirin/dipyridamole for non-cardioembolic)
- Anticoagulation for AF (DOACs preferred over warfarin for non-valvular AF)
- Diabetes management
- Smoking cessation
- Exercise (150 min/week moderate intensity when able)
- Diet optimization (Mediterranean or DASH pattern)
