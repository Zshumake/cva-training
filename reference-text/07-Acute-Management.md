## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** Finger-stick blood glucose is the ONLY mandatory laboratory test before administering tPA. Do not delay thrombolysis for other lab results unless there is clinical suspicion of coagulopathy.

> **Board Pearl:** Alteplase is dosed at 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg): 10% given as IV bolus over 1 minute, remaining 90% infused over 60 minutes. Tenecteplase is dosed at 0.25 mg/kg (max 25 mg) as a single IV bolus.

> **Board Pearl:** BP must be less than 185/110 BEFORE tPA and maintained below 180/105 for 24 hours AFTER tPA. If BP cannot be brought below these thresholds, tPA is CONTRAINDICATED.

> **Board Pearl:** The AVERT trial demonstrated that very early mobilization (within 24 hours of stroke onset) REDUCED the odds of a favorable outcome. Current guidelines recommend waiting at least 24 hours before mobilization in stable patients.

> **Board Pearl:** The SHINE trial showed that intensive glucose control (80-130 mg/dL) did NOT improve outcomes compared to standard control (80-179 mg/dL) and caused more hypoglycemia. Target glucose is 140-180 mg/dL.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 7.1 Initial Stroke Assessment: The ABCs

**[BOARD]**

Acute stroke is a medical emergency where every minute of ischemia results in the loss of approximately 1.9 million neurons, 14 billion synapses, and 12 km of myelinated fibers. The phrase "time is brain" encapsulates the urgency of acute management.

**Systematic Initial Assessment:**

| Step | Action | Rationale |
|------|--------|-----------|
| A - Airway | Assess airway patency; intubate if GCS of 8 or less or inability to protect airway | Aspiration risk; adequate oxygenation essential |
| B - Breathing | Pulse oximetry; supplemental O2 only if SpO2 <94% | Avoid hyperoxia (no proven benefit; theoretical harm from free radicals) |
| C - Circulation | Two large-bore IVs; cardiac monitoring; BP assessment | Prepare for IV thrombolysis; identify arrhythmias |
| Glucose | Finger-stick glucose IMMEDIATELY | Hypoglycemia mimics stroke; only mandatory pre-tPA test |
| NIHSS | Standardized neurological assessment | Quantify deficit severity; guide treatment decisions |
| CT/MRI | Emergent non-contrast CT (or MRI where available) | Exclude hemorrhage; assess for early ischemic changes |
| NPO | Nothing by mouth until swallowing evaluation | Dysphagia in 37-78% of stroke patients |

**Time Benchmarks (AHA/ASA Guidelines):**

| Metric | Target Time |
|--------|------------|
| Door-to-physician | Less than 10 minutes |
| Door-to-CT completion | Less than 25 minutes |
| Door-to-CT interpretation | Less than 45 minutes |
| Door-to-needle (tPA) | Less than 60 minutes |
| Door-to-groin puncture (thrombectomy) | Less than 90 minutes |

Every 15-minute reduction in door-to-needle time is associated with 4% better independent ambulation, 3% more home discharge, and 4% less mortality.

**NPO and Swallowing Assessment:**

All stroke patients must remain NPO until a formal swallowing evaluation is completed. Dysphagia is present in 37-78% of acute stroke patients, and aspiration pneumonia is a leading preventable cause of death after stroke. A bedside swallowing screen should be performed by trained personnel before any oral intake, including medications. If the patient is NPO and needs aspirin, it can be administered rectally.

### 7.2 IV Thrombolysis with Alteplase

**[BOARD]**

Alteplase (tissue plasminogen activator, tPA) was the first FDA-approved therapy for acute ischemic stroke (1996) and remained the sole approved thrombolytic until tenecteplase gained approval in 2025.

**Alteplase Dosing and Administration:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Dose | 0.9 mg/kg (maximum 90 mg) |
| Bolus | 10% of total dose IV over 1 minute |
| Infusion | Remaining 90% IV over 60 minutes |
| Standard time window | 0-3 hours from symptom onset (FDA-approved) |
| Extended window | 3-4.5 hours (AHA/ASA guideline-recommended; ECASS III) |
| Administration route | Intravenous only |

**Efficacy by Time Window:**

| Window | NNT for Good Outcome (mRS 0-1) | Outcome Rate (tPA vs Control) |
|--------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 0-3 hours | 10 | 33% vs 23% |
| 3-4.5 hours | 20 | 35% vs 30% |
| Beyond 4.5 hours (standard) | Not significant | No benefit without imaging selection |

**Risk of Symptomatic Intracranial Hemorrhage (sICH):**
- Overall sICH rate with alteplase: 6.8% vs 1.3% with placebo
- NNH (number needed to harm) for sICH: 18
- Despite the hemorrhage risk, the net benefit favors treatment within the time window

> **Board Pearl:** The benefit of tPA is strongly time-dependent. Every 15-minute reduction in onset-to-treatment time improves outcomes. The benefit is similar regardless of age or stroke severity, though the absolute benefit is greater in more severe strokes.

**Inclusion Criteria for IV Alteplase (0-3 hour window):**

1. Clinical diagnosis of ischemic stroke causing measurable neurological deficit
2. Onset of symptoms (or last known well time) within 3 hours
3. Age 18 years or older
4. Non-contrast CT showing no hemorrhage

**Exclusion Criteria (Major):**

| Category | Exclusion |
|----------|-----------|
| Hemorrhagic | Active internal bleeding; hemorrhagic stroke on CT; known bleeding diathesis |
| Coagulation | Platelet count <100,000; INR >1.7; aPTT elevated; on DOACs with abnormal coag tests |
| Blood pressure | SBP >185 or DBP >110 despite treatment |
| Recent surgery | Major surgery or serious trauma within 14 days |
| CNS | History of intracranial hemorrhage; intracranial neoplasm, AVM, or aneurysm |
| Glucose | Blood glucose <50 mg/dL |
| Imaging | Multilobar infarct (hypodensity >1/3 of cerebral hemisphere) |
| Other | Arterial puncture at noncompressible site within 7 days |

### 7.3 ECASS III Extended Window (3-4.5 Hours)

**[BOARD]**

The ECASS III trial (Hacke et al., NEJM 2008) extended the thrombolysis window to 4.5 hours, demonstrating benefit of alteplase in the 3-4.5 hour window (52.4% vs 45.2% favorable outcome; OR 1.34; NNT approximately 14).

**Additional Exclusions for the 3-4.5 Hour Window:**

These exclusions applied specifically to the ECASS III population (though some have been relaxed in more recent guidelines):

| Criterion | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| Age >80 years | Higher hemorrhage risk (subsequently shown to still benefit; relaxed in 2019 AHA guidelines) |
| NIHSS >25 | Very severe stroke with higher hemorrhage risk |
| Oral anticoagulant use regardless of INR | Even subtherapeutic levels may increase bleeding (subsequently relaxed with qualifications) |
| History of both diabetes AND prior stroke | Combination associated with worse outcomes |

> **Board Pearl:** The ECASS III additional exclusions (age >80, NIHSS >25, diabetes + prior stroke, oral anticoagulant use) were specific to the trial population. The 2019 AHA/ASA guidelines relaxed several of these: alteplase is now recommended for eligible patients aged over 80 in the 3-4.5 hour window, and oral anticoagulant use is no longer an absolute exclusion if INR is 1.7 or less.

### 7.4 Tenecteplase

**[BOARD]**

Tenecteplase (TNK) received FDA approval for acute ischemic stroke in February 2025, marking the first new thrombolytic agent approved for stroke since alteplase in 1996.

**Tenecteplase vs. Alteplase Comparison:**

| Feature | Tenecteplase | Alteplase |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Dose | 0.25 mg/kg (max 25 mg) | 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg) |
| Administration | Single IV bolus | 10% bolus + 60-minute infusion |
| Fibrin specificity | Higher | Lower |
| Half-life | 20-24 minutes | 4-5 minutes |
| PAI-1 resistance | Greater | Less |
| Door-to-needle time | Faster (simplified administration) | Longer (requires infusion setup) |
| FDA approval for stroke | February 2025 | 1996 |
| Efficacy | Similar to alteplase | Reference standard |
| sICH risk | Similar to alteplase | Reference standard |
| Cost | Comparable | Reference standard |

**Key Clinical Evidence:**

- A 2024 meta-analysis of 11 trials (n=7,545) demonstrated similar efficacy and safety between tenecteplase 0.25 mg/kg and alteplase
- Higher doses (0.4 mg/kg or greater) may be harmful: the NOR-TEST 2 trial was stopped early due to excess hemorrhage at higher doses
- Tenecteplase is now recommended as an alternative to alteplase in the 2026 AHA/ASA guidelines

**Advantages of Tenecteplase:**
- Single bolus administration simplifies logistics (particularly for mobile stroke units, telestroke, and emergency departments)
- Faster door-to-needle times
- No requirement for continuous infusion pump
- Higher fibrin specificity (theoretically more targeted clot lysis)

> **Board Pearl:** Tenecteplase for stroke is dosed at 0.25 mg/kg (maximum 25 mg) as a single IV bolus. This is different from the cardiac dose (0.5 mg/kg for STEMI). The stroke dose is HALF the cardiac dose. Using the cardiac dose for stroke increases hemorrhage risk.

### 7.5 Extended-Window Thrombolysis (4.5-24 Hours)

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Beyond the standard 4.5-hour window, thrombolysis may be considered in selected patients with favorable imaging profiles:

- **DWI-FLAIR mismatch:** As demonstrated in the WAKE-UP trial, patients with unknown-onset stroke who have DWI-positive/FLAIR-negative mismatch on MRI can benefit from IV thrombolysis
- **Perfusion mismatch:** The EXTEND trial (Ma et al., NEJM 2019) demonstrated benefit of alteplase in the 4.5-9 hour window using CT or MRI perfusion selection (mRS 0-1: 35.4% vs 29.5%)
- **HOPE trial:** Alteplase 4.5-24 hours with CTP selection showed mRS 0-1 of 40.3% vs 26.3%
- **Tenecteplase in extended windows:** A 2025 meta-analysis showed benefit of tenecteplase 4.5-24 hours with perfusion selection when EVT was not available

### 7.6 Endovascular Thrombectomy

**[BOARD]**

Mechanical thrombectomy is the standard of care for acute ischemic stroke due to large vessel occlusion (LVO) in the anterior circulation. Five landmark trials published in 2015 (MR CLEAN, ESCAPE, EXTEND-IA, SWIFT PRIME, REVASCAT) established thrombectomy within 6 hours. The DAWN and DEFUSE-3 trials extended the window to 24 hours with imaging selection.

**DAWN Trial (Nogueira et al., NEJM 2018):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Window | 6-24 hours from last known well |
| Selection | Clinical-core mismatch (NIHSS severity disproportionate to infarct core volume) |
| Imaging | CT perfusion or MRI DWI processed by automated software |
| Target vessels | ICA or proximal MCA (M1) occlusion |
| Mismatch criteria | Group A: Age 80 or older, NIHSS of 10 or greater, core less than 21 mL |
| | Group B: Age under 80, NIHSS of 10 or greater, core less than 31 mL |
| | Group C: Age under 80, NIHSS of 20 or greater, core 31-51 mL |
| Primary outcome | Functional independence (mRS 0-2) at 90 days |
| Results | 49% vs 13% functional independence (thrombectomy vs control) |
| NNT | 2.8 |

**DEFUSE-3 Trial (Albers et al., NEJM 2018):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Window | 6-16 hours from last known well |
| Selection | Perfusion-core mismatch on CT perfusion or MRI perfusion |
| Target vessels | ICA or M1 MCA occlusion |
| Mismatch criteria | Mismatch ratio of 1.8 or greater; ischemic core less than 70 mL; penumbra volume of 15 mL or greater |
| Primary outcome | mRS score distribution at 90 days (ordinal shift analysis) |
| Results | mRS 0-2: 45% vs 17% (thrombectomy vs control) |
| Note | Stopped early for overwhelming efficacy |

**Comparison of DAWN vs. DEFUSE-3:**

| Feature | DAWN | DEFUSE-3 |
|---------|------|----------|
| Time window | 6-24 hours | 6-16 hours |
| Selection method | Clinical-core mismatch | Perfusion-core mismatch |
| Core volume limit | 21-51 mL (age/NIHSS dependent) | Less than 70 mL |
| Mismatch criterion | Clinical deficit disproportionate to core | Mismatch ratio of 1.8 or greater |
| Patient population | More restrictive | Broader (approximately 40% of DEFUSE-3 patients would not have met DAWN criteria) |
| Functional independence | 49% vs 13% | 45% vs 17% |

> **Board Pearl:** DAWN uses clinical-core mismatch (the clinical deficit is worse than expected given the small infarct core), while DEFUSE-3 uses perfusion-core mismatch (the area of hypoperfusion is much larger than the infarct core). Both demonstrate dramatic benefit of thrombectomy in the extended window. DAWN extends to 24 hours; DEFUSE-3 extends to 16 hours.

**Bridging Therapy (IVT + EVT):**

Current evidence supports bridging therapy (starting IV thrombolysis followed by thrombectomy) rather than proceeding directly to thrombectomy alone. The rationale includes:
- IV thrombolysis may begin dissolving the clot while the interventional team prepares
- Some patients may recanalize with IVT alone, avoiding the procedural risks of thrombectomy
- The IRIS meta-analysis showed IVT is beneficial if started within 2 hours and 20 minutes of onset even when thrombectomy is planned

**Large Core Infarcts:**

Recent trials (SELECT2, ANGEL-ASPECT, RESCUE-Japan LIMIT) have demonstrated benefit of thrombectomy even in patients with large core infarcts (ASPECTS 3-5 or core volume 50-100 mL), expanding eligibility beyond traditional criteria. The 2026 AHA/ASA guidelines now include expanded eligibility for EVT including large core infarcts and basilar artery occlusions.

### 7.7 Blood Pressure Management in Acute Stroke

**[BOARD]**

Blood pressure management is one of the most nuanced aspects of acute stroke care. The approach differs fundamentally based on stroke type and whether reperfusion therapy is administered.

**Blood Pressure Targets by Scenario:**

| Clinical Scenario | BP Target | First-Line Agent | Rationale |
|-------------------|-----------|-----------------|-----------|
| Ischemic stroke, NO reperfusion therapy | Treat only if SBP >220 or DBP >120 | Labetalol or nicardipine PRN | Permissive hypertension maintains collateral flow to penumbra |
| Ischemic stroke, PRE-tPA | Must be less than 185/110 | Labetalol 10-20 mg IV push; nicardipine 5 mg/hr | Must achieve before giving tPA; otherwise tPA is contraindicated |
| Ischemic stroke, POST-tPA/thrombectomy | Maintain below 180/105 for 24 hours | Nicardipine infusion (titratable) | Reduces hemorrhagic transformation risk |
| No reperfusion, when treating | Lower by approximately 15% in first 24 hours | Labetalol or nicardipine | Avoid precipitous drops |
| ICH | Target SBP less than 140 (INTERACT2) | Nicardipine infusion | Reduces hematoma expansion |
| SAH, pre-securing | SBP less than 160 | Nicardipine or labetalol | Prevent rebleeding |
| SAH, post-securing with vasospasm | Allow induced hypertension (SBP 160-200) | Phenylephrine or norepinephrine | Push blood through spastic vessels |

**Antihypertensive Agents in Acute Stroke:**

| Agent | Mechanism | Route | Onset | Advantages | Cautions |
|-------|-----------|-------|-------|------------|---------|
| Labetalol | Combined alpha/beta blocker | IV push (10-20 mg) | 5-10 min | First-line; easy bolus dosing | Avoid in severe bradycardia, asthma, heart block |
| Nicardipine | Dihydropyridine CCB | IV infusion (5-15 mg/hr) | 5-15 min | Most titratable; preferred for sustained control | Reflex tachycardia |
| Clevidipine | Dihydropyridine CCB | IV infusion (1-32 mg/hr) | 1-2 min | Ultra-short acting; very titratable | Lipid emulsion (contraindicated in soy/egg allergy) |
| Hydralazine | Direct vasodilator | IV push (10-20 mg) | 10-20 min | Available, inexpensive | Unpredictable; reflex tachycardia |

**Agents to AVOID in Acute Stroke:**

| Agent | Reason to Avoid |
|-------|----------------|
| Sublingual nifedipine | Precipitous, uncontrolled BP drop |
| Nitroprusside | Increases ICP (cerebral vasodilation); cyanide toxicity risk |
| Oral antihypertensives (acutely) | Unpredictable absorption and timing |

> **Board Pearl:** Permissive hypertension is the critical concept for ischemic stroke without reperfusion therapy. Elevated BP is a compensatory mechanism to maintain perfusion through collateral pathways to the ischemic penumbra. Aggressively lowering BP can extend the infarct. Treat only if SBP exceeds 220 or DBP exceeds 120 in the absence of reperfusion therapy.

**Key Blood Pressure Trial Data:**

- International Stroke Trial: U-shaped relationship between BP and outcomes (both high and low BP associated with worse outcomes)
- CATIS, SCAST, COSSACS, ENOS: Early BP reduction in acute stroke did NOT improve functional outcomes
- ENOS additionally found that STOPPING pre-existing antihypertensives was associated with worse outcomes including cognition
- After the acute phase (48-72 hours): Resume antihypertensives if BP exceeds 140/90

### 7.8 Intracranial Pressure Management

**[BOARD]**

Elevated ICP is a life-threatening complication of large ischemic strokes (particularly malignant MCA infarction) and hemorrhagic strokes. The Monro-Kellie doctrine states that the intracranial compartment has a fixed volume: brain parenchyma, blood, and cerebrospinal fluid must exist in equilibrium.

**ICP Management Fundamentals:**

| Parameter | Target | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Normal ICP | 5-15 mmHg | or 70-200 mm H2O |
| Treatment threshold | ICP >20 mmHg | Sustained elevation requires intervention |
| Cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP) | CPP >60 mmHg (some guidelines suggest >70) | CPP = MAP minus ICP |
| Head position | HOB elevated to 30 degrees | Promotes venous drainage from cranium |

**Stepwise ICP Management:**

| Step | Intervention | Mechanism | Notes |
|------|-------------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | HOB elevation to 30 degrees | Promotes cerebral venous drainage | First-line; universally applied |
| 2 | Head midline positioning | Avoids jugular venous compression | Avoid neck flexion and rotation |
| 3 | Adequate sedation and analgesia | Reduces metabolic demand and agitation | Pain and agitation increase ICP |
| 4 | Osmotic therapy: mannitol | Creates osmotic gradient pulling water from brain tissue | 0.25-1 g/kg IV bolus; monitor serum osmolality (hold if >320 mOsm/L) |
| 5 | Osmotic therapy: hypertonic saline | Same osmotic principle; may be preferred in hyponatremic or hypovolemic patients | 23.4% saline (30 mL IV bolus) or 3% saline infusion |
| 6 | Hyperventilation (temporary) | Hypocapnia causes cerebral vasoconstriction, reducing CBV | Target PaCO2 30-35 mmHg; TEMPORARY bridge only (rebounds with prolonged use) |
| 7 | CSF drainage (EVD) | Directly removes CSF to reduce ICP | External ventricular drain; allows continuous monitoring |
| 8 | Decompressive surgery | Removes bone flap to allow brain expansion | Definitive for malignant cerebral edema |
| 9 | Barbiturate coma | Reduces cerebral metabolic rate | Last resort; severe hemodynamic depression |

**Avoid These ICP-Raising Factors:**

- Valsalva maneuver (use stool softeners)
- Excessive suctioning
- Hyperthermia
- Seizures (treat aggressively)
- Hyponatremia
- Hypercapnia (ensure adequate ventilation)
- Head-down positioning

> **Board Pearl:** CPP = MAP minus ICP. Target CPP of 60 mmHg or greater. If ICP is elevated, you can improve CPP by either raising MAP (vasopressors) or lowering ICP (osmotic therapy, EVD, surgery). Mannitol is held when serum osmolality exceeds 320 mOsm/L to prevent renal injury.

**Signs of Herniation:**

| Sign | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| Ipsilateral fixed, dilated pupil (blown pupil) | CN III compression by uncal herniation |
| Contralateral hemiparesis (or bilateral) | Cerebral peduncle compression |
| Cushing triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations) | Medullary compression; late and ominous sign |
| Decerebrate or decorticate posturing | Midbrain or cortical compression |
| Loss of consciousness | Reticular activating system compression |

### 7.9 Glucose Management

**[BOARD]**

Both hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia worsen outcomes in acute stroke. Hyperglycemia promotes anaerobic glycolysis, lactic acidosis, free radical production, and blood-brain barrier disruption in ischemic tissue, leading to larger infarct volumes and higher hemorrhagic transformation rates. Hypoglycemia (below 60 mg/dL) can mimic stroke symptoms and independently causes neuronal injury.

**Glucose Targets and Management:**

| Parameter | Target/Action |
|-----------|--------------|
| Hypoglycemia (<60 mg/dL) | Correct IMMEDIATELY; can mimic stroke; give D50W |
| Target glucose range | 140-180 mg/dL |
| Persistent hyperglycemia (>180) | Insulin infusion protocol |
| Severe hyperglycemia (>400) | Treat aggressively; can itself be a stroke mimic |

**SHINE Trial (Johnston et al., JAMA 2019):**

| Feature | Intensive Group | Standard Group |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| Glucose target | 80-130 mg/dL | 80-179 mg/dL |
| Primary outcome (mRS 0-2 at 90 days) | No difference | No difference |
| Hypoglycemia events | Significantly MORE | Fewer |
| Conclusion | Intensive glucose control NOT beneficial; standard target (140-180) is appropriate |

> **Board Pearl:** The SHINE trial is the definitive evidence against intensive glucose control in acute stroke. Targeting 80-130 mg/dL did NOT improve outcomes compared to standard care (80-179 mg/dL) and caused significantly more hypoglycemia. Target 140-180 mg/dL. The only glucose value that must be checked before tPA is the finger-stick glucose to exclude hypoglycemia.

### 7.10 Temperature Management

**[BOARD]**

Fever occurs in 25-50% of stroke patients within the first 48 hours and is independently associated with worse outcomes. Each 1 degree Celsius rise in temperature increases metabolic demand, excitotoxicity, blood-brain barrier disruption, and free radical production in ischemic tissue.

**Temperature Management Protocol:**

| Temperature Status | Action |
|-------------------|--------|
| Normothermia (36.0-37.5 C) | Maintain; no intervention needed |
| Low-grade fever (37.5-38.5 C) | Acetaminophen 650-1000 mg every 4-6 hours; search for infection source |
| High fever (>38.5 C) | Aggressive antipyretics; cooling blankets/devices; broad infection workup |
| Hypothermia (<36.0 C) | Rewarm gradually; avoid active hypothermia |

**Key Evidence:**
- Fever worsens stroke outcomes by increasing metabolic demand in ischemic tissue
- Maintaining normothermia is recommended for the first several days
- **Induced hypothermia is NOT recommended** outside of clinical trials (unlike post-cardiac arrest)
- Common fever sources: aspiration pneumonia, UTI, DVT/PE, central fever (neurogenic)
- INTREPID trial (Greer et al., JAMA 2024): did not support induced hypothermia for stroke

### 7.11 Early Mobilization: The AVERT Trial

**[BOARD]**

The AVERT (A Very Early Rehabilitation Trial) was a landmark international RCT (Lancet 2015) that challenged the assumption that earlier mobilization after stroke is always better.

**AVERT Trial Results:**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Intervention | Very early mobilization (within 24 hours of stroke onset) |
| Control | Standard care (later mobilization) |
| Primary outcome | Modified Rankin Scale at 3 months |
| Result | Very early mobilization REDUCED the odds of favorable outcome (OR 0.73; 95% CI 0.59-0.90) |
| Implication | Do NOT mobilize patients within the first 24 hours of stroke |

**Current Recommendations:**
- Mobilize stable patients after 24 hours
- Gradually increase activity level
- Monitor for orthostatic hypotension and neurological worsening
- Individualize based on stroke severity, hemodynamic stability, and comorbidities
- Dedicated stroke unit care (Cochrane 2020) is associated with better outcomes regardless of timing

> **Board Pearl:** The AVERT trial is one of the most commonly tested acute stroke management facts on PM&R boards. Very early mobilization (within 24 hours) is HARMFUL. Wait at least 24 hours. The previous assumption that "earlier is always better" was disproven.

### 7.12 Head Position

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Head positioning after stroke has been debated:

- **HOB elevated to 30 degrees:** Standard practice if there is risk of elevated ICP, aspiration, or cardiopulmonary issues
- **HeadPoST trial (Anderson et al., NEJM 2017):** Lying flat vs. 30 degrees elevation showed NO difference in 90-day outcomes in general stroke patients
- **ZODIAC trial:** 0 degrees vs. 30 degrees before thrombectomy was stopped early; preliminary results suggested 0 degrees might be better, but confidence was limited

**Practical Approach:**
- Flat positioning may improve cerebral perfusion in ischemic stroke patients
- Elevate HOB to 30 degrees when ICP is a concern, aspiration risk is high, or respiratory status requires it
- After thrombectomy or tPA, maintain HOB at 30 degrees per institutional protocol

### 7.13 Hemorrhagic Transformation

**[BOARD]**

Hemorrhagic transformation (HT) is the conversion of an ischemic infarct into a hemorrhagic lesion. It occurs spontaneously in 5-40% of ischemic strokes and at higher rates following reperfusion therapy. The risk is proportional to the size of the infarct, the degree of hypertension, and the use of antithrombotic agents.

**ECASS Classification of Hemorrhagic Transformation:**

| Type | Description | Clinical Significance |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| HI-1 (Hemorrhagic Infarction Type 1) | Small petechiae along margins of infarct | Generally benign; does not alter management |
| HI-2 (Hemorrhagic Infarction Type 2) | More confluent petechiae within infarct, without mass effect | Usually benign; monitor closely |
| PH-1 (Parenchymal Hematoma Type 1) | Hematoma occupying less than 30% of infarct area, with mild mass effect | Clinically significant; may require intervention |
| PH-2 (Parenchymal Hematoma Type 2) | Dense hematoma occupying more than 30% of infarct area, with significant mass effect | MOST clinically significant; often fatal; associated with clinical deterioration |

> **Board Pearl:** The ECASS classification distinguishes between hemorrhagic infarction (HI-1, HI-2) and parenchymal hematoma (PH-1, PH-2). Only PH-2 (dense hematoma >30% of infarct with mass effect) is consistently associated with clinical deterioration and mortality. HI-1 and HI-2 are generally benign and may not require changes in management.

**Risk Factors for Hemorrhagic Transformation:**

- Large infarct size (low ASPECTS)
- Reperfusion therapy (tPA, thrombectomy)
- Anticoagulant use
- Severe hypertension
- Hyperglycemia
- Prolonged ischemic time before reperfusion
- Advanced age
- Significant early ischemic changes on CT

**Management of Post-tPA Hemorrhage:**

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Stop tPA infusion immediately if still running |
| 2 | Emergent non-contrast CT |
| 3 | STAT labs: fibrinogen, PT/INR, aPTT, CBC, type and screen |
| 4 | Cryoprecipitate (10 units IV) if fibrinogen <200 mg/dL |
| 5 | Tranexamic acid 1g IV over 10 minutes (or aminocaproic acid 4-5g IV) |
| 6 | Platelet transfusion if platelet count <100,000 |
| 7 | Neurosurgical consultation for possible evacuation |
| 8 | Aggressive BP control |

### 7.14 Malignant MCA Infarction and Decompressive Hemicraniectomy

**[BOARD]**

Malignant MCA syndrome refers to massive hemispheric infarction involving the entire MCA territory (sometimes with additional ACA or PCA involvement) that leads to life-threatening cerebral edema, typically peaking at 2-5 days post-stroke. Without intervention, mortality approaches 80%.

**Clinical Features of Malignant MCA Syndrome:**

- Complete MCA territory infarction (ASPECTS 0-2)
- Progressive cerebral edema with midline shift
- Declining level of consciousness
- Signs of uncal herniation (ipsilateral pupil dilation, contralateral hemiparesis)
- Typically younger patients (under 60) due to less brain atrophy (less room for swelling)

**Decompressive Hemicraniectomy (DHC) Trials:**

| Trial | Year | Results |
|-------|------|---------|
| DESTINY (Germany) | 2007 | Reduced mortality; improved survival |
| DECIMAL (France) | 2007 | Reduced mortality; improved functional outcomes |
| HAMLET (Netherlands) | 2009 | Reduced mortality; benefit concentrated in surgery within 48 hours |
| Pooled Analysis (Vahedi et al.) | 2007 | Surgery reduced mortality from 78% to 29%; mRS 0-3 increased from 21% to 43% |
| DESTINY II | 2014 | Evaluated patients over 60: reduced mortality (33% vs 70%) but high disability in survivors |

**Key Surgical Criteria:**

| Criterion | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Age | Greatest benefit in patients under 60; controversial over 60 (DESTINY II) |
| Timing | Within 48 hours of symptom onset (HAMLET showed no benefit after 48 hours) |
| Infarct size | Greater than 50% of MCA territory; DWI volume >145 mL |
| Clinical status | Declining LOC despite maximal medical therapy |
| NIHSS | Typically greater than 15 |
| Informed consent | Critical discussion with family regarding quality of life outcomes |

> **Board Pearl:** Decompressive hemicraniectomy for malignant MCA infarction dramatically reduces mortality (from approximately 80% to approximately 30%) but the majority of survivors have significant disability (mRS 3-4). The benefit is clearest in patients under 60 when surgery is performed within 48 hours. In patients over 60 (DESTINY II), mortality is reduced but most survivors have severe disability (mRS 4-5), raising ethical considerations about quality of life.

### 7.15 DVT Prophylaxis

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

All stroke patients with restricted mobility require venous thromboembolism (VTE) prophylaxis. The approach depends on stroke type and timing relative to reperfusion therapy.

| Phase | Recommendation |
|-------|---------------|
| Immediate (all patients) | Intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) devices |
| After tPA (24+ hours) | Pharmacologic prophylaxis (LMWH or unfractionated heparin) |
| Ischemic stroke, no tPA | Pharmacologic prophylaxis within 24-48 hours |
| Hemorrhagic stroke | IPC initially; pharmacologic prophylaxis when hematoma is stable (typically 24-48 hours) |

**CLOTS Trials:**
- CLOTS 1: Graduated compression stockings did NOT reduce DVT risk
- CLOTS 3: IPC devices DID reduce DVT risk (OR 0.65)

> **Board Pearl:** Graduated compression stockings (TED hose) are NOT recommended for DVT prophylaxis after stroke (CLOTS 1 trial). Intermittent pneumatic compression devices ARE effective (CLOTS 3). All immobilized stroke patients should have IPC devices applied immediately.

### 7.16 Stroke Unit Care

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Admission to a dedicated stroke unit is independently associated with improved outcomes compared to general medical or neurological wards, regardless of patient age, sex, or stroke severity.

Benefits of stroke unit care (Cochrane 2020 meta-analysis):
- Reduced mortality (OR 0.81)
- Reduced death or institutionalization (OR 0.78)
- Reduced death or dependency (OR 0.79)

The benefit of stroke unit care is attributed to:
- Standardized protocols for acute management
- Continuous monitoring with early detection of complications
- Coordinated multidisciplinary team care (neurology, nursing, PT, OT, SLP, social work)
- Early rehabilitation planning
- Protocolized secondary prevention initiation

### 7.17 Delirium After Stroke

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Post-stroke delirium occurs in approximately 25% of stroke patients. It is associated with worse functional outcomes, longer hospital stays, and higher mortality.

**Risk Factors:**
- Pre-existing cognitive impairment or dementia
- Infection (UTI, pneumonia)
- Greater stroke severity (higher NIHSS)
- Metabolic derangements
- Medication effects (anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids)
- Posterior circulation strokes

**Management:**
- Treat underlying cause (infection, metabolic abnormalities)
- Non-pharmacological strategies (reorientation, sleep hygiene, minimize tethers)
- Low-dose antipsychotics (haloperidol, quetiapine) for severe agitation
- Avoid benzodiazepines (worsen delirium except in alcohol withdrawal)

## Summary: Acute Stroke Management Algorithm

| Time Point | Key Actions |
|-----------|------------|
| 0-10 minutes | ABCs, glucose check, NIHSS, code stroke activation |
| 10-25 minutes | Non-contrast CT, CTA if LVO suspected |
| 25-45 minutes | CT interpretation; tPA eligibility determination |
| 0-60 minutes (door-to-needle) | IV alteplase or tenecteplase if eligible |
| 0-90 minutes (door-to-groin) | Thrombectomy if LVO confirmed |
| 0-24 hours | BP management, glucose 140-180, normothermia, NPO, IPC devices |
| 24 hours | Start aspirin (if no tPA) or aspirin 24h after tPA; begin mobilization |
| 24-48 hours | Swallowing evaluation; pharmacologic DVT prophylaxis; statin therapy |
| 2-5 days | Peak edema period; monitor for malignant cerebral edema |

## Board-Style Review Questions

**Question 1:** A 72-year-old woman presents 2 hours after acute onset of left hemiparesis and neglect. NIHSS is 14. Non-contrast CT shows no hemorrhage. BP is 195/108. What is the most appropriate FIRST step regarding blood pressure?

*Answer:* Administer IV labetalol (10-20 mg) or start nicardipine infusion (5 mg/hr) to bring BP below 185/110 before administering tPA. The BP must be below 185/110 before tPA can be given. Once achieved, proceed with alteplase (or tenecteplase).

**Question 2:** A patient receives alteplase at 3.5 hours after stroke onset. Six hours later, the nurse reports acute headache, vomiting, and worsening neurological examination. What is the most appropriate immediate action?

*Answer:* Stop the alteplase infusion (if still running, though it should be completed by now). Obtain emergent non-contrast CT to evaluate for hemorrhagic transformation. Draw STAT labs including fibrinogen, PT/INR, aPTT, and CBC. If CT confirms hemorrhage, administer cryoprecipitate (10 units) if fibrinogen is below 200, and consider tranexamic acid.

**Question 3:** A 55-year-old man presents 14 hours after symptom onset with right MCA syndrome (NIHSS 18). CT perfusion shows a core volume of 25 mL and penumbra of 120 mL. CTA confirms M1 occlusion. Is he a candidate for intervention?

*Answer:* Yes. He meets DAWN trial criteria (age under 80, NIHSS of 10 or greater, core less than 31 mL at 6-24 hours) AND DEFUSE-3 criteria (mismatch ratio 4.8:1, exceeding the 1.8 threshold, with core less than 70 mL at 6-16 hours). He should undergo mechanical thrombectomy.
