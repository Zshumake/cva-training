## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 9.1 Twitchell's Description of Recovery Stages

**[BOARD]**

Thomas E. Twitchell published his seminal observations on motor recovery after hemiplegia in 1951, describing a stereotyped sequence of motor recovery that occurs after upper motor neuron (UMN) lesions. His observations form the foundation for all subsequent motor recovery frameworks.

**Twitchell's Sequence:**

1. **Flaccidity** (immediate post-stroke): Complete loss of voluntary motor function with absent deep tendon reflexes. This represents loss of upper motor neuron facilitation to lower motor neurons ("spinal shock" equivalent for cerebral lesions).

2. **Return of deep tendon reflexes**: Reflexes gradually return and become hyperactive as the spinal cord circuits are released from inhibitory cortical modulation.

3. **Emergence of spasticity**: Velocity-dependent increase in muscle tone develops in a characteristic pattern (UE flexor, LE extensor). This represents unmasking of tonic stretch reflexes.

4. **Synergistic movement patterns**: Voluntary movement returns but is confined to mass movement patterns (flexion or extension synergies). Individual joint movements are not possible.

5. **Volitional movement outside synergies**: As cortical control improves, isolated voluntary movements emerge that break free from synergy patterns. Spasticity begins to diminish.

6. **Normal or near-normal function**: Fine motor control and coordination approach normal. Spasticity resolves or becomes minimal. Speed and precision improve.

Twitchell observed that not all patients progress through all stages. Many plateau at intermediate stages, and the degree of ultimate recovery correlates with the severity of the initial deficit and the integrity of the corticospinal tract.

### 9.2 Brunnstrom 7 Stages of Motor Recovery

**[BOARD]**

Signe Brunnstrom (1966) formalized Twitchell's observations into a 7-stage classification system that remains the most widely used framework for describing motor recovery after stroke. Unlike previous approaches that viewed spasticity and synergies as pathological, Brunnstrom recognized them as necessary stages in the recovery process and incorporated them into treatment.

**Brunnstrom Stages:**

| Stage | Name | Motor Characteristics | Clinical Presentation |
|-------|------|----------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Flaccidity | No voluntary movement; absent reflexes | Limb is limp; no resistance to passive movement; no active muscle contraction |
| 2 | Spasticity appearing; minimal voluntary movement | Spasticity develops; basic synergies may appear as associated reactions | Subtle movement with effort; reflexes hyperactive; associated reactions (movement in affected limb when straining with unaffected side) |
| 3 | Spasticity peaks; synergy-dependent voluntary movement | Voluntary movement possible ONLY within synergy patterns; spasticity reaches maximum | Patient can initiate flexor or extensor synergy patterns but cannot isolate individual joint movements |
| 4 | Spasticity decreasing; some out-of-synergy movement | Movement combinations that deviate from synergy patterns begin to emerge | Can begin to move joints independently; spasticity less prominent; movement still limited |
| 5 | Spasticity wanes; isolated voluntary movements | Near-normal isolated movements independent of synergies; spasticity minimal | Can reach forward, extend elbow while arm is elevated; isolated finger movements emerging |
| 6 | Coordination approaches normal; spasticity minimal | Individual joint movements with near-normal speed and coordination; minimal or no spasticity | Fine motor tasks possible; near-normal strength and dexterity; speed may still be slightly reduced |
| 7 | Normal function | Full voluntary motor control with normal speed, coordination, and strength | Indistinguishable from pre-stroke function |

**Upper Extremity Flexor Synergy Pattern (Stages 2-3):**

| Joint | Movement |
|-------|----------|
| Scapula | Retraction and elevation |
| Shoulder | Abduction and external rotation |
| Elbow | Flexion (strongest component) |
| Forearm | Supination |
| Wrist | Flexion |
| Fingers | Flexion (mass grasp) |

**Lower Extremity Extensor Synergy Pattern (Stages 2-3):**

| Joint | Movement |
|-------|----------|
| Hip | Extension, adduction, and internal rotation |
| Knee | Extension |
| Ankle | Plantarflexion and inversion |
| Toes | Flexion (curling) |

**Key Out-of-Synergy Movements (Stage 4 milestones):**

| Upper Extremity | Lower Extremity |
|----------------|----------------|
| Hand behind back (shoulder extension with internal rotation) | Knee flexion in sitting (hip flexion with knee flexion) |
| Shoulder flexion to 90 degrees with elbow extended | Ankle dorsiflexion with knee extended |
| Forearm pronation/supination with elbow at 90 degrees | Hip abduction in sitting |

> **Board Pearl:** The Brunnstrom stages are one of the most frequently tested topics on PM&R boards. Key points: (1) Stages 2-3 are dominated by synergy patterns; (2) Stage 3 has peak spasticity; (3) Stage 4 is the transition point where out-of-synergy movement begins; (4) UE follows a flexor synergy pattern; (5) LE follows an extensor synergy pattern. Not all patients reach Stage 7. Many plateau at Stages 3-4.

### 9.3 Copenhagen Stroke Study

**[BOARD]**

The Copenhagen Stroke Study (Jorgensen et al., 1995) was a landmark prospective study of 1,197 consecutive stroke patients admitted to a stroke unit that defined the natural history of neurological and functional recovery.

**Key Findings:**

| Recovery Metric | Result |
|----------------|--------|
| Neurologic recovery plateau (95% of patients) | 11 weeks from onset |
| Functional recovery plateau | 2-4 weeks AFTER neurologic plateau (due to compensatory strategies) |
| Mild strokes (initial NIHSS <5) | Best neurologic recovery by 3-5 weeks |
| Moderate strokes | Best neurologic recovery by 6-11 weeks |
| Severe strokes | Best neurologic recovery by 13-17 weeks |
| Independent walking achieved | 80% of patients; most within 6 weeks |
| Useful hand function achieved | Only 33% of patients |
| Strongest predictor of outcome | Initial stroke severity (NIHSS) |

**Clinical Implications:**

- Most neurologic recovery occurs in the first 3 months; the first 3-6 months is the critical window for intensive rehabilitation
- Functional recovery continues beyond the neurologic plateau because patients learn compensatory strategies
- Severe strokes take longer to reach maximum recovery but still have a definable plateau
- Upper extremity recovery is significantly less likely than lower extremity recovery (33% achieve useful hand function vs. 80% achieve independent ambulation)

> **Board Pearl:** "95% of patients reach their best neurologic recovery level by 11 weeks" is one of the most cited statistics in stroke rehabilitation and is highly testable. Note the distinction between neurologic recovery (actual neural function improvement) and functional recovery (ADL performance, which includes compensatory strategies and continues longer).

### 9.4 Proportional Recovery Rule

**[BOARD]**

The proportional recovery rule (Prabhakaran et al., 2008) states that most stroke patients recover approximately 70% of their potential upper extremity motor recovery within the first 3-6 months.

**Definition:**
- Potential recovery = Maximum possible score minus initial score
- Expected recovery = 0.7 times (potential recovery)
- Measured by Fugl-Meyer Upper Extremity (FM-UE) score

**Example Calculation:**
- Initial FM-UE score: 20/66
- Deficit (potential recovery): 66 - 20 = 46 points
- Expected recovery: 46 x 0.7 = 32 points
- Predicted FM-UE at 3 months: 20 + 32 = 52/66

**Two Patient Populations:**

| Group | Proportion | Recovery Pattern | Characteristics |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| "Fitters" | Approximately 70% of patients | Follow the proportional recovery rule closely | Mild-moderate initial deficits; intact CST; detectable MEPs on TMS |
| "Non-fitters" | Approximately 30% of patients | Recover FAR LESS than predicted | Severe initial deficits; significant CST damage (disrupted PLIC on DWI); absent MEPs |

**Predictors of "Non-Fitter" Status:**
- Severe initial motor deficit (FM-UE less than 12/66)
- Disrupted posterior limb of internal capsule (PLIC) on DWI
- Absent motor evoked potentials (MEPs) on transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) at 1-2 weeks
- SAFE score of 0 at 72 hours

> **Board Pearl:** The proportional recovery rule applies primarily to upper extremity motor recovery measured by Fugl-Meyer. "Fitters" (70% of patients) recover approximately 70% of their maximum possible improvement. "Non-fitters" (30%) have severe CST damage and recover far less. The PLIC on DWI is the key imaging biomarker: if it is disrupted, the patient is likely a non-fitter with poor UE recovery.

### 9.5 Recovery Predictors

**[BOARD]**

**SAFE Score (Shoulder Abduction + Finger Extension):**

| Component | How to Test | Scoring |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Shoulder abduction | Voluntary shoulder abduction against gravity | MRC scale 0-5 |
| Finger extension | Voluntary finger extension (any finger) | MRC scale 0-5 |
| Total SAFE score | Sum of both components | 0-10 |

| SAFE Score | Prognosis |
|-----------|-----------|
| 5 or greater at 72 hours | 98% probability of achieving some useful UE function |
| 0 at 72 hours | Poor prognosis for UE recovery |
| 1-4 at 72 hours | Uncertain; further assessment with TMS and MRI recommended |

**PREP2 Algorithm (Predicting REcovery Potential):**

The PREP2 algorithm combines clinical examination, neurophysiology, and neuroimaging in a stepwise fashion:

| Step | Assessment | Timing | Result |
|------|-----------|--------|--------|
| 1 | SAFE score | Day 3 | If SAFE of 5 or greater: predict "excellent" outcome (skip remaining steps) |
| 2 | TMS (motor evoked potentials) | Day 5-14 | If MEPs present: predict "good" outcome |
| 3 | MRI (PLIC integrity on DWI) | Acute phase | If PLIC intact: predict "limited" outcome; if disrupted: predict "poor" outcome |

**Additional Clinical Predictors:**

| Predictor | Timepoint | Interpretation |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| Hand movement by 4 weeks | 4 weeks | Present: approximately 70% chance of useful hand function |
| Absent hand movement by 4 weeks | 4 weeks | Poor prognosis for UE recovery |
| Sitting balance for 30 seconds + leg muscle contraction | 72 hours | 98% probability of independent ambulation |
| Each additional NIHSS point | Admission | Decreases odds of excellent outcome (mRS 0-1) by 17% |

**Corticospinal Tract Integrity:**

| Imaging Marker | Method | Prognostic Value |
|----------------|--------|-----------------|
| PLIC on DWI | DWI signal in posterior limb of internal capsule | Bright signal (restricted diffusion) in PLIC predicts poor motor outcome |
| Fractional anisotropy (FA) ratio | DTI comparing affected vs. unaffected PLIC | FA ratio <0.5 predicts poor UE motor recovery |
| CST volume on DTI | Diffusion tensor tractography of corticospinal tract | Reduced CST volume correlates with worse motor outcomes |

> **Board Pearl:** Finger extension and shoulder abduction at day 2-3 post-stroke are the two most powerful bedside predictors of UE motor recovery. Together they form the SAFE score. A SAFE score of 5 or greater at 72 hours predicts 98% probability of achieving some dexterity at 6 months. This is the single most important clinical predictor to know for board examinations.

### 9.6 Fugl-Meyer Assessment

**[BOARD]**

The Fugl-Meyer Assessment (FMA) is the gold standard measure of motor impairment after stroke. It evaluates motor function based on the Brunnstrom recovery stages, testing the ability to perform movements within and outside of synergy patterns.

**FMA Scoring:**

| Domain | Maximum Score | Components |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Upper Extremity Motor | 66 | Shoulder, elbow, forearm, wrist, hand, coordination/speed |
| Lower Extremity Motor | 34 | Hip, knee, ankle, coordination/speed |
| Total Motor | 100 | Combined UE + LE |
| Sensation | 24 | Light touch, proprioception |
| Balance | 14 | Sitting, standing |
| Joint ROM | 44 | Passive ROM of all joints |
| Joint Pain | 44 | Pain with passive ROM |
| Total FMA | 226 | All domains combined |

**FMA-UE Severity Classification:**

| FM-UE Score | Motor Impairment Level |
|-------------|----------------------|
| 0-18 | Severe |
| 19-37 | Moderate-severe |
| 38-53 | Moderate |
| 54-66 | Mild |

**FMA Item Scoring:**
- Each item scored 0, 1, or 2
- 0 = cannot perform
- 1 = performs partially
- 2 = performs fully

**Clinical Utility:**
- Tracks motor recovery progression through Brunnstrom stages
- Items are hierarchically organized (within synergy to out-of-synergy to isolated movements)
- Minimum clinically important difference (MCID): approximately 5-10 points for FM-UE
- Administration time: 30-45 minutes for full assessment

### 9.7 Rehabilitation Approaches

**[BOARD]**

Multiple rehabilitation approaches have been developed for stroke motor recovery. Each emphasizes different theoretical frameworks, though modern practice typically integrates elements from multiple approaches.

**Comparison of Major Rehabilitation Approaches:**

| Approach | Founder/Origin | Key Principles | Treatment Focus | Board-Relevant Points |
|----------|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Bobath / NDT (Neurodevelopmental Treatment) | Berta and Karel Bobath, 1940s | Inhibit abnormal tone and movement patterns; facilitate normal movement | Inhibition of spasticity; facilitation of normal postural reactions; key points of control | Most widely used worldwide; emphasis on normalizing tone before functional movement |
| Brunnstrom | Signe Brunnstrom, 1960s | Use synergies as building blocks; progress from synergy to isolated movement | Encourage synergistic movement early; progressive voluntary control | Views synergies as normal recovery stages, not pathology; uses associated reactions therapeutically |
| PNF (Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation) | Kabat, Knott, Voss, 1950s | Diagonal and spiral movement patterns that mimic functional activities; sensory input facilitates motor output | D1 and D2 diagonal patterns (flexion/extension); verbal cues; manual resistance; rhythmic stabilization | Diagonal patterns (D1, D2) are high-yield for boards; uses stretch, resistance, and irradiation to facilitate movement |
| Rood | Margaret Rood, 1940s-50s | Sensory stimulation activates motor responses; progression through ontogenetic motor development | Facilitation techniques: icing, brushing, tapping, vibration, quick stretch; inhibition: prolonged stretch, neutral warmth | Uses cutaneous sensory input to modulate tone; sensory-based approach; follows developmental sequence |
| Motor Relearning Programme (MRP) | Carr and Shepherd, 1980s | Task-specific practice; motor learning theory; active problem-solving | Practice of functional tasks in context; repetition; feedback; environmental modification | Most aligned with modern neuroplasticity evidence; task-specific training over impairment-focused treatment |

> **Board Pearl:** PNF diagonal patterns are frequently tested. D1 Flexion (UE): shoulder flexion/adduction/external rotation, elbow extension, forearm supination, wrist radial deviation. D2 Flexion (UE): shoulder flexion/abduction/external rotation, elbow extension, forearm supination. D1 and D2 each have flexion and extension components. Think of functional movements like reaching across the body (D1) or throwing a ball overhead (D2).

**PNF Diagonal Patterns (Upper Extremity):**

| Pattern | Shoulder | Elbow | Forearm | Wrist |
|---------|----------|-------|---------|-------|
| D1 Flexion | Flexion, adduction, external rotation | Extension (or flexion) | Supination | Radial deviation, extension |
| D1 Extension | Extension, abduction, internal rotation | Extension (or flexion) | Pronation | Ulnar deviation, flexion |
| D2 Flexion | Flexion, abduction, external rotation | Extension (or flexion) | Supination | Radial deviation, extension |
| D2 Extension | Extension, adduction, internal rotation | Extension (or flexion) | Pronation | Ulnar deviation, flexion |

### 9.8 Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy (CIMT)

**[BOARD]**

CIMT is one of the most evidence-based rehabilitation interventions for upper extremity motor recovery after stroke. It addresses learned nonuse by constraining the unaffected hand while intensively training the affected hand.

**CIMT Components:**
1. **Constraint:** Mitt or sling on the unaffected hand for 90% of waking hours
2. **Intensive practice:** 6 hours/day of structured task practice with the affected hand (shaping, task practice)
3. **Transfer package:** Behavioral techniques to promote use of the affected hand in daily life
4. **Duration:** Typically 2 weeks (10 consecutive weekdays)

**Modified CIMT (mCIMT):**
- Reduced intensity: 2-3 hours/day of structured practice
- Constraint for 5-6 hours/day
- Longer duration (3-4 weeks)
- More practical for most clinical settings

**EXCITE Trial (Wolf et al., JAMA 2006):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Design | Prospective, single-blind, randomized, multisite (7 US sites) |
| Population | First stroke within 3-9 months; some residual UE movement |
| Motor requirements | At least 10 degrees active wrist extension + 10 degrees active finger extension (MCP/IP) |
| Intervention | 2 weeks of CIMT vs. usual care |
| Primary outcome | Wolf Motor Function Test (WMFT): significant improvement in both performance time and functional ability |
| Duration of benefit | Improvements persisted for at least 1 year |
| Key findings | CIMT produced statistically significant AND clinically meaningful improvements in arm motor function |
| Delayed treatment group | Also improved when CIMT was provided at 12 months, suggesting benefit in chronic stroke |

**CIMT Eligibility Criteria:**

| Requirement | Minimum Motor Function |
|------------|----------------------|
| Wrist extension | At least 10 degrees active extension |
| Finger extension | At least 10 degrees active MCP and IP extension |
| No excessive spasticity | Must allow positioning for tasks |
| Cognitive ability | Must understand instructions and participate |
| Balance | Must be safe with one hand constrained |
| Adequate endurance | Must tolerate intensive practice sessions |

> **Board Pearl:** CIMT requires a minimum of 10 degrees of active wrist extension and 10 degrees of active finger extension. This criterion means that only patients with some preserved voluntary hand movement are eligible (approximately Brunnstrom Stage 4 or higher for the hand). The EXCITE trial is the largest RCT supporting CIMT and demonstrated benefits that persisted for at least 1 year.

### 9.9 Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES)

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

FES applies electrical stimulation to paralyzed muscles to produce functional movement. In stroke rehabilitation, it is used for both therapeutic benefit (neuroplasticity) and as an orthotic substitution.

**FES Applications in Stroke:**

| Application | Target Muscles | Purpose | Evidence |
|------------|---------------|---------|----------|
| Foot drop correction | Tibialis anterior, peroneal nerve | Ankle dorsiflexion during swing phase | Level I evidence supporting improved gait speed and symmetry |
| Wrist/finger extension | Extensor digitorum communis, extensor carpi radialis | Hand opening for grasping tasks | Moderate evidence for motor recovery when combined with task practice |
| Shoulder subluxation | Supraspinatus, posterior deltoid | Reduce subluxation; may reduce pain | FES effective for reducing subluxation but does not consistently reduce pain long-term |
| FES cycling | Quadriceps, hamstrings, gluteals | Lower extremity strengthening and cardiovascular fitness | Improves strength and aerobic capacity |

**FES Parameters:**
- Frequency: 20-50 Hz (most commonly 30-35 Hz)
- Pulse width: 200-350 microseconds
- Amplitude: Sufficient to produce visible contraction
- On/off time: Varies by application (typically 5-10 seconds on, 5-10 seconds off)

### 9.10 Robotic Therapy

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Robotic rehabilitation devices provide high-intensity, repetitive, task-specific practice with quantifiable measures of performance. They can deliver hundreds to thousands of movement repetitions per session, far exceeding what is achievable with conventional therapy alone.

**Key Robotic Therapy Trials:**

| Trial/Device | Target | Key Finding |
|-------------|--------|-------------|
| MIT-Manus (InMotion ARM) | Proximal UE (shoulder, elbow) | Improved UE motor function; comparable to intensive conventional therapy |
| VA ROBOTICS trial (Lo et al., NEJM 2010) | UE | Robot-assisted therapy comparable to intensive comparison therapy; both superior to usual care |
| RATULS trial (Rodgers et al., Lancet 2019) | UE | Robot-assisted training did NOT improve UE function more than usual care at 3 months |
| Lokomat (gait robot) | Gait training | No clear superiority over matched-intensity overground training |

**Current Evidence Summary:**
- Robotic therapy provides a means to deliver high-dose, high-repetition practice
- Effects are generally comparable to dose-matched conventional therapy
- Main advantage: ability to provide intensive practice when therapist availability is limited
- NOT clearly superior to conventional therapy when therapy dose is matched

### 9.11 Body-Weight Supported Treadmill Training (BWSTT)

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

BWSTT uses a harness system to partially unweight the patient over a treadmill, allowing repetitive stepping practice in patients who cannot fully bear weight.

**LEAPS Trial (Duncan et al., NEJM 2011):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Comparison | BWSTT at 2 months vs. BWSTT at 6 months vs. home exercise |
| Primary outcome | Walking ability at 1 year |
| Result | No significant difference between groups; all improved similarly |
| Conclusion | BWSTT is NOT superior to a progressive, structured home exercise program for improving walking after stroke |

> **Board Pearl:** The LEAPS trial showed that BWSTT was NOT superior to a structured home exercise program for improving walking after stroke. This challenged the assumption that technology-intensive locomotor training produces better outcomes than well-designed conventional programs. The key insight is that INTENSITY and SPECIFICITY of practice matter more than the specific modality used.

### 9.12 Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS)

**[BOARD]**

Vagus nerve stimulation paired with upper extremity rehabilitation is an emerging neuromodulation strategy for chronic stroke motor recovery.

**VNS-REHAB Trial (Dawson et al., Lancet 2021):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Design | Multicenter, randomized, triple-blinded, sham-controlled |
| Population | Chronic stroke (9 months to 10 years post-stroke) with moderate-severe UE deficits |
| Intervention | Surgically implanted VNS device; stimulation paired with UE rehabilitation exercises |
| Control | Sham stimulation (implanted device, no stimulation) + identical rehabilitation |
| Primary outcome | Change in FMA-UE score at 90 days |
| Result | VNS group: clinically meaningful FMA-UE improvement in 47% vs. 24% in sham group |
| Mechanism | VNS releases norepinephrine and acetylcholine, enhancing synaptic plasticity during motor practice |
| FDA status | FDA approved for chronic ischemic stroke UE rehabilitation (Vivistim system) |

> **Board Pearl:** VNS-REHAB is a landmark trial demonstrating that vagus nerve stimulation paired with rehabilitation improves UE motor recovery in chronic stroke. The mechanism involves release of neuromodulators (norepinephrine, acetylcholine) that enhance synaptic plasticity during concurrent motor practice. This is an example of a "priming" approach that augments the brain's ability to learn during rehabilitation.

### 9.13 Neuroplasticity Principles

**[BOARD]**

Neuroplasticity refers to the brain's ability to reorganize neural pathways, form new synaptic connections, and adapt in response to experience, learning, or injury. Understanding neuroplasticity principles is essential for designing effective rehabilitation programs.

**Key Neuroplasticity Principles (Kleim and Jones, 2008):**

| Principle | Definition | Rehabilitation Application |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------------|
| Use it or lose it | Neural circuits not actively engaged in task performance degrade | Immobilized or neglected limbs lose cortical representation; emphasize active use |
| Use it and improve it | Training that drives specific brain function leads to enhanced capability | Intensive, repetitive practice of functional tasks improves performance |
| Specificity | The nature of the training experience dictates the nature of the plasticity | Practice must be task-specific; grip training improves grip, not reaching |
| Repetition matters | Sufficient repetitions are needed to induce lasting neural changes | High-dose practice (hundreds of repetitions per session) drives neuroplastic change |
| Intensity matters | Greater training intensity induces greater plasticity | Higher-intensity programs produce larger gains; dose-response relationship |
| Time matters | Different forms of plasticity occur during different time windows | Early after stroke: perilesional reorganization; later: contralesional and ipsilesional adaptation |
| Salience matters | Training must be meaningful and engaging to drive plasticity | Motivating, goal-directed tasks enhance learning; boring exercises are less effective |
| Age matters | Plasticity occurs more readily in younger brains but is present throughout life | Older patients still benefit from neuroplasticity-based rehabilitation, though gains may be smaller |
| Transference | Plasticity from one training experience can enhance acquisition of similar behaviors | Practice on related tasks transfers to untrained tasks (but specificity limits broad transfer) |
| Interference | Plasticity in one neural circuit can interfere with another | Maladaptive plasticity (e.g., learned nonuse, synergistic movement patterns) can limit recovery |

**Critical Periods and Sensitive Periods:**

| Period | Timing | Characteristics |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Hyperacute plasticity window | First 1-3 months | Perilesional cortex most receptive to reorganization; highest potential for recovery |
| Subacute window | 3-6 months | Continued recovery, slower rate; training remains highly effective |
| Chronic phase | Beyond 6 months | Recovery still possible but requires higher-intensity interventions; neuroplasticity is not absent but is harder to induce |

### 9.14 SSRIs for Motor Recovery

**[BOARD]**

The role of SSRIs in motor recovery after stroke has been extensively studied with conflicting results.

**FLAME Trial (Chollet et al., Lancet Neurol 2011):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Drug | Fluoxetine 20 mg daily for 3 months |
| Population | Acute ischemic stroke with moderate-severe motor deficit |
| Sample size | 118 patients |
| Primary outcome | FM motor score change at 3 months |
| Result | POSITIVE: fluoxetine group showed greater motor recovery (34 vs 24 points FM improvement) |
| Interpretation | Generated enthusiasm for SSRIs as motor recovery enhancers |

**Large Confirmatory Trials (All NEGATIVE):**

| Trial | Year | N | Population | Primary Result |
|-------|------|---|-----------|---------------|
| FOCUS (UK) | 2019 | 3,127 | Acute stroke (2-15 days) | NO benefit of fluoxetine 20 mg on mRS at 6 months |
| AFFINITY (Australia) | 2020 | 1,280 | Acute stroke (2-15 days) | NO benefit of fluoxetine 20 mg on mRS at 6 months |
| EFFECTS (Sweden) | 2020 | 1,500 | Acute stroke (2-15 days) | NO benefit of fluoxetine 20 mg on mRS at 6 months |
| Combined total | | >5,900 | | Overwhelmingly negative for motor recovery |

**Harms Identified in Large Trials:**
- Increased risk of bone fractures
- Increased risk of falls
- Increased risk of seizures
- Increased risk of hyponatremia
- Possible increased risk of bleeding

> **Board Pearl:** The FLAME trial (small, positive) suggested fluoxetine enhances motor recovery, but three large confirmatory trials (FOCUS, AFFINITY, EFFECTS) involving over 5,900 patients found NO benefit of routine fluoxetine for motor recovery after stroke. Furthermore, fluoxetine increased adverse events (fractures, falls, seizures, hyponatremia). Routine SSRI use for motor recovery is NOT recommended. SSRIs remain appropriate for treatment of post-stroke depression.

### 9.15 AFO Prescribing and Hemiplegic Gait Deviations

**[BOARD]**

Hemiplegic gait is characterized by the lower extremity extensor synergy pattern during stance and inadequate flexion during swing. Understanding common gait deviations is essential for prescribing appropriate orthotic intervention.

**Common Hemiplegic Gait Deviations:**

| Deviation | Mechanism | Phase Affected | Orthotic/Intervention |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Foot drop (equinus) | Weak tibialis anterior; spastic plantarflexors | Swing phase | AFO (posterior leaf spring or hinged); FES to peroneal nerve |
| Equinovarus | Spastic tibialis posterior + gastrocsoleus; weak peroneals | Swing and stance | Rigid AFO; botox to tibialis posterior/gastrocsoleus; hinged AFO with varus correction |
| Circumduction | Inability to flex hip/knee adequately in swing; compensatory lateral swing of limb | Swing phase | Address underlying spasticity; AFO to correct foot position; knee orthosis if needed |
| Hip hiking | Compensating for inadequate swing-phase limb shortening (weak hip/knee flexors or locked knee) | Swing phase | Treat spastic quadriceps (botox to rectus femoris); AFO to improve clearance |
| Knee hyperextension (genu recurvatum) | Weak quadriceps OR spastic plantarflexors pushing knee into hyperextension; compensation for quadriceps weakness | Stance phase | AFO set in slight dorsiflexion (5 degrees) blocks plantarflexion thrust; knee orthosis |
| Vaulting | Rising on forefoot of unaffected side to clear affected leg during swing | Stance (unaffected side) | Address limb length discrepancy or inability to flex affected hip/knee |
| Trendelenburg | Weak hip abductors on affected side | Stance phase | Hip strengthening; possible hip-knee-ankle-foot orthosis for severe cases |
| Stiff-legged gait | Spastic quadriceps (particularly rectus femoris) preventing knee flexion in swing | Swing phase | Botox to rectus femoris; consider rectus femoris transfer in severe cases |
| Scissoring | Hip adductor spasticity causing legs to cross midline | Swing phase | Botox or phenol to obturator nerve/hip adductors |

**AFO Types and Indications:**

| AFO Type | Design | Indication | Biomechanical Effect |
|----------|--------|-----------|---------------------|
| Posterior Leaf Spring (PLS) | Thin, flexible posterior strut | Isolated foot drop with no mediolateral instability | Assists dorsiflexion in swing; minimal mediolateral control |
| Solid (rigid) AFO | Full posterior shell; no ankle motion | Severe plantarflexion spasticity; mediolateral instability; significant equinovarus | Blocks all ankle motion; maximum stability; may impede push-off |
| Hinged AFO | Posterior shell with ankle hinge | Foot drop with need for tibial advancement in stance; moderate spasticity | Allows dorsiflexion; blocks plantarflexion; permits more normal gait |
| Ground reaction AFO (GRAFO) | Anterior shell; rigid | Knee hyperextension (recurvatum); quadriceps weakness | Generates knee extension moment; supports stance phase stability |
| Floor reaction AFO | Rigid; set in slight dorsiflexion | Crouch gait; weak plantarflexors | Creates plantarflexion-knee extension couple |
| Carbon fiber AFO | Lightweight; energy-storing | Higher-functioning patients; community ambulators | Dynamic energy return; lighter weight; cosmetically acceptable |

> **Board Pearl:** For equinovarus foot (most common hemiplegic foot deformity), a rigid or hinged AFO with mediolateral control is appropriate. A posterior leaf spring AFO is insufficient because it does not control inversion. For genu recurvatum (knee hyperextension), set the AFO in 5 degrees of dorsiflexion to block the plantarflexion thrust that drives the knee into hyperextension; alternatively, use a ground reaction AFO (GRAFO).

## Board-Style Review Questions

**Question 1:** A patient is 3 weeks post-stroke with NIHSS 12 at onset. The affected arm shows shoulder elevation, elbow flexion, and forearm supination as a mass pattern when attempting to reach. Isolated finger movements are absent. What Brunnstrom stage best describes this patient?

*Answer:* Brunnstrom Stage 3 (spasticity peaks; voluntary movement only within synergy patterns). The patient demonstrates the upper extremity flexor synergy pattern (shoulder elevation/abduction, elbow flexion, forearm supination) without ability to move outside of this synergy. Isolated movements are not yet possible.

**Question 2:** A 65-year-old man is 4 months post-left MCA stroke. He has 15 degrees of active wrist extension and 20 degrees of active finger extension but rarely uses the affected hand in daily life. What intervention is most appropriate?

*Answer:* CIMT (Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy). He meets the motor criteria (at least 10 degrees wrist extension and 10 degrees finger extension) and demonstrates learned nonuse (has motor ability but does not use the hand). The EXCITE trial demonstrated benefit in this time window (3-9 months post-stroke).

**Question 3:** A patient at 48 hours post-stroke has SAFE score of 0. TMS at 2 weeks shows absent motor evoked potentials. MRI shows restricted diffusion in the posterior limb of the internal capsule. What is the expected UE motor recovery?

*Answer:* Poor. This patient has all three negative prognostic markers: SAFE score of 0 at 72 hours, absent MEPs on TMS, and disrupted PLIC on DWI. This patient is a "non-fitter" who will NOT follow the proportional recovery rule and will likely have minimal UE motor recovery. Rehabilitation should focus on compensatory strategies and maximizing function with the unaffected hand.
