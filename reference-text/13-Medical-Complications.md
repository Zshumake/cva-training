## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** DVT occurs in 20-75% of untreated hemiplegic stroke patients. PE is the most common cause of death between 2-4 weeks poststroke. LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg SQ daily) is the preferred pharmacologic prophylaxis for ischemic stroke.

> **Board Pearl:** The CLOTS trials are landmark studies. CLOTS 1: Graduated compression stockings (TED hose) do NOT reduce DVT and INCREASE skin complications. CLOTS 3: Intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC) significantly reduces DVT (NNT = 28). Use IPC, NOT stockings.

> **Board Pearl:** The most common bladder dysfunction after stroke is the UNINHIBITED neurogenic bladder (detrusor hyperreflexia) because stroke is an upper motor neuron lesion. The cortical micturition center normally inhibits the pontine micturition reflex; when this inhibition is lost, involuntary detrusor contractions cause urge incontinence.

> **Board Pearl:** Early seizures (<7 days) are caused by acute metabolic disturbance and cytotoxic effects at the infarct border — they do NOT require long-term antiepileptic therapy. Late seizures (>7 days) are caused by gliotic scarring and epileptogenic focus formation — they DO require ongoing antiepileptic treatment.

> **Board Pearl:** Falls occur in 25% of stroke survivors within 30 months, and hip fracture risk is 2-4x the general population. This is compounded by osteoporosis on the hemiplegic side from disuse.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 13.1 Venous Thromboembolism: DVT and PE

#### 13.1.1 Epidemiology and Pathophysiology

[BOARD] Deep venous thrombosis (DVT) is one of the most common and dangerous medical complications after stroke, affecting 20-75% of untreated hemiplegic stroke patients (depending on screening method and timing). Pulmonary embolism (PE) is the most common cause of death between 2 and 4 weeks poststroke, accounting for approximately 1-2% of stroke patients and up to 25% of early stroke deaths.

The pathophysiology follows Virchow's triad:

| Virchow Component | Mechanism in Stroke |
|---|---|
| **Venous stasis** | Hemiplegia/hemiparesis causing immobility; loss of calf muscle pump |
| **Endothelial injury** | Acute illness response; inflammatory mediators; venous compression from dependent positioning |
| **Hypercoagulability** | Acute phase response; elevated fibrinogen, factor VIII; dehydration; possible concurrent AF requiring anticoagulation adjustment |

**Risk factors specific to stroke patients:**
- Hemiplegia/immobility (strongest risk factor)
- Older age
- Obesity
- Prior DVT/PE history
- Atrial fibrillation and cardiac disease
- Dehydration
- Lower extremity paresis (DVT most common in the paretic leg)
- Hemorrhagic stroke (cannot use anticoagulant prophylaxis immediately)
- Severity of stroke (higher NIHSS)

#### 13.1.2 DVT Prophylaxis

[BOARD]

**Ischemic Stroke Prophylaxis:**

| Intervention | Recommendation | Key Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **LMWH (enoxaparin 40 mg SQ daily)** | Preferred pharmacologic agent | Reduces DVT (OR 0.55); superior to UFH |
| **UFH (5000 units SQ BID-TID)** | Alternative to LMWH | Effective but inferior to LMWH; higher HIT risk |
| **Intermittent pneumatic compression (IPC)** | All immobile patients; START on admission | CLOTS 3: significant DVT reduction; NNT = 28 |
| **Early mobilization** | Most important nonpharmacologic intervention | Reduces stasis; improves overall outcomes (but NOT <24 hours — AVERT trial) |
| **Graduated compression stockings (TED hose)** | NOT recommended alone | CLOTS 1: No DVT reduction; INCREASED skin complications |

**Hemorrhagic Stroke Prophylaxis:**

| Intervention | Recommendation | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| **IPC devices** | Mainstay; start on admission | Immediately |
| **LMWH** | Delayed start until hematoma stable | Typically 48-72 hours if no expansion on repeat CT; some centers wait 7-14 days |
| **Early mobilization** | When medically stable | As tolerated |
| **IVC filter** | Rarely indicated | Only for proven proximal DVT when anticoagulation absolutely contraindicated |

#### 13.1.3 The CLOTS Trials

[BOARD] The CLOTS (Clots in Legs Or sTockings after Stroke) trials are landmark studies for DVT prophylaxis in stroke:

| Trial | Year | Intervention | Key Finding | Clinical Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CLOTS 1** | 2009 | Thigh-length graduated compression stockings (GCS) vs. no stockings | GCS did NOT reduce DVT; INCREASED skin complications (skin breaks, ulcers, blisters) | Do NOT use compression stockings for DVT prevention in immobile stroke patients |
| **CLOTS 2** | 2010 | Thigh-length GCS vs. below-knee GCS | Neither was effective; below-knee stockings were associated with a nonsignificant trend toward higher DVT risk compared to thigh-length | Below-knee stockings are NOT a suitable alternative |
| **CLOTS 3** | 2013 | IPC vs. no IPC in immobile stroke patients (n=2876) | IPC significantly reduced DVT (8.5% vs. 12.1%); absolute risk reduction ~3.6%; reduced 30-day mortality (10.8% vs. 13.1%) | IPC IS effective and recommended for all immobile stroke patients; NNT = 28 |

> **Board Pearl:** The CLOTS trials are among the most commonly tested topics in stroke rehabilitation board examinations. Key takeaway: use IPC (CLOTS 3 positive), do NOT use compression stockings (CLOTS 1 negative). Combine IPC with pharmacologic prophylaxis (LMWH) for highest-risk patients with ischemic stroke.

#### 13.1.4 DVT/PE Diagnosis

[BOARD]

| Test | Primary Use | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Duplex ultrasound** | First-line for DVT | High sensitivity for proximal DVT (>95%); lower sensitivity for calf DVT (~60-70%) |
| **D-dimer** | Screening/rule-out | High negative predictive value; useful to RULE OUT DVT. However, often elevated poststroke (low specificity in this population) |
| **CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)** | Gold standard for PE | Rapid, widely available, high sensitivity and specificity for PE |
| **V/Q scan** | Alternative for PE | When CTPA contraindicated (renal insufficiency, contrast allergy) |
| **Venography** | Historical gold standard for DVT | Rarely used — invasive, replaced by ultrasound |

**Wells Score for DVT:**
- Stroke patients inherently score high due to paralysis (+1) and being bedridden (+1)
- This makes the Wells score less discriminative in this population
- Maintain high clinical suspicion in ALL immobile stroke patients regardless of Wells score

**Treatment of Established DVT/PE:**
- Therapeutic anticoagulation (LMWH → warfarin or DOAC) for ischemic stroke with DVT/PE
- For hemorrhagic stroke with DVT/PE: timing of therapeutic anticoagulation is complex — multidisciplinary discussion; IVC filter may be considered as bridge
- Duration: typically 3 months minimum; longer if ongoing risk factors

### 13.2 Bladder Dysfunction

[BOARD] Urinary incontinence affects 50-70% of stroke patients in the first month and is one of the strongest negative predictors of functional outcome and discharge disposition. It resolves to approximately 13% by 2 years, but persistent incontinence at 2 weeks is a poor prognostic sign.

#### 13.2.1 Neuroanatomy of Bladder Control

[COMPREHENSIVE] Normal bladder control involves a hierarchy of neural centers:

1. **Cerebral cortex (medial frontal lobe)** — Voluntary inhibition of the micturition reflex; provides conscious control over voiding
2. **Pontine micturition center (Barrington nucleus)** — Coordinates the micturition reflex: simultaneous detrusor contraction and sphincter relaxation
3. **Sacral micturition center (S2-S4)** — Parasympathetic outflow to detrusor muscle; somatic outflow to external urethral sphincter (pudendal nerve)
4. **Sympathetic outflow (T11-L2)** — Hypogastric nerve: relaxes detrusor, contracts internal sphincter (storage function)

After hemispheric stroke, the most common lesion disrupts cortical inhibition of the pontine micturition center, producing an uninhibited (spastic) neurogenic bladder.

#### 13.2.2 Bladder Types After Stroke

[BOARD]

| Bladder Type | Mechanism | Urodynamic Finding | Clinical Features | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Uninhibited Neurogenic (Spastic)** | Loss of cortical inhibition of pontine micturition center (UMN lesion — most common) | Detrusor hyperreflexia — involuntary contractions at low volumes | Urge incontinence, frequency, urgency, small residual volumes | Timed voiding schedule; anticholinergics (oxybutynin, tolterodine); behavioral training; mirabegron (beta-3 agonist) |
| **Areflexic (Flaccid)** | Acute stage — cerebral shock; detrusor underactivity | Detrusor areflexia — large volumes, overflow | Urinary retention, overflow incontinence, distended bladder, large post-void residual | Intermittent catheterization; Crede maneuver (caution — not if DSD); Valsalva (caution) |
| **Detrusor-Sphincter Dyssynergia (DSD)** | Incoordination of detrusor contraction and sphincter relaxation (less common in stroke than SCI) | High intravesical pressures, incomplete emptying | UTI risk, hydronephrosis risk, hesitancy, interrupted stream | Alpha-blocker (tamsulosin); intermittent catheterization; botulinum toxin to sphincter |

> **Board Pearl:** The most common urodynamic finding after stroke is detrusor hyperreflexia (uninhibited neurogenic bladder) because stroke is an UMN lesion that removes cortical inhibition from the pontine micturition center. Treatment: anticholinergics + timed voiding + behavioral training.

#### 13.2.3 Bladder Management Principles

[BOARD]
1. **Avoid indwelling (Foley) catheters** whenever possible — dramatically increase UTI risk (3-7% per catheter-day)
2. **Intermittent catheterization** is preferred for patients with retention or high post-void residuals
3. **Timed voiding schedule** (every 2-3 hours) for patients with urge incontinence
4. **Post-void residual (PVR) monitoring** — if PVR > 200 mL, consider intermittent catheterization
5. **Bladder diary** to track patterns and guide management
6. **Fluid management** — adequate hydration but avoid caffeine and excessive fluids before bed
7. **Prompted voiding** for cognitively impaired patients
8. **Pharmacologic management:**
   - Anticholinergics (oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin) for overactive bladder
   - Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin) for outlet obstruction or DSD
   - Mirabegron (beta-3 agonist) — alternative to anticholinergics with fewer cognitive side effects (important in stroke patients)

> **Board Pearl:** Avoid indwelling catheters in stroke patients. Intermittent catheterization is preferred for retention. Anticholinergics are the pharmacologic mainstay for uninhibited neurogenic bladder. Be cautious with anticholinergics in elderly and cognitively impaired patients (increased confusion risk) — mirabegron is an alternative with fewer CNS effects.

### 13.3 Bowel Dysfunction

[COMPREHENSIVE] Bowel dysfunction is common but often underaddressed after stroke.

**Bowel Incontinence:**
- Prevalence: ~31% acutely; improves over time
- Strongly associated with stroke severity, cognitive impairment, and immobility
- Mechanisms: reduced rectal sensation, impaired sphincter control, constipation with overflow, diarrhea from medications/tube feeds
- Management: scheduled toileting programs, dietary fiber, adequate hydration, minimizing constipating medications, biofeedback

**Constipation:**
- Prevalence: 30-55% during rehabilitation
- Causes: immobility, dehydration, opioid and anticholinergic medications, low-fiber diet, neurogenic bowel dysfunction, altered gut motility
- Management approach:
  1. Non-pharmacologic: fiber supplementation (25-35 g/day), adequate fluids (1.5-2 L/day), physical activity, optimal toileting positioning, scheduled toileting
  2. Osmotic laxatives: polyethylene glycol (first-line), lactulose
  3. Stimulant laxatives: bisacodyl, senna (short-term use)
  4. Stool softeners: docusate sodium (limited evidence for efficacy but widely used)
  5. Rectal interventions: suppositories, enemas for refractory cases

**Bowel Program Development:**
- Establish regular schedule (typically 30 minutes after a meal, leveraging gastrocolic reflex)
- Use combination of dietary optimization, scheduled toileting, and medications as needed
- Document bowel diary to track patterns
- Avoid excessive use of laxatives (dependence)

### 13.4 Cardiac Complications

[BOARD] Cardiac complications are a significant cause of morbidity and mortality after stroke, reflecting the bidirectional relationship between cardiovascular and cerebrovascular disease.

#### 13.4.1 Cardiac Events After Stroke

| Complication | Incidence | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Myocardial infarction** | 1-2% during hospitalization | Often silent (painless) in stroke patients; high mortality when it occurs |
| **Arrhythmias** | 20-35% | Most common: atrial fibrillation (both pre-existing and new-onset). Others: ventricular tachycardia, QT prolongation, ST changes, T-wave inversion |
| **Takotsubo cardiomyopathy** | 1-2% | "Stress cardiomyopathy" — apical ballooning of left ventricle; triggered by catecholamine surge; mimics acute MI; usually reversible |
| **Heart failure** | Variable | Acute decompensation of pre-existing CHF; or new cardiomyopathy from catecholamine toxicity |
| **Troponin elevation** | 5-35% | Often subclinical myocardial injury (type 2 MI from demand ischemia); associated with worse stroke outcomes |
| **ECG abnormalities** | Up to 70% | QTc prolongation, ST changes, T-wave inversions, U waves — even in absence of primary cardiac disease |

#### 13.4.2 Insular Cortex Lateralization

[BOARD] The insular cortex plays a critical role in autonomic regulation, and lateralization of autonomic function between the hemispheres has important clinical implications:

| Feature | Right Insular Cortex | Left Insular Cortex |
|---|---|---|
| **Autonomic predominance** | Sympathetic | Parasympathetic |
| **Effect of stroke** | Loss of sympathetic regulation → parasympathetic dominance → **bradycardia, hypotension** | Loss of parasympathetic regulation → sympathetic dominance → **tachycardia, hypertension, cardiac arrhythmias** |
| **Cardiac risk** | ⚠️ UNVERIFIED: Some studies suggest right insular strokes paradoxically have MORE cardiac complications due to pathological sympathetic activation, while others support the simplified model shown here. The lateralization of cardiac risk remains debated | Higher cardiac complication rate; more arrhythmias (supported by multiple studies) |
| **ECG changes** | Less common | More common (QT prolongation, ST changes) |

> **Board Pearl:** Left insular cortex strokes are associated with greater cardiac complications (arrhythmias, myocardial injury) because the left insula provides parasympathetic regulation of the heart. When damaged, unopposed sympathetic drive leads to catecholamine surge, tachycardia, and increased arrhythmia risk. Right insular strokes may cause bradycardia and hypotension.

**Clinical implication:** All acute stroke patients should have continuous cardiac monitoring for at least 24-72 hours. ECG and troponin should be obtained on admission. New-onset atrial fibrillation after stroke has implications for secondary prevention (anticoagulation for cardioembolic mechanism).

### 13.5 Poststroke Seizures

[BOARD] Poststroke seizures are classified as early or late, with fundamentally different mechanisms and treatment implications.

#### 13.5.1 Classification and Mechanisms

| Feature | Early Seizures (<7 days) | Late Seizures (>7 days) |
|---|---|---|
| **Incidence** | 2-6% ischemic; 10-15% hemorrhagic | 3-5% at 1 year; 7-20% at 5 years |
| **Mechanism** | Acute metabolic disturbance: glutamate excitotoxicity, ionic shifts, cytotoxic edema, blood-brain barrier disruption at the infarct penumbra | Chronic structural change: gliotic scarring, hemosiderin deposition, altered neuronal excitability, epileptogenic focus formation |
| **Seizure type** | Partial (focal) most common; may secondarily generalize | Partial (focal) with or without secondary generalization |
| **Risk factors** | Large cortical infarcts, hemorrhagic stroke/hemorrhagic transformation, cortical involvement, higher NIHSS | Cortical location, large lesion size, prior early seizures, hemorrhagic stroke |
| **Recurrence risk** | Low (~15-25% recurrence) | High (~50-70% recurrence; this IS epilepsy) |
| **Long-term AED?** | Generally NO — treat acutely, then discontinue | YES — long-term antiepileptic therapy indicated |
| **Prognostic impact** | Associated with worse short-term outcomes but NOT independently with mortality | Associated with increased long-term disability |

> **Board Pearl:** Early seizures (<7 days) are caused by acute metabolic disruption and generally do NOT require long-term antiepileptic drug (AED) therapy — treat the acute seizure and observe. Late seizures (>7 days) represent the development of a chronic epileptogenic focus (poststroke epilepsy) and DO require ongoing AED therapy.

#### 13.5.2 Poststroke Seizure Management

[BOARD]

| Scenario | Management |
|---|---|
| **Single early seizure** | Treat acute seizure (lorazepam); observe; AED for 2-4 weeks, then taper; no long-term AED |
| **Recurrent early seizures** | AED therapy during acute period (levetiracetam preferred); attempt taper after 3-6 months |
| **Status epilepticus** | Aggressive treatment per SE protocol; ICU admission |
| **First late seizure** | Start long-term AED therapy (levetiracetam or lamotrigine preferred) |
| **Prophylactic AEDs** | NOT recommended for ischemic stroke; may be considered short-term (7 days) for lobar ICH or hemorrhagic transformation with cortical involvement |

**AED Selection Considerations in Stroke:**
- **Levetiracetam** — Preferred: no hepatic metabolism, no drug interactions with anticoagulants, well-tolerated
- **Lamotrigine** — Good alternative; minimal drug interactions; slow titration required
- **Avoid phenytoin** if possible — enzyme induction (CYP450) affects anticoagulant metabolism; osteoporosis risk; cognitive effects
- **Avoid valproic acid** in patients on anticoagulation — inhibits platelet function, hepatotoxicity risk
- **Lacosamide** — Emerging as useful alternative; no significant drug interactions

### 13.6 Falls and Fractures

[BOARD] Falls are one of the most common and consequential complications of stroke, with significant impact on morbidity, mortality, and healthcare costs.

**Epidemiology:**
- 25% of stroke survivors fall within 30 months of stroke
- Up to 70% fall within the first 6 months after discharge
- 1-4 falls per person per year in community-dwelling stroke survivors
- Hip fracture risk: 2-4x the general population

**Risk Factors for Falls After Stroke:**
- Motor weakness (hemiparesis)
- Impaired balance (especially if Berg Balance Scale <45)
- Sensory deficits (proprioception, vision)
- Hemispatial neglect
- Cognitive impairment
- Depression and anxiety
- Medications (sedatives, antihypertensives, polypharmacy)
- Environmental hazards
- Previous falls
- Urgency urinary incontinence
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Lower extremity spasticity or contracture

**Fall Prevention Strategies:**

[COMPREHENSIVE]

| Category | Interventions |
|---|---|
| **Assessment** | Berg Balance Scale, Timed Up-and-Go (TUG), fall risk screening on admission and at transitions of care |
| **Exercise/Balance** | Individualized physical therapy; balance training; strengthening; gait training with appropriate assistive device |
| **Environmental** | Home safety assessment and modification (grab bars, lighting, remove rugs/cords, raised toilet seat, shower chair) |
| **Medication review** | Minimize sedatives, anticholinergics, polypharmacy; optimize antihypertensive timing to reduce orthostatic hypotension |
| **Assistive devices** | Appropriate device selection (cane, quad cane, walker); proper fitting; training in use |
| **Footwear** | Supportive, non-slip shoes; ankle-foot orthosis if needed |
| **Visual correction** | Updated eyeglasses; avoid bifocals during ambulation (impairs depth perception) |
| **Hip protectors** | Limited evidence; may reduce hip fracture in high-risk fallers |
| **Education** | Patient and caregiver education about fall risk; when and how to call for help; safe transfer techniques |

> **Board Pearl:** Falls occur in up to 70% of stroke survivors in the first 6 months after discharge. Hip fracture risk is 2-4x the general population. Fall prevention should be addressed before every discharge transition. Berg Balance Scale <45 indicates increased fall risk.

### 13.7 Pressure Injuries

[BOARD] Pressure injuries (formerly pressure ulcers or decubitus ulcers) are areas of localized tissue damage resulting from sustained pressure, shear, or friction, typically over bony prominences. Stroke patients are at high risk due to immobility, sensory loss, incontinence, and malnutrition.

#### 13.7.1 The Braden Scale

[BOARD] The Braden Scale for Predicting Pressure Sore Risk is the most widely used and validated assessment tool:

| Subscale | Score Range | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Sensory perception** | 1-4 | Ability to respond to pressure-related discomfort |
| **Moisture** | 1-4 | Degree to which skin is exposed to moisture |
| **Activity** | 1-4 | Degree of physical activity |
| **Mobility** | 1-4 | Ability to change and control body position |
| **Nutrition** | 1-4 | Usual food intake pattern |
| **Friction and shear** | 1-3 | Degree of friction and shearing forces on skin |

**Total Score Range: 6-23**

| Score | Risk Level | Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| **≤9** | Very high risk | Aggressive prevention: pressure-redistribution mattress, q2h repositioning, moisture management, nutrition optimization, skin assessment every shift |
| **10-12** | High risk | Enhanced prevention protocols |
| **13-14** | Moderate risk | Standard prevention protocols |
| **15-18** | Mild risk | Basic prevention measures |
| **>18** | Low risk | Routine monitoring |

**Stroke-Specific Risk Factors:**
- Immobility (hemiplegia)
- Sensory loss on affected side (cannot feel pressure)
- Incontinence (moisture damage)
- Malnutrition
- Decreased consciousness
- Decreased ability to reposition independently

#### 13.7.2 Pressure Injury Prevention

[COMPREHENSIVE]

1. **Repositioning:** Every 2 hours for bed-bound patients; offload bony prominences; use 30-degree lateral tilt position; avoid positioning directly on trochanter
2. **Pressure-redistribution surfaces:** Specialized mattresses (alternating pressure, low-air-loss, fluid-filled) for high-risk patients
3. **Skin assessment:** Systematic inspection of all bony prominences (sacrum, heels, occiput, ischial tuberosities, greater trochanters, malleoli) at least daily
4. **Moisture management:** Prompt incontinence care; barrier creams; moisture-wicking linens
5. **Nutrition optimization:** Adequate protein (1.25-1.5 g/kg/day), calories, vitamins C and A, zinc, and hydration
6. **Mobilization:** Progressive mobility as soon as medically stable
7. **Heel protection:** Float heels off the bed surface (heel suspension device or pillows)

### 13.8 Heterotopic Ossification (HO)

[COMPREHENSIVE] Heterotopic ossification (HO) is the formation of mature lamellar bone within soft tissues (muscles, tendons, ligaments) surrounding joints. While most commonly associated with TBI and SCI, HO occurs in approximately 1.3% of stroke patients.

#### 13.8.1 Clinical Features

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| **Incidence** | ~1.3% overall; higher in hemorrhagic stroke (4.75x risk), severe stroke, spastic limbs |
| **Timing** | Typically 1-4 months poststroke |
| **Common locations** | Hip (most common), shoulder, elbow, knee — typically on the paretic side |
| **Presentation** | Pain, swelling, warmth, reduced ROM in periarticular region; may mimic DVT or septic joint |
| **Risk factors** | Hemorrhagic stroke, hemorrhagic transformation, male sex, spasticity (13.78x risk), prolonged immobility, cognitive impairment |

#### 13.8.2 Diagnosis

| Test | Role | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Serum alkaline phosphatase** | Screening; elevated in active HO formation | Non-specific; elevated in many conditions |
| **Triple-phase bone scan** | Most sensitive early test | Positive within 2.5 weeks of onset; shows increased uptake in all 3 phases |
| **Plain radiograph** | Confirmatory; shows mature HO | May not be positive for 4-6 weeks after onset |
| **CT scan** | Detailed anatomic assessment | Best for surgical planning if resection considered |

#### 13.8.3 Treatment

| Phase | Treatment | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **Prevention** | Etidronate, gentle ROM exercises | Etidronate (bisphosphonate): 20 mg/kg/day x 2 weeks, then 10 mg/kg/day x 10 weeks (12-week course total). Evidence strongest in SCI/TBI; limited data in stroke |
| **Active formation** | NSAIDs (indomethacin), etidronate, gentle ROM | Indomethacin 75 mg/day; continue ROM to preserve function. Avoid aggressive stretching (may worsen HO) |
| **Mature (quiescent)** | Surgical resection if functionally limiting | Wait until HO is mature (typically 12-18 months); confirmed by normalization of bone scan. Premature resection → high recurrence rate |
| **Adjunctive** | Radiation therapy | Single-dose radiation (700 cGy) within 24 hours of surgery prevents recurrence |

> **Board Pearl:** HO after stroke is rare (~1.3%) but significantly more common with hemorrhagic stroke and spasticity. Triple-phase bone scan is the most sensitive early diagnostic test. Surgical resection should wait until HO is mature (normal bone scan, typically 12-18 months) to minimize recurrence.

### 13.9 Osteoporosis on the Hemiplegic Side

[COMPREHENSIVE] Rapid bone loss occurs on the hemiplegic side after stroke, leading to significantly increased fracture risk.

**Mechanisms:**
1. **Disuse (immobility)** — Decreased mechanical loading removes the primary stimulus for osteoblast activity (Wolff's law)
2. **Sympathetic denervation** — Altered autonomic innervation of bone on the paretic side
3. **Vitamin D deficiency** — Reduced sunlight exposure; common in institutionalized patients
4. **Nutritional deficits** — Malnutrition, reduced calcium and protein intake
5. **Medications** — Heparin (long-term), phenytoin, glucocorticoids

**Clinical Significance:**
- Bone mineral density (BMD) on the hemiplegic side decreases by 3-12% within the first year after stroke
- Proximal femur on the paretic side is most affected
- Hip fracture risk is 2-4x the general population
- Fractures typically occur on the paretic side and are often from falls

**Prevention and Management:**
- Early and ongoing mobilization and weight-bearing activity
- Calcium supplementation (1000-1200 mg/day) + Vitamin D (800-1000 IU/day)
- DEXA scan screening (especially in postmenopausal women and elderly men with stroke)
- Bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate) or denosumab if osteoporosis confirmed
- Fall prevention strategies (see section 13.6)
- Address nutritional deficits

### 13.10 Nutritional Screening and Management

[COMPREHENSIVE] Malnutrition is present in 6-62% of stroke patients at admission and worsens during hospitalization due to dysphagia, reduced consciousness, depression, and increased metabolic demands.

#### 13.10.1 The MUST Tool (Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool)

[BOARD]

| Step | Component | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 2 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | BMI | >20 kg/m2 | 18.5-20 | <18.5 |
| 2 | Unplanned weight loss (3-6 months) | <5% | 5-10% | >10% |
| 3 | Acute disease effect | Not acutely ill | — | Acutely ill AND no nutritional intake for >5 days (or likely) |

**Total Score Interpretation:**
- **0 = Low risk:** Routine screening (weekly in hospital, monthly in community)
- **1 = Medium risk:** Observe; document dietary intake for 3 days; if adequate, repeat screening; if inadequate, clinical intervention
- **2+ = High risk:** Treat — dietitian referral, nutritional supplementation, food fortification, consider enteral feeding

**Additional Nutritional Markers:**
- Serum albumin: half-life 20 days; reflects chronic nutritional status (but also an acute phase reactant — decreases with inflammation)
- Prealbumin (transthyretin): half-life 2 days; more sensitive to acute nutritional changes
- Body weight trends
- Caloric and protein intake documentation

**Nutritional Requirements After Stroke:**
- Calories: 25-35 kcal/kg/day (higher in acute phase, lower if immobile)
- Protein: 1.0-1.5 g/kg/day (higher for wound healing and pressure injury prevention)
- Adequate hydration: 30 mL/kg/day minimum
- Micronutrients: vitamins C and D, zinc, iron as indicated

### 13.11 Autonomic Dysfunction

[COMPREHENSIVE] Autonomic dysfunction after stroke can manifest in multiple organ systems and is influenced by lesion location, particularly insular cortex involvement.

**Cardiovascular Autonomic Dysfunction:**
- Orthostatic hypotension (especially with prolonged bed rest and antihypertensives)
- Heart rate variability reduction (associated with increased cardiac mortality)
- Blood pressure lability
- Insular cortex lateralization effects (see section 13.4.2)

**Thermoregulatory Dysfunction:**
- Central fever (neurogenic fever) — typically from hypothalamic or brainstem lesions
- Impaired sweating on the hemiplegic side
- Poikilothermia (body temperature varies with environment) in severe brainstem strokes

**Gastrointestinal Autonomic Dysfunction:**
- Gastroparesis
- Ileus
- Altered gut motility (contributing to constipation)

**Genitourinary Autonomic Dysfunction:**
- Bladder dysfunction (covered in section 13.2)
- Erectile dysfunction

**Respiratory Autonomic Dysfunction:**
- Central sleep apnea (brainstem strokes)
- Cheyne-Stokes respiration (bilateral hemispheric or diencephalic lesions)

**Management:**
- Address each system specifically
- Orthostatic hypotension: compression garments, fluid/salt loading, gradual position changes, midodrine if refractory
- Central fever: cooling measures; acetaminophen often ineffective for neurogenic fever; bromocriptine may help
- Screen for and treat obstructive sleep apnea (very common poststroke; CPAP improves outcomes)

### 13.12 Infectious Complications

[BOARD]

| Infection | Incidence | Key Management Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Pneumonia** | 3-10% | Leading cause of death in subacute period; dysphagia screening reduces risk; ceftriaxone prophylaxis for intubated patients reduces VAP |
| **Urinary tract infection (UTI)** | 11-15% | Avoid indwelling catheters; intermittent catheterization preferred; treat only symptomatic UTI (not asymptomatic bacteriuria) |
| **Skin/wound infections** | Variable | Related to pressure injuries, IV sites; prevention through skin care and pressure injury prevention |

**Stroke-Induced Immunodepression Syndrome (SIDS):**
[COMPREHENSIVE] Acute stroke triggers a systemic immunosuppressive response mediated by the sympathetic nervous system and hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis. This results in:
- Lymphocyte apoptosis
- Decreased cytokine production
- Impaired cellular immunity
- Increased susceptibility to pneumonia and UTI

This immunosuppression is most profound in the first week after stroke and contributes to the high infection rate in this period. It is more pronounced after large strokes and with insular cortex involvement.
