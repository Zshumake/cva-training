## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** On CT, ischemia appears DARK (hypodense) and hemorrhage appears BRIGHT (hyperdense). Mnemonic: "Blood is Bright." A normal CT does NOT rule out acute ischemic stroke within the first 6 hours.

> **Board Pearl:** DWI on MRI is the single most sensitive imaging sequence for acute ischemic stroke, detecting cytotoxic edema within minutes of onset. Sensitivity exceeds 92%, compared with approximately 60% for non-contrast CT in the first 6 hours.

> **Board Pearl:** ASPECTS of 6 or greater is the standard threshold for thrombectomy eligibility. The 10 regions are: caudate (C), lentiform (L), internal capsule (IC), insular ribbon (I), and MCA cortical regions M1 through M6.

> **Board Pearl:** DWI-positive with FLAIR-negative (mismatch) suggests stroke onset within approximately 4.5 hours and can be used to select wake-up stroke patients for thrombolysis (WAKE-UP trial).

> **Board Pearl:** The NIHSS is an 11-item scale scoring 0 to 42. It underrepresents posterior circulation deficits and does not assess balance. Scores below 5 indicate mild stroke; 5 to 9 moderate; 10 or greater severe.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 6.1 Non-Contrast CT in Acute Stroke

**[BOARD]**

Non-contrast CT (NCCT) remains the first-line imaging study in the acute stroke evaluation. Its primary role is to rapidly exclude intracranial hemorrhage, which fundamentally changes management. NCCT is fast (5-10 minutes), widely available around the clock, and has minimal contraindications.

**CT Appearance of Stroke:**

| Finding | Appearance on CT | Timing | Clinical Significance |
|---------|-----------------|--------|----------------------|
| Acute hemorrhage | Hyperdense (bright white) | Immediate | Excludes thrombolysis candidacy |
| Acute ischemia | Hypodense (dark) | Often not visible for 6-12 hours | Low sensitivity in hyperacute phase |
| Loss of gray-white differentiation | Subtle blurring of cortical ribbon | 3-6 hours | Early ischemic sign |
| Sulcal effacement | Loss of normal sulcal markings | 3-6 hours | Indicates cytotoxic edema |
| Hyperdense MCA sign | Bright linear density in MCA | Immediate | Indicates acute MCA thrombus; present in 30-40% of MCA strokes |
| Sylvian dot sign | Focal hyperdensity in Sylvian fissure | Immediate | Suggests distal MCA branch occlusion |

The sensitivity of NCCT for acute ischemic stroke in the first 6 hours is approximately 60-64%. This means that a normal CT in a patient presenting with acute neurological deficits should not be interpreted as excluding ischemic stroke. The fundamental purpose of the initial CT is to rule out hemorrhage and identify patients who may benefit from reperfusion therapy.

> **Board Pearl:** The hyperdense MCA sign is highly specific for acute MCA occlusion. It represents fresh thrombus within the vessel lumen and is NOT time-dependent or related to the degree of ischemic injury. It is present in approximately one-third of MCA strokes.

**CT Density Reference Values:**

Understanding Hounsfield units helps interpret CT findings:
- CSF: 0 HU
- Gray matter: 35-45 HU
- White matter: 25-35 HU
- Acute blood: 50-70 HU
- Calcification: 100+ HU

### 6.2 MRI in Acute Stroke

**[BOARD]**

MRI with diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI) is the most sensitive imaging modality for acute ischemic stroke. DWI detects cytotoxic edema (restricted water diffusion) within 3 to 30 minutes of symptom onset, far earlier than any CT finding becomes visible.

**MRI Sequences and Their Roles in Stroke Evaluation:**

| Sequence | What It Detects | Stroke Application | Timing |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|--------|
| DWI | Restricted water diffusion (cytotoxic edema) | Acute ischemia: BRIGHT within minutes | Minutes to days |
| ADC map | Confirms true vs. apparent restriction | TRUE restriction is DARK on ADC | Nadir at 2-3 days; pseudonormalization at approximately 10 days |
| FLAIR | T2-weighted with CSF suppression | Subacute ischemia; used in DWI-FLAIR mismatch | Becomes positive approximately 6 hours after onset |
| T1-weighted | Anatomy; fat appears bright | Arterial dissection (intramural hematoma bright on fat-suppressed T1) | Variable |
| T2-weighted | Edema, fluid (appears bright) | Vasogenic edema, chronic infarcts | Hours to permanent |
| GRE/SWI | Blood products (appear DARK) | Hemorrhage detection (acute and chronic microbleeds) | Immediate for acute blood |
| MRA | Vascular anatomy | Large vessel occlusion, stenosis, dissection | N/A |

**Key MRI Interpretation Principles:**

1. **DWI bright + ADC dark = TRUE restricted diffusion** (acute infarct)
2. **DWI bright + ADC bright = T2 shine-through** (NOT acute infarct; may represent subacute or chronic lesion)
3. **ADC nadir occurs at 2-3 days**, followed by pseudonormalization at approximately 10 days, making ADC less reliable after the first week
4. **GRE/SWI sequences are equivalent to CT for detecting acute hemorrhage**, making MRI a valid alternative to CT when available emergently

> **Board Pearl:** True restricted diffusion on DWI is confirmed by a corresponding DARK signal on the ADC map. If the lesion is bright on both DWI and ADC, this is T2 shine-through (not acute infarction). This distinction is commonly tested.

**CT vs. MRI Comparison for Stroke Evaluation:**

| Feature | CT | MRI |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Speed | 5-10 minutes | 20-45 minutes |
| Availability | Near-universal 24/7 | Limited, especially overnight |
| Acute hemorrhage | Excellent (>95% sensitivity) | Good with GRE/SWI |
| Acute ischemia (first 6 hours) | Poor (approximately 60%) | Excellent with DWI (>92%) |
| Posterior fossa imaging | Limited (bone artifact) | Superior |
| TIA evaluation | Often normal | DWI may reveal small infarcts (reclassifying TIA as stroke) |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Contraindications | Minimal (contrast allergy, pregnancy) | Pacemakers, metallic implants, claustrophobia |
| Radiation | Yes | No |

### 6.3 ASPECTS Score

**[BOARD]**

The Alberta Stroke Program Early CT Score (ASPECTS) is a 10-point quantitative topographic scoring system that assesses the extent of early ischemic changes within the MCA territory on non-contrast CT (or CT angiography source images).

**ASPECTS Scoring Method:**

A normal scan receives a score of 10. One point is subtracted for each of the 10 defined MCA territory regions that shows early ischemic change (hypoattenuation, loss of gray-white differentiation, or focal swelling).

**The 10 ASPECTS Regions:**

At the level of the basal ganglia:
1. **C** - Caudate nucleus
2. **L** - Lentiform nucleus (globus pallidus + putamen)
3. **IC** - Internal capsule (posterior limb)
4. **I** - Insular ribbon (insular cortex)
5. **M1** - Anterior MCA cortex (frontal operculum)
6. **M2** - MCA cortex lateral to the insular ribbon (anterior temporal lobe)
7. **M3** - Posterior MCA cortex (posterior temporal lobe)

At the level above the basal ganglia:
8. **M4** - Anterior MCA territory (above M1)
9. **M5** - Lateral MCA territory (above M2)
10. **M6** - Posterior MCA territory (above M3)

**ASPECTS Interpretation:**

| ASPECTS Score | Interpretation | Clinical Significance |
|--------------|---------------|----------------------|
| 10 | Normal; no early ischemic changes | Best candidate for reperfusion therapy |
| 7-9 | Small area of early ischemic change | Good thrombectomy candidate (score of 6 or greater required) |
| 4-6 | Moderate ischemic changes | Higher hemorrhagic transformation risk; historically considered the lower boundary for thrombectomy |
| 0-3 | Extensive early ischemic changes | Large established infarct; thrombectomy generally NOT beneficial |

> **Board Pearl:** ASPECTS of 6 or greater has been the standard threshold for thrombectomy eligibility in major clinical trials. However, recent trials including SELECT2, ANGEL-ASPECT, and RESCUE-Japan LIMIT have explored benefit in patients with large core infarcts (ASPECTS 3-5), demonstrating some benefit of thrombectomy even with lower ASPECTS in selected patients. The traditional threshold of 6 remains the most commonly tested value.

**Posterior Circulation ASPECTS (pc-ASPECTS):**

A parallel 10-point scoring system exists for the posterior circulation (pc-ASPECTS), which evaluates the brainstem, cerebellum, thalami, occipital lobes, and PCA territories. This is used less frequently in clinical practice but may appear on examinations.

**Automated ASPECTS:**

Software platforms (such as Rapid ASPECTS by RapidAI and Viz.ai) now provide automated ASPECTS scoring, improving inter-rater reliability and reducing interpretation time. Automated scoring has shown good correlation with expert neuroradiologist reads and is increasingly used in clinical decision-making.

### 6.4 DWI-FLAIR Mismatch for Wake-Up Stroke

**[BOARD]**

Approximately 20-25% of ischemic strokes present as "wake-up strokes," where the patient goes to sleep neurologically normal and awakens with a deficit. In these cases, the exact time of onset is unknown, which traditionally excluded patients from time-based thrombolysis eligibility.

The DWI-FLAIR mismatch concept provides a tissue-based clock to estimate stroke onset time:

- **DWI becomes positive within minutes** of ischemic onset (restricted diffusion from cytotoxic edema)
- **FLAIR becomes positive approximately 6 hours** after onset (vasogenic edema, increased water content visible on T2-weighted sequences)
- **DWI-positive / FLAIR-negative (mismatch present):** Suggests onset within approximately 4.5 hours, and the patient may be eligible for thrombolysis
- **DWI-positive / FLAIR-positive (no mismatch):** Suggests onset beyond 4.5 hours

**Key Trial: WAKE-UP (2018)**

The WAKE-UP trial (Thomalla et al., NEJM 2018) was the landmark randomized controlled trial that validated this concept. Patients with unknown-onset ischemic stroke who had DWI-FLAIR mismatch on MRI were randomized to IV alteplase versus placebo. The alteplase group had significantly better functional outcomes at 90 days (adjusted OR for favorable outcome [mRS 0-1]: 1.61; 95% CI 1.09-2.36).

**Limitations of DWI-FLAIR Mismatch:**

- Sensitivity for identifying patients within 4.5 hours is moderate (approximately 55-62%)
- High specificity and positive predictive value (approximately 88%)
- Negative predictive value is lower (approximately 19-35%), meaning a FLAIR-positive scan does not definitively exclude recent onset
- Requires MRI capability in the acute setting, which is not universally available
- Performance may differ between 1.5T and 3T magnets

### 6.5 CT Perfusion: Core vs. Penumbra

**[BOARD]**

CT perfusion (CTP) is the cornerstone of patient selection for extended-window thrombectomy (6-24 hours). It differentiates irreversibly infarcted tissue (core) from hypoperfused but potentially salvageable tissue (penumbra).

**CTP Parameters:**

| Parameter | Definition | Clinical Meaning |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| CBF (Cerebral Blood Flow) | Volume of blood flowing through brain tissue per unit time | Severely reduced in both core and penumbra |
| CBV (Cerebral Blood Volume) | Total volume of blood in a given volume of brain tissue | Reduced in core; MAINTAINED in penumbra (autoregulatory vasodilation) |
| MTT (Mean Transit Time) | Average time for blood to traverse the capillary bed | Prolonged in both core and penumbra |
| Tmax (Time to Maximum) | Time to peak of the residue function | Delayed in penumbra (Tmax >6 seconds is the standard threshold) |

**Core vs. Penumbra Identification:**

| Feature | Core (Irreversible) | Penumbra (Salvageable) |
|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| CBF | Severely reduced (<30% of normal) | Moderately reduced |
| CBV | Reduced | Maintained or increased (autoregulation) |
| Tmax | Markedly delayed | Delayed (>6 seconds) |
| DWI (MRI equivalent) | Restricted diffusion | Normal or mildly abnormal |
| Clinical significance | Destined to infarct regardless of treatment | Target of reperfusion therapy |

**Mismatch Paradigms Used in Major Trials:**

| Mismatch Type | Definition | Key Trial |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|
| Perfusion-core mismatch | CTP penumbra volume significantly exceeds core volume (ratio of 1.8 or greater) | DEFUSE-3 |
| Clinical-core mismatch | Clinical deficit severity (NIHSS) disproportionate to infarct core volume | DAWN |
| DWI-FLAIR mismatch | DWI-positive with FLAIR-negative | WAKE-UP |
| Clinical-ASPECTS mismatch | NIHSS of 10 or greater with ASPECTS of 6 or greater | Multiple early thrombectomy trials |

> **Board Pearl:** The mismatch concept is the foundation of extended-window thrombectomy. A large mismatch (penumbra much larger than core) identifies patients with substantial salvageable brain tissue. The DAWN trial required core volume less than 21 mL (for NIHSS of 10 or greater) or less than 31 mL (for NIHSS of 20 or greater) at 6-24 hours. DEFUSE-3 required a mismatch ratio of 1.8 or greater with core less than 70 mL at 6-16 hours.

**Imaging Selection by Time Window:**

| Time Window | Recommended Imaging | Rationale |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------|
| 0-4.5 hours | NCCT + CTA (sufficient) | Standard thrombolysis window; hemorrhage exclusion is primary goal |
| 4.5-24 hours | Multimodal CT (CT + CTA + CTP) or multimodal MRI | Must identify salvageable penumbra for extended-window treatment |
| Unknown onset | MRI with DWI + FLAIR preferred | DWI-FLAIR mismatch provides tissue-based timing |

### 6.6 CT Angiography for LVO Detection

**[BOARD]**

CT angiography (CTA) of the head and neck is an essential component of the acute stroke imaging protocol. It identifies large vessel occlusions (LVOs) that are candidates for mechanical thrombectomy and evaluates the extracranial carotid and vertebral arteries for stenosis or dissection.

**CTA Findings in Acute Stroke:**

- **LVO identification:** Complete occlusion of the internal carotid artery (ICA), proximal middle cerebral artery (M1 or M2 segments), basilar artery, or vertebral arteries
- **Stenosis grading:** Quantification of carotid stenosis using NASCET criteria
- **Dissection:** Intimal flap, pseudoaneurysm, or vessel narrowing
- **Collateral assessment:** Single-phase or multiphase CTA can evaluate the adequacy of leptomeningeal collateral flow

**The Spot Sign in Hemorrhagic Stroke:**

The CTA spot sign is a marker of active extravasation within an intracerebral hemorrhage (ICH). It appears as one or more focal areas of contrast enhancement within the hematoma on CTA source images or post-contrast CT.

| Feature | Clinical Significance |
|---------|----------------------|
| Definition | Focal contrast enhancement within ICH on CTA |
| Prevalence | Present in approximately 30% of acute ICH patients |
| Prognostic value | Predicts hematoma expansion, poor outcome, and higher mortality |
| Sensitivity for expansion | Approximately 51-63% |
| Specificity for expansion | Approximately 85-90% |

> **Board Pearl:** The CTA spot sign in intracerebral hemorrhage predicts hematoma expansion and worse clinical outcomes. Its presence may influence decisions about blood pressure management intensity and potential surgical intervention.

**Multiphase CTA:**

Multiphase CTA involves three phases of image acquisition (arterial, peak venous, and late venous) to assess collateral blood flow. Good collaterals are associated with better outcomes after thrombectomy, as demonstrated in the ESCAPE trial. This technique provides a time-resolved assessment of collateral filling without requiring CT perfusion software.

### 6.7 Carotid Ultrasound and Transcranial Doppler

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

**Carotid Duplex Ultrasound:**

Carotid duplex ultrasonography is a non-invasive, radiation-free, portable, and relatively inexpensive modality for evaluating extracranial carotid artery disease. It combines B-mode imaging (anatomy) with Doppler flow assessment (hemodynamics).

**Carotid Stenosis Grading by Peak Systolic Velocity:**

| PSV (cm/s) | Estimated Stenosis | Clinical Action |
|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| <125 | <50% | Medical management |
| 125-230 | 50-69% | Medical management; consider CEA if symptomatic with other criteria |
| >230 | 70-99% | CEA indicated if symptomatic (NASCET trial) |
| No detectable flow | Complete occlusion (100%) | CEA not beneficial; medical management |

Additional parameters include end-diastolic velocity (EDV) and ICA/CCA ratio. An ICA/CCA ratio greater than 4.0 suggests greater than 70% stenosis.

> **Board Pearl:** Carotid duplex ultrasound is the initial screening test for carotid stenosis. Peak systolic velocity greater than 230 cm/s suggests greater than 70% stenosis, the threshold for carotid endarterectomy in symptomatic patients (NASCET). Conventional catheter angiography remains the gold standard for definitive assessment but is rarely needed given advances in CTA and MRA.

**Transcranial Doppler (TCD):**

TCD is a non-invasive, portable, bedside ultrasound technique that evaluates intracranial blood flow velocities through temporal, orbital, and suboccipital acoustic windows.

**TCD Clinical Applications:**

| Application | Relevant Findings | Clinical Utility |
|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| SAH vasospasm monitoring | MCA mean flow velocity >120 cm/s (mild), >200 cm/s (severe) | Most important TCD application; serial monitoring guides treatment |
| Vasospasm vs. hyperemia | Lindegaard ratio (MCA/extracranial ICA velocity) >3 confirms vasospasm | Differentiates true vasospasm from global hyperemia |
| PFO detection | Bubble study with agitated saline; positive if microbubbles cross | Right-to-left shunt detection; guides PFO closure decision |
| Microembolic signals | High-intensity transient signals (HITS) | Embolic source monitoring, assessing anticoagulation adequacy |
| Sickle cell disease | Elevated MCA velocities | STOP trial: elevated velocities predict stroke risk in children |
| Intracranial stenosis | Focal velocity elevation | Screening; confirmed by CTA or MRA |
| Cerebral autoregulation | Impaired dynamic autoregulation | Research application; may guide BP management |

**Limitations of TCD:**
- Absent temporal acoustic window in 10-15% of patients (more common in elderly women and African Americans)
- Operator-dependent
- Cannot visualize vessel walls or morphology
- Limited to large intracranial vessels

### 6.8 Echocardiography: TTE vs. TEE

**[BOARD]**

Echocardiography is essential for identifying cardiac sources of embolism in stroke patients. The choice between transthoracic echocardiography (TTE) and transesophageal echocardiography (TEE) depends on the suspected mechanism and the clinical scenario.

**TTE vs. TEE Comparison:**

| Feature | TTE (Transthoracic) | TEE (Transesophageal) |
|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Invasiveness | Non-invasive | Semi-invasive (requires sedation, esophageal probe) |
| Image quality | Good for ventricles, valves | Superior for atria, septum, aortic arch |
| LV thrombus detection | Good (apical views) | Less optimal for LV apex |
| LA/LAA thrombus | Poor sensitivity | Gold standard (sensitivity >95%) |
| PFO/ASD detection | Moderate (with bubble study) | Superior (gold standard) |
| Aortic arch atheroma | Limited | Excellent |
| Valvular vegetations | Good for large vegetations | Superior for small vegetations |
| Availability | Widely available | Requires specialist, scheduled |
| Patient tolerance | Well tolerated | Requires sedation; aspiration risk |

**Indications for TEE Over TTE:**

- Young stroke patient (under 60) with cryptogenic stroke (looking for PFO, small ASD)
- Suspected LA appendage thrombus (TTE cannot reliably visualize LAA)
- Evaluation of aortic arch atheroma
- Suspected endocarditis with negative TTE
- Pre-cardioversion assessment for AF

> **Board Pearl:** TTE is the initial cardiac evaluation for most stroke patients. TEE is superior for detecting PFO, atrial septal aneurysm, LA appendage thrombus, and aortic arch atheroma. In young patients with cryptogenic stroke, TEE with agitated saline (bubble study) is the gold standard for PFO detection.

### 6.9 Cardiac Rhythm Monitoring: Holter vs. Implantable Loop Recorder

**[BOARD]**

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common cardiac source of embolism and is responsible for approximately 15-25% of all ischemic strokes. Many patients have paroxysmal AF that is not detected on standard telemetry or 12-lead ECG during hospitalization.

**Cardiac Monitoring Options:**

| Modality | Duration | AF Detection Rate | Best For |
|----------|----------|-------------------|---------|
| 12-lead ECG | Snapshot | Low (captures only if AF present at time of recording) | Initial screening; all stroke patients |
| Continuous inpatient telemetry | 24-72 hours (hospital stay) | 2-7% | Acute stroke care |
| 24-hour Holter monitor | 24 hours | 2-5% | Frequent symptoms; initial outpatient monitoring |
| 48-72 hour Holter | 48-72 hours | 5-10% | Slightly better yield |
| 30-day event recorder | 30 days | 12-16% | Cryptogenic stroke workup |
| Implantable loop recorder (ILR) | Up to 3 years | 12-30% (at 6-12 months) | Cryptogenic stroke; highest yield for paroxysmal AF |

**Key Trial: CRYSTAL-AF (2014)**

The CRYSTAL-AF trial demonstrated that an implantable cardiac monitor (Reveal LINQ) detected AF in 12.4% of cryptogenic stroke patients at 12 months, compared with only 2.0% in the conventional monitoring group. AF detection continued to increase with longer monitoring (30% at 3 years).

> **Board Pearl:** Implantable loop recorders (ILRs) have the highest yield for detecting paroxysmal AF in cryptogenic stroke, detecting approximately 6 times more AF than conventional monitoring over 12 months. Given that AF detection changes management from antiplatelet to anticoagulation therapy, prolonged monitoring is recommended for cryptogenic stroke patients.

**When to Pursue Extended Monitoring:**

- Cryptogenic stroke in patients over 55 (higher AF prevalence)
- Ischemic stroke pattern suggestive of cardioembolism (cortical wedge-shaped, multiterritorial)
- Enlarged left atrium on echocardiography
- Frequent premature atrial complexes on initial ECG
- Embolic stroke of undetermined source (ESUS)

### 6.10 NIHSS Scoring

**[BOARD]**

The National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale (NIHSS) is the most widely used clinical assessment tool for quantifying stroke severity. It is validated for clinical practice and research, is the strongest single predictor of short-term stroke outcome, and is used to guide treatment decisions including thrombolysis and thrombectomy eligibility.

**NIHSS Components (11 Categories, Score 0-42):**

| Item | Domain Assessed | Score Range | Key Testing Points |
|------|----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1a | Level of Consciousness (LOC) | 0-3 | Alert, drowsy, stuporous, coma |
| 1b | LOC Questions (month and age) | 0-2 | Must be EXACT answers; close does not count |
| 1c | LOC Commands (open/close eyes, grip/release) | 0-2 | Pantomime acceptable |
| 2 | Best Gaze (horizontal eye movement) | 0-2 | Forced deviation vs. partial gaze palsy |
| 3 | Visual Fields | 0-3 | Confrontation testing; complete hemianopia = 2, bilateral = 3 |
| 4 | Facial Palsy | 0-3 | Symmetry of facial movement; flattened nasolabial fold |
| 5 | Motor Arm (each side separately: 5a left, 5b right) | 0-4 each | Drift at 10 seconds counts; 4 = no movement |
| 6 | Motor Leg (each side separately: 6a left, 6b right) | 0-4 each | Drift at 5 seconds counts; 4 = no movement |
| 7 | Limb Ataxia | 0-2 | Finger-nose-finger, heel-shin; must be out of proportion to weakness |
| 8 | Sensory | 0-2 | Pinprick; mild vs. severe loss |
| 9 | Best Language | 0-3 | Naming, reading, describing; 3 = global aphasia or mute |
| 10 | Dysarthria | 0-2 | Read or repeat words; clarity of articulation |
| 11 | Extinction/Inattention | 0-2 | Visual, tactile, or auditory double simultaneous stimulation |

**NIHSS Severity Classification:**

| Score | Severity | Typical Outcome |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| 0-4 | Minor stroke | Good recovery likely; DAPT may be appropriate |
| 5-9 | Moderate | Variable; tPA generally indicated |
| 10-15 | Moderate-severe | tPA indicated; evaluate for thrombectomy |
| 16-20 | Severe | High probability of death or severe disability |
| 21-42 | Very severe | Very high mortality; mRS 4-6 in most survivors |

**NIHSS Scoring Principles:**

1. **Score what you SEE, not what you think:** If a patient cannot perform a test due to amputation or joint fusion, score that item as untestable (UN) rather than normal
2. **First response counts:** Do not coach or repeat instructions (except for LOC commands)
3. **Each NIHSS point reduction improves outcome:** Each additional point decreases odds of excellent outcome (mRS 0-1) by approximately 17%
4. **Three most predictive items:** Facial paresis, arm drift/weakness, and abnormal speech
5. **Time to administer:** Approximately 6-8 minutes for a trained examiner

> **Board Pearl:** The NIHSS does NOT adequately capture posterior circulation deficits. A patient with a basilar artery occlusion causing locked-in syndrome, vertigo, dysphagia, and bilateral motor deficits may have a deceptively low NIHSS score because the scale is heavily weighted toward anterior circulation and left hemisphere functions. Similarly, the NIHSS does not assess balance.

**NIHSS and Treatment Decisions:**

| NIHSS Range | Typical Treatment Consideration |
|-------------|-------------------------------|
| 0-3 | DAPT preferred over tPA for minor non-disabling stroke (PRISMS, ARAMIS trials) |
| 4-5 | Thrombolysis generally indicated; individualized decision |
| 6 or greater | Strong indication for thrombolysis if within time window |
| 10 or greater with LVO | Thrombectomy candidate |

### 6.11 Hypercoagulable Workup Indications

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

A hypercoagulable workup is NOT routinely indicated for all stroke patients. Most ischemic strokes have identifiable conventional risk factors (hypertension, diabetes, atrial fibrillation, carotid stenosis). However, certain clinical scenarios warrant evaluation for an underlying thrombophilia.

**Indications for Hypercoagulable Evaluation:**

| Clinical Scenario | Rationale |
|-------------------|-----------|
| Young stroke patient (under 50) without conventional risk factors | Higher likelihood of underlying thrombophilia |
| Cryptogenic stroke after comprehensive workup | Standard etiologies excluded |
| History of recurrent venous or arterial thrombotic events | Suggests systemic thrombophilia |
| Family history of thrombophilia | Hereditary conditions |
| Stroke in the setting of pregnancy or oral contraceptive use | Hormonal prothrombotic states |
| Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis | Strong association with thrombophilia |
| Multiple simultaneous infarcts in different vascular territories | Suggests hypercoagulable or embolic etiology |
| Livedo reticularis or other systemic signs of autoimmune disease | Antiphospholipid syndrome |

**Hypercoagulable Panel Components:**

| Test | Condition Detected | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| Antiphospholipid antibodies (anticardiolipin, anti-beta-2-glycoprotein I, lupus anticoagulant) | Antiphospholipid syndrome | Most important thrombophilia to identify; requires confirmation at 12 weeks; warfarin is preferred anticoagulant (NOT DOACs) |
| Factor V Leiden mutation | Activated protein C resistance | Most common hereditary thrombophilia; primarily venous thrombosis |
| Prothrombin G20210A mutation | Prothrombin gene mutation | Second most common; primarily venous thrombosis |
| Protein C activity | Protein C deficiency | Acute stroke and warfarin can lower levels (test in stable state) |
| Protein S activity (free) | Protein S deficiency | Same caveats as protein C |
| Antithrombin III activity | Antithrombin deficiency | Heparin use can lower levels |
| Homocysteine level | Hyperhomocysteinemia | B-vitamin supplementation reduces levels but has NOT been shown to reduce stroke recurrence |
| Sickle cell screening | Sickle cell disease | Especially in African American patients; stroke occurs in 11% of sickle cell patients by age 20 |

> **Board Pearl:** Antiphospholipid syndrome is the most clinically significant acquired thrombophilia associated with ischemic stroke. It requires positive antibodies on two occasions at least 12 weeks apart plus a clinical thrombotic event. Treatment is with warfarin (INR 2-3); DOACs are NOT recommended based on the TRAPS trial, which showed higher thrombotic event rates with rivaroxaban compared to warfarin in antiphospholipid syndrome.

**Timing Considerations:**

Many coagulation tests are affected by the acute stroke itself, anticoagulation therapy, or vitamin K antagonists. Ideally:
- Protein C, Protein S, and Antithrombin III should be tested when the patient is NOT on anticoagulation and NOT in the acute phase
- Lupus anticoagulant testing requires avoidance of heparin and warfarin
- Factor V Leiden and prothrombin gene mutations (DNA-based tests) are NOT affected by acute illness or medications

### 6.12 Additional Diagnostic Studies

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

**Lumbar Puncture:**

Lumbar puncture (LP) is indicated when subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) is clinically suspected but NCCT is negative. CT sensitivity for SAH decreases with time (approaching 98% in the first 6 hours but declining to approximately 86% at 24 hours and lower thereafter).

- **Xanthochromia** (yellow discoloration of CSF) indicates prior subarachnoid bleeding and takes approximately 6-12 hours to develop
- Elevated red blood cell count that does NOT clear across sequential tubes also suggests SAH
- LP should be performed only after CT to exclude a space-occupying lesion that could cause herniation

**Laboratory Studies in Acute Stroke:**

| Test | Timing | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| Finger-stick glucose | BEFORE tPA | ONLY mandatory pre-tPA test; hypoglycemia mimics stroke |
| CBC with platelets | Concurrent with treatment | Thrombocytopenia (<100,000) is relative tPA contraindication |
| PT/INR | Concurrent | Elevated INR (>1.7) is tPA contraindication |
| aPTT | Concurrent | If on heparin therapy |
| Troponin | Acute workup | Stroke can cause troponin elevation (neurogenic stunned myocardium) |
| BMP/CMP | Acute workup | Electrolyte abnormalities; renal function for contrast |
| HbA1c | During hospitalization | Identifies undiagnosed diabetes or poor glucose control |
| Lipid panel (fasting) | During hospitalization | Guides statin therapy |
| ESR/CRP | If vasculitis suspected | Giant cell arteritis, CNS vasculitis |
| Urine drug screen | If clinical suspicion | Cocaine and amphetamine use cause stroke |
| Pregnancy test | Women of childbearing age | Influences treatment decisions |

**Conventional (Catheter) Angiography:**

Digital subtraction angiography (DSA) remains the gold standard for vascular imaging, though it is now rarely needed purely for diagnostic purposes given advances in CTA and MRA. It is primarily used for:
- Therapeutic intervention (thrombectomy, coiling, stenting)
- Definitive evaluation of vasculitis
- Complex aneurysm characterization
- AVM grading (Spetzler-Martin classification)

The procedure carries a 0.5-1% risk of stroke, making it unsuitable as a routine screening test.

| Clinical Scenario | First-Line Imaging | Additional Studies |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Acute stroke, 0-4.5 hours | NCCT + CTA | CT perfusion if thrombectomy candidate |
| Acute stroke, 4.5-24 hours | NCCT + CTA + CTP (or multimodal MRI) | Mandatory perfusion imaging |
| Wake-up stroke, unknown onset | MRI with DWI + FLAIR | DWI-FLAIR mismatch guides treatment |
| Suspected posterior circulation stroke | MRI preferred (superior to CT for posterior fossa) | MRA for vertebrobasilar assessment |
| TIA evaluation | MRI with DWI (may reclassify TIA as stroke) | CTA or MRA of head/neck; echocardiography |
| Young stroke, cryptogenic | MRI + MRA | TEE with bubble study; hypercoagulable panel |
| Suspected SAH with negative CT | Lumbar puncture | CTA or DSA if confirmed |

### Etiologic Workup Checklist by Stroke Mechanism

| Mechanism | Key Diagnostic Studies |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Large artery atherosclerosis | CTA/MRA of head and neck; carotid duplex ultrasound |
| Cardioembolism | ECG, telemetry, TTE/TEE, prolonged cardiac monitoring |
| Small vessel disease (lacunar) | MRI with DWI; BP monitoring; HbA1c; lipids |
| Other determined etiology | Vasculitis workup, hypercoagulable panel, dissection imaging |
| Cryptogenic/ESUS | All of the above; ILR for occult AF; TEE for PFO |

## Board-Style Review Questions

**Question 1:** A 62-year-old man presents with sudden onset left hemiparesis 2 hours ago. A non-contrast CT of the head is normal. What is the MOST appropriate next step?

*Answer:* A normal CT does NOT rule out ischemic stroke. The patient remains a candidate for IV thrombolysis if he meets other criteria. Proceed with CTA and evaluate for tPA eligibility. NCCT was obtained to exclude hemorrhage, which is its primary role in the acute setting.

**Question 2:** A patient undergoes CT perfusion showing a core volume of 15 mL and a penumbra volume of 85 mL (mismatch ratio 5.7:1) at 10 hours after symptom onset. What treatment should be considered?

*Answer:* With a large perfusion-core mismatch (ratio far exceeding the 1.8 threshold) and small core volume at 10 hours, this patient meets criteria for mechanical thrombectomy per DEFUSE-3 criteria (6-16 hours, mismatch ratio of 1.8 or greater, core <70 mL).

**Question 3:** A 45-year-old woman with cryptogenic stroke has a negative TTE with bubble study. What additional cardiac evaluation is most appropriate?

*Answer:* TEE with agitated saline (bubble study) is the gold standard for PFO detection and is superior to TTE. TEE also evaluates the LA appendage and aortic arch, which are inadequately visualized on TTE. Additionally, an implantable loop recorder should be considered for prolonged AF monitoring.
