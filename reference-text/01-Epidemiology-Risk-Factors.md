## Section 1: Defining Stroke and Transient Ischemic Attack

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Stroke, also referred to as cerebrovascular accident (CVA), is fundamentally an interruption of blood supply to the brain that produces neurological deficits. There are two major pathological categories: ischemic stroke (approximately 87% of cases), in which arterial occlusion deprives neural tissue of blood flow; and hemorrhagic stroke (approximately 13%), in which a vessel ruptures and blood enters the parenchyma or subarachnoid space.

The World Health Organization defines stroke as rapidly developing clinical signs of focal or global disturbance of cerebral function lasting more than 24 hours or leading to death, with no apparent cause other than vascular origin. This traditional time-based definition has been central to clinical practice for decades.

**Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA)** has undergone a critical definitional evolution:

- **Time-based definition (classic):** A TIA is a focal neurological deficit caused by vascular insufficiency that resolves completely within 24 hours. In practice, most TIAs resolve within 60 minutes.
- **Tissue-based definition (modern):** A TIA is a transient episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischemia *without* acute infarction on imaging. Under this definition, if diffusion-weighted MRI (DWI) demonstrates restricted diffusion (indicating infarction), the event is classified as a stroke regardless of whether symptoms resolve.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The tissue-based definition of TIA is now preferred in clinical practice and research. Approximately 30-50% of patients with symptoms lasting less than 24 hours will have evidence of infarction on DWI MRI. This means many events historically classified as TIAs were in fact completed strokes by modern imaging criteria.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The shift toward a tissue-based definition reflects the growing recognition that symptom duration alone is an unreliable indicator of permanent tissue injury. Advances in neuroimaging, particularly DWI MRI, have demonstrated that many patients with brief, fully resolving symptoms still sustain permanent parenchymal injury. This has profound implications for secondary prevention strategies and prognostication.

The ischemic penumbra concept is central to understanding both stroke and TIA pathophysiology. When cerebral blood flow drops below approximately 20 mL/100g/min, neurons become electrically silent but remain structurally viable. Below approximately 10 mL/100g/min, irreversible injury begins within minutes. The penumbra represents the zone between these thresholds and is the target of all acute reperfusion therapies. Normal cerebral blood flow is approximately 50-60 mL/100g/min.

| Term | Definition | Key Feature |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Stroke | Sudden interruption of blood supply to brain causing neurological deficits lasting >24 hours or death | Permanent tissue injury on imaging |
| TIA (time-based) | Focal neurological deficit resolving within 24 hours | Symptom-based classification |
| TIA (tissue-based) | Transient neurological dysfunction without acute infarction on imaging | Imaging-based classification |
| Penumbra | Ischemic tissue surrounding infarct core (CBF 10-20 mL/100g/min) | Potentially salvageable tissue |
| Infarct core | Irreversibly damaged tissue (CBF <10 mL/100g/min) | Not salvageable |

> **Clinical Pearl:** TIA is a neurological emergency, not a benign event. The 90-day stroke risk after TIA is 10-20% without treatment, with the highest risk in the first 48 hours. Urgent evaluation and secondary prevention reduce this risk by up to 80%.

## Section 2: Epidemiology of Stroke

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Stroke epidemiology represents one of the most frequently tested topics on PM&R board examinations. The following statistics should be committed to memory:

**Incidence and Prevalence:**
- Approximately 795,000 people in the United States experience a stroke each year
- Of these, 610,000 are first-ever (new) strokes and 185,000 are recurrent strokes
- Someone in the United States has a stroke every 40 seconds
- Someone dies from a stroke every 3 minutes and 14 seconds
- Approximately 7.2 million Americans over age 20 have had a stroke
- The lifetime risk of stroke is approximately 25% for adults aged 25 and older

**Mortality Rankings:**
- Stroke is the 5th leading cause of death in the United States (per AHA 2024 statistics; note that 2023 CDC mortality data show stroke has risen back to 4th, a ranking that may be reflected in future AHA updates)
- Stroke is the 2nd most common cause of death worldwide
- Stroke is the number 1 leading cause of long-term adult disability in the United States

**Stroke Subtypes by Frequency:**

| Stroke Category | Percentage of All Strokes | Subtype Details |
|----------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| Ischemic stroke | 87% | Thrombotic (~48% of ischemic), Embolic (~26% of ischemic), Lacunar (~15-26% of ischemic) |
| Hemorrhagic stroke | 13% | ICH (~10% of all strokes), SAH (~3% of all strokes) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The 87/13 split between ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke is a consistently tested figure. Note that globally, the proportions differ: approximately 65% ischemic, 29% ICH, and 6% SAH. The US has a higher proportion of ischemic strokes relative to hemorrhagic strokes compared with many other countries.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Mortality and Morbidity Outcomes:**

The burden of stroke extends far beyond acute mortality. At 6 months following stroke, residual deficits are common:

| Deficit | Prevalence at 6 Months |
|---------|----------------------|
| Hemiparesis | 50% |
| Cognitive deficits | 46% |
| Depression | 35% |
| Unable to walk independently | 31% |
| Institutionalized | 26% |
| Hemianopia | 20% |
| Aphasia | 19% |

The 30-day case fatality rate varies from 13.5% to 23% depending on the population studied, with hemorrhagic strokes carrying substantially higher acute mortality (30-50% for ICH) compared with ischemic strokes.

**Recovery and Outcomes:**
- Approximately 50-70% of stroke survivors regain functional independence
- Only about 10% achieve near-complete recovery
- Approximately 25% recover with minor impairment
- 40% experience moderate to severe lasting disability
- Return to work occurs in approximately 50% of patients at 6-12 months
- Approximately 15-30% of stroke survivors are permanently disabled

**Trends in Stroke Epidemiology:**

Stroke mortality has declined significantly over the past 50 years owing to improvements in acute management, secondary prevention, and risk factor control. However, several concerning trends have emerged:

1. Stroke incidence in younger adults (ages 18-50) is increasing, driven by rising rates of obesity, diabetes, and hypertension in younger populations. CDC data indicate that stroke rates among adults younger than 65 increased by approximately 15% over the past decade.
2. After declining between 2002 and 2012, the stroke death rate among people ages 45-64 increased through 2021 before declining again in 2022.
3. Overall stroke prevalence rose by nearly 8% from the period 2011-2013 compared with 2020-2022 based on Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System data.

## Section 3: The Stroke Belt

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The Stroke Belt is a geographic region in the southeastern United States characterized by substantially elevated stroke incidence and mortality compared with the rest of the nation. This concept is regularly tested on board examinations.

**Key Facts:**
- The Stroke Belt has approximately 30% higher stroke mortality than the national average
- It includes the following states: North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Tennessee, Arkansas, and Louisiana
- The "Stroke Buckle" refers to the coastal regions of the Carolinas and Georgia, where stroke mortality is even more elevated (up to 40% above the national average)

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Multiple factors contribute to the geographic concentration of stroke in the Stroke Belt:

| Contributing Factor | Explanation |
|--------------------|-------------|
| Hypertension prevalence | Higher rates of uncontrolled hypertension in the southeastern US |
| Diabetes prevalence | Higher rates of type 2 diabetes, driven partly by obesity |
| Obesity | Higher rates of obesity, related to dietary patterns |
| Smoking rates | Historically higher tobacco use |
| Dietary patterns | Higher intake of sodium, saturated fat, and fried foods |
| Socioeconomic factors | Lower income, lower educational attainment, limited access to healthcare |
| Access to stroke centers | Fewer comprehensive stroke centers per capita in some rural areas |
| Racial composition | Higher proportion of African American residents, who have disproportionately elevated stroke risk |

African Americans living within the Stroke Belt carry the highest stroke mortality rates in the nation, reflecting the compounding of geographic and racial risk factors. The REGARDS (Reasons for Geographic and Racial Differences in Stroke) study has been the primary longitudinal cohort study investigating these disparities.

## Section 4: Modifiable Risk Factors

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Hypertension** is the single most important modifiable risk factor for both ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke. Chronic hypertension promotes atherosclerosis of large vessels, lipohyalinosis of small penetrating arteries (leading to lacunar infarcts), and formation of Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms (predisposing to hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage). The relationship between blood pressure and stroke risk is continuous and graded, with risk approximately doubling for each 20 mmHg increase in systolic blood pressure above 115 mmHg.

| Modifiable Risk Factor | Relative Risk Increase | Key Details |
|----------------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Hypertension | 3-5x | Most important modifiable risk factor; contributes to both ischemic and hemorrhagic stroke |
| Atrial fibrillation | 5x | Responsible for approximately 15-20% of all ischemic strokes; risk increases with age |
| Diabetes mellitus | 1.5-3x | Accelerates atherosclerosis; associated with small vessel disease |
| Smoking | 2x | Dose-dependent; risk decreases after cessation |
| Hyperlipidemia | 1.5-2x | Primarily through acceleration of atherosclerosis |
| Carotid stenosis | Variable | Depends on degree of stenosis and symptom status |
| Physical inactivity | 1.5x | Sedentary lifestyle is an independent risk factor |
| Obesity | 1.5-2x | Contributes through multiple pathways (HTN, DM, dyslipidemia) |
| Excessive alcohol use | 1.5-2x | Heavy use increases risk; light-moderate may have complex relationship |
| Sleep-disordered breathing | 2-3x | Obstructive sleep apnea is an independent risk factor |
| Illicit drug use | Variable | Cocaine and amphetamines cause acute hypertensive surges |

> **Clinical Pearl:** For board exams, remember the hierarchy: Hypertension is the number one modifiable risk factor. Atrial fibrillation confers a 5-fold increased risk and is the most common cardiac source of embolism. Among non-modifiable risk factors, age is number one.

**Atrial Fibrillation and Stroke:**

Atrial fibrillation (AF) deserves special emphasis as a stroke risk factor because it is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, its prevalence increases dramatically with age, and it is the most important treatable cause of cardioembolic stroke.

In AF, blood stasis within the left atrial appendage promotes thrombus formation. These thrombi can embolize to the cerebral circulation, with approximately 75% of cardiac emboli traveling to the brain. Embolic strokes from AF tend to be larger and carry higher mortality than non-cardioembolic strokes.

**Detection of Occult AF:**

Extended cardiac monitoring has demonstrated that many strokes classified as cryptogenic are in fact due to paroxysmal AF that was missed on standard monitoring:

| Study | Monitoring Method | AF Detection Rate | Control Rate |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| CRYSTAL AF | Implantable cardiac monitor | 8.9% at 6 months | 1.4% |
| EMBRACE | 30-day event-triggered loop recorder | 16.1% | 3.2% |
| STROKE-AF | Implantable cardiac monitor | 12.1% | 1.8% |

These findings underscore the importance of prolonged cardiac monitoring after cryptogenic stroke, as detection of AF changes management from antiplatelet therapy to anticoagulation.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Hypertension Management for Stroke Prevention:**

The 2024 AHA/ASA guideline for primary prevention of stroke emphasizes Life's Essential 8, which builds upon the earlier Life's Simple 7 by adding sleep health as a core component of cardiovascular wellness. The guideline provides updated blood pressure targets and treatment recommendations.

For primary stroke prevention, optimal blood pressure control targets systolic blood pressure below 130 mmHg in most adults. For secondary prevention (after a stroke has occurred), the evidence supports a target of less than 130/80 mmHg for most patients, though individualization is required.

**Diabetes and Stroke:**

Diabetes mellitus increases stroke risk through multiple mechanisms: acceleration of large vessel atherosclerosis, promotion of small vessel lipohyalinosis (increasing lacunar infarct risk), endothelial dysfunction, and a pro-thrombotic state. The 2024 guidelines provide a Class 1A recommendation for the use of GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, dulaglutide) in patients with diabetes and high-risk or established cardiovascular disease to reduce stroke risk, based on the SUSTAIN-6 and REWIND trials.

**Carotid Stenosis:**

Carotid artery atherosclerosis is both a marker of systemic vascular disease and a direct cause of ischemic stroke through artery-to-artery embolism or thrombotic occlusion. Management depends on symptom status and degree of stenosis:

| Clinical Scenario | Stenosis Threshold | Recommended Management |
|-------------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Symptomatic carotid stenosis | >=50% | Consider carotid endarterectomy (CEA) |
| Asymptomatic carotid stenosis | >=70% | Consider CEA (in selected patients with low surgical risk) |
| Any carotid stenosis | Any degree | Optimal medical therapy (antiplatelet, statin, BP control) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The distinction between symptomatic and asymptomatic carotid stenosis matters for surgical decision-making. Symptomatic stenosis at 50% or greater warrants consideration of CEA. Asymptomatic stenosis requires a higher threshold (70% or greater) before surgery is considered, reflecting the lower absolute risk reduction in asymptomatic patients.

**Smoking:**

Cigarette smoking approximately doubles the risk of ischemic stroke in a dose-dependent manner. The risk declines substantially after cessation, returning to near-baseline levels within 5 years of quitting. Mechanisms include endothelial injury, promotion of atherosclerosis, increased fibrinogen and platelet aggregability, and polycythemia.

**Hyperlipidemia:**

Elevated low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol promotes atherosclerosis throughout the vasculature, including the cerebral arteries. Statin therapy reduces stroke risk by approximately 15-30%, with greater reductions in patients with higher baseline cardiovascular risk. The 2024 guidelines emphasize lipid-lowering therapy as a key component of primary stroke prevention.

## Section 5: Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Age** is the single most important non-modifiable risk factor for stroke. Stroke risk approximately doubles with each decade of life after age 55. While stroke can occur at any age (including pediatric populations), the incidence rises exponentially with advancing age.

| Non-Modifiable Risk Factor | Key Details |
|---------------------------|-------------|
| Age | Most important non-modifiable risk factor; risk doubles each decade after 55 |
| Sex | Men have higher incidence at younger ages; women have higher lifetime risk and higher mortality |
| Race/Ethnicity | African Americans have nearly 2x the risk of first stroke vs. White Americans |
| Family history | Parental history of stroke increases risk approximately 2-fold |
| Genetics | Monogenic causes exist (CADASIL, sickle cell disease) but are uncommon |
| Prior stroke/TIA | History of prior cerebrovascular event substantially increases recurrent risk |

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Sex Differences in Stroke:**

The relationship between sex and stroke is nuanced. Men have a higher age-adjusted incidence of stroke, particularly in younger age groups. However, women have a higher lifetime risk of stroke (owing largely to greater longevity), higher stroke mortality, and poorer functional outcomes after stroke. Women also have unique risk factors including pregnancy, preeclampsia, oral contraceptive use, hormone replacement therapy, and migraine with aura (particularly when combined with smoking and oral contraceptive use). The 2024 AHA/ASA guidelines introduced new sex-specific screening and prevention recommendations, acknowledging these disparities.

**Racial and Ethnic Disparities:**

Stroke disparities by race and ethnicity are among the most significant health inequities in the United States:

- African Americans have approximately twice the risk of first-ever stroke compared with White Americans
- African Americans have 2-3 times higher stroke death rates compared with White Americans
- Hispanic Americans also have elevated stroke risk, particularly for ICH
- The Northern Manhattan Study (NOMAS) demonstrated that the relative rate of intracranial atherosclerosis in Black versus White individuals is approximately 5.85 times higher
- African Americans are more likely to have small vessel disease and intracranial atherosclerosis as stroke mechanisms, whereas White Americans more commonly have extracranial carotid disease

These disparities are driven by a combination of higher prevalence of traditional risk factors (hypertension, diabetes, obesity), socioeconomic factors (income, education, healthcare access), geographic factors (concentration in the Stroke Belt), and potentially genetic susceptibility.

**Genetic Factors:**

While the vast majority of strokes result from acquired risk factors, several genetic conditions predispose to stroke:

- **CADASIL** (Cerebral Autosomal Dominant Arteriopathy with Subcortical Infarcts and Leukoencephalopathy): the most common monogenic cause of cerebral small vessel disease; caused by NOTCH3 gene mutations on chromosome 19; presents with migraine with aura, recurrent subcortical lacunar infarcts, progressive cognitive decline, and mood disturbances
- **Sickle cell disease:** Carries a very high stroke risk, particularly in children; transcranial Doppler screening identifies high-risk children who benefit from chronic transfusion therapy
- **Fabry disease:** X-linked lysosomal storage disorder that can cause stroke through small vessel vasculopathy
- **Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (vascular type):** Predisposes to arterial dissection

## Section 6: Risk Stratification Scores

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

#### CHA2DS2-VASc Score

The CHA2DS2-VASc score is used to estimate the annual stroke risk in patients with non-valvular atrial fibrillation. It guides decisions regarding anticoagulation therapy. This score is one of the most commonly tested clinical tools on board examinations.

| Component | Points | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **C** - Congestive heart failure | 1 | History of heart failure or reduced LVEF |
| **H** - Hypertension | 1 | History of hypertension or on antihypertensive therapy |
| **A2** - Age >= 75 years | 2 | Two points for age 75 or older |
| **D** - Diabetes mellitus | 1 | History of diabetes or on hypoglycemic therapy |
| **S2** - Stroke/TIA/thromboembolism | 2 | Two points for prior stroke, TIA, or systemic embolism |
| **V** - Vascular disease | 1 | Prior MI, peripheral artery disease, or aortic plaque |
| **A** - Age 65-74 years | 1 | One point for age 65-74 |
| **Sc** - Sex category (female) | 1 | Female sex |

**Maximum score: 9 points**

| CHA2DS2-VASc Score | Annual Stroke Risk | Recommended Therapy |
|--------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 0 (male) or 1 (female) | ~0-0.6% | No antithrombotic therapy (or aspirin) |
| 1 (male) | ~1.3% | Consider oral anticoagulation |
| 2 or higher | ~2.2-15.2% | Oral anticoagulation recommended |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The mnemonic for CHA2DS2-VASc: Congestive heart failure, Hypertension, Age >=75 (2 points), Diabetes, Stroke/TIA (2 points), Vascular disease, Age 65-74, Sex category (female). Remember that prior stroke/TIA and age >=75 each receive 2 points, reflecting their strong association with stroke risk.

#### ABCD2 Score

The ABCD2 score estimates the short-term risk of stroke following a TIA. It is used to triage patients and determine the urgency of workup and treatment.

| Component | Criteria | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **A** - Age | >= 60 years | 1 |
| **B** - Blood pressure | SBP >= 140 or DBP >= 90 at presentation | 1 |
| **C** - Clinical features | Unilateral weakness | 2 |
| | Speech impairment without weakness | 1 |
| | Other symptoms | 0 |
| **D** - Duration | >= 60 minutes | 2 |
| | 10-59 minutes | 1 |
| | < 10 minutes | 0 |
| **D2** - Diabetes | Present | 1 |

**Maximum score: 7 points**

| ABCD2 Score | 2-Day Stroke Risk | 7-Day Stroke Risk | Risk Category |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|--------------|
| 0-3 | ~1.0% | ~1.2% | Low risk |
| 4-5 | ~4.1% | ~5.9% | Moderate risk |
| 6-7 | ~8.1% | ~11.7% | High risk |

> **Clinical Pearl:** An ABCD2 score of 4 or higher traditionally warrants urgent evaluation and admission for monitoring. However, current guidelines emphasize that all TIA patients should receive urgent workup regardless of ABCD2 score, as even low-scoring patients can have significant underlying pathology (such as high-grade carotid stenosis or intracardiac thrombus). The score should not be used in isolation to discharge patients without workup.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Limitations of Risk Scores:**

The ABCD2 score, while useful for risk stratification, has important limitations. It does not account for imaging findings (carotid stenosis, intracranial occlusion, DWI positivity), which are among the strongest predictors of early recurrent stroke. A normal ABCD2 score does not exclude the need for urgent workup. Some centers have moved toward imaging-based triage rather than relying primarily on clinical scores.

The CHA2DS2-VASc score performs well as a population-level predictor but has modest discriminative ability for individual patients. It is primarily useful for identifying patients at very low risk (in whom anticoagulation may be safely withheld) and those at moderate-to-high risk (in whom anticoagulation is clearly beneficial).

**HAS-BLED Score:**

The HAS-BLED score (Hypertension, Abnormal renal/liver function, Stroke, Bleeding history, Labile INR, Elderly, Drugs/alcohol) is used alongside CHA2DS2-VASc to assess bleeding risk in patients being considered for anticoagulation. A high HAS-BLED score does not contraindicate anticoagulation; rather, it identifies patients who need closer monitoring and modification of reversible bleeding risk factors.

## Section 7: Economic Burden of Stroke

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

- Estimated annual cost of stroke in the United States: approximately $34-56 billion (direct medical costs plus lost productivity)
- Average lifetime cost per ischemic stroke patient: $140,000-$225,000
- Stroke accounts for the largest proportion of Medicare expenditures for neurological disease
- Cost is projected to increase substantially as the population ages

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

The economic burden of stroke encompasses both direct medical costs (acute hospitalization, rehabilitation, skilled nursing facility care, outpatient services, medications, durable medical equipment) and indirect costs (lost wages, reduced productivity, caregiver burden, premature death). Indirect costs frequently exceed direct costs, though they are more difficult to quantify.

The high cost of stroke care reflects the complexity and chronicity of post-stroke disability. Inpatient rehabilitation alone can cost $15,000-$30,000 per admission. Many stroke survivors require ongoing assistance with activities of daily living, contributing to the high cost of long-term care.

From a population health perspective, stroke prevention is far more cost-effective than acute treatment and rehabilitation. Treating hypertension with inexpensive generic medications, providing anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation, and promoting smoking cessation all offer substantial returns on investment relative to the cost of treating a completed stroke and its sequelae.

| Cost Category | Estimated Annual Cost (US) |
|--------------|---------------------------|
| Direct medical costs | $25-34 billion |
| Indirect costs (lost productivity) | $12-22 billion |
| Total estimated burden | $34-56 billion |
| Average lifetime cost per patient | $140,000-$225,000 |
| Projected 2030 total cost | Potentially tripling current estimates |

## Section 8: Stroke in Special Populations

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Stroke in Young Adults (Age < 45):**

Stroke in young adults deserves special attention because the etiologic spectrum differs from that of older adults. While atherosclerosis and atrial fibrillation dominate in older populations, younger stroke patients are more likely to have:

- **Arterial dissection:** Accounts for approximately 20% of strokes in patients under age 45; carotid and vertebral dissection can occur spontaneously or following trauma (even minor, such as chiropractic manipulation)
- **Patent foramen ovale (PFO):** Present in approximately 25% of the general population but found in up to 40-50% of young cryptogenic stroke patients; paradoxical embolism through the PFO is the proposed mechanism
- **Hypercoagulable states:** Antiphospholipid syndrome (most common acquired), Factor V Leiden, prothrombin gene mutation, protein C/S deficiency
- **Drug use:** Cocaine and amphetamines cause acute hypertensive surges and vasospasm
- **Cardiac sources:** Endocarditis, structural heart disease, arrhythmias
- **Vasculitis:** Primary CNS vasculitis, systemic vasculitis (SLE, polyarteritis nodosa)
- **Genetic conditions:** CADASIL, Fabry disease, sickle cell disease, mitochondrial disorders (MELAS)

The workup in young adults should be more extensive than in older patients, including hypercoagulable studies, echocardiography with bubble study, vessel wall imaging (MRI), and consideration of lumbar puncture if vasculitis is suspected.

**Stroke in Women:**

In addition to the standard risk factors, women have sex-specific risk factors:
- Pregnancy and the postpartum period
- Preeclampsia/eclampsia (increases future stroke risk 2-5 fold)
- Gestational diabetes
- Oral contraceptive use (particularly combined estrogen-progestin formulations)
- Hormone replacement therapy
- Migraine with aura (further elevated risk when combined with smoking and oral contraceptives)
- Higher prevalence of atrial fibrillation in elderly women

## Section 9: Primary vs. Secondary Prevention

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Primary prevention** refers to strategies aimed at preventing a first-ever stroke in individuals who have never had a cerebrovascular event. **Secondary prevention** refers to strategies aimed at preventing recurrent stroke in individuals who have already experienced a stroke or TIA.

| Prevention Level | Goal | Key Interventions |
|-----------------|------|-------------------|
| Primary prevention | Prevent first-ever stroke | Blood pressure control, statin therapy, AF management, smoking cessation, healthy diet, exercise |
| Secondary prevention | Prevent recurrent stroke | All primary prevention measures PLUS etiology-specific treatments (anticoagulation for cardioembolic, CEA for carotid stenosis, etc.) |

**Secondary Prevention by Stroke Etiology:**

| Stroke Etiology | Recommended Secondary Prevention |
|----------------|--------------------------------|
| Cardioembolic (AF) | Oral anticoagulation (warfarin with INR 2-3 or DOACs: rivaroxaban, apixaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) |
| Large artery atherosclerosis | Antiplatelet therapy + high-intensity statin + BP control; consider CEA if significant carotid stenosis |
| Small vessel/lacunar | Antiplatelet monotherapy + aggressive BP control; avoid long-term dual antiplatelet (SPS3 trial) |
| Cryptogenic | Extended cardiac monitoring to detect occult AF; antiplatelet therapy; consider PFO closure in selected young patients |
| Arterial dissection | Anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy for 3-6 months; most dissections heal spontaneously |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The SPS3 trial demonstrated that long-term dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin plus clopidogrel) is HARMFUL in lacunar stroke, increasing bleeding and mortality compared with aspirin alone. This is a commonly tested fact. Short-term DAPT (21-90 days) is beneficial after minor stroke or TIA, but long-term DAPT should be avoided for lacunar stroke.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**The 2024 AHA/ASA Primary Prevention of Stroke Guideline** represents a major update from the prior 2014 version. The guideline embraces the Life's Essential 8 framework and introduces updated recommendations in seven key domains:

1. **Dietary modification:** Mediterranean diet receives a strong (Class 1) recommendation; potassium salt substitution is now formally discussed
2. **GLP-1 receptor agonists:** Class 1A recommendation for patients with diabetes and high-risk or established cardiovascular disease
3. **Blood pressure targets:** Updated guidance on optimal BP goals
4. **Lipid-lowering medications:** Expanded indications for statin therapy
5. **Antithrombotic agents:** Refined recommendations for aspirin in primary prevention
6. **Colchicine therapy:** New consideration of anti-inflammatory strategies
7. **Sex-specific preventive considerations:** New recommendations addressing pregnancy-related risk, oral contraceptive use, and hormone therapy

The addition of sleep health to the core cardiovascular wellness framework (Life's Essential 8, updated from Life's Simple 7) reflects growing evidence linking sleep-disordered breathing to stroke risk.

## Section 10: Stroke Screening and Assessment Scales

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Prehospital Screening:**
- **FAST:** Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 911
- **BE-FAST:** Adds Balance and Eyes to the original FAST; now adopted by the majority of comprehensive stroke centers as it captures posterior circulation symptoms more effectively
- **CPSS:** Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale
- **LAPSS:** Los Angeles Prehospital Stroke Screen

**Large Vessel Occlusion (LVO) Triage:**
- **RACE scale:** Best reported area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) at 0.75
- **LAMS, C-STAT, FAST-ED:** Other scales used; no current LVO scale achieves both high sensitivity and high specificity simultaneously

**NIHSS (NIH Stroke Scale):**
- 11-item scale (with sub-items); score range 0-42
- The most widely used and validated acute stroke severity scale
- Used for tPA eligibility, prognosis, and serial monitoring

| NIHSS Score Range | Stroke Severity | Clinical Implications |
|-------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| 0 | No measurable deficit | May still have significant findings |
| 1-4 | Minor stroke | Good prognosis; generally not tPA candidates |
| 5-15 | Moderate stroke | tPA candidates within time window |
| 16-20 | Moderate-severe stroke | Higher risk of hemorrhagic transformation with tPA |
| 21-42 | Severe stroke | Significant mortality risk; NIHSS >25 has high risk of hemorrhagic transformation |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The NIHSS underrepresents posterior circulation deficits and non-dominant hemisphere findings. A patient with a devastating basilar artery occlusion may have a deceptively low NIHSS score. Similarly, significant neglect from a right hemisphere stroke may not be adequately captured. Each additional NIHSS point decreases the odds of an excellent outcome by approximately 17%.
