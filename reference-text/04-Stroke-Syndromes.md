## Section 1: Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) Syndrome

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The MCA is the most commonly affected artery in stroke and produces the most recognizable clinical syndrome on board examinations. The key to MCA stroke localization is understanding the division between the superior and inferior branches, and the difference between dominant and non-dominant hemisphere presentations.

**Complete MCA Occlusion (Mainstem):**

| Deficit | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| Contralateral hemiparesis (face/arm >> leg) | Motor cortex on lateral surface; leg is medial (ACA territory) |
| Contralateral hemisensory loss (face/arm >> leg) | Sensory cortex on lateral surface |
| Contralateral homonymous hemianopia | Optic radiations in temporal and parietal lobes |
| Head and eye deviation TOWARD the lesion | Frontal eye field damage; "eyes look at the lesion" in cortical strokes |
| Global aphasia (dominant hemisphere) | Both Broca's and Wernicke's areas destroyed |
| Hemispatial neglect (non-dominant hemisphere) | Right hemisphere involvement producing left-sided inattention |

**MCA Division Syndromes:**

| Branch | Territory | Dominant Hemisphere Deficits | Non-Dominant Hemisphere Deficits |
|--------|-----------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| Superior division | Frontal and anterior parietal cortex | Broca's aphasia (nonfluent), contralateral face/arm weakness and sensory loss | Contralateral face/arm weakness, motor aprosodia |
| Inferior division | Temporal and posterior parietal cortex | Wernicke's aphasia (fluent), contralateral homonymous hemianopia, Gerstmann syndrome | Left hemispatial neglect, constructional apraxia, dressing apraxia, anosognosia |
| Lenticulostriate branches | Basal ganglia, internal capsule | Pure motor hemiparesis (face = arm = leg), NO cortical signs | Pure motor hemiparesis (face = arm = leg), NO cortical signs |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The single most important distinction on board exams: Dominant MCA = aphasia. Non-dominant MCA = neglect. Left hemispatial neglect (from right hemisphere damage) is more common, more severe, and more persistent than right neglect. Patients with anosognosia (unawareness of deficit) often have poor rehabilitation outcomes because they do not recognize their impairments.

**Aphasia Types in MCA Stroke:**

| Aphasia Type | Lesion Location | Fluency | Comprehension | Repetition | Patient Affect |
|-------------|----------------|---------|---------------|-----------|----------------|
| Broca's (expressive) | Inferior frontal gyrus (Broca's area, Brodmann 44/45) | Non-fluent, effortful, telegraphic | Relatively preserved | Impaired | Frustrated, aware of deficit |
| Wernicke's (receptive) | Posterior superior temporal gyrus (Wernicke's area, Brodmann 22) | Fluent but meaningless (paraphasias, neologisms, "word salad") | Severely impaired | Impaired | Unaware of deficit, may appear unconcerned |
| Global | Large MCA territory (both Broca's and Wernicke's areas) | Non-fluent | Severely impaired | Impaired | Variable |
| Conduction | Arcuate fasciculus (connects Broca's to Wernicke's) | Fluent | Preserved | Impaired (disproportionately poor repetition) | Aware of errors, attempts self-correction |
| Transcortical motor | Supplementary motor area or watershed zone near Broca's | Non-fluent | Preserved | PRESERVED (key distinguishing feature) | Variable |
| Transcortical sensory | Watershed zone near Wernicke's | Fluent | Impaired | PRESERVED (key distinguishing feature) | Variable |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key to aphasia classification is the triad of fluency, comprehension, and repetition. The transcortical aphasias are distinguished from their Broca/Wernicke counterparts by PRESERVED repetition. Remember: transcortical = preserved repetition (the cortical language centers are intact; it is the connections to surrounding cortex that are disrupted).

**Gerstmann Syndrome:**

Gerstmann syndrome results from a lesion of the dominant (usually left) angular gyrus (Brodmann area 39), typically caused by an inferior division MCA stroke.

The four components (Gerstmann tetrad):
1. **Agraphia** -- inability to write
2. **Acalculia** -- inability to perform calculations
3. **Finger agnosia** -- inability to identify or distinguish individual fingers
4. **Left-right confusion** -- inability to distinguish left from right

> **Clinical Pearl:** If a board question lists any combination of agraphia, acalculia, finger agnosia, and left-right confusion, the answer is dominant (left) angular gyrus lesion. This tetrad is tested with remarkable consistency.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Non-Dominant Hemisphere Syndromes in Detail:**

The non-dominant (usually right) hemisphere is specialized for spatial attention, emotional processing, and visuospatial reasoning. Damage produces a constellation of deficits that are often more functionally devastating than aphasia:

| Syndrome | Description | Clinical Impact |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Left hemispatial neglect | Failure to attend to, respond to, or orient toward stimuli in left hemispace | Cannot find food on left side of plate, does not dress left side, collides with objects on left |
| Anosognosia | Denial or unawareness of neurological deficit | Patient insists paralyzed limb is functional; poor rehab participation |
| Aprosodia | Loss of emotional tone/melody in speech | Flat, monotone speech; cannot convey or interpret emotional prosody |
| Constructional apraxia | Difficulty with spatial construction tasks | Cannot draw a clock, copy a figure, or assemble objects |
| Dressing apraxia | Difficulty dressing the contralateral side | Puts both arms in one sleeve; cannot orient clothing to body |
| Asomatognosia | Denial of ownership of contralateral limbs | "That arm doesn't belong to me" |

Left neglect from right hemisphere damage is more common and more persistent than right neglect from left hemisphere damage because the right hemisphere attends to BOTH hemispaces, while the left hemisphere primarily attends only to the right hemispace. When the right hemisphere is damaged, the intact left hemisphere can only attend to the right, leaving the left completely unattended.

## Section 2: Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) Syndrome

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

ACA stroke produces a pattern that is the mirror image of MCA stroke in terms of body part involvement: leg weakness predominates rather than face/arm weakness.

**Classic ACA Triad:**
1. Contralateral leg weakness (leg >> arm >> face)
2. Urinary incontinence (damage to medial frontal micturition center in the paracentral lobule)
3. Gait apraxia (frontal gait disorder: magnetic, shuffling gait with difficulty initiating steps)

**Additional ACA Stroke Features:**

| Feature | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Contralateral leg sensory loss | Sensory cortex for leg is on medial surface |
| Personality changes (apathy, abulia) | Medial frontal and cingulate cortex damage |
| Transcortical motor aphasia (dominant hemisphere) | SMA and medial frontal damage; reduced spontaneous speech with preserved repetition |
| Alien hand syndrome | Corpus callosum involvement (anterior); one hand acts independently against patient's will |
| Akinetic mutism (bilateral ACA) | Severe bilateral medial frontal damage; patient appears awake but makes no voluntary movement or speech |

**ACA vs. MCA Comparison:**

| Feature | ACA Stroke | MCA Stroke |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Motor deficit pattern | Leg > arm/face | Face/arm > leg |
| Sensory deficit pattern | Leg > arm | Face/arm > leg |
| Language (dominant hemisphere) | Transcortical motor aphasia | Broca's, Wernicke's, global, or conduction aphasia |
| Behavioral changes | Abulia, apathy, personality change | Neglect (non-dominant), anosognosia |
| Other key features | Urinary incontinence, gait apraxia | Gaze deviation toward lesion, hemianopia |
| Frequency | Uncommon (~3% of strokes) | Most common stroke syndrome |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The ACA triad (leg-predominant weakness + urinary incontinence + gait apraxia) is a classic board answer. If the question describes weakness that is worse in the leg than the arm or face, with urinary incontinence, the answer is ACA territory infarction. ACA strokes are uncommon (~3%) because the ACA has good collateral supply through the AComm.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Bilateral ACA Syndrome:**

When both ACAs arise from a common trunk (an anatomical variant known as "azygos ACA"), occlusion of that trunk can produce devastating bilateral ACA infarction:

- Bilateral leg weakness/paraparesis
- Akinetic mutism (profoundly apathetic, makes no voluntary effort)
- Abulia (lack of will, initiative, or motivation)
- Severe urinary incontinence
- Alien hand syndrome (one or both hands act independently)
- Personality changes

This bilateral syndrome can mimic spinal cord injury initially due to the bilateral leg involvement, but the presence of frontal lobe signs (abulia, personality changes) and preserved upper extremity function distinguishes it.

## Section 3: Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA) Syndrome

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The PCA supplies the occipital lobe (primary visual cortex), inferior temporal lobe, thalamus, and portions of the midbrain. PCA strokes predominantly present with visual deficits.

**Core PCA Stroke Features:**

| Feature | Mechanism | Clinical Details |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing | Occipital cortex infarction; macula has dual supply from PCA and MCA | Most common PCA deficit; macular sparing distinguishes from optic radiation lesions |
| Visual agnosia | Ventral visual stream (inferior temporal) damage | Cannot recognize objects by sight despite intact visual acuity |
| Prosopagnosia | Bilateral or right PCA (fusiform face area) | Cannot recognize familiar faces |
| Memory impairment | Hippocampal/medial temporal involvement | Anterograde memory deficit |
| Minimal motor deficit | Motor cortex is in MCA/ACA territory, not PCA | Key negative finding: motor function is relatively spared |

**Special PCA Syndromes:**

| Syndrome | Description | Lesion Location |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Alexia without agraphia** | Can write but cannot read (even own writing); caused by disconnection of visual cortex from angular gyrus | Left PCA infarct involving the splenium of corpus callosum + left occipital lobe |
| **Anton syndrome** | Cortical blindness with denial of visual loss; patient confabulates visual experiences | Bilateral PCA infarction destroying both occipital lobes |
| **Gerstmann syndrome** | Agraphia, acalculia, finger agnosia, left-right confusion | Left (dominant) angular gyrus; can occur with PCA or MCA inferior division infarction |
| **Color anomia/agnosia** | Cannot name or recognize colors | Left PCA territory involving lingual/fusiform gyri |
| **Peduncular hallucinosis** | Vivid, formed visual hallucinations (often of people or animals) | Midbrain/thalamic PCA perforator territory |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Alexia without agraphia is a classic disconnection syndrome. The patient can write because the left angular gyrus and motor cortex are intact (receiving information from the intact right visual cortex is irrelevant for writing). However, the patient cannot read because visual information from the intact right visual cortex cannot reach the left angular gyrus (the reading center) -- the visual information is blocked by the left occipital infarct and the splenial callosal lesion prevents right-to-left visual information transfer. This is frequently tested.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Anton syndrome (cortical blindness with anosognosia) requires bilateral PCA infarction. The patient is completely blind but denies it, confabulating answers to visual questions. This occurs because both occipital cortices are destroyed, but the patient has lost not only vision but also the awareness that vision is missing.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Thalamic Syndromes from PCA Perforating Branches:**

The PCA gives off perforating branches (thalamoperforating and thalamogeniculate arteries) that supply the thalamus. Thalamic infarction produces distinctive syndromes:

| Thalamic Syndrome | Arterial Supply | Clinical Features |
|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Paramedian thalamic infarction | Thalamoperforating arteries (from P1) | Altered consciousness, vertical gaze palsy, memory impairment, behavioral changes |
| Lateral thalamic infarction | Thalamogeniculate arteries | Hemisensory loss, Dejerine-Roussy syndrome |
| Artery of Percheron occlusion | Single artery from P1 supplying bilateral thalami | Bilateral thalamic infarction: altered consciousness, vertical gaze palsy, memory deficit |
| Polar/anterior thalamic infarction | Tuberothalamic artery (from PComm) | Memory impairment (mammillothalamic tract), personality change, apathy |

**Dejerine-Roussy Syndrome (Thalamic Pain Syndrome):**

Dejerine-Roussy syndrome is a devastating complication of lateral thalamic stroke that develops weeks to months after the initial infarct. The initial presentation is contralateral hemisensory loss. Over time (typically weeks to months), the patient develops severe, persistent, and often incapacitating contralateral pain that may be spontaneous or evoked by normally innocuous stimuli (allodynia).

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Onset | Weeks to months after thalamic stroke |
| Pain character | Burning, lancinating, or aching; often described as "the worst pain imaginable" |
| Location | Contralateral to the thalamic lesion; may involve the entire hemibody |
| Triggers | Light touch, temperature change, emotional stress |
| Associated features | Hyperpathia (exaggerated pain response to mildly noxious stimuli), allodynia (pain from non-painful stimuli) |
| Treatment | Challenging; options include amitriptyline, gabapentin/pregabalin, lamotrigine; often refractory |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Dejerine-Roussy (thalamic pain) syndrome is one of the most commonly tested thalamic stroke complications. The key temporal pattern is: initial hemisensory loss from thalamic stroke, followed weeks to months later by development of severe contralateral pain. If a board question describes a patient who had a thalamic stroke and now develops burning hemibody pain, the answer is Dejerine-Roussy syndrome.

## Section 4: Wallenberg Syndrome (Lateral Medullary Syndrome)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Wallenberg syndrome is the single most commonly tested brainstem stroke syndrome on board examinations. It results from infarction of the lateral medulla, most commonly caused by occlusion of the intracranial vertebral artery or its PICA branch.

**Complete Clinical Presentation:**

| Finding | Laterality | Structure Involved |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| Ipsilateral facial pain/temperature loss | Same side as lesion | Descending tract and nucleus of CN V (trigeminal) |
| Contralateral body pain/temperature loss | Opposite side | Lateral spinothalamic tract |
| Ipsilateral Horner syndrome | Same side | Descending sympathetic fibers |
| Dysphagia, hoarseness | Same side | Nucleus ambiguus (CN IX, X) |
| Ipsilateral cerebellar ataxia | Same side | Inferior cerebellar peduncle and/or cerebellum |
| Vertigo, nystagmus | -- | Vestibular nuclei |
| Hiccups | -- | Medullary reticular formation |
| Ipsilateral palatal weakness | Same side | Nucleus ambiguus (CN X) |

The hallmark of Wallenberg syndrome is **crossed sensory loss**: loss of pain and temperature on the ipsilateral face (descending trigeminal nucleus) and contralateral body (spinothalamic tract). This crossed pattern occurs because the facial sensory fibers descend in the medulla before crossing (they have not yet crossed at the level of the lesion), while the body sensory fibers have already crossed in the spinal cord (the ascending spinothalamic tract at the medullary level contains contralateral body information).

> **Clinical Pearl:** Wallenberg = crossed sensory loss + Horner + dysphagia + vertigo/ataxia. The mnemonic: "Don't pick a horse that can't eat" -- Do not (dysphagia) PICA (posterior inferior cerebellar artery) a Horse (Horner syndrome) that Can't Eat (crossed sensory loss). Motor function is typically SPARED in Wallenberg syndrome because the corticospinal tract runs in the medial medulla, not the lateral medulla.

**Ondine's Curse (Central Hypoventilation):**

An uncommon but important complication of lateral medullary syndrome is Ondine's curse -- failure of automatic respiration. The patient breathes normally when awake (voluntary breathing is intact) but stops breathing during sleep because the medullary respiratory center (which drives involuntary/automatic breathing) is damaged. This requires monitoring and may necessitate mechanical ventilation during sleep.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Medial vs. Lateral Medullary Syndrome Comparison:**

| Feature | Lateral Medullary (Wallenberg) | Medial Medullary (Dejerine) |
|---------|-------------------------------|----------------------------|
| Vascular supply | Vertebral artery / PICA | Anterior spinal artery / vertebral artery branches |
| Motor deficit | Typically absent (corticospinal tract spared) | Contralateral hemiparesis (pyramidal tract involvement) |
| Sensory deficit | Crossed: ipsilateral face, contralateral body (pain/temp) | Contralateral loss of proprioception and vibration (medial lemniscus) |
| Cranial nerve | CN V (descending), IX, X (nucleus ambiguus) | CN XII (hypoglossal: ipsilateral tongue deviation) |
| Horner syndrome | Present (ipsilateral) | Absent |
| Cerebellar signs | Present (ipsilateral ataxia) | Absent |
| Frequency | Much more common | Rare |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Medial medullary syndrome (Dejerine syndrome) is the "3 H" syndrome: Hemiparesis (contralateral, from pyramidal tract), Hypoesthesia for proprioception/vibration (contralateral, from medial lemniscus), and Hypoglossal paralysis (ipsilateral tongue deviation toward the lesion, from CN XII).

## Section 5: Brainstem Stroke Syndromes

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

#### The Rule of 4 for Brainstem Localization

The "Rule of 4" is a simplified framework for understanding brainstem stroke syndromes. It divides the brainstem cross-section into four medial structures and four lateral structures:

**Four Medial Structures (Supplied by Paramedian Perforators -- "Medial" Strokes):**
1. **M**otor pathway (corticospinal tract / pyramids)
2. **M**edial lemniscus (dorsal column-medial lemniscus pathway)
3. **M**edial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF; eye movement coordination)
4. **M**otor nucleus/nerve of a cranial nerve (specific to each level)

**Four Lateral Structures (Supplied by Circumferential Branches -- "Lateral" Strokes):**
1. **S**pinocerebellar pathway (ataxia)
2. **S**pinothalamic tract (contralateral pain/temperature loss)
3. **S**ympathetic pathway (Horner syndrome)
4. **S**ensory nucleus of CN V (ipsilateral facial pain/temperature loss)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Medial brainstem syndromes produce the "4 Ms": Motor (contralateral hemiparesis), Medial lemniscus (contralateral proprioception/vibration loss), MLF (eye movement abnormalities), and Motor cranial nerve (ipsilateral cranial nerve palsy). Lateral brainstem syndromes produce the "4 Ss": Spinocerebellar (ipsilateral ataxia), Spinothalamic (contralateral body pain/temp loss), Sympathetic (ipsilateral Horner), and Sensory CN V (ipsilateral face pain/temp loss).

#### Classic Midbrain Syndromes

| Syndrome | Lesion Location | Structures Involved | Clinical Features |
|----------|----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Weber syndrome** | Ventromedial midbrain (medial) | Cerebral peduncle + CN III fascicle | Ipsilateral CN III palsy (ptosis, mydriasis, "down and out" eye) + contralateral hemiparesis |
| **Benedikt syndrome** | Tegmentum of midbrain (paramedian) | Red nucleus + CN III fascicle + medial lemniscus | Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral tremor/involuntary movements (red nucleus) + contralateral sensory loss |
| **Claude syndrome** | Dorsal midbrain (tegmental) | Red nucleus + superior cerebellar peduncle + CN III | Ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral cerebellar ataxia |
| **Parinaud syndrome (dorsal midbrain)** | Dorsal midbrain (tectal) | Superior colliculus / pretectal area | Paralysis of upward gaze, convergence-retraction nystagmus, light-near dissociation of pupils |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Weber syndrome is the most commonly tested midbrain syndrome: ipsilateral CN III palsy + contralateral hemiparesis. Think of Weber as a "crossed" syndrome where the cranial nerve is on one side and the motor deficit is on the other. This crossed pattern (ipsilateral cranial nerve + contralateral body deficit) is the hallmark of all brainstem syndromes because the cranial nerve nuclei have not yet crossed while the corticospinal tract has (or will cross at the medullary pyramids).

#### Classic Pontine Syndromes

| Syndrome | Lesion Location | Structures Involved | Clinical Features |
|----------|----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Millard-Gubler syndrome** | Ventral pons (medial) | CN VI + CN VII fascicles + corticospinal tract | Ipsilateral lateral rectus palsy (CN VI) + ipsilateral facial weakness (CN VII, LMN type) + contralateral hemiparesis |
| **Foville syndrome** | Dorsal pons (tegmentum) | CN VI nucleus + PPRF + CN VII + medial lemniscus | Ipsilateral gaze palsy (eyes deviate AWAY from lesion) + ipsilateral facial weakness + contralateral hemiparesis + contralateral sensory loss |
| **Lateral pontine (AICA) syndrome** | Lateral pons | CN VII, CN VIII, lateral spinothalamic, cerebellar peduncle | Ipsilateral facial paralysis + ipsilateral hearing loss + ipsilateral ataxia + contralateral pain/temperature loss |
| **Locked-in syndrome** | Bilateral ventral pons | Bilateral corticospinal tracts + bilateral corticobulbar tracts | Quadriplegia + anarthria (cannot speak); consciousness PRESERVED; only vertical eye movements and blinking intact |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Locked-in syndrome is NOT a coma. The patient is fully conscious but can only communicate through vertical eye movements and blinking. It is caused by bilateral ventral pontine infarction (basilar artery occlusion) that destroys the motor pathways while sparing the reticular activating system (which is dorsal). The reticular formation (consciousness) is in the tegmentum, so it is preserved. This is one of the most important brainstem syndromes to recognize because the patient APPEARS unresponsive but is actually fully aware.

#### Brainstem Eye Movement Pearl

In brainstem stroke, the direction of eye deviation helps localize the lesion:

| Lesion Location | Eye Deviation Rule | Explanation |
|----------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Cortical (frontal eye field) | Eyes deviate TOWARD the lesion | Frontal eye field normally drives eyes to the opposite side; when damaged, the intact contralateral FEF pushes eyes toward the lesion side |
| Pontine (PPRF/CN VI nucleus) | Eyes deviate AWAY from the lesion | PPRF drives eyes to the same side; when damaged, eyes are pushed away by the intact contralateral PPRF |

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Eyes look toward the lesion in cortical stroke, away from the lesion in pontine stroke." An alternative mnemonic: "Eyes look toward a destructive cortical lesion and away from a destructive pontine lesion." If a hemiplegic patient's eyes look toward the weak side (toward the paralyzed limbs), the lesion is in the pons, not the cortex.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Top of the Basilar Syndrome:**

Top of the basilar syndrome results from occlusion at the rostral end of the basilar artery, affecting the midbrain and thalamus via the posterior cerebral and perforating arteries.

| Feature | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| Altered alertness/somnolence | Reticular formation and thalamic involvement |
| Behavioral abnormalities | Thalamic and temporal lobe involvement |
| Memory deficits | Hippocampal and thalamic involvement |
| Oculomotor abnormalities | CN III, vertical gaze centers |
| Pupillary abnormalities | Pretectal area |
| Visual field deficits | PCA territory (occipital cortex) |
| Peduncular hallucinations | Midbrain tegmentum; vivid, formed visual hallucinations |

**Internuclear Ophthalmoplegia (INO):**

INO results from a lesion of the medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF) in the brainstem. On attempted lateral gaze:
- The ipsilateral eye (to the MLF lesion) fails to adduct
- The contralateral eye (attempting abduction) develops nystagmus

The most common cause in young adults is multiple sclerosis. The most common cause in older adults is small vessel (lacunar) brainstem infarction.

## Section 6: Cerebellar Stroke Syndromes

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

| Cerebellar Artery | Parent Vessel | Territory | Distinguishing Features of Infarction |
|-------------------|---------------|-----------|---------------------------------------|
| PICA | Vertebral artery | Lateral medulla + inferior cerebellum | Wallenberg syndrome (lateral medullary); cerebellar edema can cause mass effect requiring decompression |
| AICA | Proximal basilar | Lateral pons + middle cerebellum + inner ear | Ipsilateral facial paralysis (CN VII) + hearing loss/tinnitus (CN VIII via internal auditory artery) + ataxia |
| SCA | Distal basilar | Superior cerebellum + rostral pons | Ipsilateral cerebellar ataxia + contralateral pain/temperature loss + possible ipsilateral Horner; tremor or choreoathetoid movements |

**PICA Cerebellar Infarction -- Surgical Emergency:**

Large PICA territory cerebellar infarctions can become life-threatening due to the confined space of the posterior fossa. Cerebellar edema can compress the brainstem (causing respiratory arrest) and obstruct the fourth ventricle (causing obstructive hydrocephalus). This represents a neurosurgical emergency.

Indications for posterior fossa decompression:
- Cerebellar hematoma or edematous infarct >3 cm
- Evidence of brainstem compression
- Obstructive hydrocephalus from fourth ventricle compression
- Clinical deterioration despite medical management

> **Clinical Pearl:** Cerebellar PICA infarction can present as a surgical emergency. The initial presentation may be vertigo, ataxia, and vomiting (which can be mistaken for benign vertigo), but progressive cerebellar edema can rapidly cause brainstem compression and death. Any patient with suspected cerebellar stroke requires close neurological monitoring.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**HINTS Test for Posterior Fossa Stroke:**

The HINTS examination (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) is a bedside tool that is MORE sensitive than MRI in the first 48 hours for detecting posterior fossa stroke in patients presenting with acute vestibular syndrome (vertigo, nystagmus, gait instability).

| Test Component | Peripheral (Benign) Pattern | Central (Stroke) Pattern |
|---------------|----------------------------|-------------------------|
| Head impulse test | ABNORMAL (corrective saccade present) | NORMAL (no corrective saccade) |
| Nystagmus | Unidirectional (horizontal only) | Direction-changing or vertical |
| Test of skew | Negative (no vertical misalignment) | POSITIVE (skew deviation present) |

The central pattern can be remembered as: **N**ormal head impulse, **D**irection-changing nystagmus, **S**kew deviation present = stroke until proven otherwise.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The HINTS exam outperforms early MRI for detecting posterior fossa stroke. A "normal" head impulse test in a patient with acute vertigo should raise concern for a central (stroke) cause rather than a peripheral (vestibular neuritis) cause. This is counterintuitive: the "normal" finding is the abnormal one in this context.

## Section 7: Lacunar Syndromes -- Detailed Localization

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The five classic lacunar syndromes are defined by the ABSENCE of cortical signs. If cortical signs (aphasia, neglect, visual field cuts, gaze preference) are present, the diagnosis is NOT a lacunar syndrome.

| Syndrome | Deficits | Most Common Location(s) | Key Features |
|----------|---------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Pure motor hemiparesis** | Contralateral face, arm, and leg weakness (approximately equal); no sensory loss | Posterior limb of internal capsule; basis pontis; corona radiata | Most common lacunar syndrome (45-57%); weakness is uniform across face/arm/leg |
| **Pure sensory stroke** | Contralateral face, arm, and leg numbness/paresthesias; no motor deficit | VPL nucleus of thalamus | Paresthesias may be persistent and disabling |
| **Ataxic hemiparesis** | Cerebellar-type ataxia + weakness on the SAME side (contralateral to lesion) | Posterior limb of internal capsule; basis pontis | Ataxia is disproportionate to degree of weakness |
| **Sensorimotor stroke** | Combined contralateral hemiparesis + hemisensory loss | Thalamus + adjacent posterior limb of internal capsule | Largest of the lacunar syndromes; thalamocapsular junction |
| **Dysarthria-clumsy hand syndrome** | Dysarthria + dysphagia + contralateral hand clumsiness/weakness | Basis pontis; genu of internal capsule | Least common; dysarthria is prominent |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Pure motor hemiparesis is the most common lacunar syndrome and the one most likely to be tested. The posterior limb of the internal capsule is the most commonly tested location. Remember: in pure motor hemiparesis, face = arm = leg weakness (approximately equal involvement), distinguishing it from MCA stroke where face/arm >> leg. The absence of cortical signs is the DEFINING feature of all lacunar syndromes.

## Section 8: Visual Field Deficits by Lesion Location

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Visual field deficits are among the most precisely localizable neurological findings and are heavily tested on board examinations.

**Visual Pathway and Associated Deficits:**

| Lesion Location | Visual Field Deficit | Key Features |
|----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Optic nerve (one eye) | Monocular vision loss (ipsilateral eye) | Amaurosis fugax (TIA) or central retinal artery occlusion; caused by ICA/ophthalmic artery disease |
| Optic chiasm (central) | Bitemporal hemianopia | Classically from pituitary adenoma or AComm aneurysm compressing the chiasm |
| Optic tract | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia | WITHOUT macular sparing; may be incongruous (slightly different in each eye) |
| Temporal lobe (Meyer's loop) | Contralateral homonymous superior quadrantanopia ("pie in the sky") | MCA inferior division or temporal lobe lesion |
| Parietal lobe (upper radiations) | Contralateral homonymous inferior quadrantanopia ("pie on the floor") | MCA or parietal lobe lesion |
| Occipital cortex (PCA territory) | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia WITH macular sparing | PCA infarct; macular sparing due to dual supply from MCA and PCA |
| Bilateral occipital cortex | Cortical blindness (Anton syndrome if with anosognosia) | Bilateral PCA infarct; pupils react normally (pupil reflex is subcortical) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The key distinguishing feature between an optic radiation lesion and an occipital cortex lesion is macular sparing. Occipital cortex (PCA) lesions produce hemianopia WITH macular sparing because the macula has dual blood supply. Optic radiation lesions produce hemianopia WITHOUT macular sparing. Another critical point: in cortical blindness (bilateral PCA), the pupillary light reflex is PRESERVED because the pupillary pathway bypasses the cortex (it goes from the optic tract to the pretectal area in the midbrain).

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Complete Visual Pathway Localization Table:**

| Structure | Blood Supply | Lesion Effect | Clinical Clue |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|---------------|
| Retina | Central retinal artery (from ophthalmic artery) | Monocular vision loss | Embolic: "curtain falling" over one eye |
| Optic nerve | Ophthalmic artery | Monocular vision loss; afferent pupillary defect (APD/Marcus Gunn pupil) | Relative APD on swinging flashlight test |
| Optic chiasm | Circle of Willis branches | Bitemporal hemianopia (crossing nasal fibers) | Classically pituitary adenoma, AComm aneurysm, craniopharyngioma |
| Optic tract | Anterior choroidal artery, PComm | Incongruous contralateral homonymous hemianopia | Relatively rare in isolation |
| Lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) | Anterior choroidal + thalamogeniculate arteries | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia; can produce sectoral patterns | Dual blood supply creates distinctive patterns |
| Temporal optic radiations (Meyer's loop) | MCA (inferior division) | Contralateral superior quadrantanopia | "Pie in the sky" from temporal lobe damage |
| Parietal optic radiations | MCA | Contralateral inferior quadrantanopia | "Pie on the floor" from parietal lobe damage |
| Occipital cortex (striate cortex) | PCA | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing | Macular sparing = PCA territory; congruent deficit |
| Bilateral occipital cortex | Bilateral PCA | Cortical blindness; normal pupillary reflexes | Anton syndrome if anosognosia present |

**Macular Sparing Explanation:**

The macular region of the occipital cortex (at the occipital pole) receives dual blood supply from both the PCA and MCA (via distal branches of the angular or posterior temporal arteries). When the PCA is occluded, the MCA's contribution preserves the macular representation. This dual supply is unique to the macular cortex; the peripheral visual field representation, located more anteriorly on the medial occipital surface, is supplied exclusively by the PCA and is lost when the PCA is occluded.

## Section 9: Spinal Cord Stroke Syndromes

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Although less common than cerebral stroke, spinal cord infarction produces distinctive syndromes that appear on board examinations:

**Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome:**

| Feature | Present/Absent | Explanation |
|---------|---------------|-------------|
| Bilateral motor paralysis below lesion | Present | Corticospinal tracts (anterior cord) |
| Bilateral pain/temperature loss below lesion | Present | Spinothalamic tracts (anterolateral cord) |
| Proprioception/vibration | PRESERVED | Dorsal columns (posterior cord, supplied by posterior spinal arteries) |
| Bladder dysfunction | Present | Autonomic pathways in anterolateral cord |

**Posterior Spinal Artery Syndrome:**
- Very rare in isolation
- Loss of proprioception and vibration below the lesion
- Motor and pain/temperature sensation preserved
