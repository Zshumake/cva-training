## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** Repetition is the KEY discriminating feature in aphasia classification. Transcortical aphasias have INTACT repetition because the perisylvian language zone is preserved — the lesion is in the surrounding watershed territory. All other aphasias (Broca, Wernicke, Global, Conduction) have IMPAIRED repetition because the perisylvian zone itself is damaged.

> **Board Pearl:** Conduction aphasia is caused by damage to the arcuate fasciculus connecting Wernicke area to Broca area. The hallmark is disproportionately impaired REPETITION with preserved fluency and comprehension. Patients demonstrate "conduit d'approche" — progressive self-correction attempts toward the target word.

> **Board Pearl:** Hemispatial neglect is most commonly associated with RIGHT parietal lobe lesions (especially the inferior parietal lobule and temporoparietal junction). It is distinct from hemianopia: neglect patients fail to attend to the contralesional side even when visual pathways are intact.

> **Board Pearl:** Pseudobulbar affect (PBA) is treated with dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta) — the only FDA-approved treatment. PBA is involuntary, stimulus-disproportionate crying or laughing, which is distinct from depression.

> **Board Pearl:** The MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) is more sensitive than the MMSE for detecting poststroke cognitive impairment, particularly in the domains of executive function, visuospatial ability, and attention. MoCA is the preferred screening tool in stroke rehabilitation.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 12.1 Aphasia: Classification, Localization, and Recovery

#### 12.1.1 Definition and Epidemiology

[BOARD] Aphasia is an acquired language disorder caused by brain damage — most commonly left hemisphere stroke — that impairs the ability to formulate, comprehend, or both formulate and comprehend linguistic messages. It affects approximately 15-38% of acute ischemic stroke patients. Aphasia must be distinguished from motor speech disorders (dysarthria, apraxia of speech), which affect the production of speech sounds rather than the symbolic processing of language.

Language dominance resides in the left hemisphere in approximately 95-99% of right-handed individuals and 60-70% of left-handed individuals. Crossed aphasia (aphasia from a right hemisphere lesion in a right-handed person) occurs in fewer than 5% of right-handed aphasic patients.

#### 12.1.2 The Three Parameters of Aphasia Classification

[BOARD] Aphasia classification is based on three core parameters, each assessed at the bedside:

1. **Fluency** — Is the patient's spontaneous speech output fluent or nonfluent?
   - **Fluent:** Speech flows easily with normal phrase length (5+ words), rate, and prosody, even though content may be empty, paraphasic, or nonsensical
   - **Nonfluent:** Speech is effortful, halting, telegraphic, with reduced phrase length (1-3 words) and impaired prosody

2. **Comprehension** — Can the patient understand spoken language?
   - Tested by following commands of increasing complexity: "Close your eyes" → "Point to the ceiling, then touch your nose" → "Before touching your ear, point to the door"

3. **Repetition** — Can the patient repeat spoken phrases?
   - Tested with phrases of increasing length and complexity: "No ifs, ands, or buts"
   - Repetition distinguishes perisylvian aphasias (impaired repetition) from transcortical aphasias (intact repetition)

A fourth parameter, **naming**, is impaired in virtually all aphasia types but is the sole deficit in anomic aphasia.

#### 12.1.3 Aphasia Classification Table

[BOARD] This table is high-yield for board examinations:

| Aphasia Type | Fluency | Comprehension | Repetition | Naming | Lesion Site | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Global** | Nonfluent | Impaired | Impaired | Impaired | Large left MCA territory (frontal + temporal + parietal) | Most severe; all language functions devastated; often limited to stereotyped utterances |
| **Broca (Expressive)** | Nonfluent | Intact | Impaired | Impaired | Left inferior frontal gyrus (Brodmann areas 44, 45) | Effortful, telegraphic speech; preserved comprehension; aware of deficits; frustrated |
| **Wernicke (Receptive)** | Fluent | Impaired | Impaired | Impaired | Left superior temporal gyrus (Brodmann area 22) | Fluent but empty speech ("word salad"); phonemic and semantic paraphasias; often unaware of errors |
| **Conduction** | Fluent | Intact | Impaired | Impaired | Arcuate fasciculus (white matter connecting Broca and Wernicke areas) | Disproportionately poor repetition; conduit d'approche; phonemic paraphasias |
| **Transcortical Motor** | Nonfluent | Intact | **INTACT** | Impaired | Anterior or superior to Broca area (anterior watershed) | Like Broca BUT with intact repetition; reduced initiation; may be echolalic |
| **Transcortical Sensory** | Fluent | Impaired | **INTACT** | Impaired | Posterior to Wernicke area (posterior watershed) | Like Wernicke BUT with intact repetition; empty fluent speech; echolalia |
| **Mixed Transcortical (Isolation Aphasia)** | Nonfluent | Impaired | **INTACT** | Impaired | Bilateral watershed zones (isolation of perisylvian zone) | Global aphasia pattern BUT with intact repetition; echolalia is prominent; classically from watershed infarcts (cardiac arrest, hypotension) |
| **Anomic** | Fluent | Intact | Intact | **Impaired** | Angular gyrus or various left hemisphere locations | Mildest aphasia; word-finding difficulty is the sole deficit; circumlocutory speech; common residual pattern |

> **Board Pearl:** Transcortical aphasias have INTACT repetition because the perisylvian language zone (Broca area + arcuate fasciculus + Wernicke area) is intact. The lesion lies OUTSIDE this zone, in the watershed territory. Think: "Transcontinental railroad — the patient can REPEAT the trip." All transcortical aphasias preserve repetition.

> **Board Pearl:** Mixed transcortical aphasia (isolation aphasia) results from bilateral watershed infarcts that isolate the perisylvian language zone. It is classically caused by prolonged hypotension or cardiac arrest. The perisylvian zone is intact (so repetition is preserved), but it is functionally disconnected from the rest of the cortex.

#### 12.1.4 Paraphasias

[COMPREHENSIVE] Paraphasias are characteristic errors in aphasic speech:

| Type | Definition | Example | Associated Aphasia |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phonemic (Literal)** | Substitution, addition, deletion, or transposition of phonemes within a word | "tevel" for "table"; "glear" for "clear" | Conduction, Broca |
| **Semantic (Verbal)** | Substitution of a semantically related word | "chair" for "table"; "dog" for "cat" | Wernicke |
| **Neologistic** | Production of a nonexistent word with no phonemic or semantic relationship to the target | "blikter" for "table" | Wernicke (severe) |
| **Circumlocution** | Talking around a word when unable to retrieve it | "the thing you sit at to eat" for "table" | Anomic (most characteristic) |

#### 12.1.5 The Aphasia Decision Tree

[BOARD] This algorithm is the practical approach to classifying aphasia at the bedside:

```
Step 1: Is speech FLUENT or NONFLUENT?
     |                        |
   FLUENT                  NONFLUENT
     |                        |
Step 2: Is COMPREHENSION intact?   Step 2: Is COMPREHENSION intact?
   |            |               |            |
  YES          NO              YES          NO
   |            |               |            |
Step 3:       Step 3:         Step 3:      Step 3:
Can REPEAT?   Can REPEAT?    Can REPEAT?  Can REPEAT?
 |     |       |      |       |     |      |      |
YES   NO      YES    NO      YES   NO    YES    NO
 |     |       |      |       |     |      |      |
 ↓     ↓       ↓      ↓       ↓     ↓      ↓      ↓

Anomic  Conduction  TC      Wernicke  TC     Broca  Mixed   Global
                  Sensory             Motor          TC
```

Step 4: Check NAMING (impaired in all aphasias except normal).

- If fluent + intact comprehension + intact repetition + impaired naming = **Anomic aphasia**
- If fluent + intact comprehension + intact repetition + intact naming = **Normal**

#### 12.1.6 Aphasia Recovery Patterns

[BOARD]

| Aphasia Type | Typical Recovery Pattern | Prognosis |
|---|---|---|
| **Anomic** | Best recovery — mildest form; naming deficits often improve significantly with therapy | **Best** prognosis of all aphasia types |
| **Broca** | Comprehension remains strong; verbal output improves but often remains nonfluent with telegraphic speech | **Moderate** — expressive deficits frequently persist long-term |
| **Wernicke** | Comprehension typically improves over first 3-6 months; paraphasic errors decrease | **Variable** — comprehension often improves but self-monitoring deficits may persist |
| **Conduction** | Repetition and naming improve; phonemic paraphasias decrease | **Good** — often evolves into anomic aphasia pattern |
| **Transcortical Motor** | Often recovers well due to intact perisylvian zone; speech initiation improves | **Good** — intact repetition is a favorable sign |
| **Transcortical Sensory** | Variable; comprehension may improve but often limited due to watershed etiology | **Moderate** |
| **Global** | Limited recovery if lesion is large (full MCA territory); some may evolve to Broca pattern if posterior regions recover | **Poorest** — large lesions involving both Broca and Wernicke areas |

**Recovery Timeline:**
- Most improvement occurs in the first 3-6 months
- Copenhagen Stroke Study benchmarks:
  - Mild aphasia: maximum recovery at ~2 weeks
  - Moderate aphasia: maximum recovery at ~6 weeks
  - Severe aphasia: maximum recovery at ~10 weeks
- Recovery typically plateaus after 1 year but is not absolute — chronic aphasia can still improve with intensive therapy
- Early recovery driven by reperfusion of ischemic penumbra; later recovery driven by cortical reorganization and neuroplasticity

**Prognostic Factors for Aphasia Recovery:**
- Severity at onset is the strongest predictor of long-term outcome
- Younger age: better prognosis
- Higher educational level: better prognosis
- Higher Barthel Index (less overall disability): better prognosis
- Lesion size: smaller lesions recover better
- Aphasia type: anomic best, global worst

#### 12.1.7 Aphasia Rehabilitation

[BOARD]

| Therapy | Description | Best Indication | Evidence Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Constraint-Induced Language Therapy (CILT/CIAT)** | Forced use of verbal communication; gestural and written communication restricted | Chronic aphasia with some verbal output; analogous to CIMT for motor recovery | Strong — best-studied specific approach |
| **Melodic Intonation Therapy (MIT)** | Uses singing and melodic patterns to recruit right hemisphere for speech production | Broca (nonfluent) aphasia with intact comprehension | Moderate — effective for nonfluent aphasias |
| **Semantic Feature Analysis (SFA)** | Structured approach to word retrieval by analyzing semantic features of target word | Anomic aphasia; word-finding difficulties | Moderate |
| **Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC)** | Communication boards, speech-generating devices, apps | Severe/global aphasia with limited verbal output | Supportive — enhances functional communication |
| **Intensive speech-language therapy** | >2 hours/week of SLP sessions | All aphasia types | Strong — intensive therapy is superior to less intensive therapy |

> **Board Pearl:** Intensive speech-language therapy (>2 hours/week) is more effective than less intensive therapy. CILT/CIAT is the best-studied specific aphasia therapy. MIT is specifically indicated for Broca aphasia (nonfluent with intact comprehension) because it recruits the right hemisphere's melodic processing capabilities.

**Pharmacologic Interventions for Aphasia:**

[BOARD] No pharmacologic intervention has demonstrated proven long-term benefit for aphasia recovery:
- **FLAME trial:** Fluoxetine initially appeared to help aphasia recovery
- **FOCUS, AFFINITY, EFFECTS trials:** Three large RCTs showed SSRIs have NO benefit for poststroke recovery, including aphasia — effectively refuting FLAME
- **Memantine + CIAT:** Some additional benefit in combination
- **Piracetam, bromocriptine, donepezil:** Studied with mixed or negative results
- **tDCS (transcranial direct current stimulation):** Improves naming ability but NOT functional communication (Cochrane review)
- **TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation):** Promising in research (1-Hz rTMS to contralesional hemisphere + intensive therapy improves WAB scores) but not yet standard clinical practice

> **Board Pearl:** SSRIs do NOT improve aphasia or stroke recovery (FOCUS/AFFINITY/EFFECTS trials). This is a frequently tested fact.

### 12.2 Speech vs. Language: A Critical Distinction

[BOARD] Confusing speech and language leads to incorrect localization and inappropriate treatment. They are fundamentally different processes:

| Feature | Speech | Language |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | Motor production of sounds (articulation, phonation, resonance, prosody) | Symbolic system for communication (semantics, syntax, comprehension, expression) |
| **Disorder** | Dysarthria, dysphonia, apraxia of speech | Aphasia |
| **Neural basis** | Motor cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum, brainstem cranial nerve nuclei, peripheral nerves | Left perisylvian cortex (Broca, Wernicke, arcuate fasciculus), association cortex |
| **Patient presentation** | Slurred, imprecise, or abnormal-sounding speech; BUT the patient knows the correct words and constructs grammatically correct sentences | May have clear articulation but wrong words (paraphasias), cannot find words, or cannot understand spoken language |
| **Comprehension** | NORMAL | May be impaired (Wernicke, Global, Transcortical Sensory) |

**Key Motor Speech Disorders:**

| Disorder | Definition | Characteristics | Common Causes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dysarthria** | Motor speech disorder from neuromuscular impairment affecting articulation, phonation, resonance, or prosody | Consistent, predictable errors; slurred speech; may affect rate, volume, pitch | UMN lesion (spastic), LMN lesion (flaccid), cerebellar (ataxic), extrapyramidal (hypokinetic/hyperkinetic), mixed |
| **Dysphonia** | Abnormal voice quality from vocal cord dysfunction | Breathy (paralysis), strained-strangled (spastic), hoarse | Vagus nerve injury, recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, UMN lesion |
| **Apraxia of Speech** | Motor programming disorder — inability to plan and sequence articulatory movements | Inconsistent errors; groping for correct positions; difficulty with longer/more complex words; preserved automatic speech ("hello," counting) | Left frontal lesion (near or overlapping Broca area); distinct from both dysarthria and aphasia |

> **Board Pearl:** A patient with dysarthria has slurred speech but NORMAL language — they know the right words but cannot articulate them clearly. A patient with aphasia may have clear articulation but produce the WRONG words (paraphasias) or be unable to find words at all. Apraxia of speech is characterized by INCONSISTENT articulatory errors with groping, distinguishing it from dysarthria (consistent errors).

### 12.3 Hemispatial Neglect

[BOARD] Hemispatial neglect (also called unilateral spatial neglect, hemi-inattention) is the failure to attend to, explore, or respond to stimuli in the contralesional space, despite intact primary sensory pathways. It is one of the most disabling cognitive consequences of stroke and is a strong negative prognostic indicator for functional recovery.

#### 12.3.1 Epidemiology and Anatomy

- Occurs in 25-50% of acute right hemisphere stroke patients
- Much less common after left hemisphere stroke (~10%)
- Most commonly associated with right inferior parietal lobule and temporoparietal junction lesions
- Also seen with right frontal, right thalamic, and right basal ganglia lesions
- Left hemispatial neglect (from right brain lesions) is more severe and persistent than right neglect (from left brain lesions) — because the right hemisphere attends to BOTH hemifields, while the left hemisphere primarily attends to the right hemifield only

#### 12.3.2 Types of Neglect

[COMPREHENSIVE]

| Type | Definition | Clinical Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Personal (body) neglect** | Failure to attend to the contralesional side of one's own body | Does not groom, dress, or acknowledge the left arm/leg |
| **Peripersonal (near space) neglect** | Failure to attend to contralesional space within arm's reach | Eats food only from the right side of the plate; reads only the right side of text |
| **Extrapersonal (far space) neglect** | Failure to attend to contralesional far space | Does not notice people entering from the left; collides with objects on the left while navigating |
| **Sensory (input) neglect** | Deficit in perception of contralesional stimuli | Fails to extinguish on double simultaneous stimulation |
| **Motor (output/intentional) neglect** | Failure to initiate movements toward contralesional space | Does not reach with either hand toward the left side |
| **Representational neglect** | Deficit in the mental representation of contralesional space | When asked to imagine and describe a familiar scene, describes only right-sided features |

#### 12.3.3 Bedside Tests for Neglect

[BOARD]

| Test | Procedure | Abnormal Finding |
|---|---|---|
| **Line Bisection** | Draw a horizontal line; ask patient to mark the midpoint | Deviation of the mark toward the ipsilesional (right) side indicates left neglect |
| **Cancellation Tests** (letter, star, bell) | Array of targets on a page; patient must find and cross out all targets | Omission of targets on the contralesional (left) side |
| **Clock Drawing** | Ask patient to draw a clock face with all 12 numbers | All numbers crowded on the right (ipsilesional) side; left side empty |
| **Copying** | Copy a simple drawing (flower, house) | Omission of left-sided features (missing petals on left, half a house) |
| **Double Simultaneous Stimulation** | Simultaneous visual or tactile stimulation on both sides | Patient reports only the ipsilesional (right) stimulus — extinction |
| **Reading** | Read a sentence or paragraph | Omission of left-sided words or letters ("lunch" read as "unch") |
| **Catherine Bergego Scale** | Behavioral assessment of neglect in ADLs (10 items) | Most ecologically valid measure of functional neglect impact |

#### 12.3.4 Anosognosia

[BOARD] Anosognosia is the lack of awareness of one's own neurological deficit — the patient genuinely does not recognize that they are impaired. It is most commonly associated with right hemisphere lesions (especially right parietal and insular cortex) and frequently co-occurs with hemispatial neglect.

**Clinical significance:** Anosognosia is a major barrier to rehabilitation because patients who do not recognize their deficits:
- Lack motivation to participate in therapy
- Attempt unsafe activities (transfers, ambulation) without awareness of fall risk
- Resist recommended adaptive equipment or supervision
- Have poorer functional outcomes and longer hospital stays

**Key distinctions:**
- **Anosognosia:** Unawareness of deficit (neurological; right hemisphere)
- **Anosodiaphoria:** Awareness but indifference to deficit (right hemisphere)
- **Denial:** Psychological defense mechanism (any hemisphere; reactive to understanding the disability)

> **Board Pearl:** Hemispatial neglect is a stronger negative prognostic indicator than hemiplegia alone. Patients with neglect have worse functional outcomes, longer rehabilitation stays, and lower discharge FIM scores than patients with equivalent motor impairment without neglect.

#### 12.3.5 Neglect Rehabilitation

[COMPREHENSIVE]

| Approach | Mechanism | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Visual scanning training** | Teach patient to systematically scan leftward | Moderate — improves performance on neglect tests; limited generalization to ADLs |
| **Prism adaptation** | Patient wears prism glasses that shift visual field rightward; after-effect causes leftward recalibration | Promising — multiple RCTs show short-term improvement |
| **Limb activation therapy** | Active movement of the neglected (left) limb in the neglected (left) space | Moderate evidence |
| **Environmental modifications** | Place items, call light, visitors on the neglected side; approach patient from the left | Standard practice; limited formal evidence |
| **Mirror therapy** | Use mirror to create visual feedback of the neglected limb | Some evidence for improving awareness |

### 12.4 Apraxia

[BOARD] Apraxia is the inability to perform skilled, purposeful movements despite intact motor function, sensation, comprehension, and coordination. It results from damage to motor planning areas, typically in the left hemisphere (dominant for motor planning in most individuals).

#### 12.4.1 Types of Apraxia

| Type | Definition | Lesion Site | Clinical Presentation | Testing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ideomotor** | Inability to execute a learned motor act on command, despite ability to perform it spontaneously | Left parietal lobe (supramarginal gyrus) or premotor cortex; corpus callosum | Can describe the action, can perform it automatically ("use your toothbrush" — fails on command but brushes teeth normally in routine) | "Show me how you would use a comb" (without actual comb) |
| **Ideational** | Loss of the conceptual plan for a multi-step action sequence; does not know WHAT to do | Left parietal lobe (extensive); bilateral lesions | Missequences steps, misuses objects, uses wrong tool for task (e.g., tries to brush teeth with a fork) | Multi-step task: "Make a cup of tea" (fill kettle, boil water, add teabag, pour) |
| **Constructional** | Inability to copy, draw, or construct spatial arrangements | Right parietal lobe (most common) or left parietal | Cannot copy a cube, clock, interlocking pentagons; cannot assemble blocks or puzzles | Copy geometric figures; clock drawing; block design |
| **Dressing** | Inability to orient and put on clothing correctly | Right parietal lobe (associated with neglect) | Puts clothes on backwards, inside-out; cannot orient sleeve to arm; neglects left side of dressing | Observe during ADLs |
| **Buccofacial (Oral)** | Inability to perform voluntary facial movements on command | Left frontal operculum (near Broca area) | Cannot pucker lips, stick out tongue, or blow on command; can do so reflexively | "Stick out your tongue"; "Show me how you would blow out a candle" |

> **Board Pearl:** Ideomotor apraxia = knows WHAT to do but cannot execute it on command (can do it automatically). Ideational apraxia = does not know WHAT to do (lost the concept/sequence). Constructional apraxia = most commonly associated with RIGHT parietal lesions and frequently co-occurs with neglect.

### 12.5 Gerstmann Syndrome

[BOARD] Gerstmann syndrome results from a lesion of the dominant (usually left) angular gyrus (Brodmann area 39) at the junction of the temporal, parietal, and occipital lobes. It produces a classic tetrad of deficits:

| Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Finger agnosia** | Inability to identify, distinguish, name, or select individual fingers on self or examiner |
| **Agraphia** | Acquired inability to write (distinct from aphasia; isolated writing deficit) |
| **Acalculia** | Inability to perform arithmetic calculations |
| **Left-right disorientation** | Inability to distinguish left from right on self or others |

> **Board Pearl:** Gerstmann syndrome = Angular gyrus = Finger agnosia + Agraphia + Acalculia + Left-right disorientation. The angular gyrus is in the dominant parietal lobe at the temporoparietal-occipital junction. This is a classic board question.

**Mnemonic:** "FAAL" — Finger agnosia, Agraphia, Acalculia, Left-right confusion

### 12.6 Cognitive Domains Affected After Stroke

[COMPREHENSIVE] Poststroke cognitive impairment affects approximately 46% of stroke survivors at 6 months and encompasses multiple interrelated domains:

#### 12.6.1 Cognitive Domains

| Domain | Definition | Anatomy | Assessment Tools | Impact on Rehabilitation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Attention** | Ability to focus, sustain, shift, and divide attention | Right frontal and parietal (sustained attention); prefrontal (divided attention) | Trail Making Test A, digit span forward, cancellation tests | Foundation for all other cognitive functions; impaired attention undermines all therapy |
| **Memory** | Encoding, storage, and retrieval of information | Hippocampus (encoding); frontal (working memory/retrieval); distributed cortical (long-term storage) | MoCA memory subtests, RBMT, word list learning | Affects ability to learn new compensatory strategies; carry-over between therapy sessions |
| **Executive Function** | Planning, problem-solving, cognitive flexibility, inhibition, judgment | Prefrontal cortex (dorsolateral, ventromedial, orbitofrontal) | Trail Making Test B, clock drawing, MoCA abstraction, Wisconsin Card Sort, Tower of London | Critical for safe independent living; poor executive function predicts inability to manage medications, finances, and safety |
| **Processing Speed** | Rate at which cognitive operations are performed | Widespread white matter tracts; subcortical structures | Symbol Digit Modalities Test, TMT-A timing | Slowed processing is extremely common after stroke; affects participation in therapy and daily functioning |
| **Visuospatial** | Perception and manipulation of spatial relationships | Right parietal lobe (primary); bilateral occipitoparietal | Block design, figure copy, line bisection, Rey Complex Figure | Affects navigation, driving, dressing, constructional tasks |
| **Language** | Comprehension and expression of symbolic communication | Left perisylvian cortex | Boston Naming Test, Token Test, WAB, BDAE | Covered extensively in aphasia section above |

#### 12.6.2 Cognitive Screening: MoCA vs. MMSE

[BOARD]

| Feature | MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment) | MMSE (Mini-Mental State Examination) |
|---|---|---|
| **Score range** | 0-30 (normal ≥26) | 0-30 (normal ≥24) |
| **Administration time** | ~10 minutes | ~10 minutes |
| **Executive function** | Yes — extensive (TMT-B adaptation, clock drawing, abstraction, verbal fluency) | Limited — serial 7s, spelling backward |
| **Visuospatial** | Yes — cube copy, clock drawing | Limited — intersecting pentagons |
| **Attention** | Yes — digit span, vigilance, serial subtraction | Yes — serial 7s, spelling backward |
| **Memory** | Yes — delayed recall (5 words with no cues, then category and multiple-choice cues) | Yes — recall (3 words) |
| **Language** | Yes — naming, sentence repetition, verbal fluency | Yes — naming, repetition, following commands |
| **Sensitivity to mild cognitive impairment** | **HIGH** — designed to detect MCI | **LOW** — ceiling effect in mild impairment |
| **Sensitivity to executive dysfunction** | **HIGH** | **LOW** |
| **Cost** | Free (requires certification training) | Copyrighted (requires license fee) |
| **Education correction** | +1 point if ≤12 years education | No formal correction |

> **Board Pearl:** The MoCA is more sensitive than the MMSE for detecting poststroke cognitive impairment because it includes more challenging tasks for executive function, visuospatial processing, and attention. The MMSE has a significant ceiling effect and misses many patients with mild-moderate cognitive impairment. MoCA is the preferred screening tool in stroke rehabilitation settings.

### 12.7 Vascular Cognitive Impairment and Vascular Dementia

[COMPREHENSIVE] Vascular cognitive impairment (VCI) encompasses the full spectrum of cognitive dysfunction attributable to cerebrovascular disease, from mild vascular cognitive impairment to vascular dementia.

**Vascular Dementia Subtypes:**

| Subtype | Mechanism | Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Multi-infarct dementia** | Multiple cortical infarcts (often cardioembolic) | Stepwise cognitive decline; focal neurological signs; multiple stroke history |
| **Strategic infarct dementia** | Single infarct in a critical location (e.g., thalamus, angular gyrus, caudate, hippocampus) | Sudden cognitive decline after single stroke; deficit pattern depends on location |
| **Small vessel disease (subcortical)** | Chronic white matter ischemia (leukoaraiosis); lacunar infarcts | Executive dysfunction, slowed processing speed, apathy, gait disorder; "subcortical" cognitive profile |
| **Hemorrhagic** | Cognitive impairment following ICH | Depends on location and volume; thalamic and caudate hemorrhages most commonly cause dementia |

**Key Distinguishing Features from Alzheimer Disease:**

| Feature | Vascular Dementia | Alzheimer Disease |
|---|---|---|
| **Onset** | Often sudden or stepwise | Gradual, insidious |
| **Course** | Stepwise or fluctuating | Progressive, continuous decline |
| **Predominant deficit** | Executive dysfunction, processing speed | Memory (encoding/retrieval) |
| **Neurologic signs** | Focal deficits (hemiparesis, sensory loss, reflex asymmetry) | Usually absent until late |
| **Gait** | Often impaired early (small vessel disease) | Usually preserved until late |
| **Imaging** | Infarcts, white matter disease (leukoaraiosis) | Medial temporal atrophy, hippocampal atrophy |
| **Risk factors** | Hypertension, diabetes, AF, hyperlipidemia, stroke history | Age, ApoE4 genotype, family history |

> **Board Pearl:** Vascular dementia (especially subcortical small vessel disease) predominantly affects executive function and processing speed, in contrast to Alzheimer disease, which predominantly affects memory. A "subcortical" cognitive profile (slow processing, poor executive function, apathy) with gait impairment and white matter disease on imaging suggests vascular etiology.

### 12.8 Pseudobulbar Affect (PBA)

[BOARD] Pseudobulbar affect (PBA), also called emotional incontinence, pathological laughing and crying, or involuntary emotional expression disorder (IEED), is characterized by involuntary, sudden, and frequent episodes of crying and/or laughing that are disproportionate or incongruent with the patient's underlying emotional state.

#### 12.8.1 Pathophysiology

PBA results from disruption of the corticobulbar pathways that normally modulate the brainstem centers controlling emotional expression (the "emotional motor system"). Bilateral lesions anywhere along these pathways — cortex, internal capsule, brainstem — can produce PBA by releasing the brainstem emotional centers from cortical inhibition.

**Key anatomic correlates:**
- Bilateral corticobulbar tract lesions (most common mechanism)
- Cerebellum (emerging evidence for cerebellar role in emotional modulation)
- Brainstem nuclei (pontine nuclei)

#### 12.8.2 Epidemiology

- Affects approximately 20% of stroke survivors
- More common with bilateral lesions, brainstem lesions, and right hemisphere lesions
- Also common in ALS, MS, TBI, and neurodegenerative diseases
- Significantly underrecognized and undertreated

#### 12.8.3 PBA vs. Depression

[BOARD] This comparison is high-yield:

| Feature | Pseudobulbar Affect (PBA) | Poststroke Depression |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature of emotional expression** | Involuntary, sudden, unpredictable | Sustained, pervasive mood change |
| **Congruence with mood** | Disproportionate or incongruent (laughing at funerals, crying without sadness) | Congruent with underlying sad mood |
| **Duration of episodes** | Brief (seconds to minutes); stereotyped | Sustained (weeks to months); pervasive |
| **Trigger** | May have minimal or no trigger; any emotional stimulus | Related to negative thoughts, losses, hopelessness |
| **Associated symptoms** | No anhedonia, sleep disturbance, or suicidal ideation | Anhedonia, sleep disturbance, appetite change, guilt, concentration difficulty, possible suicidal ideation |
| **Pathology** | Bilateral corticobulbar tract lesion (release phenomenon) | Frontal-subcortical circuit dysfunction; serotonergic depletion |
| **Screening tool** | CNS-LS (Center for Neurologic Study-Lability Scale); ≥13 suggests PBA | PHQ-9, BDI, Hamilton |
| **Treatment** | Dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta) — FDA-approved; also SSRIs and TCAs | SSRIs (first-line); SNRIs; psychotherapy |

> **Board Pearl:** PBA is INVOLUNTARY emotional expression that is DISPROPORTIONATE to or INCONGRUENT with the patient's underlying mood. Depression is a SUSTAINED mood disorder. They can co-exist. Nuedexta (dextromethorphan 20 mg/quinidine 10 mg) is the only FDA-approved treatment for PBA. Dosing: 1 capsule daily for 7 days, then 1 capsule every 12 hours.

[BOARD]

### 12.9 Post-Stroke Fatigue

[COMPREHENSIVE] Post-stroke fatigue (PSF) is a subjective experience of persistent tiredness, exhaustion, or lack of energy that is disproportionate to activity level and not fully relieved by rest. It is one of the most common and distressing symptoms reported by stroke survivors.

**Epidemiology:**
- Prevalence: 25-85% (depending on timing and definition)
- May persist for years after stroke
- Distinct from depression, though often comorbid
- Present even in patients with mild strokes and good motor recovery

**Assessment:**
- Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS)
- Multidimensional Fatigue Inventory (MFI)
- Patient self-report and functional impact assessment

**Management:**
- Exclude and treat contributing factors: depression, sleep disorders (OSA is common poststroke), anemia, thyroid dysfunction, medication side effects, deconditioning
- Graded exercise programs
- Energy conservation strategies and activity pacing
- Cognitive behavioral therapy
- Modafinil or methylphenidate may be considered as adjuncts (limited evidence)
- Education: normalize fatigue as a consequence of brain injury, not laziness

### 12.10 Return to Work and Driving After Stroke

#### 12.10.1 Return to Work

[COMPREHENSIVE] Approximately 50% of working-age stroke survivors return to work within 6-12 months, but job retention at 1 year is lower. Return to work is influenced by:

**Positive predictors:** Younger age, less severe stroke, white-collar occupation, higher education, self-employment, preserved cognition, absence of aphasia, strong social support, employer accommodation

**Negative predictors:** Severe motor deficits, aphasia, cognitive impairment (especially executive dysfunction), depression, physically demanding occupation, lack of employer flexibility

**Vocational rehabilitation services:** Supported employment, job coaching, workplace modifications, gradual return-to-work programs

#### 12.10.2 Driving After Stroke

[BOARD] Driving is a complex instrumental activity requiring multiple cognitive and physical capabilities. Stroke may impair any of these, creating safety risks.

**Assessment framework:**
1. **Medical stability** — Seizure-free interval (typically 3-6 months, varies by state)
2. **Visual** — Adequate visual fields (most states require 120-degree horizontal field); visual acuity
3. **Cognitive** — Attention (divided and sustained), processing speed, executive function, visuospatial processing
4. **Motor** — Adequate strength and coordination for vehicle operation; adaptive equipment if needed
5. **Formal driving evaluation** — Comprehensive Driving Evaluation (CDE) by a Certified Driver Rehabilitation Specialist (CDRS), including behind-the-wheel assessment

**Key points:**
- No universal "stroke = cannot drive" rule; assessment must be individualized
- Most guidelines recommend no driving for at least 1 month after stroke (AHA)
- Visual field deficits (hemianopia) are the most common barrier; some patients compensate with head turning
- Hemispatial neglect is generally considered incompatible with safe driving
- State laws vary significantly regarding reporting requirements and driving restrictions

> **Board Pearl:** Hemispatial neglect is generally considered incompatible with safe driving because the patient fails to attend to an entire hemifield — a deficit that cannot be reliably compensated for. Hemianopia alone, in contrast, may be compensable with training in head-turning strategies, though this varies by jurisdiction.
