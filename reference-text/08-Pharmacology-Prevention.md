## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** Aspirin 50-325 mg should be started within 24-48 hours of ischemic stroke onset. If tPA was administered, wait 24 hours before starting aspirin. Aspirin can be given rectally if the patient is NPO.

> **Board Pearl:** DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel) for 21 days is recommended for minor stroke (NIHSS 3 or less) or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 of 4 or greater) started within 24 hours of onset (CHANCE, POINT). Long-term DAPT beyond 90 days is NOT recommended and increases mortality (SPS3).

> **Board Pearl:** DOACs are preferred over warfarin for nonvalvular AF. Warfarin remains required for mechanical heart valves and antiphospholipid syndrome. DOACs are contraindicated in mechanical valves (RE-ALIGN trial showed harm with dabigatran).

> **Board Pearl:** Carotid endarterectomy is indicated for symptomatic stenosis of 70% or greater (NASCET). The CREST trial showed similar outcomes for CEA and carotid stenting, but stenting had higher periprocedural stroke risk while CEA had higher MI risk.

> **Board Pearl:** PFO closure is superior to medical therapy alone for secondary stroke prevention in patients under 60 with cryptogenic stroke and high-risk PFO features (atrial septal aneurysm, large shunt). Key trials: RESPECT, CLOSE, DEFENSE-PFO.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 8.1 Early Antiplatelet Therapy: Aspirin

**[BOARD]**

Aspirin is the foundation of acute antiplatelet therapy after ischemic stroke. Two landmark trials established its role:

**IST (International Stroke Trial, 1997)** and **CAST (Chinese Acute Stroke Trial, 1997):**
- Combined enrollment: approximately 40,000 patients
- Aspirin started within 48 hours of stroke onset
- Modest but significant reduction in recurrent ischemic stroke and death
- NNT = 79 for death/dependency avoidance
- Established aspirin as a standard acute stroke treatment

**Aspirin Dosing and Administration:**

| Scenario | Initial Dose | Maintenance Dose | Timing |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|--------|
| Acute ischemic stroke (no tPA) | 160-325 mg loading dose | 50-100 mg daily | Within 24-48 hours |
| Post-tPA | None in first 24 hours | 50-100 mg daily | Start at 24 hours after tPA |
| NPO patient | 300 mg rectal suppository | Oral when swallowing cleared | Immediately |
| Low-risk TIA (ABCD2 <4) | 160-325 mg | 50-100 mg daily | Immediately |

**Key Pharmacology Points:**
- Mechanism: Irreversible COX-1 inhibition blocks thromboxane A2 synthesis
- Duration of platelet inhibition: Lasts the lifetime of the platelet (7-10 days)
- All doses between 50-325 mg/day are equally effective for secondary stroke prevention
- Higher doses increase GI bleeding risk without additional efficacy

> **Board Pearl:** Aspirin is the ONLY antiplatelet or antithrombotic that should be started within the first 24 hours after ischemic stroke (without tPA). It is delayed 24 hours after tPA to minimize hemorrhagic risk. There is no evidence supporting higher doses (>325 mg) for improved efficacy.

### 8.2 Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT)

**[BOARD]**

Short-term DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel for 21 days) is the standard of care for minor ischemic stroke or high-risk TIA, based on three pivotal trials.

**DAPT Trial Comparison:**

| Feature | CHANCE (2013) | POINT (2018) | THALES (2020) |
|---------|---------------|--------------|---------------|
| Population | Minor stroke (NIHSS 3 or less) or high-risk TIA | Minor stroke (NIHSS 3 or less) or high-risk TIA (ABCD2 of 4 or greater) | Minor-moderate stroke (NIHSS 5 or less) or high-risk TIA |
| Time to randomization | Within 24 hours | Within 12 hours | Within 24 hours |
| DAPT regimen | ASA + clopidogrel (300 mg load) | ASA + clopidogrel (600 mg load) | ASA + ticagrelor (180 mg load, then 90 mg BID) |
| DAPT duration | 21 days | 90 days | 30 days |
| After DAPT period | Clopidogrel monotherapy | Clopidogrel monotherapy | ASA monotherapy |
| Primary outcome | 90-day stroke: 8.2% vs 11.7% | Major ischemic events: 5.0% vs 6.5% | Stroke or death: 5.5% vs 6.6% |
| Relative risk reduction | 32% | 25% | 17% |
| Major bleeding | No significant increase | Increased (0.9% vs 0.4%) | Increased (0.5% vs 0.1%) |
| Key finding | 21-day DAPT is effective and safe | Benefit concentrated in first 21 days; bleeding increased after | Ticagrelor alternative; benefit in CYP2C19 LOF carriers |
| Population | 5,170 (predominantly Chinese) | 4,881 (international) | 11,016 (international) |

**INSPIRES Trial (2023):**
- Extended the enrollment window to 72 hours (vs. 12-24 hours in prior trials)
- ASA + clopidogrel within 72 hours was still beneficial
- n=6,100; predominantly Chinese population

**Meta-Analysis of DAPT Trials:**
- DAPT reduces recurrent stroke by approximately 30% compared to aspirin alone
- Absolute major bleeding increase: 0.2-0.4%
- Net clinical benefit favors DAPT for 21 days in the appropriate population

**DAPT Indications and Contraindications:**

| Indication for DAPT | NOT Indicated for DAPT |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| Minor stroke (NIHSS 3 or less) within 24 hours | Moderate-severe stroke (NIHSS >5) |
| High-risk TIA (ABCD2 of 4 or greater) | Cardioembolic stroke (needs anticoagulation) |
| Intracranial large artery atherosclerosis (70-99% stenosis) | Hemorrhagic stroke |
| | Long-term use beyond 21-90 days |

> **Board Pearl:** The optimal DAPT duration for minor stroke/TIA is 21 days (CHANCE protocol). The POINT trial initially used 90 days but post-hoc analysis showed benefit was concentrated in the first 21 days with bleeding risk increasing thereafter. AHA/ASA guidelines recommend 21-day DAPT starting within 24 hours for minor stroke (NIHSS 3 or less) or high-risk TIA.

### 8.3 CYP2C19 Pharmacogenomics and Clopidogrel

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Clopidogrel is a prodrug that requires hepatic conversion to its active metabolite by CYP2C19. Loss-of-function (LOF) alleles (CYP2C19 *2 and *3) result in reduced clopidogrel efficacy.

**CYP2C19 LOF Allele Prevalence:**
- White: approximately 25%
- African American: approximately 30%
- East Asian: approximately 55-60%

**Key Pharmacogenomic Trial Evidence:**

| Trial | Finding |
|-------|---------|
| CHANCE (subanalysis) | Clopidogrel benefit seen only in CYP2C19 noncarriers |
| CHANCE-2 (2021) | Ticagrelor + ASA superior to clopidogrel + ASA in CYP2C19 LOF carriers |
| POINT (subanalysis) | No significant difference by CYP2C19 status (but higher loading dose may have overcome LOF) |

**Clinical Implications:**
- Pharmacogenomic testing is reasonable before starting clopidogrel, particularly in East Asian populations
- If a patient is a CYP2C19 poor metabolizer, consider ticagrelor instead of clopidogrel
- Routine CYP2C19 testing is not yet universally recommended but is increasingly incorporated into clinical practice

### 8.4 Long-Term Antiplatelet Therapy

**[BOARD]**

After the acute DAPT period (21 days for minor stroke/TIA), patients transition to single antiplatelet therapy for long-term secondary prevention.

**Long-Term Antiplatelet Options:**

| Agent | Dose | Key Trial | Relative Efficacy |
|-------|------|-----------|-------------------|
| Aspirin | 50-100 mg daily | IST, CAST | Baseline comparator |
| Clopidogrel | 75 mg daily | CAPRIE | 8.7% RRR vs aspirin for combined vascular events |
| ASA + extended-release dipyridamole (Aggrenox) | 25/200 mg BID | ESPS-2, ESPRIT | Superior to ASA alone |
| Cilostazol | 100 mg BID | CSPS, CSPS II | Reasonable in East Asian populations; less bleeding |

**PRoFESS Trial (2008):**
- Clopidogrel vs. Aggrenox (ASA + dipyridamole)
- No significant difference in recurrent stroke
- Establishes equivalence between the two agents
- Aggrenox causes headache in up to 40% (most common reason for discontinuation)

> **Board Pearl:** Long-term DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel beyond 90 days) is NOT recommended. The SPS3, MATCH, and CHARISMA trials all demonstrated that prolonged DAPT increases bleeding without meaningful additional stroke prevention. The SPS3 trial specifically showed increased MORTALITY with long-term DAPT in lacunar stroke patients.

### 8.5 Long-Term DAPT Harm: Key Negative Trials

**[BOARD]**

| Trial | Population | Duration | Finding |
|-------|-----------|----------|---------|
| SPS3 (2012) | Lacunar stroke | Mean 3.4 years | DAPT increased all-cause mortality and major hemorrhage with no stroke reduction |
| MATCH (2004) | Recent TIA/stroke on clopidogrel | 18 months | Adding aspirin to clopidogrel increased bleeding without reducing ischemic events |
| CHARISMA (2006) | Atherosclerotic disease or risk factors | Median 28 months | No benefit of DAPT in primary prevention; modest benefit in symptomatic subgroup but increased bleeding |

> **Board Pearl:** The SPS3 trial is a frequently tested negative trial. It randomized patients with lacunar stroke to aspirin alone vs. aspirin + clopidogrel for long-term use. Long-term DAPT INCREASED mortality (HR 1.52) in the lacunar stroke population with no reduction in recurrent stroke. This is the definitive evidence against chronic DAPT for secondary stroke prevention.

### 8.6 Anticoagulation for Atrial Fibrillation

**[BOARD]**

Atrial fibrillation is the most common indication for anticoagulation in stroke patients. DOACs have largely replaced warfarin as first-line therapy for nonvalvular AF.

**Warfarin:**

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Mechanism | Vitamin K antagonist; inhibits factors II, VII, IX, X and proteins C, S |
| Target INR | 2.0-3.0 for AF |
| Monitoring | Regular INR checks; narrow therapeutic window |
| Time in therapeutic range (TTR) | Goal >65-70% for adequate protection |
| Advantages | Long track record; reversible with vitamin K, FFP, or PCC; required for mechanical valves and antiphospholipid syndrome |
| Disadvantages | Frequent monitoring; drug-food interactions; slow onset; variable dosing |

**Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs):**

| DOAC | Mechanism | Dose for AF | Key Trial | Half-Life | Renal Elimination |
|------|-----------|------------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Dabigatran (Pradaxa) | Direct thrombin inhibitor | 150 mg BID (75 mg BID if CrCl 15-30; note: 110 mg BID is the European/Canadian dose, not FDA-approved in the US) | RE-LY | 12-17 hours | 80% |
| Rivaroxaban (Xarelto) | Factor Xa inhibitor | 20 mg daily with food (15 mg if CrCl 15-50) | ROCKET-AF | 5-9 hours | 36% |
| Apixaban (Eliquis) | Factor Xa inhibitor | 5 mg BID (2.5 mg BID if 2 of 3: age of 80 or greater, weight 60 kg or less, Cr of 1.5 or greater) | ARISTOTLE | 12 hours | 27% |
| Edoxaban (Savaysa) | Factor Xa inhibitor | 60 mg daily (30 mg if CrCl 15-50, weight 60 kg or less, or P-gp inhibitor) | ENGAGE AF-TIMI 48 | 10-14 hours | 50% |

**DOAC vs. Warfarin Key Findings:**

| Feature | DOACs (as a class) | Warfarin |
|---------|--------------------|---------|
| Stroke/systemic embolism | Similar or superior | Reference |
| Intracranial hemorrhage | Significantly LOWER (approximately 50% reduction) | Higher |
| Major bleeding | Similar or lower | Reference |
| GI bleeding | May be higher (dabigatran 150, rivaroxaban, edoxaban 60) | Lower |
| Monitoring | Not required | Frequent INR monitoring |
| Reversibility | Specific reversal agents available | Vitamin K, FFP, PCC |
| Drug interactions | Fewer | Extensive |

**Warfarin is REQUIRED (DOACs Contraindicated) for:**

| Condition | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| Mechanical heart valves | RE-ALIGN trial: dabigatran caused more thromboembolic AND bleeding events vs warfarin |
| Antiphospholipid syndrome | TRAPS trial: rivaroxaban inferior to warfarin; higher thrombotic events |
| Moderate-severe mitral stenosis | Classified as "valvular AF"; DOAC trials excluded these patients |

> **Board Pearl:** DOACs are preferred over warfarin for most patients with nonvalvular AF and stroke. The critical exceptions requiring warfarin are mechanical heart valves (RE-ALIGN showed harm with dabigatran) and antiphospholipid syndrome (TRAPS showed harm with rivaroxaban). Know these two exceptions for board examinations.

### 8.7 Timing of Anticoagulation After Ischemic Stroke

**[BOARD]**

Early anticoagulation after ischemic stroke must balance the risk of recurrent embolism against the risk of hemorrhagic transformation. The traditional approach uses infarct size to guide timing.

**The 1-3-6-12 Day Rule (Traditional Framework):**

| Infarct Size | Start Anticoagulation | Rationale |
|-------------|----------------------|-----------|
| TIA (no infarct) | Day 1 (immediately) | No parenchymal injury; high early recurrence risk |
| Small infarct | Days 3-4 (24-96 hours) | Low hemorrhagic transformation risk |
| Moderate infarct | Days 6-7 (4-7 days) | Moderate hemorrhagic transformation risk |
| Large infarct | Days 12-14 (7-14 days) | High hemorrhagic transformation risk; peak edema period |

**Aspirin Bridging:**
- If anticoagulation is delayed beyond 48 hours, bridge with aspirin until anticoagulation is initiated
- Once anticoagulation starts, aspirin is typically discontinued (unless there is a concomitant indication such as recent coronary stent)

**CATALYST Meta-Analysis (Dehbi et al., Lancet 2025):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Design | Patient-level meta-analysis of 4 RCTs (ELAN, TIMING, OPTIMAS, START) |
| Population | 5,400+ patients with AF-related stroke |
| Comparison | Early DOAC (within 4 days) vs. later DOAC (7-14 days) |
| Primary outcome | Composite of recurrent ischemic stroke and sICH at 30 days |
| Results | Early DOAC: 2.1% vs Later: 3.0% (favoring early initiation) |
| sICH risk | No significant increase with early DOAC |
| Conclusion | Early DOAC initiation (within 4 days) appears safe for mild-to-moderate ischemic stroke |

**ELAN Trial (Fischer et al., NEJM 2023):**
- Early (within 48 hours) vs. later (days 6-7) direct anticoagulation with DOACs
- Composite of recurrent ischemic stroke, systemic embolism, major extracranial bleeding, symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage, or vascular death: 2.9% early vs. 4.1% later
- Non-significant trend favoring early initiation

> **Board Pearl:** The CATALYST meta-analysis supports early DOAC initiation (within 4 days) for mild-to-moderate AF-related ischemic stroke. This has shifted practice toward earlier anticoagulation than the traditional 1-3-6-12 day rule. However, large infarcts with hemorrhagic transformation or mass effect still warrant delayed anticoagulation. Aspirin should bridge the gap if anticoagulation is delayed beyond 48 hours.

### 8.8 Early Anticoagulation: What NOT to Do

**[BOARD]**

Early full-dose anticoagulation is NOT recommended for:

| Scenario | Evidence |
|----------|---------|
| Unselected acute ischemic stroke | IST trial: heparin reduced ischemic stroke but increased hemorrhagic stroke; net zero benefit |
| Acute AF-related stroke (with heparin) | 2007 meta-analysis: reduced ischemic stroke but increased sICH; no net benefit |
| Progressing stroke | TOAST trial: heparin did NOT halt progression of stroke symptoms |
| ESUS (Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source) | NAVIGATE ESUS, RE-SPECT ESUS: rivaroxaban and dabigatran NOT superior to aspirin |

**Anticoagulation IS Appropriate Acutely For:**
- Intracardiac thrombus (LV thrombus, mechanical valve with recent embolism)
- Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (even with hemorrhagic component)
- Weight-based heparin without bolus (for selected cardioembolic indications)

### 8.9 Statin Therapy

**[BOARD]**

Statins should be started or continued as soon as possible after acute ischemic stroke. Statin discontinuation during hospitalization is associated with decreased survival.

**SPARCL Trial (Stroke Prevention by Aggressive Reduction in Cholesterol Levels, 2006):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Drug | Atorvastatin 80 mg daily |
| Population | Patients with recent stroke or TIA, no known CAD |
| Primary outcome | Reduced recurrent fatal or nonfatal stroke by 16% (HR 0.84) |
| Secondary outcomes | Reduced major cardiovascular events; reduced need for revascularization |
| Side effect | Slight increase in hemorrhagic stroke (55 vs 33 events), primarily in patients with prior hemorrhagic stroke |
| LDL target achieved | Approximately 73 mg/dL |

**Statin Pleiotropic Effects (Beyond Cholesterol Lowering):**

| Effect | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Endothelial function improvement | Upregulates endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) |
| Anti-inflammatory | Reduces CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha; stabilizes atherosclerotic plaque |
| Antithrombotic | Reduces tissue factor expression; decreases platelet reactivity |
| Antioxidant | Reduces oxidative stress in vessel walls |
| Plaque stabilization | Increases fibrous cap thickness; reduces lipid core |
| Neuroprotective | May reduce infarct volume in animal models |

> **Board Pearl:** Start high-intensity statins (atorvastatin 40-80 mg or rosuvastatin 20-40 mg) as soon as possible after ischemic stroke. The SPARCL trial demonstrated a 16% relative risk reduction in recurrent stroke with atorvastatin 80 mg. Do NOT discontinue statins during acute hospitalization, as discontinuation is associated with worse outcomes. The target LDL for high-risk vascular patients is less than 70 mg/dL.

### 8.10 Carotid Endarterectomy (CEA)

**[BOARD]**

Carotid endarterectomy is the surgical removal of atherosclerotic plaque from the carotid bifurcation. It is one of the most well-studied surgical procedures in medicine.

**NASCET (North American Symptomatic Carotid Endarterectomy Trial, 1991):**

| Stenosis Degree | Benefit of CEA | 2-Year Stroke Rate (CEA vs Medical) |
|----------------|---------------|-------------------------------------|
| 70-99% symptomatic | STRONG benefit | 9% vs 26% (ARR 17%) |
| 50-69% symptomatic | MODERATE benefit | 15.7% vs 22.2% (ARR 6.5%) |
| <50% symptomatic | NO benefit | No difference |

**ACAS (Asymptomatic Carotid Atherosclerosis Study, 1995):**
- Asymptomatic stenosis of 60% or greater
- Modest benefit of CEA (5-year stroke risk: 5.1% vs 11.0%)
- NNT = 17 over 5 years
- Benefit only if surgical complication rate is less than 3%

**Timing of CEA After Stroke:**
- Optimal timing: Within 2 weeks of the index event (early CEA)
- Benefit decreases with delay (highest recurrence risk in first 2-4 weeks)
- Avoid surgery within the first 48-72 hours of large stroke (hemorrhagic transformation risk)

**CEA Surgical Pearls:**
- Perioperative stroke rate should be less than 6% for symptomatic stenosis and less than 3% for asymptomatic stenosis
- Most common cranial nerve injury: vagus nerve (recurrent laryngeal branch) causing hoarseness
- Other nerve injuries: hypoglossal (tongue deviation), marginal mandibular branch of facial nerve
- Hyperperfusion syndrome: headache, seizures, and ICH post-CEA due to sudden restoration of flow to chronically hypoperfused brain

### 8.11 Carotid Artery Stenting (CAS) vs. CEA

**[BOARD]**

**CREST Trial (Carotid Revascularization Endarterectomy vs. Stenting Trial, 2010):**

| Feature | CEA | CAS |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Primary endpoint (composite) | Similar (6.8% vs 7.2%) | Similar |
| Periprocedural stroke | LOWER (2.3%) | HIGHER (4.1%) |
| Periprocedural MI | HIGHER (2.3%) | LOWER (1.1%) |
| Age interaction | Better in patients of 70 or older | Better in patients under 70 |
| Cranial nerve injury | Present (approximately 4%) | Absent |
| Long-term restenosis | Lower | Higher |

**Current Indications for CAS Over CEA:**

| Favor CAS | Favor CEA |
|-----------|-----------|
| Age under 70 | Age 70 or older |
| High surgical risk (prior neck radiation, contralateral occlusion, recurrent stenosis after CEA) | Standard surgical risk |
| Tandem intracranial lesion | Accessible bifurcation anatomy |
| Prior radical neck dissection | Long, heavily calcified lesion |

> **Board Pearl:** CREST showed equivalent composite outcomes for CEA and CAS, but the individual components differed: CAS had a higher periprocedural stroke rate, while CEA had a higher MI rate. Patients over 70 did better with CEA; patients under 70 did better with CAS. For most symptomatic patients with standard surgical risk, CEA remains preferred.

### 8.12 PFO Closure

**[BOARD]**

Patent foramen ovale (PFO) is present in approximately 25% of the general population but is found in up to 40-50% of young patients with cryptogenic stroke. Paradoxical embolism through the PFO is the proposed mechanism.

**PFO Closure Trial Summary:**

| Trial | Year | Device | Population | Result |
|-------|------|--------|-----------|--------|
| RESPECT | 2017 (extended follow-up) | Amplatzer PFO Occluder | Age 18-60, cryptogenic stroke, PFO | HR 0.55 for recurrent stroke favoring closure |
| CLOSE | 2017 | Multiple devices | Age 16-60, PFO with atrial septal aneurysm OR large shunt | HR 0.03 (dramatic reduction; NNT = 20 over 5 years) |
| DEFENSE-PFO | 2018 | Amplatzer PFO Occluder | Cryptogenic stroke + high-risk PFO features | 0% vs 12.9% recurrent stroke at 2 years |
| REDUCE | 2017 | GORE HELEX/CARDIOFORM | Age 18-59, cryptogenic stroke, PFO | HR 0.23 for recurrent stroke |

**Criteria for PFO Closure Consideration:**

| Criterion | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Age | 18-60 years (best evidence) |
| Stroke type | Cryptogenic (extensive workup excludes other causes) |
| PFO features | High-risk features: atrial septal aneurysm, large shunt (>20 microbubbles), hypermobility of septum |
| Imaging | Embolic-appearing infarct pattern (cortical, wedge-shaped) |
| No AF | Extended cardiac monitoring must exclude paroxysmal AF |
| Not ESUS alone | Must meet criteria for PFO-attributable stroke |

> **Board Pearl:** PFO closure reduces recurrent stroke in patients under 60 with cryptogenic stroke and high-risk PFO features (atrial septal aneurysm or large shunt). The CLOSE trial showed the most dramatic benefit. Remember: PFO closure is NOT indicated for all patients with PFO and stroke. It requires cryptogenic mechanism after thorough workup AND high-risk PFO anatomy. New-onset AF after device closure occurs in approximately 4-5% and is usually self-limited.

### 8.13 Rivaroxaban + Aspirin for Atherosclerotic Disease

**[BOARD]**

**COMPASS Trial (Eikelboom et al., NEJM 2017):**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| Population | Stable atherosclerotic vascular disease (CAD, PAD, or carotid disease) |
| Regimen | Rivaroxaban 2.5 mg BID + ASA 100 mg daily vs. ASA 100 mg alone |
| Primary outcome | Major adverse cardiovascular events (stroke, MI, CV death) reduced by 24% |
| Stroke reduction | 42% relative reduction in ischemic stroke |
| Bleeding | Major bleeding increased (3.1% vs 1.9%) but NO increase in fatal or intracranial bleeding |
| Net clinical benefit | Favored combination therapy |
| Applicable population | Stable polyvascular atherosclerotic disease |

**Prior Stroke Subgroup:**
- Patients with prior stroke had even greater absolute risk reduction (ARR 2.8% vs 1.3% overall)

> **Board Pearl:** The COMPASS trial established rivaroxaban 2.5 mg BID + aspirin as an option for patients with stable atherosclerotic vascular disease. This is NOT a standard first-line stroke secondary prevention regimen but is applicable to patients with polyvascular disease (e.g., carotid stenosis + PAD + CAD). The rivaroxaban dose is 2.5 mg BID (much lower than the 20 mg daily AF dose).

### 8.14 Reversal Agents

**[BOARD]**

**Anticoagulant Reversal Agent Table:**

| Anticoagulant | Reversal Agent | Mechanism | Dose | Onset | Notes |
|---------------|---------------|-----------|------|-------|-------|
| Warfarin | Vitamin K (phytonadione) | Replenishes vitamin K for factor synthesis | 10 mg IV | 6-24 hours | Slow onset; use PCC or FFP for immediate reversal |
| Warfarin | 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) | Provides factors II, VII, IX, X | 25-50 units/kg (INR-based) | Minutes | Preferred for urgent reversal; faster and more predictable than FFP |
| Warfarin | Fresh frozen plasma (FFP) | Provides all clotting factors | 10-15 mL/kg | 30-60 min | Large volume; requires thawing; risk of volume overload |
| Dabigatran | Idarucizumab (Praxbind) | Monoclonal antibody fragment binding dabigatran | 5 g IV (2 x 2.5 g vials) | Minutes | Specific reversal; RE-VERSE AD trial |
| Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban | Andexanet alfa (Andexxa) | Recombinant modified factor Xa decoy | Low or high dose (based on agent/timing) | Minutes | Expensive; rebound anticoagulation may occur; ANNEXA-4 trial |
| All DOACs (if specific agent unavailable) | 4-factor PCC | Non-specific procoagulant effect | 50 units/kg | Minutes | Off-label; reasonable alternative if specific reversal agent unavailable |
| Heparin (UFH) | Protamine sulfate | Binds and neutralizes heparin | 1 mg per 100 units of heparin (max 50 mg) | Minutes | Risk of hypotension, bradycardia, anaphylaxis (fish allergy) |
| LMWH (enoxaparin) | Protamine sulfate | Partial reversal (60-80% of anti-Xa activity) | 1 mg per 1 mg enoxaparin (if within 8 hours) | Minutes | Incomplete reversal; anti-Xa monitoring |
| Aspirin | Platelet transfusion | Provides functional platelets | 1-2 units | 30-60 min | Controversial; limited evidence of benefit in ICH |
| Clopidogrel | Platelet transfusion | Provides functional platelets | 1-2 units | 30-60 min | Same limitations as aspirin reversal |
| tPA (alteplase) | Cryoprecipitate | Provides fibrinogen and factor VIII | 10 units IV | 30-60 min | If fibrinogen <200 mg/dL |
| tPA (alteplase) | Tranexamic acid (TXA) | Antifibrinolytic; blocks plasmin | 1 g IV over 10 min | Minutes | Stabilizes clot by preventing fibrinolysis |

> **Board Pearl:** Know the specific reversal agents: idarucizumab for dabigatran and andexanet alfa for factor Xa inhibitors (rivaroxaban, apixaban, edoxaban). If specific reversal is unavailable, 4-factor PCC (50 units/kg) is a reasonable alternative for all DOACs. For warfarin, 4-factor PCC is preferred over FFP for urgent reversal due to faster onset and more predictable response.

### 8.15 ESUS and Anticoagulation: Negative Trials

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Embolic Stroke of Undetermined Source (ESUS) was proposed as a clinical entity that might benefit from anticoagulation over antiplatelet therapy. However, two major trials were negative:

| Trial | Drug | Comparator | Result |
|-------|------|-----------|--------|
| NAVIGATE ESUS (2018) | Rivaroxaban 15 mg daily | Aspirin 100 mg daily | No benefit; increased bleeding with rivaroxaban |
| RE-SPECT ESUS (2019) | Dabigatran 110 mg BID or 150 mg BID | Aspirin 100 mg daily | No significant difference; trend toward more GI bleeding |

**Conclusion:** Anticoagulation is NOT indicated for ESUS. Antiplatelet therapy remains standard unless a specific cardioembolic source (occult AF, PFO) is identified through further workup.

### 8.16 Blood Pressure Control for Secondary Prevention

**[COMPREHENSIVE]**

Long-term blood pressure control is the single most important modifiable risk factor for recurrent stroke.

**Key Evidence:**
- SPS3 (lacunar stroke): Targeting SBP <130 showed a trend toward reduced recurrent stroke vs. SBP 130-149 (not statistically significant for primary endpoint but reduced ICH)
- PROGRESS trial: Perindopril + indapamide reduced recurrent stroke by 43%
- SPRINT (general population): Intensive BP control (SBP <120) reduced CV events but was not specific to stroke patients

**Current Targets:**
- Resume antihypertensives after 48-72 hours of stroke onset if BP exceeds 140/90
- Long-term target: SBP less than 130/80 for most stroke patients (AHA/ASA 2024 guidelines)
- ACE inhibitors or ARBs combined with thiazide diuretics have the strongest evidence

## Board-Style Review Questions

**Question 1:** A 58-year-old man presents with a minor ischemic stroke (NIHSS 2) 18 hours ago. CT shows a small subcortical infarct. He has no atrial fibrillation. What is the most appropriate antiplatelet regimen?

*Answer:* DAPT with aspirin (loading dose 160-325 mg) plus clopidogrel (loading dose 300 mg) for 21 days, followed by single antiplatelet therapy. This is based on the CHANCE and POINT trials for minor stroke (NIHSS 3 or less) started within 24 hours. Although he is at 18 hours, the INSPIRES trial supports DAPT initiation up to 72 hours.

**Question 2:** A 72-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation has a moderate-sized ischemic stroke (ASPECTS 7). When should anticoagulation be started?

*Answer:* Based on the CATALYST meta-analysis and ELAN trial, early DOAC initiation within 3-4 days appears safe for mild-to-moderate strokes. For this moderate infarct, starting a DOAC at days 3-4 is reasonable. Bridge with aspirin until anticoagulation begins. The traditional 1-3-6-12 day rule would suggest days 6-7 for a moderate infarct, but recent evidence supports earlier initiation.

**Question 3:** A 45-year-old patient with antiphospholipid syndrome has a recurrent ischemic stroke despite rivaroxaban therapy. What change should be made?

*Answer:* Switch to warfarin with target INR 2-3. The TRAPS trial demonstrated that rivaroxaban was inferior to warfarin in antiphospholipid syndrome, with higher rates of thrombotic events. DOACs are contraindicated in antiphospholipid syndrome; warfarin is the required anticoagulant.
