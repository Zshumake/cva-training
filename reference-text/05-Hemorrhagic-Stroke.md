## Section 1: Overview of Hemorrhagic Stroke

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Hemorrhagic stroke accounts for approximately 13% of all strokes but is responsible for a disproportionately high percentage of stroke-related deaths and disability. Despite being less common than ischemic stroke, hemorrhagic stroke carries substantially higher acute mortality.

**Two Major Types:**

| Feature | Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH) | Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH) |
|---------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Percentage of all strokes | ~10% | ~3% |
| Location of bleeding | Within brain parenchyma | Between arachnoid and pia mater (subarachnoid space) |
| Most common cause | Hypertension | Ruptured saccular (berry) aneurysm |
| Classic presentation | Sudden focal deficit + headache + vomiting; gradual progression over minutes to hours | "Worst headache of my life" + neck stiffness; sudden onset, often during exertion |
| CT appearance | Hyperdense (bright white) mass within brain parenchyma | Blood in basal cisterns and sulci |
| 30-day mortality | 30-50% | 40-50% |
| Key complication | Hematoma expansion, herniation from mass effect | Vasospasm and delayed cerebral ischemia (days 3-14) |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Acute blood appears BRIGHT (hyperdense, white) on non-contrast CT. This is the opposite of acute ischemic stroke, which appears dark (hypodense) or may not be visible at all in the first 6-12 hours. A non-contrast CT is the first imaging study in any acute stroke because the primary decision point is: is there blood (hemorrhagic) or not (ischemic)? Blood, bone, and contrast all appear bright on CT, which is why contrast must be avoided in the initial evaluation.

**Hemorrhagic Stroke Prognosis vs. Ischemic Stroke:**

An important concept for rehabilitation physicians: hemorrhagic strokes have higher acute mortality than ischemic strokes, but survivors of hemorrhagic stroke tend to have better functional outcomes than survivors of ischemic stroke of comparable initial severity. This occurs because hemorrhagic strokes cause tissue damage partly through mass effect and edema rather than through direct neuronal death, and as the hematoma is resorbed and edema resolves, some of the initial neurological deficit recovers.

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hemorrhagic stroke = higher acute mortality BUT better functional recovery if the patient survives the acute phase. This is a frequently tested concept. Patients and families should be counseled that while the initial period is dangerous, survivors often achieve meaningful recovery.

## Section 2: Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

ICH results from rupture of a blood vessel directly into the brain parenchyma, forming a hematoma that causes damage through direct tissue destruction, mass effect on surrounding structures, and elevated intracranial pressure.

**Etiology:**

| Cause | Percentage | Key Details |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Hypertension | Most common overall | Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms of lenticulostriate and other small perforating arteries |
| Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA) | Most common in elderly/normotensive | Lobar hemorrhages; recurrent; associated with Alzheimer disease |
| Anticoagulant therapy | Increasingly common | Warfarin, DOACs, heparin; highest mortality ICH subtype |
| Vascular malformations | Variable | AVM, cavernous malformations |
| Hemorrhagic transformation of ischemic stroke | Variable | Particularly after thrombolysis |
| Brain tumor (hemorrhagic metastasis) | Uncommon | Melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, choriocarcinoma, thyroid |
| Drug use | Variable | Cocaine and amphetamines (acute hypertensive surge, vasospasm) |
| Coagulopathy | Variable | Thrombocytopenia, DIC, liver failure |

**ICH Locations and Associations:**

| Location | Frequency | Usual Cause | Clinical Features |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Putamen/basal ganglia** | 35-50% (most common) | Hypertension | Contralateral hemiparesis, hemisensory loss, homonymous hemianopia, aphasia or neglect depending on hemisphere |
| **Thalamus** | 10-15% | Hypertension | Contralateral sensory loss, upgaze palsy ("eyes look at the nose"), aphasia or neglect; may extend into ventricles causing IVH and hydrocephalus |
| **Pons** | 5-10% | Hypertension | Rapidly fatal in many cases; coma, quadriplegia, pinpoint reactive pupils, hyperthermia, decerebrate posturing |
| **Cerebellum** | 5-10% | Hypertension | Sudden ataxia, vertigo, headache, vomiting; may compress brainstem; SURGICAL EMERGENCY if >3 cm |
| **Lobar (cortical)** | 20-30% | Amyloid angiopathy, AVM | Varies by lobe; more common in elderly normotensive patients; recurrence risk is high with CAA |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The putamen is the most common site of hypertensive ICH because it is supplied by the lenticulostriate arteries, the vessels most susceptible to hypertensive damage (Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms). Cerebellar hemorrhage >3 cm is the most important ICH to recognize because it is a surgical emergency -- posterior fossa decompression can be life-saving. The question "when does ICH require surgical intervention" almost always points to cerebellar hemorrhage with brainstem compression.

**Thalamic Hemorrhage Eye Findings:**

Thalamic hemorrhage characteristically produces downward and inward deviation of the eyes (upgaze palsy with convergence), sometimes described as "eyes looking at the nose" or "eyes looking at the tip of the nose." This results from compression of the dorsal midbrain vertical gaze center. Extension of the hemorrhage into the third ventricle and then the fourth ventricle can cause acute obstructive hydrocephalus.

**Pontine Hemorrhage:**

Pontine hemorrhage is one of the most devastating forms of ICH. Massive pontine hemorrhage produces a characteristic presentation: rapid onset of coma, quadriplegia, pinpoint but reactive pupils (due to disruption of descending sympathetic fibers, but parasympathetic pupillary constriction pathway in the midbrain is preserved), bilateral decerebrate posturing, hyperthermia, and loss of horizontal eye movements. The mortality rate is extremely high.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Hematoma Expansion:**

Hematoma expansion is the leading cause of early neurological deterioration in ICH. Approximately one-third of ICH patients experience significant hematoma growth (>33% volume increase or >6 mL absolute increase) within the first 24 hours, with most expansion occurring in the first 6 hours. Hematoma expansion is associated with worse functional outcomes and higher mortality.

Predictors of hematoma expansion include:
- CT angiography "spot sign" (active contrast extravasation within the hematoma)
- Anticoagulant use
- Presentation within 3 hours of onset
- Larger initial hematoma volume
- Irregular hematoma shape

This is the rationale for early blood pressure control and anticoagulant reversal: limiting hematoma expansion is the primary treatable factor in acute ICH management.

## Section 3: ICH Score

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The ICH Score is a validated clinical grading scale that predicts 30-day mortality after intracerebral hemorrhage. It is the most widely used prognostic tool for ICH and is frequently tested on board examinations.

**ICH Score Components (Total: 0-6 points):**

| Component | Criteria | Points |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| GCS score 3-4 | Severe impairment of consciousness | 2 |
| GCS score 5-12 | Moderate impairment of consciousness | 1 |
| GCS score 13-15 | Mild or no impairment | 0 |
| ICH volume >= 30 mL | Large hematoma (measured by ABC/2 method) | 1 |
| ICH volume < 30 mL | Smaller hematoma | 0 |
| Intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) present | Blood extension into ventricles | 1 |
| Infratentorial origin | Brainstem or cerebellar location | 1 |
| Age >= 80 years | Elderly patient | 1 |

**ICH Score and 30-Day Mortality:**

| ICH Score | 30-Day Mortality | Clinical Interpretation |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| 0 | 0% | Excellent prognosis |
| 1 | 13% | Good prognosis |
| 2 | 26% | Moderate prognosis |
| 3 | 72% | Poor prognosis |
| 4 | 97% | Very poor prognosis |
| 5 | 100% | Uniformly fatal in original study |
| 6 | 100% | Uniformly fatal |

> **Clinical Pearl:** An ICH Score of 5 or 6 is associated with 100% 30-day mortality in the original validation study. However, this score should NOT be used in isolation for withdrawal-of-care decisions because (1) self-fulfilling prophecy bias may inflate mortality (if care is withdrawn based on the score, the prediction becomes self-fulfilling), and (2) individual patients may outperform the statistical prediction. The ICH Score is a prognostic tool for counseling, not a deterministic predictor.

**ABC/2 Method for Hematoma Volume:**

The ABC/2 method is a bedside estimation of hematoma volume from CT:
- A = largest diameter of hemorrhage on the CT slice with the largest area (cm)
- B = diameter perpendicular to A on the same slice (cm)
- C = number of CT slices showing hemorrhage multiplied by slice thickness (cm)
- Volume = (A x B x C) / 2 (in mL or cm3)

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**FUNC Score:**

The FUNC Score is a complementary prognostic tool that predicts functional outcome (independence) rather than mortality. It considers ICH volume, age, ICH location, GCS score, and pre-ICH cognitive impairment. The FUNC Score may be more useful than the ICH Score for rehabilitation counseling because it addresses the question patients and families care about most: "Will I regain independence?"

## Section 4: ICH Management

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Blood Pressure Management:**

| Clinical Scenario | Target | Evidence |
|------------------|--------|---------|
| SBP 150-220 mmHg | Lower SBP to <140 mmHg within 1 hour | INTERACT2: trend toward benefit; improved functional outcomes on ordinal analysis |
| SBP >220 mmHg | Lower to <220 first, then gradual reduction to 140-160 | Aggressive immediate lowering may cause renal injury |

**Key Blood Pressure Trials:**

| Trial | Comparison | Key Finding |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **INTERACT2** | Intensive (<140) vs. guideline (<180) BP lowering | Trend toward benefit with intensive lowering; improved functional outcomes on ordinal shift analysis (secondary endpoint) |
| **ATACH-2** | Intensive (<140) vs. standard (<180) BP lowering | No benefit from intensive target; intensive group had INCREASED renal adverse events |

> **Clinical Pearl:** ATACH-2 effectively ended the push for very aggressive BP lowering (below 140) in ICH. While INTERACT2 suggested a trend toward benefit, ATACH-2 found no additional benefit with harm (renal injury). Current practice targets SBP <140 but avoids excessively rapid or aggressive reduction. For board exams, know that aggressive BP lowering to <140 is "safe but not proven beneficial" and carries risk of renal injury.

**Anticoagulant Reversal:**

This is one of the most high-yield ICH management topics for board examinations. When a patient on anticoagulation develops ICH, emergent reversal is critical to limit hematoma expansion.

| Anticoagulant | Reversal Agent | Details | Onset |
|--------------|---------------|---------|-------|
| **Warfarin** | 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) + IV vitamin K 10 mg | PCC preferred over FFP (faster, more complete reversal, smaller volume); target INR <1.4 within 1 hour | PCC: minutes; Vitamin K: 6-12 hours |
| **Dabigatran** (direct thrombin inhibitor) | Idarucizumab (Praxbind) | Specific reversal agent; 5g IV; immediate and complete reversal | Within minutes |
| **Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban** (factor Xa inhibitors) | Andexanet alfa (Andexxa) | Recombinant modified factor Xa; binds and sequesters Xa inhibitors | Within minutes |
| **Heparin** (unfractionated) | Protamine sulfate | 1 mg protamine per 100 units of heparin given in prior 2-3 hours | Within minutes |
| **Enoxaparin** (LMWH) | Protamine sulfate (partial reversal) | Only ~60% effective at reversing LMWH | Partial effect |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The reversal agent table is one of the most commonly tested ICH topics. Know the specific antidote for each anticoagulant: Warfarin = 4-factor PCC + vitamin K. Dabigatran = idarucizumab. Factor Xa inhibitors (rivaroxaban, apixaban) = andexanet alfa. Heparin = protamine sulfate. PCC is preferred over FFP for warfarin reversal because it is faster, provides more complete reversal, and avoids the large volume needed for FFP (reducing the risk of volume overload and TRALI).

**Hemostatic Therapies:**

| Therapy | Trial | Key Finding |
|---------|-------|-------------|
| Platelet transfusion | PATCH trial | HARMFUL -- potential worsening of outcomes; do NOT give platelet transfusions for ICH (even if on antiplatelet therapy) |
| Recombinant Factor VIIa (rFVIIa) | FAST trial, FASTEST trial | Reduces hematoma growth BUT does NOT improve clinical outcomes (no mortality or functional benefit) |
| Tranexamic acid | TICH-2 trial | No functional outcome benefit |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Three critical "do NOTs" for ICH hemostatic therapy: (1) Do NOT give platelet transfusions (PATCH trial showed harm). (2) rFVIIa shrinks hematomas but does NOT improve outcomes (FAST trial). (3) Tranexamic acid does NOT improve outcomes (TICH-2). These are frequently tested as "which of the following is NOT recommended" type questions.

**Surgical Management:**

| Scenario | Evidence | Recommendation |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| Cerebellar hemorrhage >3 cm or with brainstem compression | Strong clinical consensus | SURGICAL DECOMPRESSION indicated -- life-saving |
| Lobar ICH with clinical deterioration | ENRICH trial | Minimally invasive surgery (endoscopic evacuation) IMPROVED outcomes for lobar ICH |
| Supratentorial ICH (general) | STICH, STICH II | Open craniotomy showed NO clear benefit for routine supratentorial ICH |
| IVH with hydrocephalus | Clinical consensus | External ventricular drain (EVD) placement |

> **Clinical Pearl:** ENRICH trial (2024) is a landmark: minimally invasive parafascicular surgery improved outcomes for lobar ICH. This is in contrast to the negative STICH and STICH II trials of open craniotomy. The key distinction: minimally invasive surgery benefits lobar ICH, while open craniotomy does not benefit routine supratentorial ICH. Cerebellar hemorrhage >3 cm remains a clear surgical indication regardless of approach.

**Other ICH Management Principles:**

| Intervention | Recommendation |
|-------------|----------------|
| Seizure prophylaxis | NOT recommended routinely; treat seizures when they occur |
| ICP management | Head of bed elevation to 30 degrees; mild sedation; osmotic therapy (hypertonic saline or mannitol); target ICP <20 mmHg, CPP >60 mmHg |
| Temperature management | Maintain normothermia; treat fever aggressively |
| DVT prophylaxis | Intermittent pneumatic compression devices immediately; pharmacological prophylaxis (LMWH) can begin after 24-48 hours once hematoma stability is confirmed |
| Glucose management | Avoid hyperglycemia; target 140-180 mg/dL |
| Dysphagia screening | Before any oral intake; aspiration risk is high |

## Section 5: Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

CAA is the most common cause of spontaneous lobar hemorrhage in elderly normotensive patients. It is caused by deposition of beta-amyloid protein in the walls of small and medium cortical and leptomeningeal vessels, weakening them and predisposing to rupture.

**Key Features:**

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Demographics | Elderly (>65 years); prevalence increases with age |
| Location | LOBAR (cortical) hemorrhages exclusively; SPARES deep structures (putamen, thalamus, brainstem, cerebellum) |
| Pattern | Recurrent hemorrhages in DIFFERENT lobar locations over time |
| Associated condition | Alzheimer disease (same beta-amyloid pathology) |
| MRI finding | Multiple cortical and subcortical microbleeds on GRE/SWI sequences (gradient echo/susceptibility-weighted imaging) |
| Diagnosis | Boston criteria: probable CAA = multiple lobar hemorrhages in patient >=55 with no other cause |
| Anticoagulation | Relatively CONTRAINDICATED due to high rebleeding risk |

**CAA vs. Hypertensive ICH:**

| Feature | Hypertensive ICH | Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Typical patient | Hypertensive, any age (but older) | Elderly, normotensive |
| Location | DEEP structures (putamen, thalamus, pons, cerebellum) | LOBAR (cortical) locations |
| Recurrence pattern | Single event (unless HTN remains uncontrolled) | Multiple recurrent lobar hemorrhages over time |
| Microbleed pattern on MRI | Deep/infratentorial microbleeds | Cortical/subcortical (lobar) microbleeds |
| Associated conditions | Chronic hypertension, diabetes | Alzheimer disease, progressive dementia |
| Prevention strategy | Blood pressure control | Avoid anticoagulation; no specific prevention |

> **Clinical Pearl:** If a board question describes a normotensive elderly patient with recurrent lobar hemorrhages and multiple cortical microbleeds on MRI, the answer is cerebral amyloid angiopathy. If the question describes a hypertensive patient with a deep hemorrhage (putamen, thalamus, pons, or cerebellum), the answer is hypertensive ICH. Location (lobar vs. deep) and patient profile (normotensive elderly vs. hypertensive) are the key distinguishing factors.

## Section 6: Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

SAH accounts for approximately 3% of all strokes and results from bleeding into the subarachnoid space (between the arachnoid and pia mater). The most common cause is rupture of a saccular (berry) aneurysm at the Circle of Willis. SAH carries extremely high mortality and morbidity: approximately 40-50% of patients die within 30 days, and approximately 50% of survivors have significant long-term disability.

**Classic Presentation:**
- Sudden-onset, severe headache (described as the "worst headache of my life") in approximately 97% of cases
- Onset is often during exertion (weightlifting, straining, sexual activity, or Valsalva maneuver)
- Neck stiffness/meningismus (blood irritates the meninges)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Photophobia
- Altered consciousness (variable)
- Focal neurological deficits are typically ABSENT initially unless there is concurrent intraparenchymal hemorrhage

**Sentinel Headache:**

Approximately 30% of patients with major SAH report a preceding "sentinel headache" or "warning leak" in the days to weeks before the catastrophic rupture. This sentinel headache likely represents a minor leakage from the aneurysm. It is often misdiagnosed as migraine or tension headache, representing a critical missed opportunity for intervention.

**Diagnosis:**

| Test | Timing | Sensitivity | Key Findings |
|------|--------|------------|-------------|
| Non-contrast CT | First 6 hours | ~95-100% | Hyperdense blood in basal cisterns, Sylvian fissures, interhemispheric fissure |
| Non-contrast CT | After 24 hours | Declines to ~80% | Sensitivity decreases as blood is resorbed |
| Lumbar puncture | If CT is negative | High | Xanthochromia (yellow CSF from bilirubin, indicating blood breakdown products); elevated RBC count that does NOT clear with successive tubes |
| CT angiography | After SAH confirmed | High | Identifies the culprit aneurysm and its morphology |
| Digital subtraction angiography (DSA) | Gold standard | Highest | Definitive identification of aneurysm; allows for simultaneous endovascular treatment |

> **Clinical Pearl:** If clinical suspicion for SAH is high but the CT is negative (which occurs in approximately 5-10% of SAH cases, particularly when imaging is delayed), a lumbar puncture MUST be performed. Xanthochromia (yellow discoloration from bilirubin, a product of hemoglobin degradation) is the key LP finding that distinguishes true SAH from a traumatic tap. Xanthochromia takes approximately 6-12 hours to develop, so very early LP may miss it.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Aneurysm Locations:**

| Location | Frequency | Clinical Significance |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| AComm (anterior communicating artery) | ~30-35% | Most common aneurysm site overall; may compress optic chiasm (bitemporal hemianopia) |
| PComm (posterior communicating artery) | ~25-30% | Second most common; classically compresses CN III (ipsilateral pupil dilation, ptosis, "down and out" eye) |
| MCA bifurcation | ~20% | Third most common; may present with focal cortical deficits |
| Basilar tip | ~5-10% | May compress brainstem or CN III; high surgical complexity |
| ICA (various segments) | ~5-10% | May involve cavernous sinus |

> **Clinical Pearl:** "A is for AComm and is the most common" -- the anterior communicating artery is the most common site for saccular aneurysms and SAH. PComm aneurysm classically produces a CN III palsy: the parasympathetic fibers run on the OUTSIDE of the nerve and are compressed first, producing a "blown pupil" (mydriasis) as the earliest sign. This is followed by ptosis (levator palpebrae) and "down and out" eye deviation (unopposed lateral rectus and superior oblique). A CN III palsy with pupil involvement = compressive/surgical cause (aneurysm) until proven otherwise, while a CN III palsy that SPARES the pupil suggests a medical cause (diabetes, ischemia).

**Risk Factors for Aneurysm Rupture:**

| Risk Factor | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Aneurysm size | Larger aneurysms have higher rupture risk (especially >7 mm) |
| Location | PComm and basilar tip aneurysms have higher rupture risk per size |
| Hypertension | Increases transmural pressure |
| Smoking | Independent risk factor for rupture |
| Family history of SAH | First-degree relative with SAH increases risk 3-5 fold |
| Genetic conditions | Autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD), Ehlers-Danlos type IV, Marfan syndrome |
| Female sex | Higher rupture risk than males |
| Cocaine/sympathomimetic use | Acute hypertensive surge |

## Section 7: SAH Grading Scales

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

#### Hunt and Hess Scale

The Hunt and Hess scale grades SAH severity based on clinical presentation. Higher grades predict worse outcomes and guide management decisions (particularly regarding surgical timing).

| Grade | Clinical Presentation | Approximate Mortality |
|-------|----------------------|---------------------|
| I | Asymptomatic or minimal headache, slight nuchal rigidity | ~5% |
| II | Moderate to severe headache, nuchal rigidity, no neurological deficit other than possible cranial nerve palsy | ~10% |
| III | Drowsiness, confusion, mild focal deficit | ~15-20% |
| IV | Stupor, moderate to severe hemiparesis, possible early decerebrate posturing | ~30-40% |
| V | Deep coma, decerebrate posturing, moribund appearance | ~70-80% |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hunt and Hess grade 1 = roughly asymptomatic. Grade 5 = coma. For board purposes, remember that grades I-III are generally considered "good grade" and are treated with early aneurysm repair. Grades IV-V are "poor grade" and carry substantially higher mortality. The scale goes from 1 (best) to 5 (worst), which mirrors the morbidity/mortality continuum.

#### Fisher Scale (Original and Modified)

The Fisher scale is a radiographic grading system based on the amount and distribution of blood seen on CT. It predicts the risk of delayed vasospasm.

**Original Fisher Scale:**

| Grade | CT Finding | Vasospasm Risk |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | No blood detected | Low |
| 2 | Diffuse thin layer of blood (<1 mm thick) | Moderate |
| 3 | Localized clot and/or thick layer of blood (>1 mm) | HIGH (most predictive) |
| 4 | Diffuse or no subarachnoid blood but with intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) | Moderate |

**Modified Fisher Scale (more commonly used now):**

| Grade | CT Finding | Vasospasm Risk |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 0 | No SAH or IVH | Minimal |
| 1 | Thin SAH, no IVH | Low |
| 2 | Thin SAH with IVH | Moderate |
| 3 | Thick SAH, no IVH | High |
| 4 | Thick SAH with IVH | Highest |

> **Clinical Pearl:** On the original Fisher scale, grade 3 (thick localized clot) carries the HIGHEST vasospasm risk, not grade 4. This counterintuitive grading is a classic board trap. On the Modified Fisher scale, the grading is more intuitive (higher = worse). For exams, know that thick clot burden on CT is the strongest predictor of subsequent vasospasm.

## Section 8: SAH Complications and Management

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

#### Vasospasm and Delayed Cerebral Ischemia (DCI)

Vasospasm is the most feared complication of SAH and is the leading cause of morbidity and mortality in patients who survive the initial hemorrhage. It occurs because blood products in the subarachnoid space cause inflammation and contraction of the arterial smooth muscle, narrowing the cerebral arteries and reducing blood flow.

**Vasospasm Timeline:**

| Event | Timing |
|-------|--------|
| Onset of vasospasm | Days 3-4 after hemorrhage |
| Peak vasospasm risk | Day 7 (one week) |
| Resolution | Days 12-14 (approximately 2 weeks) |
| Clinical monitoring window | Days 3-14 post-hemorrhage |

**Management of Vasospasm:**

| Intervention | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Nimodipine** | Calcium channel blocker; given orally 60 mg every 4 hours for 21 days; reduces poor outcomes from vasospasm; standard of care for ALL SAH patients |
| **Hypertensive therapy** | If symptomatic vasospasm develops, induce hypertension (triple-H therapy historically: hypervolemia, hemodilution, hypertension; current focus is on hypertension alone) |
| **Endovascular rescue** | Intra-arterial vasodilators (verapamil, nicardipine) or balloon angioplasty for medically refractory vasospasm |
| **Transcranial Doppler (TCD) monitoring** | Serial monitoring of MCA velocities; mean velocity >120 cm/s suggests vasospasm; >200 cm/s indicates severe vasospasm |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Nimodipine is the ONLY pharmacological intervention proven to improve outcomes in SAH vasospasm. It is given to ALL SAH patients for 21 days, starting on admission. Importantly, nimodipine does not prevent angiographic vasospasm (the vessels still narrow); its benefit is believed to be neuroprotective rather than vasodilatory. The timeline to remember: vasospasm peaks at day 7 and resolves by day 14. This is one of the most commonly tested SAH concepts.

#### Other SAH Complications

| Complication | Timing | Management |
|-------------|--------|-----------|
| Rebleeding | Highest risk in first 24 hours | Early aneurysm repair (surgical clipping or endovascular coiling); BP control |
| Hydrocephalus (acute) | First 72 hours | External ventricular drain (EVD); occurs in 20-30% of SAH patients |
| Hydrocephalus (chronic) | Weeks to months | VP shunt; occurs in 10-20% of survivors |
| Seizures | Acute or delayed | Treat when they occur; prophylaxis is controversial |
| Hyponatremia | Days 3-14 | Most commonly from cerebral salt wasting (NOT SIADH); treat with normal saline; avoid fluid restriction (which worsens cerebral perfusion) |
| Cardiac complications | Acute | Troponin elevation, wall motion abnormalities, arrhythmias (catecholamine surge); can mimic acute MI |
| Neurogenic pulmonary edema | Acute | Sympathetic surge causes increased pulmonary capillary permeability |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Hyponatremia after SAH is usually from cerebral salt wasting (CSW), NOT SIADH. This distinction matters: CSW is treated with normal saline replacement (the patient is volume depleted), while SIADH is treated with fluid restriction. Fluid restriction in SAH is DANGEROUS because it worsens cerebral perfusion and can precipitate vasospasm-related ischemia. For SAH hyponatremia, give saline.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Aneurysm Treatment: Clipping vs. Coiling:**

| Feature | Surgical Clipping | Endovascular Coiling |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Approach | Open craniotomy; metal clip placed across aneurysm neck | Catheter-based; platinum coils packed into aneurysm sac |
| Evidence | ISAT trial: coiling had better outcomes at 1 year than clipping for ruptured aneurysms amenable to either approach | Increasingly preferred for many aneurysm locations |
| Advantages | Complete obliteration; lower recurrence rate; better for wide-neck aneurysms | Less invasive; shorter recovery; better for posterior circulation aneurysms |
| Disadvantages | Requires craniotomy; brain retraction injury risk | Higher recurrence/retreatment rate; may require adjunctive devices for wide-neck aneurysms |
| Timing | Early repair (within 72 hours) to prevent rebleeding | Early repair preferred |

## Section 9: Arteriovenous Malformations (AVM)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

AVMs are congenital vascular lesions consisting of a tangled network of abnormal arteries and veins without an intervening capillary bed. Blood flows directly from high-pressure arteries to low-pressure veins, creating a risk of hemorrhage. AVMs present with hemorrhage (approximately 50%), seizures (approximately 25%), headache, or progressive neurological deficit from "steal" phenomenon.

**Annual hemorrhage risk:** Approximately 2-4% per year for unruptured AVMs; higher after an initial hemorrhage event.

#### Spetzler-Martin Grading System

The Spetzler-Martin system grades AVMs from I (lowest risk) to V (highest risk) based on three characteristics that predict surgical risk.

| Feature | Scoring | Points |
|---------|---------|--------|
| AVM size | Small (<3 cm) | 1 |
| | Medium (3-6 cm) | 2 |
| | Large (>6 cm) | 3 |
| Eloquent cortex involvement | Non-eloquent | 0 |
| | Eloquent (motor, sensory, language, visual cortex; hypothalamus, thalamus, brainstem, cerebellar peduncles) | 1 |
| Deep venous drainage | Superficial only | 0 |
| | Deep (internal cerebral veins, basal veins, precentral cerebellar vein) | 1 |

**Total Spetzler-Martin Score: 1-5 points**

| Grade | Total Score | Surgical Risk | Recommendation |
|-------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| I | 1 | Very low | Surgery recommended |
| II | 2 | Low | Surgery usually recommended |
| III | 3 | Moderate | Individualized decision |
| IV | 4 | High | Surgery usually not recommended |
| V | 5 | Very high | Surgery generally contraindicated |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Spetzler-Martin grading system evaluates three factors: Size (1-3 points), Eloquence (0-1), and Deep drainage (0-1). Grade I AVMs are ideal surgical candidates. Grade V AVMs are generally inoperable. Grade III is the "gray zone" requiring multidisciplinary discussion. A Grade VI has been informally added to describe completely inoperable AVMs.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**AVM Treatment Options:**

| Treatment | Mechanism | Advantages | Limitations |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Microsurgical resection | Open surgery to remove AVM | Complete cure; immediate elimination of hemorrhage risk | Invasive; risk of new neurological deficits; limited to lower grades |
| Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife) | Focused radiation causes gradual obliteration | Non-invasive; suitable for deep/eloquent locations | Takes 1-3 years for obliteration; hemorrhage risk persists during latency |
| Endovascular embolization | Catheter-based injection of embolic agents into feeding arteries | Can reduce AVM size before surgery or radiosurgery | Rarely curative alone; adjunctive role |
| Observation | Conservative management | Avoids procedural risk | Annual hemorrhage risk continues |

The ARUBA trial (A Randomized Trial of Unruptured Brain AVMs) found that medical management alone was superior to interventional therapy for unruptured AVMs over a relatively short follow-up period. This controversial trial shifted practice toward conservative management for many unruptured AVMs, though the debate continues regarding the long-term implications given the cumulative lifetime hemorrhage risk.

## Section 10: Special Topics in Hemorrhagic Stroke

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Warfarin-Related ICH:**

Warfarin-related ICH has the highest mortality of any ICH subtype, approximately 50-65%. The hematoma expands more rapidly and over a longer period compared with spontaneous ICH. Supratherapeutic INR is the strongest risk factor, but ICH can occur even at therapeutic INR (2-3).

Reversal protocol:
1. IV Vitamin K 10 mg (slow infusion; effect takes 6-12 hours)
2. 4-factor PCC (Kcentra) dosed based on INR and body weight; target INR <1.4 within 1 hour
3. FFP is second-line only (requires large volume, slower onset, risk of volume overload and transfusion reactions)
4. Recheck INR within 1 hour of PCC administration

**Resuming Anticoagulation After ICH:**

Restarting anticoagulation after ICH is one of the most challenging clinical decisions. General principles:
- If the indication for anticoagulation is strong (mechanical heart valve, high CHA2DS2-VASc score), restarting may be considered after 4-8 weeks
- Lobar ICH (suggestive of amyloid angiopathy) has a higher rebleeding risk, making anticoagulation resumption more dangerous
- Deep ICH (hypertensive) has a lower rebleeding risk and may be more amenable to anticoagulation resumption
- Individual risk-benefit analysis is essential; no universal guideline exists

**Cocaine and Amphetamine-Associated ICH:**

Cocaine and amphetamines are important causes of ICH in young adults. The mechanism involves acute hypertensive surge and vasospasm leading to vessel rupture. Cocaine use is associated with both ICH and SAH. Management includes aggressive blood pressure control, benzodiazepines for sympathomimetic toxicity, and avoidance of beta-blockers (which can cause unopposed alpha-adrenergic stimulation and worsen hypertension in the cocaine-intoxicated patient).

**Hemorrhagic Transformation of Ischemic Stroke:**

Hemorrhagic transformation refers to bleeding into an area of ischemic infarction. It occurs in two forms:

| Type | Description | Clinical Significance |
|------|-------------|---------------------|
| Hemorrhagic infarction (HI) | Petechial hemorrhage within the infarct | Usually asymptomatic; common finding |
| Parenchymal hematoma (PH) | Confluent hematoma with mass effect | May be symptomatic; can cause neurological deterioration |

Risk factors for hemorrhagic transformation include large infarct size, thrombolytic therapy, anticoagulation, cardioembolic mechanism, and hyperglycemia. Symptomatic hemorrhagic transformation after tPA occurs in approximately 6% of treated patients.

## Section 11: Comparison of Major Hemorrhagic Stroke Trials

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

| Trial | Topic | Comparison | Key Result |
|-------|-------|-----------|------------|
| INTERACT2 | BP management in ICH | Intensive (<140) vs. guideline (<180) | Trend toward benefit with intensive; improved ordinal mRS |

| STICH II | Surgery for lobar ICH | Early surgery vs. initial conservative | No clear benefit for routine early surgery |

| MISTIE III | Minimally invasive thrombolysis for ICH | tPA injection into hematoma vs. standard | Did not meet primary endpoint, but patients with residual clot <15 mL had better outcomes |

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

**Acute ICH Management Checklist:**

| Step | Action | Target/Details |
|------|--------|---------------|

| 8 | Calculate ICH Score | Prognostication; guide goals-of-care discussions |
| 9 | DVT prophylaxis | IPC devices immediately; pharmacological prophylaxis after 24-48 hours |
| 10 | Dysphagia screen | Before any oral intake |
