## [BOARD] Key Concepts

> **Board Pearl:** Poststroke depression occurs in approximately 31% of patients within 5 years (up to 50% by some estimates). It is the most common psychiatric complication of stroke and is associated with worse functional outcomes, higher mortality, and lower quality of life.

> **Board Pearl:** SSRIs are the first-line pharmacologic treatment for poststroke depression. However, the 2024 VA/DoD guidelines recommend AGAINST using antidepressants prophylactically to PREVENT poststroke depression (changed from neutral in 2019 to "weak against" in 2024).

> **Board Pearl:** PBA is INVOLUNTARY emotional expression that is DISPROPORTIONATE or INCONGRUENT with the patient's underlying mood. Depression is a SUSTAINED mood disorder. PBA = Nuedexta (dextromethorphan/quinidine). Depression = SSRIs + psychotherapy.

> **Board Pearl:** Caregiver burden after stroke is higher than caregiver burden for cancer, though evidence suggests dementia caregiving may produce equal or greater burden. Stroke caregiver burden is an independent predictor of patient institutionalization and caregiver depression. Addressing caregiver needs is an essential component of stroke rehabilitation.

> **Board Pearl:** The disability paradox describes the observation that many individuals with serious disabilities report good or excellent quality of life — contradicting the assumptions of healthy observers. Pre-stroke advance directives may not reflect the patient's actual preferences once they have adapted to the disability.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 14.1 Poststroke Depression (PSD)

#### 14.1.1 Epidemiology

[BOARD] Poststroke depression is the most common neuropsychiatric complication of stroke:

- **Prevalence:** Approximately 31% within 5 years (pooled estimate); estimates range from 18-61% depending on diagnostic criteria, timing, and methodology
- **Peak incidence:** First 3-6 months after stroke, but may develop at any time
- **Persistence:** Without treatment, poststroke depression persists in approximately 50% of patients at 1 year
- **Underdiagnosis:** Despite its prevalence, PSD is recognized and treated in fewer than half of affected patients
- **Impact on outcomes:** PSD is independently associated with:
  - Worse functional recovery
  - Longer rehabilitation stays and lower FIM gains
  - Increased mortality (both cardiac and overall)
  - Poorer quality of life
  - Increased healthcare utilization
  - Reduced participation in rehabilitation

#### 14.1.2 Pathophysiology

[COMPREHENSIVE] Poststroke depression is a multifactorial condition with both neurobiological and psychosocial contributions:

**Neurobiological Factors:**

| Mechanism | Description |
|---|---|
| **Biogenic amine disruption** | Stroke damages ascending monoaminergic projections (serotonergic, noradrenergic, dopaminergic) from the brainstem to the cortex and limbic structures, reducing neurotransmitter availability |
| **Frontal-subcortical circuit disruption** | Damage to connections between the prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia (especially left frontal and basal ganglia lesions) disrupts mood regulation circuits |
| **Neuroinflammation** | Acute stroke triggers a systemic inflammatory response (elevated IL-1, IL-6, TNF-alpha, CRP) that persists and contributes to depressive symptoms through neuroinflammatory pathways |
| **HPA axis dysregulation** | Poststroke cortisol elevation and impaired dexamethasone suppression, similar to major depression |
| **Glutamate excitotoxicity** | Excessive glutamate release damages limbic structures involved in mood regulation |
| **Neuroplasticity impairment** | Reduced BDNF and impaired neurogenesis in the hippocampus — the same mechanisms implicated in primary major depression |

**Psychosocial Factors:**
- Grief reaction to sudden loss of function, independence, and identity
- Social isolation and loss of social roles
- Financial stress and employment loss
- Relationship changes and altered family dynamics
- Existential distress and confrontation with mortality
- Caregiver dependency and loss of autonomy

**Lesion Location Debate:**

The question of whether specific lesion locations predispose to PSD remains debated:
- **Early studies (Robinson et al.):** Left frontal and left basal ganglia lesions associated with higher PSD risk, especially in the acute period
- **Meta-analyses:** Mixed results; some support left hemisphere association in acute phase, but location effect diminishes over time
- **Current consensus:** PSD is likely multifactorial; stroke severity and disability level are stronger predictors than lesion location alone

#### 14.1.3 Risk Factors for PSD

[BOARD]

| Risk Factor | Strength of Association | Clinical Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Stroke severity** | Strong | Higher NIHSS = higher PSD risk |
| **Functional disability** | Strong | Greater disability = greater PSD risk (bidirectional relationship) |
| **Cognitive impairment** | Strong | Impaired cognition complicates both diagnosis and treatment |
| **Prior history of depression** | Strong | Single strongest premorbid risk factor |
| **Social isolation** | Strong | Lack of social support increases risk and persistence |
| **Female sex** | Moderate | Slight female preponderance (similar to primary depression) |
| **Younger age (working age)** | Moderate | Greater loss of roles and expectations |
| **Aphasia** | Moderate | Communication barriers increase isolation and frustration; also complicates screening |
| **Prior stroke** | Moderate | Cumulative effect of disability |
| **Living alone** | Moderate | Reduced social support |
| **Insomnia** | Moderate | Both risk factor and symptom |

#### 14.1.4 Screening for PSD

[BOARD] All stroke patients should be screened for depression at regular intervals: during acute hospitalization, at rehabilitation admission, at discharge, and at follow-up visits (3, 6, and 12 months).

| Screening Tool | Items | Scoring | Advantages | Limitations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **PHQ-9** | 9 items | 0-27; ≥10 suggests moderate depression | Brief; validated in stroke; well-established cutoffs; monitors treatment response | Requires verbal or written response; challenging in severe aphasia |
| **PHQ-2** | 2 items (anhedonia + depressed mood) | 0-6; ≥3 positive screen | Ultra-brief screen; high sensitivity | Low specificity; positive screen requires follow-up with PHQ-9 |
| **BDI (Beck Depression Inventory)** | 21 items | 0-63; ≥14 mild, ≥20 moderate, ≥29 severe | Comprehensive; widely used in research | Long; somatic items may overlap with stroke symptoms |
| **Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression (HAM-D)** | 17 or 21 items | Clinician-rated | Gold standard in research | Time-consuming; requires trained rater; not practical for screening |
| **GDS (Geriatric Depression Scale)** | 15 or 30 items | Yes/No format; higher score = more depression | Specifically designed for elderly; avoids somatic overlap | Not validated specifically for stroke; may miss PSD in younger patients |
| **Stroke Aphasic Depression Questionnaire (SADQ)** | 21 items (10-item short form) | Observer-rated based on behaviors | Designed for patients with aphasia who cannot self-report | Requires trained observer; assesses observable signs, not subjective experience |

> **Board Pearl:** The PHQ-9 is the most practical screening tool for poststroke depression in patients who can communicate. For patients with severe aphasia who cannot self-report, the SADQ (observer-rated) is specifically designed for screening. All stroke patients should be screened for depression during hospitalization, at rehabilitation, and at follow-up visits.

**Diagnostic Challenges in Stroke:**
- **Somatic overlap:** Fatigue, insomnia, appetite changes, psychomotor slowing, and concentration difficulties occur in stroke even without depression — making diagnosis difficult
- **Aphasia:** Patients with language deficits cannot report subjective depressive symptoms; observer-rated tools and behavioral assessment are essential
- **Anosognosia:** Patients with impaired awareness may not recognize or report emotional distress
- **Cognitive impairment:** May mimic or coexist with depression ("pseudodementia" vs. true vascular cognitive impairment)
- **Emotional lability/PBA:** May be mistaken for depression

#### 14.1.5 Treatment of PSD

[BOARD]

**Pharmacologic Treatment:**

| Treatment | Level of Evidence | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **SSRIs (first-line)** | Strong (multiple RCTs and meta-analyses) | Citalopram, sertraline, escitalopram most studied. 2024 VA/DoD guidelines: recommend SSRIs or SNRIs for treatment of PSD. Sertraline 50-100 mg or escitalopram 10-20 mg typical doses |
| **SNRIs** | Moderate | Venlafaxine, duloxetine — alternative first-line; may be preferred with comorbid pain or fatigue |
| **TCAs** | Moderate (older data) | Nortriptyline — effective but anticholinergic side effects limit use in elderly and cognitively impaired stroke patients |
| **Methylphenidate** | Limited (open-label studies) | Rapid onset (days vs. weeks for SSRIs); may be useful as adjunct for apathy and fatigue while waiting for SSRI to take effect; dose: 5-20 mg/day |
| **Mirtazapine** | Limited | May be helpful if insomnia and appetite loss are prominent; sedating; weight gain |
| **Prophylactic antidepressants** | **Weak against** (2024 VA/DoD) | NOT recommended for PSD prevention; previous neutral stance changed to "weak against" due to lack of benefit and concerns about fracture risk with long-term use |

> **Board Pearl:** SSRIs are the first-line treatment for poststroke depression. The FOCUS, AFFINITY, and EFFECTS trials showed that routine SSRI use does NOT improve overall stroke recovery — SSRIs should be used for TREATMENT of depression, not for stroke recovery enhancement. Prophylactic antidepressants are now recommended AGAINST (2024 VA/DoD guidelines).

**Important SSRI Considerations in Stroke:**
- **Bleeding risk:** SSRIs inhibit platelet serotonin uptake, theoretically increasing bleeding risk. However, recent large-scale analyses indicate early SSRI use in acute ischemic stroke is NOT associated with clinically significant increased bleeding
- **Drug interactions:** Monitor for interactions with anticoagulants (warfarin — CYP2C9 inhibition by fluoxetine/fluvoxamine), antiplatelet agents, and other medications
- **Hyponatremia:** SIADH risk, especially in elderly — monitor sodium in first 2 weeks
- **Falls:** SSRIs may slightly increase fall risk, particularly in the first weeks
- **Onset of action:** 2-4 weeks; counsel patients and families about delay; consider methylphenidate bridge for severe cases

**Nonpharmacologic Treatment:**

| Treatment | Evidence | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** | Moderate | Adapted for stroke (shorter sessions, simplified materials, repetition, caregiver involvement); effective for mild-moderate PSD |
| **Behavioral Activation** | Moderate | Focus on increasing pleasant activities and reducing avoidance; may be more feasible than full CBT in cognitively impaired patients |
| **Problem-Solving Therapy** | Moderate | Structured approach to addressing practical problems; good for patients with identified situational stressors |
| **Motivational Interviewing** | Emerging | Enhancing engagement in rehabilitation and health behaviors |
| **Exercise** | Moderate | Aerobic exercise and structured physical activity improve mood; incorporate into rehabilitation programming |
| **Mindfulness-Based Interventions** | Emerging | Growing evidence for mindfulness and meditation in PSD; adapted protocols for stroke |
| **Peer Support** | Emerging | Stroke survivor support groups; peer mentoring programs |

**Combination approach recommended:** SSRIs + psychotherapy (CBT or behavioral activation) is likely more effective than either alone, consistent with evidence in primary major depression.

### 14.2 Depression vs. Pseudobulbar Affect: A Detailed Comparison

[BOARD] This comparison table is essential for board preparation:

| Feature | Poststroke Depression | Pseudobulbar Affect (PBA) |
|---|---|---|
| **Nature** | Sustained mood disorder | Involuntary emotional expression disorder |
| **Emotional expression** | Congruent with underlying sad mood | Disproportionate or incongruent with mood |
| **Duration** | Pervasive — weeks to months | Brief episodes — seconds to minutes |
| **Onset** | Gradual or subacute | Sudden, unpredictable |
| **Triggers** | Related to negative thoughts, losses, hopelessness | Minimal trigger; any emotional stimulus (or none at all) |
| **Predominant emotion** | Sadness, hopelessness, anhedonia | Crying AND/OR laughing (may be incongruent) |
| **Associated symptoms** | Anhedonia, sleep disturbance, appetite change, guilt, poor concentration, fatigue, possible suicidal ideation | No anhedonia, no sleep/appetite disturbance, no suicidal ideation |
| **Vegetative symptoms** | Present (insomnia/hypersomnia, weight change, psychomotor changes) | Absent |
| **Patient experience** | Patient FEELS sad | Patient may NOT feel sad despite crying (or may not feel happy despite laughing) |
| **Pathology** | Serotonergic/monoamine depletion; frontal-subcortical circuit dysfunction | Bilateral corticobulbar tract lesion; release of brainstem emotional centers from cortical inhibition |
| **Screening tool** | PHQ-9, BDI, HAM-D | CNS-LS (Center for Neurologic Study-Lability Scale; ≥13 = PBA) |
| **Treatment** | SSRIs (first-line); SNRIs; psychotherapy | Dextromethorphan/quinidine (Nuedexta) — FDA-approved; SSRIs off-label |
| **Prevalence** | ~31% within 5 years | ~20% |
| **Can coexist** | Yes — PSD and PBA frequently co-occur | Yes |

> **Board Pearl:** The key distinguishing feature is CONGRUENCE. In depression, the emotional expression matches the underlying mood (the patient cries because they feel sad). In PBA, the emotional expression is disproportionate to or disconnected from the underlying mood (the patient cries without feeling sad, or laughs at a funeral).

### 14.3 Emotional Lability

[COMPREHENSIVE] Emotional lability (also termed emotionalism or emotional incontinence when severe) refers to an increased tendency toward exaggerated or easily triggered emotional responses. It exists on a spectrum from mild lability (easily tearful but generally proportionate to mood) to severe PBA (involuntary, disproportionate episodes).

**Epidemiology:**
- Approximately 20% of stroke survivors
- More common with right hemisphere lesions and brainstem/cerebellar lesions
- May improve spontaneously over the first year but persists in many patients

**Impact:**
- Social embarrassment and withdrawal
- Misdiagnosis as depression (leading to inappropriate SSRI monotherapy when Nuedexta is needed)
- Interference with rehabilitation participation (crying episodes interrupt therapy)
- Caregiver distress

**Management:**
- Education (patient and family) that this is a neurological condition, not voluntary
- Behavioral strategies: distraction, topic change, deep breathing
- Pharmacologic: Nuedexta for severe PBA (see Chapter 12); low-dose SSRIs for milder lability
- Address comorbid depression if present

### 14.4 Poststroke Anxiety

[BOARD] Anxiety disorders are the second most common psychiatric complication after stroke but receive far less clinical attention than depression.

**Epidemiology:**
- Prevalence: approximately 25% of stroke survivors
- Phobic disorder is the most common subtype (including agoraphobia and specific phobias — especially fear of falling)
- Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) is also common
- Co-occurs with depression in approximately 50% of cases
- May be more prevalent than recognized because screening is inconsistent

**Risk Factors:**
- Pre-existing anxiety disorder
- Female sex
- Younger age
- Greater disability
- Depression (strong bidirectional association)
- Prior stroke
- Social isolation

**Clinical Manifestations:**

| Anxiety Subtype | Presentation After Stroke | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|
| **Phobic disorder** | Fear of falling (most common), fear of another stroke, agoraphobia, social phobia | Most common |
| **Generalized anxiety** | Chronic, excessive worry about health, recovery, finances, dependence | Common |
| **Panic disorder** | Recurrent unexpected panic attacks; may be confused with cardiac symptoms | Less common |
| **PTSD** | Intrusive memories of stroke event; hyperarousal; avoidance of medical settings | Emerging recognition |

**Treatment:**
- **SSRIs/SNRIs** — First-line pharmacologic (same agents used for depression)
- **CBT** — Effective for poststroke anxiety; adapted for cognitive limitations
- **Exposure therapy** — For phobic avoidance (especially fear of falling); graded exposure within PT
- **Relaxation training** — Progressive muscle relaxation, deep breathing, guided imagery
- **Benzodiazepines** — Generally AVOIDED in stroke patients (sedation, fall risk, cognitive impairment, dependence). Use only for acute situations, brief duration
- **Buspirone** — Alternative anxiolytic without sedation; limited evidence in stroke specifically

> **Board Pearl:** Poststroke anxiety affects ~25%, with phobic disorder (especially fear of falling) being the most common subtype. SSRIs and CBT are first-line treatments. Benzodiazepines should be AVOIDED due to sedation, fall risk, and cognitive impairment.

### 14.5 Sexuality After Stroke

[COMPREHENSIVE] Sexual dysfunction after stroke is common, affecting 50-80% of stroke survivors, yet it is one of the most undertreated aspects of stroke recovery. Both patients and healthcare providers frequently avoid discussing sexuality.

**Prevalence and Types:**
- Decreased libido: 50-70%
- Erectile dysfunction: 50-75% of men
- Decreased frequency of sexual activity: 60-80%
- Fear of causing another stroke during sexual activity: 30-50%
- Decreased sexual satisfaction: 40-60%

**Contributing Factors:**

| Factor Category | Specific Contributions |
|---|---|
| **Physical** | Motor weakness, spasticity, sensory loss, fatigue, pain, incontinence, limited positioning options |
| **Neurological** | Altered sensation (hemibody), central pain, autonomic dysfunction affecting arousal |
| **Psychological** | Depression, anxiety, body image changes, role changes, fear of another stroke, loss of confidence |
| **Pharmacologic** | SSRIs (decreased libido, anorgasmia), beta-blockers, antihypertensives, antiepileptics |
| **Relational** | Changed dynamics from patient-caregiver relationship; partner anxiety; communication barriers (aphasia) |

**Management Approach:**
1. **Initiate discussion** — Healthcare providers should ask about sexuality; patients rarely raise the topic themselves
2. **PLISSIT model** — Graduated intervention framework:
   - **P**ermission: Give permission to discuss sexual concerns; normalize the topic
   - **L**imited **I**nformation: Provide factual information (sexual activity is safe; not a stroke risk factor; positions and adaptations available)
   - **S**pecific **S**uggestions: Timing (when rested, not after meals), positioning adaptations, communication strategies, managing specific physical barriers
   - **I**ntensive **T**herapy: Referral to sex therapist or specialist for complex issues
3. **Medication review** — Adjust or switch medications contributing to sexual dysfunction
4. **Treat contributing conditions** — Depression, pain, spasticity
5. **PDE-5 inhibitors** (sildenafil, tadalafil) — Can be used for erectile dysfunction in stroke patients after the acute phase; contraindicated with nitrates

### 14.6 Caregiver Burden

[BOARD] Informal caregivers (typically spouses or adult children) are the backbone of poststroke care, and the burden they bear is substantial and often underaddressed.

**Key Facts:**
- Caregiver burden after stroke is **higher than caregiver burden for cancer**, though evidence suggests **dementia caregiving may produce equal or greater overall burden**. The sudden onset and severity of physical disability in stroke create distinct burdens compared to the progressive trajectory of dementia
- 30-68% of stroke caregivers experience depressive symptoms
- Caregiver depression is an independent predictor of patient institutionalization
- Caregiver health deteriorates: increased cardiovascular risk, immune dysfunction, sleep disruption
- Financial impact: lost wages, out-of-pocket healthcare costs, home modifications

**Risk Factors for Caregiver Burden:**

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| Patient aphasia | Profound communication barrier; caregiver must interpret needs |
| Patient cognitive impairment | Increased supervision needs; safety concerns |
| Patient behavioral changes | Aggression, apathy, disinhibition strain relationships |
| Patient incontinence | Time-intensive management; social embarrassment |
| Severity of disability | Greater physical care demands |
| Caregiver social isolation | Loss of social network, hobbies, employment |
| Female caregiver | Higher reported burden (though may reflect reporting bias) |
| Spousal caregiver | 24/7 availability expectation; role change from partner to caregiver |

**Interventions for Caregiver Support:**

| Intervention | Description |
|---|---|
| **Psychoeducation** | Stroke education, symptom management, expected recovery trajectory, behavioral management strategies |
| **Respite care** | Temporary relief through adult day programs, home health aides, short-term facility placement |
| **Caregiver support groups** | Peer connection; normalization of experience; problem-sharing |
| **Caregiver skills training** | Transfer techniques, safe handling, medication management, emergency recognition |
| **Psychological support** | Individual therapy or counseling for caregiver depression and anxiety |
| **Telehealth monitoring** | Remote support and check-ins; reduces isolation in rural caregivers |
| **Financial counseling** | Benefits navigation, insurance advocacy, workplace accommodation |

> **Board Pearl:** Caregiver burden after stroke exceeds that for cancer; evidence regarding comparison with dementia is mixed. Addressing caregiver needs is not optional — it is an essential component of comprehensive stroke rehabilitation. Caregiver depression independently predicts patient institutionalization.

### 14.7 Advance Care Planning

[COMPREHENSIVE] Advance care planning (ACP) takes on unique dimensions in stroke because of the sudden onset and the frequent inability of the patient to participate in decision-making at the time decisions are most needed.

#### 14.7.1 Unique Challenges in Stroke

| Challenge | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sudden onset** | Unlike cancer or progressive neurological disease, there is no preparation time for goals-of-care conversations |
| **Decisional incapacity** | Most stroke patients with severe strokes cannot participate in their own care decisions acutely (aphasia, reduced consciousness, cognitive impairment) |
| **Surrogate decision-making** | Family members must make critical decisions (intubation, surgery, PEG, code status) with limited information and high emotional distress |
| **Prognostic uncertainty** | Stroke prognosis is difficult to predict in the first 24-72 hours; premature pessimism may lead to withdrawal of care that could have resulted in meaningful recovery |
| **Self-fulfilling prophecy** | Clinician pessimism about outcomes may lead to less aggressive treatment, which in turn leads to worse outcomes — confirming the initial pessimistic prognosis |

#### 14.7.2 The Disability Paradox

[BOARD] The disability paradox is the well-documented observation that many individuals living with significant disabilities report good or excellent quality of life — contradicting the expectations and predictions of healthy observers (including healthcare providers, family members, and even the patients themselves prior to the disability).

**Clinical Implications:**
- Pre-stroke advance directives may not accurately reflect what the patient would want AFTER experiencing disability and adapting to it
- Healthy surrogates and clinicians consistently underestimate the quality of life achievable with disability
- Time-limited trials (e.g., "treat aggressively for 72 hours, then reassess") are a strategy for managing prognostic uncertainty while allowing recovery time
- Goals of care should be revisited repeatedly as the patient's condition and preferences evolve

> **Board Pearl:** The disability paradox teaches us that pre-stroke advance directives may not reflect the patient's actual preferences after disability. Patients adapt to disability more successfully than they (or their families) would have predicted before the event. This is a reason to counsel caution in making early, irreversible decisions about withdrawal of care.

#### 14.7.3 Communication Frameworks

**Best-Case / Worst-Case Scenario Framework:**
- Presents a range of possible outcomes rather than a single prognostic number
- Avoids false precision in an inherently uncertain situation
- Helps families understand the spectrum of recovery
- Example: "In the best case, your mother may regain some independence with help. In the worst case, she may remain dependent for most daily activities and require long-term facility care."

**Triggers for Serious Illness Conversations:**
- Age ≥80 years and hospitalized for stroke
- Requiring mechanical ventilation
- Predicted major neurological deficits based on imaging and clinical examination
- Requiring long-term artificial nutrition (PEG tube consideration)
- Family request for goals-of-care discussion
- Decline in condition or development of major complications

### 14.8 Palliative Care Integration

[BOARD] Palliative care is NOT synonymous with hospice or end-of-life care. It can and should be integrated from the time of stroke presentation, concurrent with acute treatment and rehabilitation.

**Domains of Palliative Care in Stroke:**

| Domain | Application |
|---|---|
| **Pain management** | Central poststroke pain (thalamic pain), hemiplegic shoulder pain, spasticity-related pain, headache |
| **Symptom management** | Fatigue, insomnia, dyspnea, nausea, constipation, agitation |
| **Communication** | Serious illness conversations; prognostic counseling; shared decision-making |
| **Psychosocial support** | Depression, anxiety, existential distress, spiritual care |
| **Care coordination** | Transitions between ICU, acute care, rehab, home; insurance navigation |
| **Caregiver support** | Respite, counseling, practical assistance |
| **Advance care planning** | Goals of care, healthcare proxy designation, code status |

**Pain After Stroke:**
- Approximately 30% of stroke survivors experience significant pain
- Types:
  - **Hemiplegic shoulder pain** — most common pain complaint; subluxation, impingement, adhesive capsulitis, rotator cuff pathology
  - **Central poststroke pain (CPSP)** — thalamic pain syndrome; burning, allodynic, dysesthetic; onset often weeks to months after stroke
  - **Spasticity-related pain** — muscle spasms, contractures
  - **Headache** — 6-44% in ischemic stroke
  - **Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)** — shoulder-hand syndrome

**Central Poststroke Pain Pharmacotherapy:**
- First-line: gabapentin or pregabalin
- Second-line: lamotrigine
- Third-line: amitriptyline (older evidence base; anticholinergic side effects limit use in elderly)
- Adjuncts: duloxetine (dual serotonergic-noradrenergic), topical capsaicin, tramadol

#### 14.8.1 Hospice Eligibility Criteria

[BOARD] For patients with severe stroke and poor prognosis, hospice referral may be appropriate. General eligibility criteria include:

**General Hospice Criteria:**
- Karnofsky Performance Status ≤40% (or equivalent ECOG ≥3)
- Unintentional weight loss >10% over 6 months
- Serum albumin <2.5 g/dL
- Dependent for most ADLs
- Progressive nutritional decline despite adequate intake

**ICH-Specific Hospice Imaging Criteria:**
- Infratentorial hemorrhage ≥20 mL
- Supratentorial hemorrhage ≥50 mL
- Presence of intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH)
- Midline shift ≥1.5 cm

**Stroke-Specific Considerations:**
- Persistent vegetative state or minimally conscious state
- Recurrent aspiration pneumonia despite maximal management
- Progressive decline in functional status despite rehabilitation
- Patient/family decision to focus on comfort

> **Board Pearl:** Hospice eligibility criteria for stroke include: Karnofsky ≤40%, weight loss >10% in 6 months, albumin <2.5 g/dL. For ICH: supratentorial hemorrhage ≥50 mL, infratentorial ≥20 mL, IVH present, or midline shift ≥1.5 cm.

### 14.9 Return to Community and Social Participation

[COMPREHENSIVE] The ultimate goal of stroke rehabilitation is not merely physical recovery but full reintegration into community life and meaningful social participation.

#### 14.9.1 Community Reintegration Challenges

| Domain | Common Barriers | Interventions |
|---|---|---|
| **Transportation** | Cannot drive; limited public transit access; financial cost of transportation services | Driving evaluation and training; community transportation services; ride-sharing assistance; volunteer driver programs |
| **Social isolation** | Loss of social network; communication barriers (aphasia); mobility limitations; embarrassment about deficits | Stroke support groups; community recreation programs; peer mentoring; telehealth social connections |
| **Leisure and recreation** | Physical limitations reduce participation in previous hobbies; fatigue; depression | Adaptive recreation programs; therapeutic recreation; new activity exploration; community center programs |
| **Role changes** | Loss of breadwinner, homemaker, partner, parent roles | Vocational rehabilitation; occupational therapy for role adaptation; family therapy; support groups |
| **Financial** | Lost income; increased healthcare costs; insurance barriers | Social work referral; benefits counseling; disability applications; community resources |
| **Housing** | Home accessibility barriers; stairs, narrow doorways, bathroom safety | Home modification assessment; occupational therapy for home safety; accessible housing resources |

#### 14.9.2 Assessment of Community Participation

| Tool | Measures | Format |
|---|---|---|
| **Stroke Impact Scale (SIS)** | 8 domains including social participation, mobility, hand function, ADLs, emotion, communication, memory, physical function | Patient-reported; 59 items |
| **Reintegration to Normal Living Index (RNLI)** | Overall community reintegration across multiple domains | 11 items; patient-reported |
| **Frenchay Activities Index** | Instrumental ADLs (IADL) in the community | 15 items; designed for stroke |
| **London Handicap Scale** | Participation restriction across 6 dimensions | Patient-reported |
| **Community Integration Questionnaire (CIQ)** | Home integration, social integration, productive activity | 15 items |

### 14.10 Post-Stroke Apathy

[COMPREHENSIVE] Apathy is a distinct neuropsychiatric syndrome characterized by diminished motivation manifested as reduced goal-directed behavior, cognition, and emotion. It overlaps with but is distinct from depression.

**Prevalence:** 20-40% of stroke survivors
**Distinction from depression:** Apathy = reduced motivation WITHOUT sadness, guilt, hopelessness, or suicidal ideation. Depression = reduced motivation WITH pervasive sadness and other depressive symptoms. They may co-occur.

**Anatomy:** Most commonly associated with frontal-subcortical circuit damage (anterior cingulate cortex, basal ganglia, thalamus) and bilateral ACA territory strokes.

**Impact:** Apathetic patients participate less in rehabilitation, have lower FIM gains, and require more prompting and cueing to engage in therapy.

**Treatment:**
- Methylphenidate or amantadine may improve apathy (limited evidence)
- SSRIs may help if comorbid depression is present but do not treat pure apathy
- Behavioral activation strategies
- Structured schedules with external cuing and prompting
- Environmental enrichment
- Caregiver education about apathy (often misinterpreted as laziness or noncompliance)

## Summary of Psychiatric/Psychosocial Complications

| Complication | Prevalence | First-Line Treatment | Key Board Fact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Poststroke depression** | ~31% within 5 years | SSRIs + psychotherapy | Strongest predictors: severity, disability, prior depression history. Prophylactic antidepressants NOT recommended (2024 VA/DoD) |
| **Pseudobulbar affect** | ~20% | Nuedexta (dextromethorphan/quinidine) | Involuntary, disproportionate, incongruent emotional expression; distinct from depression |
| **Anxiety** | ~25% | SSRIs + CBT | Phobic disorder (fear of falling) is the most common subtype; avoid benzodiazepines |
| **Apathy** | 20-40% | Methylphenidate, behavioral activation | Distinct from depression (no sadness); frontal-subcortical etiology |
| **Emotional lability** | ~20% | Education; Nuedexta if severe; SSRIs if mild | Spectrum from mild lability to severe PBA |
| **Fatigue** | 25-85% | Treat contributing factors; exercise; energy conservation | Distinct from depression; may persist years after stroke |
| **Sexual dysfunction** | 50-80% | Address physical, psychological, pharmacologic factors | Undertreated; providers should initiate discussion |
| **Caregiver depression** | 30-68% | Support, respite, counseling | Burden exceeds cancer caregiving; comparison with dementia is mixed |
