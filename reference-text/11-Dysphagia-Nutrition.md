> **Board Pearl:** Dysphagia screening must occur BEFORE any oral intake (food, liquid, or oral medications). Formal screening programs reduce aspiration pneumonia rates from approximately 5.4% to 2.4%.

## [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

### 11.1 Epidemiology and Clinical Significance

[BOARD] Dysphagia is one of the most common and clinically significant complications of stroke, affecting 25-65% of acute stroke patients when assessed by clinical bedside evaluation, and 37-78% when instrumental assessment methods such as videofluoroscopic swallow study (VFSS) or fiberoptic endoscopic evaluation of swallowing (FEES) are employed. The wide range in reported prevalence reflects differences in assessment methods, timing of evaluation, stroke severity, and lesion location.

**Prevalence by Stroke Location:**

| Stroke Location | Approximate Dysphagia Prevalence | Clinical Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Brainstem (especially lateral medullary) | ~67% | Most severe, most persistent; pharyngeal phase predominantly affected |
| Left hemisphere | ~28% | Oral phase predominantly affected; associated with apraxia of swallowing |
| Right hemisphere | ~21% | Pharyngeal delay; associated with laryngeal penetration |
| Bilateral hemispheric | High (varies) | Severe and often persistent; both phases affected |
| Cerebellar | Variable | Coordination deficits during oral phase |

[COMPREHENSIVE] The clinical consequences of dysphagia are substantial and far-reaching. Aspiration pneumonia is the leading cause of death in the subacute poststroke period, and dysphagia is its primary risk factor. Beyond pneumonia, dysphagia leads to malnutrition and dehydration, which impair wound healing, immune function, and rehabilitation participation. Patients with dysphagia have longer hospital stays, higher healthcare costs, and increased mortality compared with stroke patients who swallow normally. The psychosocial impact is also significant: mealtime becomes a source of anxiety and social isolation rather than pleasure and connection.

> **Board Pearl:** Brainstem strokes have the highest prevalence of dysphagia (~67%) and the worst prognosis for swallowing recovery. Lateral medullary (Wallenberg) syndrome is the prototype, causing ipsilateral pharyngeal weakness, vocal cord paralysis, and absent gag reflex due to damage to the nucleus ambiguus.

**Natural History and Recovery:**

[BOARD] Approximately 90% of poststroke dysphagia resolves spontaneously within 2 weeks for hemispheric strokes. This rapid recovery is driven by resolution of edema, reperfusion of ischemic penumbra tissue, and cortical reorganization. However, brainstem dysphagia may be prolonged or permanent because the central pattern generators for swallowing are located in the medullary reticular formation, and damage to these structures is less amenable to cortical compensation.

Recovery milestones for prognostication:
- **2 weeks:** Most hemispheric dysphagia has resolved; persistent dysphagia at this point warrants instrumental evaluation and nutritional planning
- **4 weeks:** The decision point for PEG tube placement versus continued nasogastric tube feeding
- **3-6 months:** Ongoing improvement possible with active rehabilitation; beyond this, gains are more incremental
- **1 year:** Persistent dysphagia at this stage is likely chronic and requires long-term management strategies

### 11.2 Anatomy and Physiology of Normal Swallowing

[BOARD] Normal swallowing (deglutition) is a complex sensorimotor process that involves more than 30 pairs of muscles, 5 cranial nerves, and coordination between the cerebral cortex, brainstem swallowing centers, and peripheral neuromuscular structures. Understanding the four phases of swallowing and their neural control is essential for localizing the phase of dysfunction and selecting appropriate interventions.

#### 11.2.1 The Four Phases of Swallowing

| Phase | Duration | Key Events | Voluntary/Reflexive | Primary Neural Control |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Oral Preparatory** | Variable | Mastication, bolus formation, containment by lips and buccal musculature | Voluntary | CN V (mastication), CN VII (lip seal, buccal tone), CN XII (tongue manipulation) |
| **Oral Transit (Propulsive)** | ~1 second | Tongue propels bolus posteriorly against hard palate toward oropharynx; triggers swallow reflex | Voluntary | CN XII (tongue propulsion), CN V (floor of mouth) |
| **Pharyngeal** | ~1 second | Soft palate elevation (velopharyngeal closure), hyolaryngeal excursion, epiglottic deflection, pharyngeal constriction, UES relaxation and opening | Reflexive (once triggered) | CN IX, CN X (pharyngeal plexus), brainstem swallowing center (nucleus ambiguus, nucleus tractus solitarius) |
| **Esophageal** | 8-20 seconds | Primary peristalsis propels bolus through esophagus to stomach; LES relaxation | Reflexive/Autonomic | CN X (vagus nerve), enteric nervous system |

[COMPREHENSIVE] **The Oral Preparatory Phase** begins when food enters the mouth and involves mastication (chewing), salivary mixing, and formation of a cohesive bolus. The muscles of mastication (masseter, temporalis, medial and lateral pterygoids) are innervated by the motor division of CN V (trigeminal nerve). Lip closure (orbicularis oris) and buccal tone (buccinator) are controlled by CN VII (facial nerve) and are essential for preventing food from spilling from the mouth or pocketing in the lateral sulci. The tongue, innervated by CN XII (hypoglossal nerve), manipulates the food bolus, positioning it between the teeth for grinding and then collecting the chewed material into a cohesive bolus on the tongue surface.

**The Oral Transit Phase** commences when the tongue initiates posterior propulsion of the bolus. The anterior tongue tip elevates to contact the hard palate, and a wave of tongue elevation moves the bolus posteriorly in a stripping action. When the bolus reaches the anterior faucial pillars and the base of tongue, the pharyngeal swallow response is triggered. This transition point is critical: if the pharyngeal swallow is delayed, the bolus may fall prematurely into the pharynx before the airway is protected, resulting in aspiration.

**The Pharyngeal Phase** is the most critical from an airway protection standpoint and the most commonly impaired after stroke. Once the pharyngeal swallow is triggered, a rapid, highly coordinated sequence of events occurs within approximately one second:

1. **Velopharyngeal closure** — The soft palate (levator veli palatini, via CN X) elevates to close the nasopharynx, preventing nasal regurgitation
2. **Hyolaryngeal excursion** — The hyoid bone and larynx move anteriorly and superiorly, driven by the suprahyoid muscles (mylohyoid, geniohyoid, digastric, stylohyoid). This movement contributes to both epiglottic deflection and UES opening
3. **Epiglottic deflection** — The epiglottis inverts to cover the laryngeal inlet, providing a physical barrier against aspiration
4. **Vocal fold adduction** — True and false vocal folds close (via recurrent laryngeal nerve, CN X), creating a second layer of airway protection
5. **Pharyngeal constriction** — Sequential contraction of the superior, middle, and inferior pharyngeal constrictors (CN X) propels the bolus inferiorly
6. **UES relaxation and opening** — The cricopharyngeus muscle (tonic contraction at rest) relaxes, and the mechanical pull from hyolaryngeal excursion opens the UES, allowing the bolus to enter the esophagus

**The Esophageal Phase** begins as the bolus passes through the UES and is propelled through the esophagus by primary peristalsis (striated muscle in the upper third, smooth muscle in the lower two-thirds). The lower esophageal sphincter (LES) relaxes to allow passage into the stomach. This phase is primarily under vagal control and enteric nervous system regulation.

#### 11.2.2 Cranial Nerves Essential for Swallowing

[BOARD]

| Cranial Nerve | Name | Specific Roles in Swallowing |
|---|---|---|
| **CN V** | Trigeminal | **Motor:** Muscles of mastication (temporalis, masseter, medial and lateral pterygoids) for chewing. **Sensory:** Oral cavity sensation (touch, temperature, pain) from anterior 2/3 of tongue and palate; triggers protective reflexes |
| **CN VII** | Facial | **Motor:** Lip closure (orbicularis oris) for oral containment; buccal tone (buccinator) to prevent food pocketing in cheeks. **Sensory:** Taste from anterior 2/3 of tongue; salivary glands (submandibular, sublingual) |
| **CN IX** | Glossopharyngeal | **Motor:** Stylopharyngeus muscle (elevates pharynx and larynx). **Sensory:** Pharyngeal sensation (afferent limb of gag reflex); taste from posterior 1/3 of tongue. Initiates pharyngeal swallow trigger |
| **CN X** | Vagus | **Motor:** Soft palate elevation (levator veli palatini); pharyngeal constrictors (pharyngeal plexus with CN IX); laryngeal muscles for vocal fold closure (recurrent laryngeal nerve); UES relaxation; esophageal peristalsis. The most critical cranial nerve for swallowing |
| **CN XII** | Hypoglossal | **Motor:** All intrinsic tongue muscles and most extrinsic tongue muscles (genioglossus, styloglossus, hyoglossus). Controls tongue manipulation, bolus formation, and posterior propulsion — the "engine" of oral swallowing |

> **Board Pearl:** The nucleus ambiguus in the lateral medulla contains motor neurons of CN IX and X that control pharyngeal and laryngeal muscles. It is the critical brainstem structure for the pharyngeal swallow reflex. Damage to this nucleus in lateral medullary (Wallenberg) syndrome causes the most severe poststroke dysphagia.

#### 11.2.3 Cortical and Brainstem Swallowing Centers

[COMPREHENSIVE] Swallowing is controlled by a distributed neural network that includes:

- **Primary motor and somatosensory cortex** (precentral and postcentral gyri) — Voluntary control of oral phase
- **Insula and operculum** — Sensory integration and swallowing planning
- **Anterior cingulate cortex** — Initiation and motivation for swallowing
- **Brainstem swallowing center** — The central pattern generator (CPG) for swallowing, located in the medullary reticular formation adjacent to the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS, sensory relay) and the nucleus ambiguus (NA, motor output)
- **Cerebellum** — Timing and coordination of swallowing movements

An important clinical principle is that the cortical representation of swallowing is bilateral but asymmetric. Most individuals have a "dominant" swallowing hemisphere (which may or may not correspond to handedness). After a unilateral hemispheric stroke, recovery of swallowing function often depends on the unaffected hemisphere's ability to assume control — a form of cortical reorganization. This explains why most hemispheric dysphagia resolves within 2 weeks: the nondominant swallowing hemisphere compensates. In contrast, brainstem strokes damage the CPG directly, and recovery depends on repair of the brainstem circuits themselves, which is far less robust.

### 11.3 Clinical Assessment of Dysphagia

#### 11.3.1 Bedside Swallowing Evaluation (BSE)

[BOARD] The bedside swallowing evaluation (also called the clinical swallowing examination, CSE) is the initial assessment performed by a speech-language pathologist (SLP) at the bedside. It serves as a screening tool to identify patients at risk for aspiration and to guide decisions about oral feeding safety and the need for instrumental assessment.

**Components of the Bedside Swallowing Evaluation:**

1. **Level of consciousness and cognition** — Alertness, attention, ability to follow commands
2. **Oral motor examination:**
   - Lip closure and symmetry (CN VII)
   - Tongue strength, range of motion, and coordination (CN XII)
   - Palatal elevation and symmetry (CN X)
   - Jaw strength and range (CN V)
   - Dentition and oral hygiene status
3. **Laryngeal examination:**
   - Voice quality (wet, gurgly, hoarse, breathy)
   - Voluntary cough strength
   - Laryngeal elevation on palpation during dry swallow
4. **Trial swallows** with various consistencies:
   - Ice chips, thin liquids, thickened liquids, purees, soft solids
   - Observe for cough, throat clearing, wet/gurgly voice quality, delayed swallow initiation, nasal regurgitation, oral residue
5. **3-oz (90 mL) water swallow test:**
   - Patient drinks 3 oz of water continuously without stopping
   - Positive test: coughing, choking, or wet-hoarse voice quality during or within 1 minute after drinking
   - Sensitivity for aspiration: 64-79%; Specificity: 61-81%

**Limitations of Bedside Evaluation:**

[BOARD] The most critical limitation is that bedside evaluation detects only 40-60% of aspiration events. The remaining 40-60% involve **silent aspiration** — aspiration of food or liquid below the level of the vocal folds without triggering a cough reflex. Silent aspiration occurs because of impaired laryngeal sensation (damage to the superior laryngeal nerve or brainstem sensory nuclei). It is more common in patients with brainstem strokes, bilateral hemispheric strokes, reduced consciousness, and severe stroke severity.

> **Board Pearl:** The 3-oz water swallow test has sensitivity of only 64-79% for detecting aspiration. Any patient with suspected dysphagia, especially with risk factors for silent aspiration (brainstem stroke, bilateral lesions, reduced alertness, weak voluntary cough), should be referred for instrumental assessment (VFSS or FEES).

#### 11.3.2 Videofluoroscopic Swallow Study (VFSS)

[BOARD] The videofluoroscopic swallow study (VFSS), also known as the modified barium swallow study (MBS), is the gold standard for dysphagia evaluation. The patient swallows barium-coated food and liquid of various consistencies while continuous fluoroscopic imaging records the entire swallowing sequence in real time from lateral and anteroposterior (AP) views.

**Advantages of VFSS:**
- Visualizes all four phases of swallowing
- Detects aspiration and penetration with precise timing relative to the swallow
- Identifies the specific phase and mechanism of dysfunction
- Allows real-time testing of compensatory strategies (chin tuck, head rotation, bolus modification) during the study
- Evaluates multiple consistencies (IDDSI levels 0 through 7)
- Uses standardized scoring (Penetration-Aspiration Scale)
- Provides dynamic visualization that can be reviewed frame-by-frame

**Limitations of VFSS:**
- Requires patient transport to a radiology suite (not portable)
- Involves radiation exposure (limits repeat testing frequency)
- Uses artificial bolus materials (barium) that differ from real food
- Does not directly visualize laryngeal structures (endoscopic view is superior for this)
- Requires patient cooperation and ability to sit upright
- Limited examination time due to radiation (typically 5-10 minutes of fluoroscopy)

#### 11.3.3 Fiberoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES)

[BOARD] FEES involves transnasal passage of a flexible endoscope to the level of the hypopharynx, where the larynx, pharynx, and surrounding structures are directly visualized during swallowing of food and liquid boluses (with food coloring added for visibility).

**Advantages of FEES:**
- Portable — can be performed at the bedside (ICU, nursing home, home health)
- No radiation exposure — can be repeated as often as needed
- Excellent visualization of laryngeal structures (vocal fold closure, pooling of secretions)
- Superior for assessing secretion management
- Detects aspiration and penetration
- Can assess sensory function (laryngeal adductor reflex testing)
- Uses real food (not barium)

**Limitations of FEES:**
- Cannot visualize the oral phase
- "White-out" phenomenon: during the pharyngeal swallow, the pharyngeal walls collapse around the endoscope, creating a brief period when the swallow itself is not visualized. Aspiration is inferred from residue seen before and after the white-out
- Cannot assess esophageal phase
- Requires trained endoscopist
- Some patient discomfort from nasal passage

#### 11.3.4 VFSS vs. FEES Comparison Table

[BOARD]

| Feature | VFSS (Modified Barium Swallow) | FEES |
|---|---|---|
| **Status** | Gold standard | Complementary study |
| **Oral phase visualization** | Yes — fully visualized | No — not visualized |
| **Pharyngeal phase** | Fully visualized in real time | Partially (white-out during swallow) |
| **Esophageal phase** | Yes | No |
| **Aspiration detection** | Excellent (direct visualization) | Good (inferred from residue) |
| **Silent aspiration detection** | Excellent | Good |
| **Radiation exposure** | Yes | No |
| **Portability** | Requires radiology suite | Bedside, ICU, home, SNF |
| **Secretion assessment** | Limited | Excellent |
| **Laryngeal visualization** | Indirect | Direct (superior) |
| **Food used** | Barium-coated (artificial) | Real food with dye |
| **Repeat testing** | Limited by radiation | Unlimited |
| **Strategy testing** | Yes — real-time | Limited |
| **Sensory testing** | Not available | Available (LAR testing) |
| **Patient requirements** | Must sit upright in radiology | Can be reclined, bedside |
| **Cost** | Higher (facility, radiologist) | Lower (portable equipment) |

> **Board Pearl:** VFSS is the gold standard because it visualizes ALL phases of swallowing and allows real-time testing of compensatory strategies. FEES is the best alternative when bedside assessment is needed, when there is no radiation suite access, or when laryngeal and secretion assessment is the priority. The two studies are complementary, not competing.

### 11.4 Penetration-Aspiration Scale (PAS)

[BOARD] The Penetration-Aspiration Scale (PAS), developed by Rosenbek and colleagues in 1996, is an 8-point ordinal scale used during VFSS (and adapted for FEES) to quantify the severity of airway invasion during swallowing. It is the standard scoring system used in clinical practice and research.

**Key Definitions:**
- **Penetration:** Material enters the laryngeal vestibule but remains ABOVE the level of the vocal folds
- **Aspiration:** Material passes BELOW the level of the vocal folds into the trachea

| PAS Score | Category | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Normal | Material does not enter the airway |
| **2** | Penetration | Material enters the airway, remains above the vocal folds, and is ejected from the airway |
| **3** | Penetration | Material enters the airway, remains above the vocal folds, and is NOT ejected from the airway |
| **4** | Penetration | Material enters the airway, contacts the vocal folds, and IS ejected from the airway |
| **5** | Penetration | Material enters the airway, contacts the vocal folds, and is NOT ejected from the airway |
| **6** | Aspiration | Material enters the airway, passes below the vocal folds, and IS ejected into the larynx or out of the airway |
| **7** | Aspiration | Material enters the airway, passes below the vocal folds, and is NOT ejected from the trachea despite effort |
| **8** | Silent Aspiration | Material enters the airway, passes below the vocal folds, and NO effort is made to eject (no cough reflex) |

> **Board Pearl:** PAS score 8 = Silent Aspiration — material enters the trachea and the patient makes NO effort to clear it. This is the most dangerous finding because the patient and clinicians may be unaware that aspiration is occurring. PAS 8 is a primary indication for nil per os (NPO) status and alternative nutrition.

**Clinical Interpretation:**
- PAS 1-2: Normal to mildly impaired; generally safe for oral diet
- PAS 3-5: Penetration without clearance; increased aspiration risk; may require diet modification and compensatory strategies
- PAS 6-7: Aspiration with some response; requires immediate intervention (diet modification, compensatory strategies, possible NPO)
- PAS 8: Silent aspiration; highest risk; typically requires NPO and instrumental reassessment after treatment

### 11.5 IDDSI Framework

[BOARD] The International Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Initiative (IDDSI) Framework, published in 2016 and now adopted globally, provides standardized terminology and definitions for texture-modified foods and thickened liquids. It replaced the previous patchwork of national systems (NDD in the US, NPSA in the UK, etc.) with a single international standard, improving communication across healthcare settings and countries.

The IDDSI Framework uses a continuum of 8 levels (0-7), identified by numbers, text labels, and color codes:

**Drink Levels (0-4):**

| IDDSI Level | Label | Color | Description | Clinical Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **0** | Thin | White | Regular, unmodified liquids (water, coffee, juice) | Standard fluids; highest aspiration risk for dysphagia patients |
| **1** | Slightly Thick | Gray | Slightly thicker than water; flows quickly through a syringe | Mild dysphagia; slows bolus transit slightly |
| **2** | Mildly Thick | Pink | Flows slower; effort needed to drink through a straw | Moderate dysphagia; previously "nectar thick" |
| **3** | Moderately Thick | Yellow | Cannot drink through straw; can be drunk from a cup; smooth without lumps | More significant dysphagia; previously "honey thick" |
| **4** | Extremely Thick | Green | Cannot be drunk from a cup; must be eaten with a spoon; does not drip | Severe dysphagia; previously "pudding thick" |

**Food Levels (3-7):**

| IDDSI Level | Label | Color | Description | Clinical Application |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **3** | Liquidised | Yellow | Smooth texture; no lumps; can be drunk from a cup or eaten with a spoon | Severe oral/pharyngeal dysfunction |
| **4** | Pureed | Green | Smooth, no lumps; cohesive; falls off spoon in a single spoonful; too thick to drink | Significant chewing or pharyngeal impairment |
| **5** | Minced & Moist | Orange | Soft, moist particles ≤4 mm; can be mashed with fork; minimal chewing required | Moderate chewing difficulty; some tongue control preserved |
| **6** | Soft & Bite-Sized | Blue | Soft, tender pieces ≤1.5 cm; can be mashed with fork; requires some chewing | Mild-moderate chewing difficulty |
| **7** | Regular/Easy to Chew | Black | Normal everyday foods of various textures (Regular); or soft and tender without specific size restrictions (Easy to Chew) | Normal diet or minimal modification needed |

> **Board Pearl:** The IDDSI framework replaces older terminology (NDD levels). Key equivalencies: Thin = IDDSI 0, Nectar-thick ≈ IDDSI 2 (Mildly Thick), Honey-thick ≈ IDDSI 3 (Moderately Thick), Pudding-thick ≈ IDDSI 4 (Extremely Thick). Thin liquids (IDDSI 0) carry the highest aspiration risk in dysphagic patients.

**Key Testing Methods:**
- **Flow test (syringe test):** A standardized 10 mL syringe is used to determine drink levels. The amount of liquid remaining in the syringe after 10 seconds of flow determines the IDDSI level.
- **Fork drip test and spoon tilt test:** Used for food levels to determine appropriate texture classification.
- These standardized methods allow objective verification of diet consistency at the bedside.

### 11.6 Aspiration and Aspiration Pneumonia

#### 11.6.1 Aspiration: Mechanisms and Silent Aspiration

[BOARD] Aspiration occurs when oropharyngeal contents (food, liquid, saliva, or gastric refluxate) pass below the vocal folds into the tracheobronchial tree. After stroke, aspiration can occur by several mechanisms:

1. **Before the swallow (preswallowing aspiration):** Bolus falls into the pharynx and airway before the pharyngeal swallow is triggered, due to delayed swallow initiation or reduced oral control
2. **During the swallow:** Incomplete vocal fold closure, reduced hyolaryngeal excursion, or impaired epiglottic deflection allows material to enter the airway during the pharyngeal phase
3. **After the swallow (postswallowing aspiration):** Residual material pools in the valleculae or piriform sinuses after the swallow and then falls into the reopened airway

**Silent aspiration** — aspiration without any cough reflex or signs of distress — occurs in approximately 40-60% of aspirating stroke patients. It is "silent" because the sensory receptors in the larynx and subglottic space that normally trigger the protective cough reflex are impaired. Silent aspiration is more common with:
- Brainstem strokes (damage to sensory nuclei)
- Bilateral hemispheric strokes
- Reduced level of consciousness
- Severe stroke (high NIHSS)
- Sensory cranial nerve involvement (CN IX, X)

> **Board Pearl:** Silent aspiration is the clinical justification for instrumental swallowing assessment. Since 40-60% of aspiration events are silent (no cough), a "negative" bedside evaluation does NOT rule out aspiration. Any patient with risk factors for silent aspiration or with clinical suspicion despite a negative bedside screen should undergo VFSS or FEES.

#### 11.6.2 Aspiration Pneumonia vs. Aspiration Pneumonitis

[COMPREHENSIVE] These are frequently confused but have different pathophysiology and management:

| Feature | Aspiration Pneumonia | Aspiration Pneumonitis |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Aspiration of colonized oropharyngeal secretions or contaminated material | Aspiration of sterile gastric acid (chemical injury) |
| **Pathophysiology** | Infection (bacterial proliferation in lung parenchyma) | Chemical burn and inflammatory response (NOT initially infectious) |
| **Onset** | Hours to days after aspiration event | Acute — within hours of aspiration |
| **Presentation** | Fever, productive cough, leukocytosis, infiltrate on chest imaging | Acute respiratory distress, hypoxemia, bronchospasm |
| **Typical organisms** | Anaerobes, gram-negative rods, Staphylococcus aureus | No pathogen initially (may become superinfected later) |
| **Treatment** | Antibiotics (empiric coverage for polymicrobial infection) | Supportive care; antibiotics NOT initially indicated |
| **Classic eponym** | None | Mendelson syndrome (aspiration of gastric contents during anesthesia) |
| **Risk factors** | Poor oral hygiene, dysphagia, tube feeding, impaired consciousness | Reduced consciousness, vomiting, gastric distention, supine position |
| **Chest imaging** | Infiltrate in dependent lung segments (RLL most common) | Bilateral or diffuse infiltrates; may progress to ARDS |

> **Board Pearl:** Not all aspiration leads to pneumonia. The development of aspiration PNEUMONIA requires aspiration of colonized material (contaminated with bacteria). This is why oral hygiene is one of the most effective strategies for preventing aspiration pneumonia — it reduces the bacterial load in the oropharynx.

#### 11.6.3 Aspiration Pneumonia Risk Factors and Prevention

[BOARD] **Risk Factors for Aspiration Pneumonia:**
- Poor oral hygiene (colonized oral secretions) — most modifiable risk factor
- Dysphagia with documented aspiration
- Silent aspiration (no protective cough)
- Tube feeding (does NOT eliminate aspiration risk; gastric reflux and aspiration of oral secretions still occur)
- Impaired consciousness or sedation
- Reduced cough effectiveness
- Immune compromise
- Volume and chronicity of aspiration
- Dependent positioning (supine)
- Mechanical ventilation

**Evidence-Based Prevention Strategies:**

1. **Dysphagia screening before any oral intake** — reduces aspiration pneumonia rates from ~5.4% to ~2.4%
2. **Aggressive oral hygiene** — toothbrushing, oral swabs, chlorhexidine rinse; reduces bacterial colonization of oropharynx
3. **Appropriate diet texture modification** (IDDSI-guided)
4. **Compensatory swallowing strategies** (chin tuck, head rotation)
5. **Upright positioning** during and for 30-60 minutes after meals
6. **Supervised feeding** with appropriate pacing
7. **Respiratory hygiene** — incentive spirometry, cough augmentation
8. **Medication review** — minimize sedatives, anticholinergics
9. **Ceftriaxone 2g IV within 12 hours of intubation** — reduces VAP and mortality in intubated stroke patients

### 11.7 Compensatory and Rehabilitative Swallowing Strategies

[BOARD] Swallowing interventions are divided into two categories: **compensatory strategies** (immediate effect, improve safety during eating without changing underlying physiology) and **rehabilitative exercises** (strengthen muscles and improve coordination over time to change swallowing physiology). Some techniques function as both.

#### 11.7.1 Compensatory Strategies (Postural and Bolus Modifications)

| Strategy | Mechanism | Indication | Instructions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chin Tuck (Chin Down)** | Narrows airway entrance; widens valleculae; pushes tongue base posteriorly toward pharyngeal wall | Delayed pharyngeal swallow; reduced tongue base retraction; laryngeal penetration | Tuck chin toward chest before and during swallow |
| **Head Rotation (Turn) to Weak Side** | Closes piriform sinus on the turned side; directs bolus through the stronger pharyngeal channel | Unilateral pharyngeal weakness (e.g., lateral medullary stroke) | Turn head toward the weaker/paretic side during swallow |
| **Head Tilt to Strong Side** | Uses gravity to direct bolus down the stronger side of the oral cavity and pharynx | Unilateral oral weakness | Tilt head toward the stronger side |
| **Bolus Size Reduction** | Smaller boluses are easier to control and less likely to overwhelm weakened pharyngeal clearance | Reduced pharyngeal contraction; delayed swallow trigger | Use smaller spoon; take small sips |
| **Bolus Consistency Modification** | Thicker liquids move more slowly, giving more time for airway protection; softer foods require less chewing | Based on VFSS/FEES findings specific to consistency | Modify per IDDSI level as prescribed |
| **Alternating Solids and Liquids** | Liquid washes residual solid material from pharynx | Pharyngeal residue after solid swallows | Take a sip of liquid between bites of food |

> **Board Pearl:** Chin tuck and head rotation to the weak side are the two most commonly tested compensatory strategies on PM&R board examinations. Chin tuck protects the airway; head rotation to the weak side directs the bolus through the stronger channel.

#### 11.7.2 Rehabilitative Swallowing Exercises

[BOARD]

| Exercise | Target | Mechanism | Instructions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Effortful Swallow** | Tongue base retraction; pharyngeal clearance | Increases posterior tongue base contact with pharyngeal wall; improves bolus propulsion through pharynx | Swallow hard, squeezing all muscles of the throat; push tongue firmly against palate |
| **Mendelsohn Maneuver** | Hyolaryngeal excursion; UES opening | Volitionally prolonging laryngeal elevation during swallow keeps the UES open longer, allowing more time for bolus passage | During swallow, feel the Adam's apple rise; hold it at the highest point for 2-3 seconds before releasing |
| **Supraglottic Swallow** | Airway closure | Voluntary closure of vocal folds before and during swallow; immediate post-swallow cough clears penetrated material | Take a deep breath, hold breath, swallow while still holding, immediately cough after swallow |
| **Super-Supraglottic Swallow** | Enhanced airway closure | Combines supraglottic swallow with effortful swallow for maximal airway protection; closes false vocal folds | Bear down while holding breath, swallow with effort, cough immediately after |
| **Shaker Exercise (Head-Lift)** | UES opening; suprahyoid muscle strengthening | Isometric and isokinetic exercise of suprahyoid muscles (mylohyoid, geniohyoid, anterior digastric) increases UES opening through improved hyolaryngeal excursion | Lying flat: raise head to look at toes (keep shoulders on surface). Isometric: hold 1 min, rest 1 min, repeat 3x. Isokinetic: 30 repetitions of head raises. 3x daily for 6 weeks |
| **Masako Maneuver (Tongue Hold)** | Pharyngeal wall contraction | Tongue protrusion during swallow forces pharyngeal wall to compensate by increasing posterior pharyngeal wall movement | Hold tongue between front teeth (protruded); swallow saliva while holding tongue forward |
| **Lee Silverman Voice Treatment (LSVT)** | Laryngeal adduction; voice and swallowing | Originally developed for Parkinson disease; increases vocal fold adduction and respiratory support | "Think loud" — speak with exaggerated loudness; improves supraglottic closure |

> **Board Pearl:** The Shaker exercise strengthens suprahyoid muscles to improve UES opening. It is the only exercise proven to reduce aspiration by directly improving UES opening diameter. The Mendelsohn maneuver targets the same physiology (UES opening) but through volitional prolongation of an already-occurring movement rather than through exercise-induced strengthening.

### 11.8 Enteral Nutrition: Nasogastric Tube vs. PEG vs. PEJ

[BOARD] When oral nutrition is unsafe or insufficient, enteral feeding is required. The choice of access route depends on expected duration of dysphagia, aspiration risk, and patient status.

#### 11.8.1 Decision Algorithm

```
Dysphagia requiring enteral nutrition?
        |
Expected duration <4 weeks? ──Yes──> Nasogastric (NG) tube
        |
       No (>4 weeks or permanent)
        |
High aspiration risk with ──Yes──> Consider PEJ (post-pyloric)
gastric reflux/vomiting?
        |
       No
        |
PEG tube placement (standard)
```

#### 11.8.2 Comparison of Enteral Access Routes

| Feature | Nasogastric (NG) Tube | PEG (Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy) | PEJ (Percutaneous Endoscopic Jejunostomy) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Indication** | Short-term (< 4 weeks) | Long-term (> 4 weeks) | Long-term with high aspiration risk or gastroparesis |
| **Placement** | Bedside, no sedation needed | Endoscopic, requires sedation | Endoscopic or fluoroscopic, requires sedation |
| **Site** | Nasal passage to stomach | Through abdominal wall into stomach | Through abdominal wall into jejunum |
| **Timing after stroke** | Immediate when NPO indicated | Typically placed at 2-4 weeks if dysphagia persisting; FOOD trial suggested early PEG at 7-14 days is safe | Similar to PEG |
| **Advantages** | Rapid, reversible, no procedure risk | More comfortable, better cosmesis, easier long-term use, better bolus feeding tolerance | Reduced aspiration risk from gastric reflux |
| **Disadvantages** | Nasal irritation, sinusitis, self-removal, aspiration of refluxed feeds, social stigma | Procedure risk (bleeding, infection, peritonitis), stoma complications, does NOT eliminate aspiration risk | Requires continuous feeding (no bolus), clogging, diarrhea, more complex management |
| **Aspiration risk** | Moderate-high (gastric reflux common) | Moderate (gastric reflux still possible) | Lowest (post-pyloric delivery) |
| **Mortality** | Similar mortality to PEG in FOOD trial | Similar mortality to NG in FOOD trial; trend toward worse functional outcomes with early PEG vs. NG in first 2-3 weeks | Limited data; reserved for high-risk patients |

> **Board Pearl:** The FOOD trial (2005) compared NG tube vs. early PEG in dysphagic stroke patients. Early PEG (within 2-3 weeks) was actually associated with a trend toward worse functional outcomes compared with NG tube (absolute risk increase of 7.8% for death or poor outcome), with no significant difference in mortality. Current practice: NG tube as bridge if dysphagia expected to resolve in <4 weeks; PEG if expected to persist beyond 4 weeks (placed after the acute period, not as an early intervention).

[COMPREHENSIVE] **Important Clinical Points About Tube Feeding and Aspiration:**

Tube feeding does NOT eliminate aspiration risk. Patients with PEG tubes can still aspirate:
- Refluxed gastric contents
- Oral secretions (the patient still swallows saliva even when NPO)
- Medications administered through the tube if they reflux

Therefore, even tube-fed patients benefit from:
- Head of bed elevation to 30-45 degrees during and after feeds
- Oral hygiene programs (reducing bacterial colonization of secretions)
- Monitoring for gastric residuals (controversial; current trend is toward liberal thresholds)
- Management of gastroesophageal reflux

### 11.9 Tracheostomy and Swallowing

[COMPREHENSIVE] Tracheostomy is common in stroke patients requiring prolonged mechanical ventilation. The presence of a tracheostomy tube affects swallowing through several mechanisms:

**How Tracheostomy Impairs Swallowing:**
1. **Reduced hyolaryngeal excursion** — The tracheostomy tube tethers the larynx to the anterior neck, limiting upward and forward movement during swallowing
2. **Loss of subglottic air pressure** — Air bypasses the larynx through the tracheostomy, eliminating the subglottic air pressure that normally aids vocal fold closure and generates cough
3. **Disuse atrophy** — Prolonged tracheostomy leads to weakness of pharyngeal and laryngeal musculature
4. **Reduced laryngeal sensation** — Airflow bypass desensitizes the laryngeal mucosa
5. **Cuff effects** — An inflated cuff can compress the esophagus posteriorly (though this is debated) and eliminates all airflow through the larynx

#### 11.9.1 The Passy-Muir Valve (PMV)

[BOARD] The Passy-Muir valve is a one-way speaking valve placed on the hub of the tracheostomy tube. It allows air in through the tracheostomy during inspiration but closes during expiration, redirecting exhaled air upward through the larynx and out through the mouth and nose.

**Benefits of the Passy-Muir Valve for Swallowing:**
- Restores subglottic air pressure, improving vocal fold adduction and cough effectiveness
- Restores sensation to the larynx and pharynx through airflow across the mucosa
- Improves olfaction and taste (air flows through the nasal passages)
- Restores voicing (air passes through the vocal folds)
- Improves hyolaryngeal excursion by restoring more normal airflow dynamics
- Facilitates secretion management

**Requirements for Passy-Muir Valve Use:**
- The tracheostomy cuff MUST be fully deflated (to allow exhaled air to pass around the tube and up through the larynx)
- Adequate airway patency above the tracheostomy
- Patient must tolerate cuff deflation without desaturation
- No complete upper airway obstruction

> **Board Pearl:** The Passy-Muir valve improves swallowing in tracheostomy patients by restoring subglottic air pressure and laryngeal sensation. The tracheostomy CUFF MUST BE DEFLATED before PMV placement. Placing a PMV with an inflated cuff is dangerous and can cause suffocation (air cannot exit in any direction during exhalation).

**Tracheostomy Decannulation and Swallowing:**
Decannulation (removal of the tracheostomy tube) is the ultimate goal. Prerequisites typically include:
- Ability to tolerate cuff deflation for 24-48 hours
- Adequate secretion management (suctioning frequency < every 4-6 hours)
- Adequate oxygenation on room air or minimal supplemental O2
- Safe swallowing (demonstrated on FEES or VFSS with cuff deflated)
- No ongoing need for mechanical ventilation

### 11.10 Special Considerations

#### 11.10.1 Medication Administration in Dysphagia

[COMPREHENSIVE] Patients with dysphagia who are NPO or on modified diets present medication administration challenges:

- **Liquid formulations** should be used when available (note that liquid medications may be thin and pose aspiration risk — can be thickened with direction from pharmacy)
- **Crushing tablets** is acceptable for some medications but NOT for sustained-release, enteric-coated, or sublingual formulations
- **Dissolving in thickened liquid** is preferred for patients on thickened liquids
- **Alternative routes** (IV, transdermal, rectal, sublingual) should be considered
- **Tube administration** requires attention to medication-tube interactions (e.g., phenytoin and tube feeds — separate by 2 hours)

#### 11.10.2 Oral Hygiene Programs

[COMPREHENSIVE] Oral hygiene is one of the most impactful and underutilized interventions for reducing aspiration pneumonia:

- **Toothbrushing** at least twice daily reduces oropharyngeal bacterial colonization
- **Chlorhexidine oral rinse** (0.12%) further reduces bacterial load
- **Suctioning oral secretions** in patients with impaired secretion management
- **Denture care** — ill-fitting dentures should be refitted or replaced; dentures should be cleaned daily
- **Oral assessment** at every nursing shift change

Studies demonstrate that systematic oral hygiene programs reduce aspiration pneumonia rates by approximately 50% in at-risk populations.

#### 11.10.3 Nutritional Screening and Monitoring

[COMPREHENSIVE] Malnutrition is present in 6-62% of stroke patients at admission and worsens during hospitalization. The Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST) is a validated screening instrument:

**MUST Scoring:**

| Component | Score 0 | Score 1 | Score 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| BMI | >20 kg/m2 | 18.5-20 | <18.5 |
| Unplanned weight loss (3-6 months) | <5% | 5-10% | >10% |
| Acute disease effect | None | Acutely ill with no nutritional intake for >5 days (or likely) | — |

- **Score 0:** Low risk — routine screening
- **Score 1:** Medium risk — observe, dietary monitoring
- **Score 2+:** High risk — treat: dietitian referral, nutritional supplementation, enteral feeding if needed

Additional nutritional markers to monitor: serum albumin (half-life 20 days; reflects chronic nutritional status), prealbumin (half-life 2 days; more sensitive to acute changes), body weight trends, caloric intake records.
