## Section 1: Overview of the Cerebral Blood Supply

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The brain receives approximately 15-20% of total cardiac output despite comprising only about 2% of total body weight, reflecting its extraordinarily high metabolic demands. Cerebral blood flow is maintained at approximately 50-60 mL per 100 grams of tissue per minute through the process of cerebral autoregulation, which keeps flow relatively constant across a mean arterial pressure (MAP) range of approximately 60-150 mmHg.

The cerebral blood supply is organized into two major systems:

1. **Anterior (carotid) circulation:** Supplies approximately 80% of total cerebral blood flow; derived from the paired internal carotid arteries
2. **Posterior (vertebrobasilar) circulation:** Supplies approximately 20% of total cerebral blood flow; derived from the paired vertebral arteries that merge to form the basilar artery

These two systems are interconnected at the base of the brain through the Circle of Willis, an arterial anastomosis that provides collateral pathways in the event of proximal vessel occlusion.

| System | Source Arteries | Major Branches | Percentage of CBF | Structures Supplied |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Anterior circulation | Internal carotid arteries (L and R) | MCA, ACA, anterior choroidal, ophthalmic | ~80% | Frontal lobes, parietal lobes, temporal lobes (lateral), basal ganglia, internal capsule |
| Posterior circulation | Vertebral arteries (L and R), basilar artery | PCA, PICA, AICA, SCA, pontine perforators | ~20% | Occipital lobes, brainstem, cerebellum, thalamus, inferior temporal lobes |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The anterior circulation supplies approximately 80% of cerebral blood flow and the posterior circulation supplies approximately 20%. The MCA alone carries the majority of the anterior circulation's flow and is the most commonly affected vessel in stroke. These percentages are commonly tested.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Cerebral Autoregulation:**

Cerebral autoregulation is the brain's ability to maintain constant cerebral blood flow despite fluctuations in systemic blood pressure. This process operates across a MAP range of approximately 60-150 mmHg. Below 60 mmHg, cerebral blood flow decreases linearly with pressure (risking ischemia), and above 150 mmHg, flow increases uncontrollably (risking hypertensive encephalopathy and hemorrhage).

In chronic hypertension, the autoregulatory curve shifts rightward, meaning the brain becomes accustomed to higher perfusion pressures and is vulnerable to ischemia at "normal" blood pressures. This is the physiological basis for permissive hypertension in the acute stroke period: rapidly lowering blood pressure in a chronically hypertensive patient may worsen cerebral ischemia by dropping perfusion below the shifted autoregulatory threshold.

The cerebral perfusion pressure (CPP) is calculated as:

**CPP = MAP - ICP**

where MAP is the mean arterial pressure and ICP is the intracranial pressure. Normal ICP is approximately 5-15 mmHg (the treatment threshold is typically 20 mmHg), and the target CPP is greater than 60 mmHg. Maintaining adequate CPP is a fundamental goal of neurocritical care.

## Section 2: The Circle of Willis

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The Circle of Willis is an arterial anastomotic ring located at the base of the brain, surrounding the optic chiasm and the infundibulum (stalk) of the pituitary gland. It connects the anterior and posterior circulations and provides the potential for collateral blood flow when one or more feeding arteries are compromised.

**Components of the Circle of Willis (listed systematically):**

| Component | Connects | Function |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Anterior communicating artery (AComm) | Left ACA to right ACA | Bridges the two anterior circulations |
| Anterior cerebral arteries (ACA) | A1 segments, left and right | Anterolateral components of the circle |
| Internal carotid arteries (ICA) | Terminal portions, left and right | Feed the anterior circle |
| Posterior communicating arteries (PComm) | ICA to PCA on each side | Bridge anterior and posterior circulations |
| Posterior cerebral arteries (PCA) | P1 segments, left and right | Posterior components of the circle |
| Basilar artery tip | Provides the PCAs | Feeds the posterior circle |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Only 20-25% of the population has a complete, fully functional Circle of Willis. The most common variant is hypoplasia or absence of one or both PComm arteries. Patients with an incomplete circle have less collateral reserve and may tolerate carotid or vertebral occlusion poorly. This is a commonly tested anatomical fact.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Common Anatomical Variants:**

| Variant | Prevalence | Clinical Implication |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| Hypoplastic or absent PComm (one or both) | ~30-40% | Reduced collateral between anterior and posterior systems; higher risk with carotid occlusion |
| Fetal PCA (PCA arises from ICA rather than basilar) | ~15-30% | PCA territory depends on the anterior circulation; carotid disease can cause occipital lobe stroke |
| Hypoplastic A1 segment | ~10-15% | Both ACAs may be supplied by one ICA; occlusion of that ICA can cause bilateral ACA infarcts |
| Absent AComm | ~5% | No cross-filling between anterior circulations |
| Duplicate or fenestrated arteries | Variable | Generally incidental; may be confused with aneurysms on imaging |

The fetal PCA variant is of particular clinical importance. When the PCA arises primarily from the ICA rather than from the basilar artery, carotid stenosis or occlusion can produce visual field deficits (occipital lobe infarction) that would typically be attributed to posterior circulation disease. Approximately 15-30% of the population has a fetal PCA configuration on at least one side.

**Aneurysm Sites:**

The Circle of Willis is the most common location for saccular (berry) aneurysms, which are the primary cause of non-traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage.

| Location | Frequency | Clinical Significance |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| AComm artery | ~30-35% | Most common site; may compress optic chiasm causing bitemporal hemianopia |
| PComm artery | ~25-30% | Second most common; may compress CN III causing ipsilateral pupil dilation and "down and out" eye |
| MCA bifurcation | ~20% | Third most common; may produce focal deficits |
| Basilar tip | ~5-10% | May compress brainstem or CN III |
| ICA (various segments) | ~5-10% | May involve the cavernous sinus |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The AComm is the most common site for saccular aneurysms (board answer: "A is the first letter of the alphabet and is the most common"). The PComm aneurysm classically compresses CN III, producing a "surgical third nerve palsy" with pupil involvement (mydriasis, ptosis, "down and out" eye). In contrast, a "medical third nerve palsy" (from diabetes or ischemia) typically spares the pupil because the parasympathetic fibers run on the outside of the nerve and are affected first by external compression.

## Section 3: Anterior Circulation

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

#### Internal Carotid Artery (ICA)

The internal carotid artery arises from the common carotid bifurcation at approximately the level of the C3-C4 vertebral body (upper border of the thyroid cartilage). It enters the skull through the carotid canal in the petrous temporal bone and traverses the cavernous sinus before terminating in the supraclinoid space by bifurcating into the MCA and ACA.

**Key ICA branches (in order from proximal to distal):**

1. **Ophthalmic artery:** First major intracranial branch; supplies the retina via the central retinal artery. Occlusion causes monocular vision loss (amaurosis fugax if transient, central retinal artery occlusion if permanent). The ophthalmic artery is important because amaurosis fugax is a TIA symptom indicating ipsilateral carotid disease.

2. **Posterior communicating artery (PComm):** Connects the ICA to the PCA; site of aneurysms that can compress CN III.

3. **Anterior choroidal artery:** Arises from the ICA just distal to the PComm origin. Despite its small caliber, it supplies critical deep structures.

4. **Terminal bifurcation:** The ICA terminates by splitting into the MCA (which continues laterally as the direct continuation of the ICA) and the ACA (which courses medially).

#### Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA)

The MCA is the largest branch of the ICA and is the direct continuation of the ICA in terms of blood flow direction. It is the most commonly affected artery in stroke and produces the most recognizable clinical syndrome.

**MCA Segments:**

| Segment | Name | Course | Significance |
|---------|------|--------|-------------|
| M1 | Horizontal/sphenoidal segment | Courses laterally in the Sylvian fissure | Gives off lenticulostriate arteries; most common site of embolic occlusion |
| M2 | Insular segment | Courses over the insula within the Sylvian fissure | Divides into superior and inferior divisions |
| M3 | Opercular segment | Courses over the frontal, temporal, and parietal opercula | Exit from the Sylvian fissure |
| M4 | Cortical segment | Branches over the lateral cortical surface | Terminal cortical branches |

**MCA Divisions:**

| Division | Territory Supplied | Key Structures | Deficits When Occluded (Dominant) | Deficits When Occluded (Non-Dominant) |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Superior division | Frontal and anterior parietal cortex | Motor cortex (face/arm), Broca's area | Broca's aphasia, contralateral face/arm weakness and sensory loss | Contralateral face/arm weakness, motor aprosodia |
| Inferior division | Temporal and posterior parietal cortex | Wernicke's area, angular gyrus, optic radiations | Wernicke's aphasia, hemianopia, Gerstmann syndrome | Left hemispatial neglect, constructional apraxia, dressing apraxia |

**Lenticulostriate Arteries:**

The lenticulostriate arteries are small perforating branches that arise from the M1 segment of the MCA. They are among the most clinically important vessels in cerebrovascular disease.

- Supply the putamen, globus pallidus, caudate head, and posterior limb of the internal capsule
- Called the "arteries of stroke" (named by Jean-Martin Charcot) because they are the most common site of both hypertensive ICH and lacunar infarction
- Lack significant collateral supply; occlusion produces predictable deep infarcts
- Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms form on these vessels in chronic hypertension, predisposing to rupture
- Lipohyalinosis of these vessels is the most common mechanism of lacunar infarction

> **Clinical Pearl:** The lenticulostriate arteries are called the "arteries of stroke" for good reason. Hypertensive rupture of these vessels causes the most common form of ICH (putaminal hemorrhage), and lipohyalinosis of these vessels causes the most common lacunar syndrome (pure motor hemiparesis from posterior limb of internal capsule involvement). Both entities are high-yield board topics.

#### Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA)

The ACA courses medially from the ICA bifurcation, passes over the optic chiasm, and then ascends in the interhemispheric fissure along the medial surface of the frontal and parietal lobes. The two ACAs are connected by the anterior communicating artery (AComm).

**ACA Segments:**

| Segment | Name | Course |
|---------|------|--------|
| A1 | Pre-communicating segment | From ICA bifurcation to AComm |
| A2 | Post-communicating/infracallosal segment | From AComm to genu of corpus callosum |
| A3-A5 | Distal segments | Curve around the corpus callosum |

**ACA Territory:**
- Medial surface of the frontal and parietal lobes
- Motor cortex for the lower extremity (including foot, leg, and hip)
- Sensory cortex for the lower extremity
- Supplementary motor area
- Medial frontal cortex (micturition center, personality, motivation)
- Corpus callosum (anterior portion)
- Cingulate gyrus

**Recurrent artery of Heubner:** A branch of the proximal ACA (A1 or A2) that supplies the head of the caudate nucleus and anterior limb of the internal capsule. Occlusion can produce contralateral face and arm weakness with minimal leg involvement (mimicking an MCA stroke) along with abulia.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

#### Anterior Choroidal Artery

The anterior choroidal artery is a small but clinically significant vessel that arises from the ICA just distal to the PComm origin. Despite its diminutive caliber, it supplies several critical deep brain structures.

**Structures supplied:**
- Posterior limb of the internal capsule (lateral portion)
- Optic tract
- Lateral geniculate body
- Medial temporal lobe (hippocampal region)
- Choroid plexus of the lateral ventricle

**Classic occlusion triad:**
1. Contralateral hemiplegia (internal capsule)
2. Contralateral hemisensory loss (thalamic and internal capsule fibers)
3. Contralateral homonymous hemianopia (optic tract/lateral geniculate body)

This triad can mimic an MCA syndrome, but anterior choroidal artery infarction tends to produce equal involvement of the face, arm, and leg (unlike the face/arm predominance of MCA strokes). In practice, the full syndrome is uncommon because of collateral supply.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The anterior choroidal artery occlusion produces a triad of contralateral hemiplegia, hemisensory loss, and homonymous hemianopia. It mimics MCA stroke but with equal face/arm/leg involvement rather than the face and arm predominance typical of MCA infarction.

## Section 4: Posterior Circulation

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

#### Vertebral Arteries

The vertebral arteries are the first and largest branches of the subclavian arteries. They ascend through the transverse foramina of the cervical vertebrae (C6 through C1), enter the skull through the foramen magnum, and merge at the pontomedullary junction to form the basilar artery.

**Key vertebral artery branches:**
- **Posterior inferior cerebellar artery (PICA):** Arises from the intracranial vertebral artery before the vertebral-basilar junction; supplies the lateral medulla and inferior cerebellar surface
- **Anterior spinal artery:** Formed by branches from both vertebral arteries; supplies the anterior two-thirds of the spinal cord
- **Posterior spinal arteries:** Supply the posterior one-third of the spinal cord

#### Basilar Artery

The basilar artery is formed by the confluence of the two vertebral arteries at the pontomedullary junction. It ascends along the ventral surface of the pons and terminates at the pontomesencephalic junction by bifurcating into the two posterior cerebral arteries.

**Key basilar artery branches:**

| Branch | Origin | Territory | Associated Syndrome |
|--------|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| AICA (Anterior Inferior Cerebellar Artery) | Proximal basilar | Lateral pons, middle cerebellar peduncle, inner ear (via internal auditory artery) | Lateral pontine syndrome: ipsilateral facial paralysis (CN VII), hearing loss (CN VIII), ataxia; contralateral pain/temperature loss |
| SCA (Superior Cerebellar Artery) | Distal basilar | Superior cerebellar surface, superior cerebellar peduncle, rostral pons | Ipsilateral cerebellar ataxia, contralateral pain/temperature loss, ipsilateral Horner syndrome; may produce tremor |
| Pontine perforating arteries | Along basilar trunk | Paramedian and lateral pons | Various pontine syndromes; locked-in syndrome if bilateral paramedian pontine infarction |
| PCA (Posterior Cerebral Artery) | Terminal basilar bifurcation | Occipital lobes, inferior temporal lobes, thalamus, midbrain | Visual field deficits, memory impairment, thalamic syndromes |

> **Clinical Pearl:** Memorizing the three cerebellar arteries and their distinguishing features is essential for board exams. All three produce ipsilateral cerebellar ataxia plus contralateral pain and temperature loss. The distinguishing features: PICA = lateral medullary signs (dysphagia, hoarseness, Horner). AICA = lateral pontine signs (CN VII facial palsy, CN VIII hearing loss). SCA = superior cerebellar signs with possible tremor.

#### Posterior Cerebral Artery (PCA)

The PCA arises from the terminal bifurcation of the basilar artery and supplies the occipital lobe (primary visual cortex), inferior temporal lobe, thalamus (via thalamoperforating and thalamogeniculate branches), and portions of the midbrain.

**PCA Segments:**

| Segment | Name | Course |
|---------|------|--------|
| P1 | Pre-communicating segment | From basilar bifurcation to PComm |
| P2 | Ambient segment | Courses around the midbrain in the ambient cistern |
| P3 | Quadrigeminal segment | Courses around the posterior midbrain |
| P4 | Cortical branches | Terminal cortical branches to occipital and temporal lobes |

**Key PCA perforating branches:**

| Branch Group | Territory | Clinical Significance |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Thalamoperforating arteries | Paramedian thalamus, midbrain | Infarction causes altered consciousness, behavioral changes, vertical gaze palsy |
| Thalamogeniculate arteries | Lateral thalamus | Infarction causes Dejerine-Roussy syndrome (thalamic pain) |
| Posterior choroidal arteries | Posterior thalamus, pineal, choroid plexus | May contribute to hydrocephalus if occluded |

**Artery of Percheron:** A single unpaired artery that arises from one P1 segment and supplies both paramedian thalami. Occlusion produces bilateral thalamic infarction, a distinctive pattern that should immediately suggest this anatomical variant. Patients present with altered consciousness, vertical gaze palsy, memory impairment, and behavioral changes.

> **Clinical Pearl:** The Artery of Percheron is a single vessel that supplies both thalami. Its occlusion produces bilateral paramedian thalamic infarcts, which should be recognized on imaging as a distinctive "butterfly" or symmetric bilateral thalamic pattern. This is a frequently tested anatomical variant.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Vertebral Artery Dominance:**

In most individuals, the vertebral arteries are asymmetric in caliber. The left vertebral artery is dominant (larger) in approximately 50% of people, the right is dominant in approximately 25%, and they are co-dominant in approximately 25%. Vertebral artery dominance has clinical implications: hypoplasia of one vertebral artery reduces the posterior circulation's reserve capacity, and occlusion of the dominant vertebral artery is more likely to produce symptoms than occlusion of the non-dominant artery.

**Subclavian Steal Syndrome:**

When the subclavian artery is stenosed proximal to the origin of the vertebral artery, blood flow in the ipsilateral vertebral artery may reverse direction (retrograde flow), "stealing" blood from the posterior circulation to supply the subclavian artery and the ipsilateral arm. The resulting vertebrobasilar insufficiency can produce dizziness, diplopia, ataxia, or syncope, typically triggered by arm exercise. In practice, subclavian steal is usually asymptomatic unless concurrent carotid disease reduces the collateral capacity of the anterior circulation.

## Section 5: Vascular Territories and the Homunculus

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

The motor and sensory homunculus is a topographic representation of the body on the cerebral cortex. Understanding its relationship to vascular territories is essential for localizing stroke syndromes.

**Key Mapping Principles:**

The homunculus is arranged with the leg and foot represented on the medial surface of the hemisphere (ACA territory) and the face, tongue, and hand represented on the lateral surface (MCA territory). The representation is "inverted" such that the foot and genitalia are at the top of the hemisphere (medially) and the face is at the bottom (laterally near the Sylvian fissure).

| Body Part | Cortical Location | Vascular Territory | Deficit Pattern in Stroke |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Foot, leg, hip | Medial cortical surface | ACA | ACA stroke: leg > arm/face weakness |
| Trunk, shoulder | ACA-MCA watershed zone | Borderzone | Watershed infarct: proximal arm/leg weakness ("man in a barrel") |
| Hand, fingers | Lateral cortical surface (large representation) | MCA | MCA stroke: hand and fine motor deficits prominent |
| Face, tongue, throat | Lateral cortical surface (inferior) | MCA | MCA stroke: facial droop, dysarthria, dysphagia |

- The hand and face have disproportionately large cortical representations, meaning that even small infarcts in these areas produce significant functional deficits
- ACA stroke produces leg-predominant weakness (leg > arm > face) because the leg cortex is medial
- MCA stroke produces face and arm-predominant weakness (face/arm > leg) because the face and arm cortex is lateral
- The tongue, pharynx, and larynx cortex is located inferiorly near the Sylvian fissure in the MCA territory

> **Clinical Pearl:** "Upside-down man on the brain": the homunculus is inverted, with the foot medially (ACA) and the face laterally (MCA). ACA infarct = leg weakness. MCA infarct = face/arm weakness. If you see leg-predominant weakness, think ACA. If you see face/arm-predominant weakness, think MCA. This is one of the most fundamental stroke localization principles.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Cortical Organization Beyond the Homunculus:**

The primary motor cortex (Brodmann area 4) is located in the precentral gyrus and is organized somatotopically according to the homunculus. The primary somatosensory cortex (Brodmann areas 3, 1, 2) is located in the postcentral gyrus with a similar somatotopic arrangement.

Beyond motor and sensory cortex, the lateral cortical surface (MCA territory) also contains the language centers (Broca's area in the inferior frontal gyrus and Wernicke's area in the posterior superior temporal gyrus) in the dominant hemisphere, and the areas responsible for spatial attention and awareness in the non-dominant hemisphere. This is why MCA strokes produce aphasia (dominant hemisphere) or neglect (non-dominant hemisphere) in addition to motor and sensory deficits.

The supplementary motor area (SMA) is located on the medial frontal cortex (ACA territory) and is involved in motor planning, particularly for bilateral and sequential movements. SMA lesions from ACA infarction can produce transient akinetic mutism or motor neglect.

## Section 6: Major Cerebral Arteries and Their Territories (Comprehensive Summary)

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

| Artery | Territory Supplied | Key Structures | Classic Deficit Pattern |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| MCA | Lateral hemisphere | Motor/sensory cortex (face, arm), Broca's and Wernicke's areas, basal ganglia, internal capsule (lenticulostriate branches) | Contralateral face/arm > leg weakness, aphasia (dominant) or neglect (non-dominant), hemianopia |
| ACA | Medial hemisphere | Motor/sensory cortex (leg), SMA, corpus callosum, cingulate gyrus | Contralateral leg > arm weakness, urinary incontinence, gait apraxia, personality changes |
| PCA | Occipital and inferior temporal lobes | Primary visual cortex, thalamus, hippocampus, medial temporal lobe | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing, memory impairment, visual agnosia |
| Anterior choroidal | Deep structures | Posterior limb of internal capsule (lateral), optic tract, lateral geniculate body | Contralateral hemiplegia + hemisensory loss + hemianopia (equal face/arm/leg) |
| Basilar (perforators) | Pons | Pontine nuclei, corticospinal tracts, CN nuclei V-VIII | Various pontine syndromes; complete occlusion = locked-in syndrome |
| Vertebral/PICA | Lateral medulla, inferior cerebellum | Lateral medullary structures, vestibular nuclei, inferior cerebellar peduncle | Wallenberg syndrome (crossed sensory loss, Horner, dysphagia, ataxia) |
| AICA | Lateral pons, middle cerebellum | Lateral pons, CN VII, CN VIII (internal auditory artery), middle cerebellar peduncle | Lateral pontine syndrome (facial paralysis, hearing loss, ataxia) |
| SCA | Superior cerebellum, rostral pons | Superior cerebellar surface, superior cerebellar peduncle | Cerebellar ataxia, contralateral pain/temperature loss, possible Horner |

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Collateral Circulation:**

The brain has multiple levels of collateral circulation that can partially compensate for vessel occlusion:

1. **Circle of Willis:** Primary collateral pathway; provides cross-filling between hemispheres (via AComm) and between anterior and posterior circulations (via PComm)
2. **Leptomeningeal anastomoses (pial collaterals):** Connections between distal branches of the ACA, MCA, and PCA on the cortical surface; these are the primary collaterals for slowly progressive stenosis
3. **External carotid to internal carotid connections:** Via the ophthalmic artery (ECA -> facial/maxillary -> ophthalmic -> ICA); can provide flow in chronic ICA occlusion
4. **Tectal and posterior pericallosal anastomoses:** Minor posterior collateral pathways

The adequacy of collateral circulation is a major determinant of stroke severity. Two patients with identical vessel occlusions can have vastly different outcomes depending on the robustness of their collateral networks. This is why some patients with chronic ICA occlusion may be asymptomatic while others with the same finding have devastating strokes.

## Section 7: Watershed (Borderzone) Infarcts

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Watershed infarcts (also called borderzone infarcts) occur at the junction between two adjacent vascular territories, where perfusion pressure is lowest. These infarcts are caused by systemic hypoperfusion (severe hypotension, cardiac arrest, cardiac surgery) rather than focal vessel occlusion, though they can also occur in the setting of severe proximal vessel stenosis combined with a hypotensive episode.

**Types of Watershed Infarcts:**

| Type | Location | Between Which Territories | Classic Presentation |
|------|----------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| External (cortical) anterior | Superior frontal and parasagittal cortex | ACA-MCA borderzone | Proximal arm weakness, shoulder weakness ("man in a barrel" if bilateral) |
| External (cortical) posterior | Parieto-occipital cortex | MCA-PCA borderzone | Visual disturbances, transcortical sensory aphasia |
| Internal (subcortical) | Corona radiata, periventricular white matter | Deep perforating branches of MCA | Hemiparesis that may mimic lacunar stroke |

> **Clinical Pearl:** The "man in a barrel" syndrome results from bilateral anterior watershed infarcts between the ACA and MCA territories. The patient has bilateral proximal arm weakness (arms hang at sides like a person trapped in a barrel) with relative preservation of the face, hands, and legs. This localizes to the border zone where the ACA and MCA territories meet on the cortical convexity, which corresponds to the proximal arm representation on the homunculus. Think of this syndrome whenever you see bilateral proximal upper extremity weakness following a period of severe hypotension.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Mechanisms of Watershed Infarction:**

Watershed infarcts can occur through two mechanisms:

1. **Hemodynamic (low-flow):** Systemic hypotension reduces perfusion throughout the brain, but the watershed zones are affected first because they are at the "end of the pipeline" and receive the lowest perfusion pressure. This is the classic mechanism seen after cardiac arrest, severe hemorrhage, or prolonged hypotension during surgery.

2. **Embolic (microembolic shower):** Small emboli that are too small to occlude a major vessel may preferentially lodge in the watershed zones where the small caliber of terminal arterioles creates a natural "sieve" effect. This mechanism may contribute to watershed infarcts seen in the setting of carotid stenosis, where artery-to-artery microembolism combines with hemodynamic compromise.

In clinical practice, watershed infarcts often occur in the setting of preexisting severe carotid stenosis superimposed on an episode of systemic hypotension. The stenosis limits the upstream perfusion pressure, and the hypotension drops it below the critical threshold in the watershed territories.

**Imaging Features:**

Watershed infarcts have a characteristic appearance on MRI: they are typically small, multiple, and linearly arranged along the border between two vascular territories. The cortical watershed infarcts appear as a "string of pearls" pattern in the parasagittal cortex, while the internal watershed infarcts appear as a "rosary" pattern in the periventricular white matter.

## Section 8: Venous Drainage of the Brain

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

While stroke is most commonly arterial in origin, cerebral venous thrombosis (CVT) is an important cause of both venous infarction and hemorrhagic stroke, particularly in young adults and in peripartum women.

**Superficial Venous System:**
- Superior sagittal sinus: Runs along the superior midline; drains into the confluence of sinuses
- Lateral (transverse) sinuses: Drain posterolaterally into the sigmoid sinuses and then into the internal jugular veins
- Superficial cerebral veins: Drain the cortical surface into the superior sagittal sinus and lateral sinuses

**Deep Venous System:**
- Internal cerebral veins: Paired veins draining the deep cerebral structures
- Vein of Galen: Formed by the confluence of the internal cerebral veins; a midline structure
- Straight sinus: Formed by the vein of Galen and the inferior sagittal sinus; drains into the confluence of sinuses

**Cavernous Sinus:**
- Located lateral to the sella turcica
- Contains the ICA and CN VI within the sinus; CN III, CN IV, and V1/V2 run within the lateral wall
- Cavernous sinus thrombosis produces: painful ophthalmoplegia, proptosis, chemosis, CN VI palsy (lateral rectus weakness is the earliest sign because CN VI traverses through the sinus itself)

## Section 9: Cerebrospinal Fluid Circulation and Hydrocephalus

### [BOARD] Key Concepts

Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is produced primarily by the choroid plexus within the lateral ventricles (approximately 500 mL/day; total CSF volume at any time is approximately 150 mL). The normal flow pathway is:

**Lateral ventricles** -> (Foramen of Monro) -> **Third ventricle** -> (Cerebral aqueduct of Sylvius) -> **Fourth ventricle** -> (Foramina of Luschka [lateral] and Magendie [medial]) -> **Subarachnoid space** -> absorbed by arachnoid granulations into the superior sagittal sinus

**Hydrocephalus Types:**

| Type | Mechanism | Communication Status | Key Features | Common Causes |
|------|-----------|---------------------|-------------|---------------|
| Communicating (non-obstructive) | Impaired CSF absorption at arachnoid granulations | CSF pathways are patent | All ventricles dilated; SAH blood products can clog granulations | SAH, meningitis, NPH |
| Non-communicating (obstructive) | Physical obstruction of CSF flow within ventricles | CSF pathways are blocked | Ventricles dilated proximal to obstruction only | Tumor, aqueductal stenosis, IVH |

**Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH):**

NPH is a form of communicating hydrocephalus that is important in rehabilitation medicine. It presents with the classic triad (in order of appearance):
1. Gait disturbance (magnetic, shuffling gait; first and most responsive to treatment)
2. Urinary incontinence (second)
3. Dementia (third and least responsive to treatment)

The mnemonic is "Wacky, Wet, and Wobbly" (dementia, incontinence, gait disorder). Diagnosis involves large-volume lumbar puncture (removing 30-50 mL of CSF) with assessment for clinical improvement; definitive treatment is ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt placement.

> **Clinical Pearl:** In the context of stroke, hydrocephalus most commonly occurs as a complication of SAH (communicating, from blood products blocking arachnoid granulations) or cerebellar hemorrhage/infarction (non-communicating, from posterior fossa mass effect compressing the fourth ventricle). Acute hydrocephalus from posterior fossa pathology is a neurosurgical emergency requiring external ventricular drainage.

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

**Post-Stroke Hydrocephalus:**

Hydrocephalus is a serious complication of hemorrhagic stroke:

- **After SAH:** Acute hydrocephalus develops in approximately 20-30% of SAH patients within the first 72 hours, caused by blood products blocking CSF flow through the ventricular system or impairing absorption at the arachnoid granulations. Chronic hydrocephalus (requiring VP shunt) develops in approximately 10-20% of SAH survivors.

- **After ICH with intraventricular extension (IVH):** Blood within the ventricles can obstruct CSF flow, particularly at the narrow cerebral aqueduct or fourth ventricle outflow foramina. External ventricular drainage (EVD) is indicated for IVH with hydrocephalus.

- **After cerebellar stroke:** Both cerebellar hemorrhage and large cerebellar infarction (particularly PICA territory) can cause obstructive hydrocephalus by compressing the fourth ventricle. This is a neurosurgical emergency requiring posterior fossa decompression and/or EVD placement.

## Section 10: The Spinal Cord Blood Supply

### [COMPREHENSIVE] Detailed Discussion

Although spinal cord stroke is far less common than cerebral stroke, it is tested on board examinations and has important rehabilitation implications.

**Arterial Supply:**
- **Anterior spinal artery:** Formed by branches from both vertebral arteries; supplies the anterior two-thirds of the spinal cord (corticospinal tracts, spinothalamic tracts, anterior horn motor neurons)
- **Posterior spinal arteries (paired):** Supply the posterior one-third of the spinal cord (dorsal columns)
- **Radicular arteries:** Segmental arteries from the aorta that reinforce spinal cord blood flow; the largest is the **artery of Adamkiewicz** (great radicular artery), which arises from the aorta at approximately the T9-T12 level on the left side and is the major blood supply to the lower two-thirds of the spinal cord

**Anterior Spinal Artery Syndrome:**

Occlusion of the anterior spinal artery produces the most common spinal cord vascular syndrome:
- Bilateral motor paralysis below the level of the lesion (corticospinal tracts)
- Bilateral loss of pain and temperature below the level (spinothalamic tracts)
- Preservation of dorsal column function: proprioception, vibration, and fine touch are spared (supplied by the posterior spinal arteries)
- Autonomic dysfunction (bladder, bowel, blood pressure)

> **Clinical Pearl:** Anterior spinal artery syndrome is characterized by bilateral motor paralysis with loss of pain and temperature sensation but sparing of proprioception and vibration (dorsal columns). The most common cause is aortic surgery or aortic dissection compromising flow through the artery of Adamkiewicz. This dissociated sensory loss pattern (spinothalamic lost, dorsal column spared) is a classic board question.
